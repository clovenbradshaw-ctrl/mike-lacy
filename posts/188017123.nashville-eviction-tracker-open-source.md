I’m very aware this can be used to hold against people, and it’s why my version of the site had this info redacted. But here’s what I want to make clear: that database already exists. </p><p>The free version of our General Sessions Court platform provides a very easy tool to type in anyone’s name and see if someone tried to get them evicted from a property. There’s good reason to think that many if not most landlords have access to platforms that gather data from sources like this when evaluating potential tenants. </p><p>This is why having even just an attempted, bad-faith eviction on your record can be catastrophic for your ability to stay housed. People who use these tools will, however, tell you that it is the best way to protect themselves and hold people accountable when they mistreat people’s property or break contracts.</p><p>I would like to use the same logic and say that we need ways to hold landlords, lawyers, judges, and Council Members accountable just as much (if not more) for how they shape our housing system. But, we have not been given the tools to do that easily. And when you look at what the data reveals, you can see why.</p><p>So often we use the talking points and data points given to us by the developer community: number of homes, area median income, median rent. But there is much more to this complex system that is leading to people becoming rent burdened and, for an increasing number of Nashvillians, homeless.</p><p>I’m providing this code because it can help build projects that can accomplish things that hold <em>the system</em> accountable. It can be used to provide our city vital feedback about how our housing system is operating. It can help build a dataset that can see this:</p><ol><li><p><strong>Evictions by Council Districts</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>The landlords who evict the most people</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>The shockingly few law firms that do the bulk of our city’s evictions</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>The judgement amounts that people are being charged with</strong></p></li></ol><p>And with more tweaking, it could do much more, such as tell you how many people are given judgements without actually appearing in court.</p><p>Our society’s priorities show up in what we bother to make useful and easy. </p><p>We’ve built data platforms that are useful and easy if you want to have a reason to reject a potential tenant. We’ve made it very hard, however, to tell who is operating an eviction mill and profiting off the dislocation of our neighbors, leading many of them to slide into homelessness and become wards of the state in some form or the other. It has been hard to tell how much this has happened, where, and to whom.</p><p>Until now.</p></blockquote><div class="pullquote"><p>Information is power.

I was on the phone with Francis this past Sunday morning talking about her difficulty receiving the funds from the Community Safety grant her domestic violence support non-profit had been awarded while I was at the Mayor’s Office. Everything seemed fine then. By that night she was in the hospital and was told she’d been unconscious for 10 minutes, suffered a grand mal seizure, and if not for close by first responders, she would have died.

Back to that morning.

I haven’t written publicly about this. I think it’s just been too raw. I’ve been transposing a lot of my anguish about this onto Metro Arts, a separate, yet disturbingly similar situation where small organizations are treated not just as second-class citizens, but often spoken of in terms reminiscent of Reagan-era buzzwords of Welfare Queens or Benefits Cheats.

We talked for about an hour, catching up, I advised her on who could help her navigate Nashville Metro’s bureaucracy. It had been a long road for Francis to get this $45,000 grant to provide support for domestic violence victims.

Francis (which is not her real name), is a domestic violence survivor herself. She founded the organization in 2015 to help others come to terms with the violence and disdain that had been inflicted upon them.

Before the Mayor’s Office of Community Safety gave Francis her first grant, there never had been much of anything in the non-profit’s bank account that didn’t immediately go to events and support services for survivors.

Her group was exactly who the Mayor’s Office of Community Safety under Mayor Cooper (now defunct) was meant to support, grassroots community groups that were victims of the “first dollar problem,” the non-profit equivalent of the Hollywood Catch-22 that you can only get in a film if you have an agent, and you can only have an agent if you’re in movies.

We connected her with the professional development services provided by The Village, and her small seed of an organization began to germinate.

In October the City Council awarded her a $45,000 grant. She wasn’t given the money outright, but things were on the up and up. And then they weren’t.

A new board member decided that Francis wasn’t the type of person who should be trusted with $45,000, despite the City Council and Mayor’s Office thinking differently, and an attempted coup took place.

According to Francis, this board member, a stranger before joining the board, allegedly cyber stalked with fake social media accounts and spread rumors about her. It culminated in Francis considering returning the grant and shuttering her non-profit to make the board member go away.

The other members of Francis’ board took her side and the attempted Executive Directorcide was averted. But by this point, John Cooper was no longer Mayor and the Office of Community Safety now did not exist, and the funds had transitioned to the Metro Health Department’s Behavioral Health and Wellness Division.

Something went wrong during the transition of funds from the Mayor’s Office of Community Safety and the Health Department.

I haven’t written publicly about this. I think it’s just been too raw. I’ve been transposing a lot of my anguish about this onto Metro Arts, a separate, yet disturbingly similar situation where small organizations are treated not just as second-class citizens, but often spoken of in terms reminiscent of Reagan-era buzzwords of Welfare Queens or Benefits Cheats.

But now that’s it’s budget season, I’ve been pushed by others to speak out more directly on this issue that close to my heart.

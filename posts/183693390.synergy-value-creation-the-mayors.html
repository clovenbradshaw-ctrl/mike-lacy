ChoicePoint’s History Prior to Wilson’s “synergy value creation”

Nashville’s Chief of Operations and Performance’s LinkedIn boasts that she “coordinated the overall integration of the $4.1B acquisition of ChoicePoint by LexisNexis in 2008, resulting in over $150M in annual synergy value creation.”

That’s the language of corporate achievement—integration, acquisition, synergy value creation. It says nothing about what was actually being integrated: the databases, the contracts, the institutional relationships, the corporate DNA. To understand what Wilson spent three years folding into LexisNexis Risk Solutions, you have to examine what ChoicePoint actually was—and what ChoicePoint itself had absorbed along the way.

An International Data Heist

In April 2002, FOIA documents obtained by the Electronic Privacy Information Center revealed that ChoicePoint held contracts with the Immigration and Naturalization Service (INS) to provide “citizen registry, motor vehicle, and other information for Brazil, Argentina, Mexico, Columbia, and Costa Rica.”

The revelation triggered investigations across Latin America. According to the Chicago Tribune, ChoicePoint purchased the information for $250,000 in 2001 from a Mexican consumer information company called Bases de Datos. Bases de Datos claimed it bought the information from another company—whose representative “apparently has gone into hiding” before investigators could question him. ChoicePoint returned 10 disks containing voter information to Mexican authorities, but, as the Tribune reported, “Mexican prosecutors want[ed] proof–beyond a company news release–that ChoicePoint destroyed its original copy of the suspected voter files.”

In response, the U.S. Department of Homeland Security stopped using ChoicePoint’s data on Mexican citizens, a department spokesman said. “It was an extremely useful database, but it was [only] one of the tools we used,” said Garrison Courtney, the spokesman. “We have a lot of other, different databases.”

Meanwhile, Mexican prosecutors want proof–beyond a company news release–that ChoicePoint destroyed its original copy of the suspected voter files. The Mexicans sent a delegation to company offices in Alpharetta, Ga., to witness the erasing of the files, but the company first requested a statement from Mexico exonerating its employees of any wrongdoing.

The 2000 Election Connection

A subsidiary of ChoicePoint, Database Technology (DBT), also found itself at the crossroads of electoral scandal, and may have actually tipped the 2000 election in favor of George W. Bush.

DBT Online provided Florida with the voter purge lists used in the 2000 presidential election. The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights found that state officials instructed DBT to use loose matching criteria—accepting 90 percent name matches rather than exact matches—over the company’s objections. DBT vice president George Bruder testified that supervisors of elections present “wanted to be as exacting as possible on the matches,” but was overruled. Approximately 8,000 people who committed misdemeanors in Texas were incorrectly identified as Florida felons. George W. Bush’s certified margin of victory was 537 votes.

ChoicePoint acquired DBT Online on May 16, 2000—mere months after Florida contracted for the purge lists but before Election Day. The $440 million deal made the voter file contract ChoicePoint’s responsibility through the contested election and subsequent congressional investigations.

This was the company Wilson helped consolidate into LexisNexis.

By September 2009, ChoicePoint was fully integrated into LexisNexis Risk Solutions—the same entity that now holds a $22.1 million contract with ICE. The data broker that helped determine who could vote in Florida in 2000 is now helping determine who gets deported in 2025. And the person who integrated that infrastructure is now directing Nashville’s surveillance procurement.

The Pinkerton Connection

The surveillance apparatus Wilson helped integrate into LexisNexis had roots stretching back to one of the most notorious names in American labor history.

The Pinkerton National Detective Agency, founded in 1850, became synonymous with violent strikebreaking. Between 1866 and 1892, Pinkertons participated in 70 labor disputes opposing over 125,000 strikers. The 1892 Homestead Strike—where 300 Pinkerton agents battled Carnegie steelworkers, leaving twelve dead—prompted Congress to pass the Anti-Pinkerton Act of 1893, banning federal agencies from hiring the company. The La Follette Committee hearings (1936-1941) later exposed how the agency had pivoted from armed force to industrial espionage, maintaining labor spies “in practically every union in the country.”

By the 1990s, Pinkerton had rebranded as a security and risk management firm. In 1999, Swedish security giant Securitas AB acquired Pinkerton for $384 million. But the agency’s employee screening division—the descendant of its labor surveillance apparatus—took a different path.

In July 2001, ChoicePoint acquired Pinkerton’s pre-employment screening and drug testing business. The deal included Esteem, described as “a contributory database of retail employees who have admitted to workplace theft”—in practice, a corporate blacklist. Subscribing retailers would submit “admission statements” extracted during pressure-filled interrogations by store loss prevention officers. Workers—often coerced into signing without understanding the consequences—would be flagged and locked out of retail employment industry-wide for seven years, even when no criminal charges were ever filed.

When LexisNexis acquired ChoicePoint in 2008, Esteem came with it. LexisNexis eventually settled the resulting class action in 2014 and suspended the database acknowledging that “Esteem “retail theft contributory database” wrongfully labels job seekers as criminals even if they have not been convicted of a crime”, but not before it contributed to the “$150M in annual synergy value creation” Ms. Wilson’s resume highlights. Along with the profits came the Pinkerton surveillance model, absorbed into the data broker that now contracts with all levels of law enforcement, including ICE.

LexisNexis Risk Solutions Today

The corporate entity that emerged from the ChoicePoint integration now holds a $22.1 million contract with ICE running through 2026.

ICE budgetary justification documents, cited in an 80-organization coalition letter to DHS, state the agency requires LexisNexis access because “policy or legislative changes” have caused “an increase in the number of law enforcement agencies and state or local governments that do not share information about real time incarceration of foreign-born nationals with ICE.”

Civil liberties organizations have characterized this as ICE using data brokers to circumvent sanctuary policies. A 2022 Cook County hearing—the first of its kind nationwide—investigated whether ICE’s use of LexisNexis data violated the county’s sanctuary ordinance.

ICE’s express intentions in forming this contract were to subvert local democratic lawmaking: ICE contracted with LexisNexis for the purpose of subverting sanctuary laws.[183] According to contract documents, ICE required access to the Justice Intelligence database (a database containing incarceration records and jail booking data) due to “policy or legislative changes” that have resulted in an “increase in the number of law enforcement agencies and state or local governments that do not share information about real time incarceration of foreign-born nationals with ICE.”[184]

The Broader Context

The LexisNexis acquisition of ChoicePoint occurred during a specific moment in American history: the post-9/11 expansion of domestic surveillance infrastructure. When lawmakers defunded Total Information Awareness in 2003, similar capabilities migrated to private data brokers and intelligence agencies.

The 2008 merger consolidated two significant nodes in this privatized data network:
Seisint, Inc., which had built the MATRIX system for the Florida Department of Law Enforcement (and which LexisNexis later acquired for $775 million in September 2004) and ChoicePoint, which had been selling Latin American government databases to U.S. immigration authorities since before DHS existed.

Tennessee’s Information Protection Act (TIPA), effective July 1, 2025, explicitly preempts local governments from enacting stronger privacy protections—including Metro Nashville. Section 47-18-3315 bars local ordinances that conflict with state data privacy standards. Simultaneously, TIPA authorizes data controllers and processors to share information with law enforcement whenever they “reasonably and in good faith” believe someone may have violated any law—including federal immigration law. No warrant required. No subpoena. Just a reasonable belief. Nashville has no mechanism to prevent surveillance vendors from sharing data with ICE, and state law now prohibits the city from creating one.

Wilson coordinated the integration of ChoicePoint and LexisNexis. LexisNexis Risk Solutions now holds ICE contracts that the agency says are necessary because of sanctuary policies. Wilson is involved in Nashville’s surveillance technology coordination.

ChoicePoint held contracts with the Immigration and Naturalization Service (INS) to provide citizen registry and identification data for Latin American countries before DHS even existed. Wilson coordinated the integration of ChoicePoint into LexisNexis in 2008, and according to her own LinkedIn and the City of Atlanta’s official press release, she “provided oversight of integration projects across all businesses and functions.” The merged entity became LexisNexis Risk Solutions. LexisNexis Risk Solutions now holds a $22.1 million contract with ICE for its Accurint product—a surveillance database built on the combined data infrastructure of both companies. ICE agents ran over 1.2 million searches in LexisNexis databases over a seven-month period.

Wilson did not create ChoicePoint’s immigration enforcement business. But she integrated it into the corporate entity that still profits from it today.

The MOU Fight

Following sustained community opposition, Council amendments stripped explicit line items for LeoSight and Fivecast from the MOU. But despite removing those surveillance platforms, the Mayor’s Office still appears to be strong-arming the Nashville Downtown Partnership to provide Metro with significant police and surveillance infrastructure:

  • CSC Video: Centralized display system to monitor live camera feeds

  • Armored Rescue Vehicle: SWAT transport vehicle

  • Mobile Command Post: Vehicle-based command center for “large events and security incidents”

  • Tactical Support Post: Mobile SWAT command post

Community advocates are asking that a new MOU be drafted between Council and the Nashville District Management Corporation—the entity that actually received the state grant and contracts with NDP—rather than routing everything through the Mayor’s Office. Ironically, early indications suggest the community’s proposed structure would actually increase funding available to NDP, allowing more resources for public restrooms, outreach services, and safety ambassadors rather than police equipment.

These facts are documented in the public record. Readers can assess for themselves what connections, if any, exist between them.

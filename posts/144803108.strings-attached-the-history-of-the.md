The Symphony’s negotiator was Phil Bredesen.</p><p></p><p></p><div><hr></div><div class="captioned-image-container"><figure><a class="image-link image2 is-viewable-img" target="_blank" Tchaikovsky </h2><p></p><p>All the history I give here is from contemporary news sources, mostly from <a href="https://www.proquest.com/hnpnashvilletennesseanshell/publication/publications_2044080?accountid=33208">The Tennessean Archives,</a> which track the Symphony’s financial crisis from start to… well… it seems to have never really finished.</p><p>The 1988 season was gearing up to have a $900k shortfall <a href="https://wpln.org/post/timeline-nashville-symphonys-fiscal-ups-and-downs/">according to Bredesen and the board</a>, though the Orchestra union leaders thought this figure was exaggerated.</p><p>The nearly million dollar purported deficit was seen by some as a <a href="https://drive.google.com/drive/u/0/search?q=1988">negotiation tactic</a> to lower the Orchestra union’s demands. But Bredesen <a href="https://drive.google.com/file/d/1_JJM3TEgJKcDG6hB-RLJzVNeh1s6I_-x/view?usp=sharing">drove a hard bargain</a>: reduce the number of performers, lower your wages, and sign the contract, <em>or we file for bankruptcy.</em></p><p>The Tennessean described it as the board “lobbing the ball” onto the Orchestra’s side of the court, pushing the blame onto artists for their greed in fighting for a living wage from a board comprised of millionaires.</p><p>The striking Orchestra members held tense meetings, calling the Board’s bluff and waiting for them to back down. A June 13, 1988, <a href="https://www.proquest.com/hnpnashvilletennesseanshell/docview/1909016052/45C09E08953145A8PQ/1?accountid=33208">Tennessean article</a> retells the last night before the bankruptcy filing deadline.</p><blockquote><p>Unemployed Nashville Symphony musicians met behind closed doors last night and early today in an 11 hour attempt to stop orchestra boardmembers from filing for bankruptcy protection today.</p><p>Symphony board member Phil Bredesen was waiting outside the Blair School of Music auditorium at Vanderbllt University late last night In hopes of negotiating with the musicians who began their meeting at about 9 p.m. </p><p>"If they vote to allow me Into the meeting, I think we'll be able to work things out in an hour or two," Bredesen said.</p></blockquote><p>This was Bredesen’s offer:</p><p>To reduce the Symphony from 86 to 60 players, and to cut their base salaries from $17,500 to $15,873 ($42k a year in 2024 dollars).</p><p>Bredesen, it must be stated, was a negotiator holding out over a $1,627 salary cut. Having sold his controlling interest in HealthAmerica Corp two years earlier, he was worth at least <em>two hundred million dollars</em>.</p><p>The image of that night is stark and cinematic to me. The northerner businessman, worth nearly a quarter billion dollars, sitting outside an auditorium, daring striking musicians paid less than half the area median income to blink first and accept their subjugation, or be blamed for killing the Symphony.</p><p>The musicians didn’t blink. </p><p>They didn’t even let Bredesen in the room that night.</p><p>Bredesen and the board filed the Bankruptcy documents in Federal Court the next day.</p><p>And still the union held.</p><p>Apparently, it takes a while to go bankrupt, there’s more than just one document you gotta hand in, and the Board wasn’t ready to tell Nashville that it was a major metropolitan city, “Music City” no less, with no Symphony. </p><p>In July, Bredesen brought a new deal back to the union, this time with a smaller reduction of players, and something new. </p><p>Bredesen offered the union a carrot, a show of good faith from <em>the city</em> that would help make up for the fundraising shortfalls of the board and management.</p><p>Along with a smaller cut to the number of players and a new head of fundraising, the “Orchestra's executive committee” the Tennesseean wrote, “have recently been encouraged by a verbal agreement between symphony board chairwoman Martha Ingram and Mayor Bill Boner.” </p><p>That agreement was for a new type of funding mechanism from the city that, in this first year alone, would add $250,000 to the Symphony’s coffers.&nbsp;</p><p>Mayor Bill Boner (whom Bredesen lost to in his bid for mayor the previous year) offered the Symphony a quarter million dollars that would be distributed by the Metro Arts Commission in exchange for free public concerts.&nbsp;</p><p>This was the invention of Metro Arts funding as we know it today. It was not a well-thought-out plan for how to best invest in the city’s art, to enhance its vibrancy or character. It was, to recycle Arts Commissioner Nefflin’s words, a “selfish and shortsighted” backroom deal that used public funds to lure working-class artists off the picket line.</p><p>It would change the city forever.</p><p></p><p></p><div><hr></div><h2>The Fall of Boner &amp; The Election of the Symphony’s Savior</h2><p></p><p></p><p>This is the fun part where we get to talk about Bill Boner.</p><p>While this is partly a detour through a burning trailer park for the fun of it, there is something to glean from rehashing the shit show that was the one-term mayorship of William Hill Boner.</p><p>Bill Boner was, in virtually every way possible, the opposite of Phil Bredesen. It was a contrast, stark and public, that would help Bredesen replace Boner in 1992, his first step to ascending to the governorship. </p><p>So. Bill Boner… Where to begin…</p><p>Bill Boner is the kind of four-term U.S. Congressman whose <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Boner#cite_note-:0-6">Wikipedia page</a> goes into explicit details about his performance in a single high school basketball game. An East Nashville native, he had a Clinton-esque preternatural aptitude for the political game, effortlessly being elected governor of Boys State and high school class president. </p><p>After managing a gas station and teaching P.E. at Trevecca Nazarene, he decided to run for Clifford Allen’s U.S. Congressional seat under the assumption that he wouldn’t be much competition as he was close to dead. “All of Allen's opponents except Boner withdrew from the race,” his unsourced Wikipedia page claims, “apparently out of concern for ‘kicking a man when he's down.’” Boner had no such concerns. Allen died and Boner went to Washington.</p><p>His ten years in Congress were uneventful — except for that time he gave his third wife Betty, a defense contractor, a $50,000 contract, prompting him to resign amid a congressional ethics investigation. He was only unemployed briefly, quickly pivoting to a campaign for Mayor and beating out the untested out-of-towner Phil Bredesen.</p><p>That’s when shit started getting real weird.</p><p>In 1990 Bill appeared on the Phil Donahue show and announced his engagement to Country Singer Traci Peel, despite Boner still being married to Betty.</p><p>Peel was enamored with the aptly named Boner. <a href="https://drive.google.com/file/d/1WRjhrdtvlh0en7srpS5da0-nZMJgFtEN/view?usp=sharing">One night</a> the two were invited for a meeting with a reporter from the Nashville Banner at a T.G.I. Friday’s. Arriving with Peel, Boner pointed from across the restaurant at the reporter and bellowed out “She interrupted me while I was having sex tonight!” </p><p>Peel then infamously told the reporter the 45-year-old was able to have sex with her for “up to seven hours.”</p><p>The LA Times reports that his constituents weren’t entertained by these revelations, and soon bumper stickers were being printed saying: “Seven Hours for Traci. Three Years for Metro.”</p><p>Also, one time he went “undercover” wearing a fake mustache to see how easy it was to buy drugs in Nashville and went on Oprah to talk about it.</p><div While Nashville might embrace him today as, say, a horrifically successful anti-CRT podcasting powerhouse — in 1988 he was not a person the establishment could rely on to represent their priorities, or perhaps more importantly, social status.</p><p>That’s why it’s worth noting that, even though the Metro Arts deal with the Symphony was Boner’s and Board Chair Martha Ingram's brainchild, it was Phil Bredesen who got the credit for saving the Symphony.</p><p>The <a href="https://www.proquest.com/hnpnashvilletennesseanshell/docview/1909052375/2D635C78FF0D4137PQ/6?accountid=33208&amp;sourcetype=Historical%20Newspapers">Tennessean</a> was sure to frame Bredesen as a politician, even though he’d never won an election. “Bredesen, a former candidate for Nashville mayor and U.S. representative, has taken an integral part in efforts to prevent the symphony's permanent demise.”</p><p>Bredesen's credit for preventing the Symphony's dissolution was not for applying his business acumen to help it evolve with the times and sell more tickets. Nor did he simply use his $200M net worth to shore up the $900k deficit, the fault of poor fundraising in a Reagan economy that had less use for corporate philanthropy.</p><p>In truth, the Symphony did build up their donations with the help of Board Chair (and Bredesen political backer) Martha Ingram and fundraiser Carroll Shanks. But in every instance, it was Bredesen, not Ingram, Shanks or the Symphony’s publicist, who was positioned in the media to talk about how the “Symphony was saved.”</p><p>"I think a lot of wounds have been healed,” the <a href="https://www.proquest.com/hnpnashvilletennesseanshell/docview/1909075950/B72B12B1AEC8487EPQ/6?accountid=33208&amp;sourcetype=Historical%20Newspapers">Tennessean</a> quoted him in December 1988. “I won't say they're all gone, but we are making a lot of progress."</p><p>Bredesen’s work for the Symphony’s influential board, chaired by philanthropic juggernaut Martha Ingram, proved his allegiance to a particular vision of Nashville. Thrashing an artist’s union into submission is what it took to prove, to the donor class and Nashville’s right-of-center liberal constituency, his bona fides as a “social liberal/fiscal conservative”. </p><p>It was this brand of compassionate yet tight-fisted liberalism that helped him cruise into the Mayorship against the progressive Betty Nixon. Nixon, a Nashville native, was endorsed by women’s groups,  community activists, and a Black political caucus. She had been a Nashville City Council<a class="footnote-anchor" data-component-name="FootnoteAnchorToDOM" id="footnote-anchor-2" href="#footnote-2" target="_self">2</a> Member <a href="https://news.vanderbilt.edu/2016/08/30/community-activist-who-made-vanderbilt-a-better-place-dies/">at the same time</a> as she worked in the state government while earning a master’s degree in business administration. </p><p>Truly “dancing backwards in high heels” the Tennessee Lookout’s Holly McCall reflected on her <a href="https://tennesseelookout.com/2023/07/21/dancing-backwards-in-high-heels/">in 2023</a>. Like so many women in the spotlight, Nixon was required to overachieve, but this effort was held against her.</p><p>Her political qualifications, much more evident than Bredesen’s, only led Nixon to be branded “shrill” and <a href="https://tennesseelookout.com/2023/07/21/dancing-backwards-in-high-heels/">“probably a lesbian”</a> by political commentators. Her $55,000 Vanderbilt salary and $109,000 of political donations were no match for Bredesen’s personal wealth and $955,000 of political donations.</p><p>Nixon was trounced by Bredesen, who won not just the Mayorship, <a href="https://www.proquest.com/hnpnashvilletennesseanshell/docview/1909482125/DEA1C3D789A348BCPQ/2?accountid=33208&amp;sourcetype=Historical%20Newspapers">but a referendum</a> he bankrolled with $30,000 of campaign contributions that kept control of Metro’s internal auditor within the Mayor’s Office. The referendum would have given the City Council split control over auditing the city’s finances. The “insider candidate” as Betty Nixon branded him, had won not just the Mayorship but tightened the reins on how the city could be held accountable for its financial dealings.</p><div

There were mixed results at Metro Council last night.

An initial win rejecting a resolution for 15 new surveillance cameras that were explicitly categorized as “surveillance” was followed later in the night by three successive approvals for expanded tactical equipment for MNPD.

For those of us fighting against expanding the surveillance and policing state in Tennessee, in the context of a federal assault on similar blue cities and what is by any reasonable definition a federal insurgency on Minneapolis, the stakes are dire, and the loss deeply felt.

I needed to pause, repeatedly, to remember what was won, however:

  • They didn’t slip this in unnoticed

  • They didn’t get the deeply invasive surveillance softwares Fivecast and LeoSight (the latter from the same executive as Fusus)

  • They were shown for who they really are, with previously muted Council Members and enormous advocacy orgs alike denouncing the push on the Council Floor and at public rallies

  • Solidarity grounded in resistance across the political spectrum (but especially the usually divided Left) is the strongest its been in generations

The vote on the $150K camera resolution, coming after twenty-two impassioned public comment pleas to vote down all the resolutions (against two speaking in favor) resulted in a startling victory.

The twenty-four speakers were allowed to speak for more than the typical allotted time because that resolution, and that resolution alone, was allowed to be categorized by the Mayor’s Office and Metro Legal as being related to “surveillance.”

My thinking on the bus home was that this was probably what made that one untenable. “Everyone’s accepted that the police is fully militarized,” my fellow bus rider who was also at Council reflected, “15 years ago, not so much. But now, give MNPD basically tank? Sure? Why not? But ‘surveillance’, ooh that sounds spooky.”

An interpretation spread amongst texts afterwards, the sinking suspicion: “Did we get played? Did they let the one that came after the public hearing fail knowing once the crowds had dispersed the others, not technically called ‘surveillance’ would sail through?”

The loss of $150K for surveillance cameras to MNPD is a drop in the bucket to a department that got a $24M raises YoY, and it’s hard not to see it as a lamb brought to the slaughter, “give them some red meat and they’ll clear out by the time we vote on the real stuff.”

Yet, it seems odd to attribute Dick Cheney-level machinations to a strategy that cost its purported winners far more than they won.

To those of us close to this fight though, winning last night was not considered a likely outcome. The victory over the cameras was seen as an unexpected step in the right direction.

There was a white board the night before, with different versions of how the night was likely to go. Never was there a version where there were 20 Nays with as few as 15 Yeas.

The proponents of expanding MNPD were embodied in the high-ranking representatives of the Nashville Downtown Partnership. After the city agreed to accept millions in policing equipment—arranged behind closed doors between parties in active insurance negotiations over liability for a destroyed parking structure with damage estimates rumored to be up to $100m—they were grinning like the cat that caught the canary.

Having the opportunity to fork over to MNPD millions in policing equipment (in the wake of a retooling of Tennessee immigration laws in partnership with Stephen Miller, a documented booster of white supremacist culture) seemed to delight the NDP’s leadership that was present.

And being able to do so against the wishes of a diverse crowd, mixed in terms of ethnicity, gender identity, socioeconomic status and political identity, all of whom had cited substantiated and credible fears of the misuse of this equipment — from across the gallery it seemed to only make the victory sweeter.

For anyone who contemplates electoral math or the need for positive net promoter scores from consumers, the expanding contingent of opposition to these efforts would trigger red alerts. The writing is clearly on the wall—except to those who have had their autonomy unchecked for decades, metastasizing as parasites alongside public infrastructure, or to those who’ve risen to the heights of power that a mid-sized Southern city has to offer with the thinnest veneer of “progressive” bona fides.

A repeated refrain of those who asked Metro Council to turn down these equipment donations (all of which at the last minute had been turned into “grants” that required delivery of certain “public information” from MNPD to the State of Tennessee), is that these purchases did not make them feel safer.

I was assured by Deputy Chief Gilder that this info they were going to give to the State would be limited to de-identified information, useful only to confirm the grant funds were being used successfully. I wondered how one could ever confirm if this was the case. I reflected that the abolished Community Oversight Board that might have looked into this no longer exists in part because (as per the Nashville Banner) of the efforts of Deputy Chief Gilder.

Decades of anti-oversight efforts shrouded in Liberal corporatocratic sanding down of public input on public policy have created a crisis of credibility I was experiencing first hand. Gilder told me point blank that no personally identifiable information was going to be shared with State Officials, including we might assume the newly contemplated Tennessee State Department of Homeland Security. But what kind of muck raker would I be to take him at his word given his track record?

And what’s more, as I’ve written about, no data needs to be formally shared if a 3rd party data processor is brought into the mix, with the Tennessee Information Protection Act enshrining the rights of data processors to collaborate with law enforcement in the event they believe someone has violated state, federal, or local law, regulations, or even “rules.”

Nearly 550 Nashvillians had filled out a survey by the end of the evening suggesting alternative uses of these funds, all in alignment with the approved uses provided by the State. When asked if there would be a problem with using these funds for purposes other than what the Mayor’s Office was requesting, Mayor O’Connell’s Legislative Affairs Director responded by pleading ignorance—as if the thought had never occurred to him, despite it being raised repeatedly since the Surveillance MOU was first discovered. He said he would “get back” to the Council Member, a promise that, if ever intended to be fulfilled, would come too late to be of any use.

Observation: Defining things matters.

The one item labeled as “surveillance” was overwhelmingly shot down. The other items were not labeled as “surveillance” under the surveillance ordinance requiring a public hearing—despite price points and technical specifications that unambiguously indicated technology capable of capturing, analyzing, or integrating data to identify particular persons or groups. Coincidentally, the ordinance was written by the very same Dave Rosenberg now claiming these items were “not surveillance.”

Regardless of if this was tossed off as red meat for the piggies, Council balked at it, perhaps because people intuitively know that being spied on is not synonymous with safety. It didn’t hurt that Mayor O’Connell’s own words were read into the record during the public hearing.

In discussing with Council Members how it was parsed that some of this was “surveillance” but some of it was just equipment, the assessment was “this was the best that could be agreed to.”

The determination of “what is surveillance”—something that is supposed to be objective, the very reason why Dave Rosenberg while on Council created this law—now that he was in power and charged with accomplishing Mayor O’Connell’s will (or Stephen Miller’s; it’s hard to parse the origin of these ambitions), was treated as something to be negotiated rather than discussed like adults.

Living, as I believe I do, in a world where words mean things, but also one where American mothers are shot dead in the street with impunity, I think we should think more carefully about building unaccountable systems capable of great violence. Others seem to disagree. Or more to the point, aren’t psychologically developed enough to enter into genuine discussion on the topic.

Leaving the Council gallery, I couldn’t help but toss Ben Simpson, COO of the Nashville Downtown Partnership, a friendly “see you at budget season.” The newly discovered budgetary oversight over the Nashville Downtown Partnership’s funding vehicle—wherein the Metro Council has full authority over their yearly budget, including the nearly $500k salary for the nonprofit’s CEO Tom Turner—is not something they appear concerned with.

For the Downtown Partnership’s leadership and Mayor O’Connell, it remains unclear which represented the greater victory: securing the tactical equipment they sought, or overriding the will of those who opposed them.

Their victory is one that, to all of our great loss, will most certainly be pyrrhic.

While they may feel they have been competently playing 4D chess, twisting the plain language of laws and downplaying the horrors of current events and the credible threats to the lives of the people they purport to serve — something else entirely is being done by us.

We are not playing games. We are building a credible opposition. And we are building it by giving the Mayor’s Office and the Nashville Downtown Partnership (the embodiment of the downtown class of business elites that control the city), rope to hang themselves.

We have given them opportunities to demonstrate that they are either interested in the wellbeing of Nashvillians, or interested in the careers they will have after their tour of duty in the public sector where they will retreat from public life, occupying rarefied air as the elder statesmen of mid-sized city elites, the very definitions of kings of shit mountain.

We have given them ample off ramps, opportunities to be “good partners”, to fact check us, to provide a reasonable narrative for why they are doing what they’re doing, to show that they are acting in “good faith”, to be the people their websites claim them to be.

And they, delighted at the opportunity to flaunt their ability to be small hearted, anti-democratic, collusive, petulant, and dismissive of the cries of their populace for protection from the fascist horde bearing down on them, have shown themselves for what they are.

Small. Small men whose place in history, if they are remembered at all, will be as examples of those who did not heed the warning of Dr. King that CM Suara read on the Council floor last night:

“He who passively accepts evil is as much involved in it as he who helps to perpetrate it. He who accepts evil without protesting against it is really cooperating with it.”

Last night was not a game.

But it was a test.

And the majority of Metro Council failed.

The Nashville Downtown Partnership and the businesses it represents have made it explicit what side of Trump’s war on America’s soul they’re on.

And no one has failed more than Freddie O’Connell.

Politics, though it is a system that must be categorized as “complex”, follows some laws of simple mechanics. To all great political actions is an equal and opposite reaction.

Mayor O’Connell may have won the majority of his tactical gear for use on Nashvillians last night — but in this fight the resistance to the Liberal acquiescence to fascism has grown in leaps and bounds, grounded not in rhetoric or personality but in objective reality and a MetaModern vision of a society that listens to its members.

Here is a startling and unexpected outcome: NOAH, an advocacy group representing 50,000+ congregants is in lockstep with the force for a safer future that doesn’t involve investing fruitlessly in policing.

Anecdotally, otherwise not-in-the-loop Nashvillians, who have taken Andy Ogle’s word for it that O’Connell was an effective progressive, are having the scales drop from their eyes.

And solidarity has never been stronger amongst the Left, long the place where the harshest criticism was for its own members, it is now moving with a center-less unity, across cities and contexts, the victories of one movement the lesson of another.

For the opportunity to grow our skills, expand our base, develop our talking points, flex our power, define our terms, and envision a future that is not made behind closed doors by people who would trade democracy for dictatorship in a heartbeat, I say: thank you.

Congratulations on (mostly) winning your little game.

This is not a game to us, however. While you’ve been doing that, we have been learning how to govern.

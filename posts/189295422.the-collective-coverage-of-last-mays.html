On February 19th, Lighthouse Reports — a small Dutch investigative nonprofit that doesn't even publish its own stories — coordinated a simultaneous drop across five outlets: the Nashville Banner, NewsChannel 5, Mother Jones, Nashville Noticias, and the University of Memphis's Institute for Public Service Reporting. Each owned a distinct piece — Lighthouse built the data infrastructure from a million-plus ICE records, the Banner did on-the-ground sourcing, NC5 brought broadcast and video forensics, Mother Jones provided the national policy frame, Nashville Noticias provided the community trust that let the team reach deportees across five countries, and Memphis extended the investigation geographically.

Cross-outlet collaborations exist — the Panama Papers are the landmark — but those are global stories investigated by major international newsrooms with massive budgets. This was a city-level accountability story, executed by small outlets, using international data infrastructure none of them could afford alone, with a community-language outlet built in as a structural partner rather than an afterthought. That configuration, as far as I can tell, is new.

When Paramount Pictures was bought by oil company Gulf and Western in 1966, it was seen as the death nell for cinema. A bombastic tycoon, Charlie Bluhdorn, was now in charge of the studio that made The 10 Commandments, Rear Window, and Sunset Boulevard. The prognostications of cinema’s death were both true and untrue. Under the capricious billionaire who actually liked movies, Paramount had a renaissance, producing Rosemary’s Baby, the Godfather films, and Raiders of the Lost Arc.

But Bluhdorn’s foray into the media that shapes our world also signified a new era of private capital’s attention to the public’s attention. For every classic he allowed auteurs to bring into being, money that previously had no interest in the business of show swallowed up our stories.

I know this story better about Hollywood than I do about journalism, but the same thing happened there. Movies don’t make much money, on average, never have. And selling papers and running news websites has even tighter margins, and with perhaps the exception of Watergate and the OJ Simpson Trial, there’s never any Avengers: Endgame style tent poles to get you through long winters of minimal growth.

There’s a shot in the movie Spotlight that shows the editor making his way into the Boston Globe with a billboard for AOL (which had already swallowed Warner) looming over the building.

The billboard hangs like a guillotine above the type of long form investigative journalism needed for the deeply reported work that the Boston Globe specialized in. And that type of long form journalism under the banner of big trusted name in media their dedicated team of reporters were allowed to engage in, the film argues, is the only thing that can bring great systemic injustice to light.

Like the death of cinema stemming from the 1966 sale of Paramount to Gulf and Western, this is both true and not true.

Let’s talk about who put this all together.

Lighthouse Reports is a small nonprofit investigative outfit founded in the Netherlands in 2019. They don't publish their own stories. They have no platform, no brand audience, no masthead. What they have is a team of data scientists, open-source investigators, and financial forensics specialists — skill sets that most newsrooms can't afford — and they channel those resources into collaborations with publishing partners who tell the stories their own way, to their own audiences.

Their founder, Daniel Howden, made the deliberate decision to cut the platform entirely, because he'd noticed that nonprofit investigative newsrooms were spending nearly half their budgets building and maintaining their own websites for small loyal readerships, when the actual impact always came through co-publishing with outlets that had real reach. So Lighthouse is just the investigations unit. They lead the research, build the data infrastructure, and then step back. Their partners share everything with each other and nobody dictates how anyone else tells the story. It's contextual expertise trusted at the scale where it lives — which, if you've been reading, is exactly the point.

This is emergence. This is the type of strange, mycelial networks that form when an entire industry has been “disrupted to death.” In the forest of dead mastheads, sawn apart for scrap wood by the AOL Time Warners of the world (themselves now being fed into the chipper), new types of life are growing on the rotted stumps that private equity didn’t even bother to uproot.

I don’t know most of the local folks involved, like Steve Cavendish (who I’m told did a lot of the media coordination in the lead up to the launch) — but the stunning work of collaboration speaks for itself. What I’m seeing, what gives me not hope, hope is too passive a word, what gives me certainty, is what this team assembled across bylines, mastheads and continents to break down what happened last May, when our collective sense of safety broke down.

It’s something we’ve seen glimmers of in big media the last decades. Giant stories about sexual misconduct at the highest levels involved collaboration from writers and editors large outlets pooling resources. When the Snowden leaks, Panama Papers came out we got glimpses of conference rooms with reporters from across the spectrum scrubbing through printouts and taping them to glass walls, scenes I’d be lying if I said I didn’t fantasize about.

But I am not talking about what I’m doing here. I don’t think Substack is the new New York Times. We are not going to Reddit some sort of grand reckoning out of the Epstein Files, which is precisely why they allowed them to be dropped. Truth can hide by being excluded from the given, or it can be obscured by collapsing clarity in the meant. We do need contextual expertise often to tell us what things mean, and having too many gloveless hands at a crime scene can make the truth harder to ferret.

My desire, with local news media, is for professionals to fill the accountability space the big journalism has left open so I people like me can just comment on it. I’ve only ever broken a story because I couldn’t get someone else to do it. I hope that’s changing.

The point is not to uplift the people on these bylines as the new, infallible Woodwards and Bernsteins. There is something powerful about the fact that this is not a byline-driven project. The problems are too diffuse and complex for any one muckraker to take out all the trash. The thing to emulate is the coordination itself — distributed, emergent, across domains, expertise and scale. And its this, I want you to look at this.

My theory of change is that before a thing can fully come into being, it needs to be designated — pointed at, circled, named as real. This is my attempt to do that. If you want to know where to invest your emotional energy and your desire to resist with impact, look at how these stories were created and presented.

Nature abhors a vacuum, and in the space left by mainstream media to tell the unvarnished truth about our country’s rising fascism, people are filling it — finding the budgets, time, and courage in couch cushions and coffee cups. New partnerships, new collaborations, new ways of telling stories on new platforms with new voices and the oldest standards for journalistic integrity. The impossible is becoming textual.

There is a new and ancient form coming into being in collaborations like this. There’s a lot to be said about it philosophically, but philosophy is not the ground from which new forms grow. It’s the study of what was.

I say this as a deep, obnoxious lover of theory who just picked up a copy of What is a Thing? by Martin Heidegger at McKay’s — the new forms will not be grounded in theory. They will be grounded in doing the work.

The impulse to get everyone aligned on a precise theory of change, to adopt an ideological commitment, to march in lockstep toward a shared blueprint — this is not the way. Not because its “too hierarchical”. Not because it’s “not woke.” But because it doesn’t work. The only thing that does work is doing the work, the work that begins with yourself.

It’s not that alignment doesn’t matter, but the kind of alignment that obsesses over alignment mistakes the map for the territory.

No people that arrived somewhere worth going knew, step by step, how to get there. They knew where they wanted to go. They knew how they wanted to treat each other on the way. That was enough. And more that that is too much.

And wanting more alignment than that, wanting deference to expertise that that is unnecessarily intellectualized, abstracted and obfuscated often comes from the impulse to get somewhere else entirely. A somewhere where the person wanting alignment actually just want to be the doing all the aligning.

What we’ve learned from cognitive science and the physiology of movement is that the most important things a body needs to catch a ball is to imagine the catch — the feeling of it landing in your hand and being grasped tightly in your palm. You cannot catch by thinking through each particular flex of muscle, each micro-adjustment of posture, as if you distrust the individual parts of your body to know what is best for them. That is the work of your individuals muscles, themselves operating a form of proto-consciousness that is operating a subroutine of intuitive prediction to complete the task at hand.

We talk about being uncoordinated, in our bodies and our social movements. But I argue that, as beings that have been suffering at the hands of psychological oppression for centuries, we are over-coordinated. Over-coordiation is when you do not trust your muscles, the contextual experts that are most incentivized to get their movements right. That kind of thinking doesn’t help you catch the ball. It only serves the ego of the mind, which, once it fails at thinking its way to the goal, will argue that it simply needs more thought, more control over the body.

This is precisely the logic of the catastrophe we are in — the distrust of people to know what is best for them. What we share in a society is the “commons”. And what the science of the commons requires, as Elinor Ostrom spent her life demonstrating, is not rigid alignment but flexible, permeable coordination. Contextual expertise trusted at the scale where it lives. Rules that emerge from the people who use the resource, not imposed by authorities who have never touched it. Truth gained through truth-telling — not through decree, not through ideology, but through the slow, stubborn, joyful practice of people telling each other what they actually see.

The alignment that works is alignment on values — on how we treat each other, which begins and ends with the words we use to speak to each other, the words we choose to designate the shared vision we will instantiate together. Not a program. Not a platform. A way of being together that trusts the parts to know what is best for them once the destination is clear.

The monomyth of the lone hero is the operating system of the very crises we are fighting. Freedom will not be won by individuals. But it will be won as a collective, and the work of winning will bring, at every step, sorrow and joy. And if you are finding yourself fighting and feuding, defining and excluding, and not finding joy, I must say, comrade, that is not the vibe.

“The Owl of Minerva takes flight at dusk,” Hegel wrote. The owl of wisdom only flies when the day’s work is done — we can only know what a thing was once it has finished becoming what it is and is dying. The potent ground of new forms is not in philosophizing about what could be or aligning on first principles. It is in grounded, lived experience of the present and ourselves. The only thing to be agreed on is how we should treat each other. All else is derivative.

So in whatever you are doing to make our world a better place, do it a bit more like this. Give up the myth of your own individual magic that will save the world. Now, before us, the dead trees become alive.

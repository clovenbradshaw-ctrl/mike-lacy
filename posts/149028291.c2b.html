I’ve been a bit AWOL on my writing / podcasting about Metro Arts, and I feel a bit bad about that. I’m working more and don’t fully have the bandwidth like I did, and my clinical hyperfixation isn’t very easy to steer. But I don’t plan on leaving the discussions on the topic behind.

But I also want whatever I do on the subject to be useful, and to build empathy and clarity for those invested in the arts. At one point my self-assigned role in arts ecosystem felt like it could best be described as shaking a stick at a chaotic swarm, shouting to passersby: “Lookout! Bees! We all need to look at these bees!”

Lately, I’ve been busy working to design a space where we can have conversations about how we can address things “systemically.” How can we build trust and honesty, and a foundation and be willing to hear each other’s stories, points of view, and maybe, just maybe, make collective decisions based on data and the lived experiences of those who are directly affected by our decisions?

I am not an expert in building community and harmony by any means. I have been called “spicy” by former supervisors, and a past colleague once stopped me mid-sentence (where I though I was being pretty friendly) and asked, “wait, am I being interrogated?” It won’t be for me to train people on this. But maybe we can take the class together.

What I am good at is knowing which way the wind is blowing. I can see that intentional, relationship-building, empathy-enhancing, openness is what we need to solve our complex social problems (and a situation like Metro Arts being comparatively low-hanging fruit to whats coming).

I’m willing to admit that I have much work to do to be open to other people’s points of view, to understand how they made choices I wouldn’t, and even to accept them if they’re unwilling to accept me.

Perhaps these are easy things for me, a straight-cis-white-dude, to admit and accept.

I will say though, for whatever little its worth, that I think this live and let live, accepting the suchness of others, is important not because it’s nice, but because it’s necessary. Necessary for us to make the kinds of complex decisions we’re going to need to face from here on out. Maybe good people vs bad people is a useful paradigm for binary decision-making, but the types of decisions we’re stuck on collectively are never binary. If they were, we’d probably have already solved them.

So — it’s in the spirit of experimenting with non-confrontational, collaborative decision-making that I raise up a debate that currently exists in the Metro Arts eco-system. We will be, for the rest of this article, practitioners of systems theory. We know that heavy-handed actions can often make things much worse in complex social systems, so we’re going to look for small leverage points that can have large, positive knock-on effects.

There’s one thing everyone in the Arts community in Nashville can agree on, it’s that they don’t want to be in this situation again. What’s the most likely way we can avoid that, and, just maybe, move beyond a zero-sum conclusion to this stalemate, and get us closer to a win-win-win solution.

The Dilemma

The Metro Arts world currently has two paths it could take. I’d argue it’s not a crisis which way this particular decision goes, but it is important we’re intentional about it.

As self-ordained practitioners of Systems Theory, you and I know that the best way to effect lasting change in systems is by carefully identifying and adjusting the “leverage points” that are best suited to steer the system toward our desired outputs. And with two possible versions of a leverage point sitting before the City Council right now to reorient Metro Arts, we have a dilemma on our hands.

The pedantic classics student in me wants to highlight that dilemma comes from the Greek διλήμματα. “Di” meaning two, “lemma” meaning solution. To the Greeks, to have a dilemma didn’t mean, as it is often used now, “we’ve got a problem”. Rather, it meant “we have more than one solution.”

In Euclid’s “Elements”, the origin of modern geometry, some proofs had multiple solutions or lemmas. In math, not all solutions are equal, some are more elegant than others.

The Ordinance

The bill or “ordinance” being discussed in the Metro Council regarding the revision of the Metro Code with respect to the Metro Arts Commission, BL2024-476. As is often the case, several alternative “amendments” have been submitted, and these use different leverage points to make issues like we saw with Metro Arts this past year less likely.

A previous version of this Ordinance was put forth by Council Member Styles, and the current one was put up by Council Member Porterfield. The core difference between Amendments A and B get to some important questions that those interested in seeing the Metro Arts Commission should consider.

Amendment A

Only slightly different than the version submitted by Council Member Porterfield, it adjusts how the Metro Arts Commission works in two ways.

  • Defines Commissioner service on the board more clearly as “staggered terms of four years”.

  • Requires that Metro Arts re-submits its funding criteria for grant recipients every year [Council already has oversight over this in the Metro Code, but it hasn’t been submitted in 20+ years]. Metro Council would update this by resolution (easier to pass than an ordinance).

  • Requires that Metro Arts indicates if the funding criteria has changed since the previous year.

Amendment B

This version, more aligned with Council Member Style’s original version of this ordinance, is more or less the same as above, but with additional stipulations.

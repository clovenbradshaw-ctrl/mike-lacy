Charlie Weingartner used to commute by bike and work hospitality shifts across the city — Germantown, the Gulch, Melrose. He then moved into engineering, which put him on second shift, and he couldn’t get to community meetings. Most of the spaces accessible for the public to involve themselves in their own governance happen after the traditional work day. So toward the end of his twenties, he made intentional career moves so he could participate more in local democracy.

He is now chair of the strategic planning sub-committee of Vision Zero, Nashville’s instance of an international movement to drop pedestrian deaths to nil. Weingartner is clear-eyed on the up-hill battle, already pedestrian deaths are trending higher for 2026 than last year.

To make a city safer for pedestrians requires coordinating with government institutions to make hard decisions about “the right-of-way.” These institutions have budgets that are set by elected officials, who in turn have campaigns that are funded by business interests and power players that often do not prioritize the same things as people like Charlie.

There is perhaps no better example of this in action than the benches.

Outside of this role, Charlie is what an attendee of a recent community feedback session called “one of the real leaders of the city.” He is someone, according to this commenter, a person who is “actually meeting the community’s needs.” And Charlie is one of the real leaders, this person argued, that the “official” leaders (elected or otherwise) appear to be “at war with.”

He is one of the people who built the guerrilla benches.

Background: In August 2025, Nashville’s Department of Transportation removed eight metal benches from Korean Veterans Boulevard and replaced them with large concrete spheres, citing “city beautification efforts.” A community group called To Nashville With Love began building and placing handmade wooden benches in response. The city began confiscating those too. The Nashville Scene covered the original removal; the Nashville Banner covered the guerrilla bench movement.

If there is a war, Weingartner does not identify as a combatant. Nor is his affect and biography one of a warrior, or even a politician. His father was a Presbyterian minister. Growing up, serving the community, Charlie told me, wasn’t something you did to feel good about yourself. It was just how you existed in society. He is what you’d hope a civic leader would be: cool-headed, detail-oriented, systems-thinking, deeply open to feedback and revising his perspective.

Yet what Charlie stands for, values that are deeply Tennessean, like the right to self-governance and the belief that the government and private entities should not control public space with absolute autonomy, seem to be anathema to what the leaders in Nashville are hell-bent on having.

Charlie’s interview on News Channel 5, the day of the benches’ rescue.

We talked at Switchyards in Germantown on a Wednesday morning. He had been up since before six, emailing NDOT about something unrelated. A week earlier, Nashville’s Department of Transportation had been discovered storing seventeen handmade community benches in a city warehouse — benches the department had been quietly removing from public spaces while telling the organizers, who were working through official channels to get them approved, that they weren’t the ones doing it.

In November 2025, NDOT had told To Nashville With Love organizer Robin Lovett Owen directly that they would not remove the benches and wanted to work with the group. They had already begun removing them.

The recovery mission had happened four days prior. Kelly, another organizer, had driven a truck to the NDOT property and started loading while workers watched and said nothing. She waved and smiled at them. They acknowledged her. Nobody stopped her.

Charlie wasn’t surprised NDOT had the benches. He was surprised they hadn’t thrown them away.

NDOT’s consistent public message was that the benches were “in the right-of-way” — language Charlie recognized as deliberately misleading. “People would imagine that we are setting benches in the road,” he said. He noted that the Venn diagram of people who build and place these benches and people who care about protecting ADA access is a single overlapping circle. They had been, he said, incredibly careful and judicious about placement. He would have entertained a conversation about any bench needing repositioning. That conversation never came.

I asked him about hostile architecture more broadly — the concrete balls installed on Korean Veterans Boulevard where the original benches had been, the design choices that make sitting structurally impossible without explicitly telling you’re not welcome. He didn’t hesitate.

“There are powers at play that don’t want homeless people around, but they also don’t want to fix homelessness in the appropriate manner. The result of that is hostile architecture.”

Then I asked about the sentence someone had posted when the benches were recovered. For every seat removed, two shall take its place. He recognized the structure immediately.

“If you’re trying to get rid of something, the act of getting rid of it brings more attention to it, and then it compounds the awareness around it. That’s the origin of the Streisand effect.”

The movement that followed broke down along no typical demographic lines. Transportation advocates, housing groups, religious organizations, and a high school robotics team that won an award for donating twenty benches to the cause. Charlie’s read on why: everybody sits. It has a low barrier to entry and a high threshold of impact. Anyone trying to make the community better in any way can look at a bench and recognize it as a real, tactical expression of that same goal.

The people who removed the benches represent a different theory of the city.

Council Member Jacob Kupin, whose District 19 covers the benches’ former home, initially described the removal in a Nashville Scene interview as an accomplishment he’d long sought, achieved through partnership with NDOT and the Nashville Downtown Partnership. As the public reaction mounted, his recollection evolved.

By the time Kupin met with me for a lengthy oral history interview, he could not recall whether the Nashville Downtown Partnership had played any role. Contemporary reporting just after their removal contradicts this: an NDOT official told the Scene that the department had “recently begun working with the Nashville Downtown Partnership to add more concrete spheres to Korean Veterans Boulevard as part of a public art and beautification project.”

Kupin took office in September 2023. His predecessor in District 19, Freddie O’Connell, held the seat from 2015 until he was elected mayor in 2023. Both men served on NDP’s board as ex officio members — the seat comes with the district, and its history.

NDP is an extraordinarily powerful consortium of downtown business interests, and understanding what happened to the benches requires understanding what the organization has been allowed to become. It has been receiving Metro special assessment funds without authorization dating back to 2004 — a fact I documented at length in {Rich Text}, and a fact Kupin himself surfaced on a phone call with me, when, after scrubbing through archived Nashville legislation, he realized 2004 was the last year their finances had been reviewed by the council. He set a deadline: April 15, 2026. This week, that deadline arrives.

The budget that lands on the council’s desk — twenty-two years late — will be the first time Metro has been formally asked to look at what NDP has been doing with public money and public authority since the second term of George W. Bush.

At the center of that organization is Tom Turner, who has led NDP for over 15 years. His compensation, now exceeding $511,000 annually, more than doubled between 2010 and 2022 — from $207,627 to crossing the half-million mark — during the same period in which no budget was submitted for council review.

What NDP accumulated is considerable. A private security operation, run through Solaren Risk Management, whose contractors were cited by the State of Tennessee for 62 violations — including impersonating police officers — and whose off-duty THP troopers, a whistleblower described as explicitly directed to target unhoused people while leaving tourists alone. An armed force with arrest powers stood up under the NDP’s authority without public debate. A downtown library parking garage destroyed on its watch when a Block by Block subcontractor stored propane tanks unsafely, causing a fire and partial collapse that shuttered the library for months.

And threaded through a $15 million memorandum of understanding only partially visible to the public — filed the Friday before Thanksgiving likely to minimize scrutiny — a surveillance infrastructure the council has never been formally asked to approve: [clarification edit: under discussion with NDP were] license plate reader networks, real-time crime center integrations, and vendor relationships with companies like LeoSight and Fivecast that extend NDP’s intelligence footprint well past anything an elected body sanctioned. It was later claimed that [the tech that made the MOU] was always meant to be purchased for MNPD, but nothing in the MOU indicated that Metro would be the actual owner of these technologies.

Public records requests obtained from the Mayor’s Office last year revealed working documents depicting the collaboration between MNPD, Mayor O’Connell’s office, and NDP. Despite writing the submitted and signed MOU obligating NDP to buy nation-state-level surveillance software and an armored vehicle, the Mayor’s Office claimed the whole point of the MOU was to restrict what NDP could purchase.

The alleged sticking points, according to records provided by the Mayor’s Office amid the Council fights over the equipment, were license plate readers (LPRs) and off-duty officers.

Apparently, the Mayor’s Office thinks LPRs are fine for MNPD, as the Mayor indicated to Fox17 that he is open to MNPD receiving them, in line with their requested FY27 budget enhancements. And, as per the off-duty cops, whatever concerns the Mayor’s Office has had, Mr. O’Connell has never publicly indicated any interest in having their budget expenditures for this policing submitted to Council for approval to comply with Metro law, despite his decade-plus of direct oversight of NDP operations in District 19 and Metro Nashville generally.

There has been no indication that anyone intends to hold NDP or Metro accountable for over two decades of unauthorized transactions. This is a remarkable posture given what those two decades contain — and it is a posture that spans both Kupin’s tenure and O’Connell’s, two men who each inherited the same seat, the same board appointment, and the same unreviewed ledger, and each deferred.

A poll is being circulated via Clear Insights to gauge public interest in how the $15M in public funds should be used. Being sent a mere three days before the NDP budget is due speaks to the risible gestures the consultant class in Nashville takes toward collective governance.

It’s unclear what this last-minute outreach is costing the NDP and, by extension, the businesses required by law to pay special assessments to fund their operations. The cost will be clear, one would hope, when they finally submit their budget to the Metro Council. A previous survey of community members was taken, a poll that took place before NDP was contractually obligated to purchase policing gear for Metro Nashville with these funds. Its results are offered here free of charge.

When the scandal of the seventeen kidnapped benches broke out of Bluesky containment and into half a dozen local news stories, Kupin reached out to another organizer and me to correct the record. He wanted it understood that he had, in fact, always been supportive of the community benches. He provided emails dating back to last fall — beginning with one sent two weeks after the Scene‘s August coverage, in which he asked then-NDOT director Diana Alarcon whether her department could restore the handmade benches to the very spots he’d asked her to clear.

It turns out that the coalition frustrated with how District 19 is being governed, by both it's current Council Member and the Mayor who used to occupy the same seat, is broader than anyone in that governance structure anticipated. It includes:

  • People waiting for buses with nowhere to sit.

  • Transit advocates and urbanists who voted for Choose How You Move and watched the city immediately undercut it in concrete spheres of hypocrisy.

  • Downtown business owners outraged at the Mayor’s apathy toward their property tax increases, while their legally required special assessment fees go toward destroying public parking garages and violating people's civil liberties. O’Connell’s signature homelessness department has failed.

  • The unhoused pushed off the last legal places they could rest, while a city offers housing services that result in civil rights violations.

  • Activists sounding alarms about surveillance infrastructure being assembled without public consent under a federal government that has made clear what it intends to do with that data.

  • Fiscal conservatives that have noticed that a nonprofit CEO is collecting half a million dollars a year in government-collected funds that the council responsible for approving them hasn’t reviewed in twenty-two years.

None of these people are natural allies. All of them are looking at the same stretch of downtown and asking the same questions.

And it is not only the public. Even within Metro government, department heads appear to have reached the end of their patience with the equivocation, the buck-passing, the positions held until they become inconvenient and then quietly abandoned.

The communications Kupin unsolicitedly provided me, in an effort to defend his record as a supporter of the guerrilla benches, reveal something else: the men running District 19 are driving everyone crazy.

The email chain tracks his back-and-forth with NDOT officials over the benches, including the now-departed director Diana Alarcon.

Kupin appears to have first asked NDOT to remove the benches in a prior email he did not provide; NDOT removed them. Kupin then asked NDOT’s director, in a Sept 4 email, to restore the community-built ones that replaced the Metro ones he had asked to be removed.

“I really love them and would like to see more of them on KVM and around the city downtown,” he told Diana Alarcon of NDOT.

Alarcon responded with a single sentence, a sentiment relatable to anyone who has had to deal with the men who rule downtown:

“You are killing me!”

This weekend I wrote a piece about Nashville’s political landscape that was, as is my wont, quite didactic and hectoring. I scrapped it. Metro Nashville Council Member Quin Evans Segall recently published something that crystallized what I was trying to articulate, with far more grace than I’m capable:

Radical honesty in governance is the first step in making sure that we save this system of governance. Deep, abiding, and consistent radical honesty is my plea for 2026. Honesty must be our group project, and we cannot afford any missing members. Trust in government only comes if everyone in our government can be trusted.

This is it. Exactly.

And yet, I think we all know that people rarely begin to provide radical honesty out of elegant exhortation. I have not found this to be true in my personal life, that simply asking people to be more truthful effectuates much change in their behavior. Perhaps in young children. Rarely in adults. Never with politicians.

In politics, I have found that honesty arrives only when power is exercised in a way that makes it a requirement. CM Evan Segall’s call is necessary — but it won’t be sufficient unless there are consequences for not delivering the transparency being requested from the Council that approves the Mayor’s budget.

Even though asking for honesty is not the tool in itself needed to obtain transparency, it is important to publicly notice its requirement. I think we should consider the call for “Radical Honesty” not so much a naive West Wingean cry out to the angels of our better nature, but the benchmark of a performance improvement plan (PIP) for our mayoral administration.

The point of a PIP is to set a reasonable goal that someone worthy of retaining their position should be able to attain. When assigning a PIP, you may or may not have confidence that the employee can rise to the occasion. But ethics, HR policies, and election cycles such as they are, it behooves us, as the employer of our public servants, to allow them to make a good faith effort to perform their tasks at the level requisite for the position they have been hired for.

But when their contract is up, no sensible employer would rehire someone who struggled, after years on the job, to perform the most basic task they were hired to do. And in the case of an elected official, that is engaging transparently and honestly with the public.

The anger I felt last year wasn’t about bad decisions—politicians make those every day. It was about the refusal to be honest about what those decisions were, who made them, and why. Evans Segall’s framing of what we should demand—Radical Honesty about limits, process, and accountability—is the right foundation for the work ahead.

What she named isn’t just good politics. It’s one of the Seven Pillars of Cooperative Power-Sharing (see below) required for sustaining democracy.

We cannot share power if we do not tell the truth about how it is used. And when people do not tell the truth about their power, the natural assumption is that this is because they do not want to share it.

We’ll explore the other Pillars of Cooperative Power-Sharing this year. But I’m glad she surfaced this one so clearly, because it has to come first.

A Serious Matter Unmoored from Serious Discourse

Last month a public fight erupted over surveillance. What you saw from sources like me was lengthy analysis linking to public documents with clear delineation between what we think we know, why we think we know it, what we’re assuming, and what we think should happen. I took getting the facts right seriously enough that I co-wrote an article with a national surveillance researcher that was professionally fact-checked and edited.

The other side did not engage on similar terms. What they projected was interpretation (”This is guardrails—you’re not against oversight, are you?”), authority (“Trust us”), and intentions (”We are good people, so what we want is good”).

I don’t think I was the only one who noticed a lack of candor from the local administration.

Truth cannot harm a democracy. But it can really burn when autocracy has been allowed to metastasize.

CM Evans Segall posted the following on January 2, 2026. She did not directly cite the surveillance MOU, though it seems safe to say it was on her mind:

Trust has to come before we can effectuate change. This will require a radical honesty. We must start with every single part of a law or policy from the beginning and work through it in a fully open way. It is not enough to say “here is my fact sheet and why I think this policy is good.” We must instead say “this is why we’re talking about it, how we got to this point, where we could have done things differently, and why we didn’t.” When we mess up, we have to say that. When we’re late to a problem, we have to cop to that. When we have a limitation, we have to share that. When we don’t know, we have to admit that.

This is what Radical Honesty looks like in practice: government modeling the pillar of Feedback and Truth Telling.

I am not sure how CM Evans Segall will eventually vote on measures that could give more surveillance technology to MNPD. And if I were engaging in the transactional form of real politicking we saw last month coming from the Mayor’s Office, it would behoove me to withhold any praise of her until she acquiesced to my will. But that is not how people like me operate. We know clearly that the way we do politics is usually more important than what we use politics to do.

As dangerous as I believe unregulatable1 surveillance contracts are in the hands of private corporations—virtually all of whom have federal contracts and can now share data with law enforcement at will—it is vastly more dangerous to embrace a post-truth political discourse, regardless of if you have an (R) or a (D) next to your name on the ballot.

A political “win” within a democracy that brushes aside truth is a victory for autocracy. And autocracy (whether it be in the form of the conservative God Emperor model of DC, or the “Progressive” Dictatorial Technocrat-In-Chief that is the “strong mayor” goal for Nashville) is not the vibe.

Information Warfare vs. Collective Governance

I’ve gotten compliments from the other side for the pro-privacy work I’ve been part of. “You had a head start,” someone on the other team told me. “You hit the ground running with messaging and you did a great job with your extra airtime.”

Thanks. But that misses the point.

I said: “No, I don’t think that’s why we’re winning. I think it is because, based on all the evidence presented, we are right. This is a bad deal, and Metro Council can get a better one. This isn’t about PR—it’s about what is true. But am I off base? What did I get wrong?”

I’m still waiting for an answer.

But let's say, for the sake of argument, that more surveillance technology in today’s political climate is actually a very good thing for everyone involved and there is little downside to it. If this is true, it should hold up to examination, whether they come in the form of Substacks or Instagram posts or parody fact sheets.

Let’s say there truly are good reasons to earmark more surveillance besides just that Bill Lee, Larry Ellison, Donald Trump, or the NFL want it and our leaders desperately want to appease those people. If it is true that these things are good for Nashvillians, please, I beg you, show up in the marketplace of ideas with your facts in line to demonstrate this.

But what we see, consistently, is not an engagement on the merits of this technology, but a further obscuring of the venue where decisions are being made about their purchase. And when there is public discussion on the topic, its proponents offer little more than hand waving and doublespeak and seeming contempt at even being asked to explain their actions.

To many current leaders, politics is nothing but information warfare. There is no reality to be observed—only narratives to be managed. Facts don't describe reality; they're just ammunition for the story you already wanted to tell, conveniently ignored when they point to truths that are inconvenient.

But reality has a way of reasserting itself.

Throughout 2025, I heard versions of “you shouldn’t be asking these questions” because “we need to be united” or “these are just personality conflicts.” But what I’ve been pointing to isn’t rhetorical or personal. It’s in black and white—often provided by the city itself through public records and testimony.

Calls for unity are easy when they ask nothing of power. They become corrosive when used to dismiss evidence, flatten disagreement, or recast accountability as disloyalty.

What Happens When You Lose Truth Telling

When honesty breaks down, democracy doesn’t collapse overnight. It degrades.

The first thing to go is shared reality. Decisions stop being argued on merits and start being defended as narratives. Evidence no longer constrains power; it becomes something to be managed or ignored. Process turns into theater.

Once shared reality fractures, accountability becomes impossible. If there is no agreed-upon truth, there is nothing to be held accountable to. Leaders are judged on whether their stories survive, not whether they are accurate.

What happens in offices responsible for objective metrics—performance data, compliance reports, outcomes tracking—when they report to a sole authority that doesn’t value honesty? They adapt. Inconvenient findings get softened, delayed, or contextualized into meaninglessness. Staff learn what questions not to ask. The office doesn’t falsify data—it learns which truths are safe to tell. Measurement stops informing power and starts serving it.

In this environment, power doesn’t seize control so much as abstract itself. Through corporate consultants, the financial class, and well-funded philanthropic intermediaries—Bloomberg Philanthropies being a prominent example that embeds corporate leaders like Nashville’s Kristin Wilson directly into high levels of government with no prior public service to speak of —democratic institutions are steadily reframed as professionalized management offices tasked with “removing the politics from government.” Decisions are cast as technical. Outcomes are treated as inevitable. Disagreement is recoded as inefficiency.

One predictable consequence is the nullification of dissent through structure. Boards and commissions are consolidated, sidelined, or bypassed. Public participation is reframed as a liability rather than a safeguard. In trying to preempt hypothetical threats, institutions remove the very mechanisms that surface real ones.

In cities like this, the leaders who flourish are skilled managers of ambiguity. They speak in partial truths and process-heavy justifications difficult to contest but impossible to interrogate. They are often described as pragmatic. But what distinguishes them most is their ability to operate without meaningful feedback—insulated from public consequence, rewarded not for telling the truth but for keeping the machinery running smoothly no matter how disconnected it becomes from the people it governs.

Without trust, power hardens. Consent gives way to compliance. Scrutiny is recast as hostility. Dissent becomes disloyalty.

In systems like this, truth-tellers become liabilities. People who surface facts are labeled divisive. The lesson is subtle but clear: honesty is risky, silence is safe.

Over time, people adjust. Expectations drop. Cynicism replaces engagement—not because people don’t care, but because caring without honesty is exhausting.

A democracy can survive bad decisions. It can survive conflict. In fact, it has emerged organically from the primordial soup of human history as the form of government that is most resilient to mistakes and political conflict. But what it cannot survive is the replacement of truth with narrative and accountability with loyalty.

That’s why Radical Honesty isn’t a style choice. It’s a structural requirement.

Truth is bitter medicine—But it doesn’t have to be

I know, as CM Segall clearly does, that truth-telling is essential to a healthy democracy. Last year I was called “fake news” and accused of sowing division for unspecified reasons. I didn’t mind it, too much. I saw it for what it was: political antibodies swarming towards a perceived threat vector within an organism that has learned to treat dissent as danger, not as diagnostic information.

I get it. To the best understanding of the people retaliating against people like me for speaking out, they believe, sincerely, that what I am saying is dangerous. Not because they have facts to counter my arguments per se, but because what I was saying was not commensurate with their emotional truth. They have taken, consistently, warnings about the impacts of their actions as accusations about the content of their character. And when everything is perceived as a personal attack, every criticism is a threat to your identity, and reality is deflected as a form of self preservation.

The expression goes: “facts don’t care about feelings.” But that’s far less insightful than the corollary: “feelings don’t care about facts.” And when everything is perceived as just information warfare in an arms race to affirm our own self worth, when we view the world in zero sum dualism of us vs them, we make ourselves incapable of living in the nuanced complexity of the now — but that is precisely where we have to live. Because the now is where truth, connection, love, and democracy live.

When you give in to the Manichaean dualism of Us vs Them politics, the them are always evil, and the “us” is always right. Aside from being an embarrassingly underdeveloped worldview to have as a grown ass adult person in a position of great power and influence, in addition to it being an incredibly dangerous perspective to have about humanity, it is, most problematically, an extraordinarily, unconscionably boring way to see the world.

Truth is a bitter medicine. When it arrives late—after years of avoidance—it can feel brutal. Like emergency treatment rather than routine care. It hurts because the system refused to listen earlier. But the pain isn’t evidence that truth is destructive; it’s evidence the medicine is working.

Truth cannot harm a democracy. But it can really burn when autocracy has been allowed to metastasize.

In healthy bodies, cells are always growing beyond their proper boundaries, and most of the time we never notice—because feedback is constant and integrated, regulating growth before it becomes disease. Like us, the body politic is never technically “cancer free.” Power, like capital, tends to accumulate. What matters is whether the system has built-in ways to notice and correct that accumulation early.

A healthy democracy doesn’t experience self regulating truth as an external assault. It absorbs it naturally. But so long as feedback is rejected—when dissent is treated as pathology rather than signal—truth can only arrive from outside.

And until the day our institutions can endure truth without vampirically recoiling from its light, the collective consciousness—sensing a gap in the autopoietic system of self-regulation—will create people willing to shine it anyway. Not because we chose this role. But because the function must be performed by someone.

A system that disables internal feedback doesn’t eliminate the need for it. It only ensures that correction arrives from without—less gently than if it had been welcomed from within.

That’s the work ahead. Not to punish, but to restore. Not to divide, but to insist on the shared reality without which no unity is possible. Radical Honesty isn’t the threat to democracy that its enemies pretend. It’s the immune system democracy needs to survive.

Quin named the standard. Radical Honesty. Now we must hold our leaders to it.

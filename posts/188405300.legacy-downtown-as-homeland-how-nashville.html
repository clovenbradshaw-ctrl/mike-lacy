On a January night at Metro Council, as members debated whether to accept surveillance camera upgrades tied to a $15 million state grant, Council Member Jordan Huffman issued a warning.

“We are one incident away from having the money turn off in Nashville.”

In a single sentence — delivered with the smooth certainty of something rehearsed — it said the quiet part out loud. Tennessee leaders aren’t primarily concerned about safety, not of tourists or residents, but about money.

There was a time in America when this would have been considered gauche. When nakedly stating that investing in the surveillance theater that keeps the billionaire class’ anxiety at bay was not their primary objective. Apparently, they are not required to wear that mask anymore.

How did we get here? How is it that our leaders are able to hold such contradictory positions in their heads:

  1. Safety is my highest priority.

  2. Profits for elite business interests is my highest priority.

Mayor O’Connell offered his own version of the logic while defending expanded surveillance downtown:

“We’re continuing to solicit feedback… but we want to make sure that everyone understands the context of how different the downtown safety environment is from other parts of the city.”

Different how?

Different because crime behaves differently?
Different because residents deserve more protection there?
Different because sidewalks operate under a separate moral code?

No. Different because of money.

Downtown is different because it is dense with revenue. It is dense with capital. It is dense with property value, tourism flow, convention bookings, alcohol sales, hotel taxes, and corporate investment. It is not merely a neighborhood. It is an asset.

And assets get defended.

Over the past two decades, Nashville hasn’t just expanded policing downtown. It has absorbed a national security framework that increasingly defines economic stability itself as something to defend.

Downtown, in effect, has become homeland.

The Super Bowl Is Once. Losing Faith in Our Leaders Is Forever.

When a city hosts something like an NFL Draft or a Super Bowl, security needs to temporarily expand. Hundreds of thousands of extra people require barricades, traffic control, and police overtime in the same way they require more trash pickup and more sanitation infrastructure. Those costs are real, and someone has to pay them. It should be the multi-billion-dollar corporations selling tickets for thousands of dollars apiece.

But that is not what happens. Instead, cities are pressured into making permanent investments in surveillance and militarized infrastructure in order to secure temporary events. The events come and go. The cameras remain.

This has become a national pattern: corporations and sports leagues promise an economic boom, then quietly condition that boom on the public sector footing the bill for hardened security systems—surveillance networks, command centers, tactical vehicles, data platforms, and “public safety” upgrades that outlast the spectacle they were supposedly built for. The message is implicit but unmistakable: pay up now, or risk losing everything.

Even worse, the promised boom often fails to materialize at the scale boosters predict. Study after study has found that mega-events routinely underdeliver economically, but the infrastructure built in their name never goes away. Almost no one seriously audits whether these long-term surveillance costs are offset by real public benefit, especially in Tennessee, where leaders obsess over revenue while resisting any meaningful investment in public welfare.

Because surveillance is not a one-time purchase. It is a permanent financial commitment. Cameras require storage, maintenance, upgrades, and staff. Data requires analysts. Analysts generate enforcement. Enforcement generates arrests, court costs, jail costs, and an ever-expanding criminal legal system that must continually justify the existence of the machinery it has built.

And in Nashville, that machinery feeds directly into an incarceration economy headquartered here, where private prison and detention interests stand ready to profit from a growing pool of criminalized bodies. In another city, this might sound like an unfortunate coincidence. In Nashville, it reads like the business model.

All of this requires rhetorical cover.

No elected official can openly say: we are building permanent surveillance infrastructure so that corporations and property owners can secure their revenue streams.

So the language shifts.

It becomes about terrorism.
It becomes about preparedness.
It becomes about critical infrastructure.
It becomes about homeland security.

After 9/11, federal doctrine formally merged economic continuity with national security. Critical infrastructure was defined as systems whose disruption would threaten not just safety, but economic stability. Tennessee institutionalized that language. The state’s Office of Homeland Security explicitly includes protecting “property and the economy” within its mission.

Economic stability became securitized.

And that securitization has political teeth.

Under the Trump administration, rhetoric escalated dramatically around domestic extremism. Officials repeatedly warned that “antifa” and anti-capitalist movements posed domestic terror threats. In 2020, President Trump publicly called for designating antifa a terrorist organization. Attorney General William Barr described “violent anarchists and extremists” as domestic terrorists. Administration messaging frequently blurred the line between anti-capitalist protest and national security threat.

When anti-capitalism can be rhetorically adjacent to domestic terrorism, protecting capital itself becomes framed as a security imperative.

Once that conceptual bridge is built, protecting a downtown revenue district is no longer just economic policy. It is homeland defense.

Opposing surveillance can be reframed as undermining safety.
Opposing militarized equipment can be reframed as risking catastrophe.
Opposing corporate underwriting can be reframed as inviting terrorism.

National security becomes the solvent that dissolves democratic debate.

And then there is Oracle.

Nashville’s East Bank deal would bring one of the world’s most powerful data infrastructure companies directly into the city’s future. Oracle is not just another tech employer. It is a major federal contractor whose cloud systems are built to handle the kinds of data modern government runs on, including national security workloads. Its co-founder Larry Ellison has been unusually explicit about what he believes that future looks like: a society blanketed with surveillance where constant monitoring keeps citizens on their “best behavior.”

That is not dystopian fiction. It is the worldview of one of the most influential men in global computing.

So when Nashville expands permanent downtown surveillance while simultaneously welcoming a surveillance-adjacent data giant into its core development plans, it is not coincidence. It is convergence. The police hardware, the corporate subsidies, the carceral economy, the federal security rhetoric, and the intelligence-linked data infrastructure are all aligning around the same governing logic.

Downtown is “different” because it has become something neighborhoods rarely get to be: an asset protected by public money. What is being built is not simply a safer district, but an insurance policy for private gains, justified through the language of national security.

Downtown is no longer just a place. It is an asset class. And in America, assets are defended.

But the private lives of our citizens and the profits that can be made by moving their bodies where billionaires want them? That’s a commodity. And commodities are not protected. Commodities are extracted. Commodities are optimized. Commodities are traded. Commodities are surveilled, measured, categorized, and priced. Commodities are not treated as human beings with rights and dignity; they are treated as inputs in a system designed to maximize yield.

The Post-9/11 Blueprint

This logic did not originate in Nashville.

After 9/11, federal homeland security doctrine formally linked “critical infrastructure protection” to national economic stability. The Department of Homeland Security defines critical infrastructure as systems whose disruption would weaken not only safety but economic security.

The wars in Iraq and Afghanistan, when finally the mask fell and it could no longer argued they were prompted by a response to the terror attacks or credible “weapons of mass destruction”, they were reframed as economic necessities to ensure the financial stability of the homeland though strategic control of oil.

The Tennessee Office of Homeland Security, housed within the Department of Safety and Homeland Security, explicitly states that its mission includes protecting “property and the economy” from acts of terrorism.

That phrasing matters. It institutionalizes the idea that economic continuity is a security objective.

Nationally, industrial security hardware has become normalized — not as extraordinary militarization, but as “preparedness.” But preparedness for what?

Our governments are increasingly are prepared to go to war against someone. The terms used in outlays like the $15M surveillance grant, “tactical command centers,” these are the language of war. Against whom are our police planning a war?

As we all vividly saw in Minneapolis, with no actual mass invasion of violent immigrants or insurgent domestic terrorists to target, the focus and surveillance and militarization can only turn on one populace: the civilians who call these places home.

The Bombing and the Real Failure

The 2020 Christmas Day bombing in Nashville was a shocking explosion that damaged dozens of downtown buildings and disrupted telecommunications, but it was not a failure of surveillance technology. It was a failure of policy, process, and follow-through.

More than a year before the blast, Nashville police visited Anthony Quinn Warner’s home after a woman reported that he was building bombs in his RV. An attorney representing her told officers that Warner “knows what he is doing and is capable of making a bomb.” Officers knocked on the door, but did not see inside the RV and did not make contact with Warner. After running standard database checks with the FBI and Department of Defense — which returned no matches — no further action was taken, because no crime had yet occurred and there was nothing to charge.

The real breakdown was not a lack of cameras or more data feeds. It was a lack of meaningful follow-up, interagency coordination, and institutional capacity to turn warning signs into effective prevention. That kind of work is mundane and difficult — it requires people to do their actual jobs, build trusting lines of communication, and think carefully about where “care” ends and “safety” begins.

But it is far easier, and far more politically excitable, to imagine a world blanketed with cameras feeding AI systems trained on amorphous, loosely defined concepts of “danger,” than to demand that bureaucracies actually fix their processes.

And the hard truth for law enforcement officers to confront is that, despite how often they are used as a cudgel against the least powerful by the most powerful, they are still members of the labor underclass, and they too will be replaced as soon as AI makes it economically feasible.

The Question Nashville Has to Answer

The real debate is not about a single camera system or one tactical vehicle. It is about how Nashville understands its downtown core and what obligations follow from that understanding. Is downtown primarily a neighborhood composed of residents, workers, and small business owners whose stability matters in its own right? Or is it a revenue-generating district whose primary function is to sustain tax flows, property valuations, and large-scale commercial activity?

The mayor’s recent exchange regarding ACME Feed & Seed clarifies the governing philosophy more than any campaign slogan could. When asked about a reported property tax increase from roughly $129,000 to approximately $600,000, Mayor O’Connell emphasized that valuations reflect market value as determined under state oversight, that the property itself is “an incredibly valuable piece of property,” and that it is up to the owner “to determine how to capture the value that’s inherent in the property.” He added that “the market evolves,” noting that new businesses open even as “beloved old businesses” close.

Taken in full context, the response was not flippant. It was doctrinal. The city does not see itself as force for preserving the character of the city and the longevity of any particular version of Nashville. It sees itself as a steward of market conditions, greased at all pointed possible to be as efficient for capital extraction by any means necessary. If valuations rise and long-standing operators cannot absorb the increase, that is understood as a function of competitive demand rather than a public policy failure. Downtown property is valuable because people want to be there, and that demand is treated as a sign of health, and the business of government stops at helping make rich people richer.

Yet when the conversation turns to security spending, the logic shifts. We are told that Nashville is “one incident away” from losing funding or undermining confidence. We are told that downtown’s safety environment is categorically different from other parts of the city. We are asked to accept that armored vehicles, mobile command posts, and expanded surveillance infrastructure are necessary safeguards for a uniquely sensitive zone.

The asymmetry is difficult to ignore. When market pressures displace an established business, that is evolution. When the possibility of a high-profile crime threatens tourism revenue, that is crisis. The city does not intervene to shield individual operators from valuation spikes, but it is prepared to invest heavily in shielding the revenue ecosystem that keeps valuations high for the billionaire class.

This is not hypocrisy so much as hierarchy. The priority is not any single storefront; it is the aggregate yield of the district. The underlying objective is to maintain confidence, maintain throughput, maintain exchange value. Downtown is treated less as a lived space and more as a critical node in a broader economic network. It is something to stabilize, something to harden, something to insure.

In that framework, the language of “public safety” begins to blur with the language of asset protection. Investments in surveillance and rapid-response capacity are justified not only by the desire to prevent harm, but by the need to prevent disruption. The feared outcome is not simply violence; it is volatility. Not only injury, but interruption.

The uncomfortable question, then, is whether Nashville is protecting the people who animate downtown or the financial architecture that extracts value from their presence. Those are related concerns, but they are not identical. A city that prioritizes housing affordability, tax equity, and small-business stability is centering community resilience. A city that prioritizes hardening infrastructure to reassure investors and event organizers is centering revenue continuity.

If downtown is “different,” it may not be because it is uniquely dangerous. It may be because it is uniquely profitable. And once profitability becomes the defining feature of a place, the policies that follow tend to resemble the defense of an asset rather than the care of a community.

The question Nashville has to answer, then, is not whether it values safety, but whether it believes that when it comes to protecting certain people’s profits, no price is too high—not even the steady erosion of everyone else’s civil liberties.

Strings Attached: The History of the Metro Arts Commission & The Nashville Symphony, Part III

An Unequal Equality

[Preface to be written]

[Need to reengage the audience with something informative but with some sort of a hook.]

Last time we talked about the difficulties that Metro Arts had running a revenue-generating festival and simultaneously giving out $500k to arts groups. It solved this in 1992 by giving up the whole making money thing.

It spun off the once profitable Summer Lights Festival to fend for itself after a literal circus clown, who worked for Ronald Reagan and Donald Trump, failed to turn around the festival from which the city had already divested.

Without the Summer Lights Festival to run, and with the resignation of the Executive Director who had made it a success, Metro Arts turned its attention to being the Metro government’s first and only arts funding mechanism. After some hiccups, the funding started to flow out of the city budget smoothly.

The first year of funding gave $250,000 to the Symphony and 24 other arts groups.

Over the next few years, the Boner administration and Metro Council seemed to be winding the subsidies down. After the first years of funding in 1988, the total Metro Arts allocations declined 23%, 17%, and then 26% year after year up until the fiscal year 1993.

Funding cuts from a state level were part of the problem. State-wide, the arts were being divested from, and the funding that Metro Arts counted on was drying up.

During that offramp period, funding was reduced for the arts overall, and the Symphony received significantly less support from the city compared to its first year. Instead of the $250,000 in 1988, in FY92 it got $135,000. But still, that was $100,000 more than the next highest recipient.

That final year of the Boner administration, Metro Arts Grants amounted to only $221,632. The Symphony still took home the lion’s share, with $110,972. Second place was tied by the Nashville Institute for the Arts and the W.O. Smith Nashville Community Music School: each received only $10k.

With the — selling tickets, yearly subscriptions, and philanthropic donations. As the soon-to-resign Executive Director of Metro Arts, Anne Brown put it in 1991, “The one saving grace is that these groups are very inventive and have learned in recent times to find other sources of income.”

She would be proved correct, in a way.

By 1992, things weren’t looking rosy for arts groups looking for a reliable source of funding for any arts organizations, even for The Symphony and the other major performing arts organizations. Things also weren’t looking great for Bill Boner.

Boner’s reputation soured after the whole bragging about your hot new wife on national television thing. And sadly, their marriage was as short-lived as their creative partnership, with their performance on the Phillip Morris Stage on Legislative Plaza at the 1991 Summer Lights Festival the last record I can find of their artistic union.

Following the discovery Boner had, shockingly, committed infidelity, the couple entered divorce processing and a dispute about access to their shared home became headline news. Traci accused Boner of using Metro Police Officers to plant recording devices in their home so that he could “seriously embarrass her.”

Their divorce in 1992 would lead to of the most cursed covers of the Tennessean I’ve come across.

Two articles about Boner and Peele’s divorce appeared above the fold, with other headlines detailing a massive prostitution sting operation, and an early Phil Williams piece on a white supremacist in Mt. Juliet accused of attempting to train a neo-nazi militia in Hickman County with the goal of “executing” Tennessean publisher and famed civil rights advocate John Seigenthaler.

I don’t know, guys. 2024 doesn’t seem so bad some times.

But things did change at City Hall in 1992. Phil Bredesen became mayor. And despite state funding for the arts drying up, funding for the arts in Nashville came back with a vengeance once he was in office.

Under Boner, Metro Arts gave out an average of $340k per year to arts organizations.

Under Bredesen, it gave out an average of $1.1M every year.

In that period, from fiscal years 1992 to 1999, The Metro Arts Commission spread the support wider than just the Symphony, establishing this fund as more than just a life raft for the increasingly solvent Symphony.

Under Bredsen The Symphony’s take-home grew year after year (until 1999). But the funds for other organizations grew at an even faster rate. Equity seemed to be coming to the arts. The End.

Just kidding.

It was true that other groups were getting a larger chunk of the overall funds as the millennium headed toward a close. But that didn’t mean that all types of arts groups were getting significant funding year after year. Rather, there was a small cohort, time and time again, that was getting the bulk of the funding.

The funding that was not going to these large arts organizations accounted for on average 22% of total grant funding each year. It was distributed via a game of musical chairs to other smaller groups. These smaller arts organizations were forced to compete for the rest, with the same groups rarely getting consistent funding.

The years from ‘89 to ‘99 averaged 33 total grant recipients. But that funding was far from evenly distributed. The top 10 recipients over that period received the vast majority of those funds, 78% of all total funding under Bredesen.

The top ten recipients of funding from Metro Arts on average received funding nearly every year after the inception of arts grants in 1989 to the end of Bredesen’s administration in 1999.

These organizations not only received almost 80% of all Metro Arts funds, they were able to count on this funding year after year. The list of the organizations that most often received funding closely mirrored the list of who got the most money

The same was not true of the smaller arts groups that competed each year to fill the roughly 25 slots that didn’t go to the same organizations each year.

Below is a chart of how often groups got funded from 1988 to 1999. The Nashville Symphony, TN Rep Theatre, The Nashville Ballet, Cheekwood, The Nashville Institute for the Art, The Nashville Children’s Theatre, The W.O. Smith Nashville Music School, and The Circle Players all received funding every year.

By 1999, what started as a bailout of a single arts organization, The Symphony, had turned into a broader public-private project, with arts funding consistently going to certain organizations, and others competing for the remainder, unable to count on it year after year to sustainably grow. It was a system of funding that was increasingly funding more than just the Symphony equally, but not every arts org was considered equal to the Symphony.

I don’t mean to frame this, however, with lots of charts and number crunching, as some big secret I dug up from newspaper archives and running pivot tables on public funding data.

What news articles from the era show is that this was, unambiguously, the point of these funds if you asked the people most responsible for their allocation. These weren’t backroom deals or covert thumbs being placed on the scales of commission votes. The expressed sentiment of city leaders, up to and including the Mayor, was that these arts groups were entitled to these funds year after year.

In 1998, Bredesen told the Tennessean: “That money was originally put in the budget with the idea that the major arts groups would have a stable source of funding they could more or less count on year after year."

In case you’re wondering why no one thought this was weird back then — they did.

Metro Art’s First Meltdown

In 1992, Alan Bostick gave a play-by-play of a Metro Arts meeting that went haywire.

On May 21, 1992, The Tennessean published Alan Bostick’s Fine Arts column, entitled: “Showdown looming for arts funds?” It ominously led with a line that could have come from 2024: “A forum titled ‘Metro Funding for the Arts’ would not appear to be another way of saying ‘Blood Bath.’”

The fighting was over the inequitable allocations between large and small organizations, and most notably the Symphony — which by 1992 received a total of $650,972, a full 47% of what all arts organizations had gotten.

By this point Bill Boner was gone, relegated to the “remember when that happened?” waste bin of Nashville’s collective unconscious. A new executive was overseeing Metro’s only arts funding.

Phil Bredesen, the Nashville Symphony board member who had used the $250,000 allocation from the city to strike a hard bargain with striking musicians, was now mayor. He’d resigned from the Symphony Board, but made no bones about expressing his priorities for steering this funding to the Symphony during the eight years of his Mayorship.

The meeting in 1992 was an Arts Commission forum, requesting public feedback about Metro Arts grant distributions. Pretty quickly the room was divided in two. Bostick documented the conflict for the Tennessean.

“It came down to an old-fashioned stand-off between 'big' arts groups and 'little' ones, a contemporary version of David and Goliath,” Bostick wrote in his “Fine Arts” column.

Bostick, however, had an interesting take on the Bible story.

He explained that this version of the story had “an important twist — Goliath’s the good guy here.”

Bostick would later serve as Senior Communications Director for the Nashville Symphony from 2006 to 2010. 

His commentary on the event is very much a “Rich Text” in the vein of this Substack. Here’s how he describes the conflict.

Jacky Christian, executive director of Old-Time Music and Dance Foundation, a group of local folk music artists, became, well, perturbed after hearing a proposal that "biggies" like the Nashville Symphony and Tennessee Repertory Theatre should receive about 90% of the grant money, the rest being divided among smaller organizations.

That proposal, for the large performing arts organizations to receive 90% of all arts funding, came from (as best I can gather from Bostick’s column) The Ingram Group.

It was this lobbying group that Jacky Christian, director of Old-Time Music and Dance Foundation, was concerned wouldn’t have her small organization’s best interest at heart when it went to lobby the Mayor on behalf of the arts.

Alan Bostick’s report of the Metro Arts meeting in 1992 continues:

[Christian] also just heard the Ingram Group was hired by the symphony courtesy of longtime symphony supporter Martha Ingram to meet with Bredesen on behalf of all Nashville arts groups, although, according to Christian, her group hadn't even heard of this, much less agreed to it.

Martha Ingram, longtime Symphony Board Chair, is not, as far as I can tell, directly related to Tom Ingram, founder of the Ingram Group.

But the two Ingrams, connected to the highest centers of power in the city and state, had a lot of weight when knocking on a mayor’s door and kindly suggesting where money for the arts should flow. That’s quite an understatement actually.

Martha Ingram is perhaps the most influential person in the history of the Nashville arts community. She had been a major booster of Mayor Bredesen. It was his hard dealing with the Symphony’s Orchestra Union in 1988 that had helped Bredesen, otherwise merely a New York businessman worth $200M, gain the reputation of doing right by the Nashville donor class that had helped him secure the support of Ingram and the broader political donor class — a pivot step to him winning him his first election on the path to becoming governor.

Tom Ingram and the Ingram Group (a political lobbying and PR firm) on the other hand, had deep ties with the highest rungs of Tennessee power. He had served as Deputy Governor of the state under Lamar Alexandar, and his group had deep ties with cultural institutions.

The Ingram Group had drawn controversy two years prior to this 1992 Metro Arts meeting for its unusual contract with the University of Tennesee’s athletic department.

The TN Finance Commission found it “highly irregular” that the firm had a $48,000 contract with the athletic department, but was also doing consulting for the University’s President, Lamar Alexander, the former governor of Tennessee. The Tennesseean asked The Ingram Group’s founder, Tom Ingram, who had served as Alexander’s Deputy Governor and Chief of Staff, if he was using UT Athletic Department funds to work for his old boss off the books.

"If someone is suggesting that we had that contract to work for Lamar they're crazy," said Ingram, Alexander's deputy when he was governor. "Any work we did for Lamar we did free because we're his friends and have been for years."

A 1991 City Council resolution required organizations to reveal who they had hired to lobby the Metro Council. In January 1992 that information was starting to become public. The Ingram Group, Nashville learned, had lobbied Metro Council on behalf of Opryland, Regional Transportation, and the Airport Authority.

They had also represented Farzin Ferdowsi, a Persian refugee who fled Iran after his father was kidnapped and murdered by the Islamic fundamentalist regime. The Ingram Group’s PR support helped Ferrdowsi flip an empire of Taco Bell chains into a successful bid to develop the Nashville Zoo.

Farzin Ferdowsi would later serve on the board of the Vanderbilt Ingram Cancer Care Center. That one was named after Martha Ingram’s husband, Bronson Ingram, and it is chaired by their son, Orrin.

Ferdowsi has also served on the board of the Tennessee Performing Arts Center (TPAC), and he is now the board chair of Sonata Bank, where he sits alongside fellow board member, Will Alexander, son of Lamar Alexander, the former Tennessee governor, later US Senator, and one-time boss of Ingram Group founder Tom Ingram.

In 2013, Tom Ingram was ranked one of the 500 most influential people in politics by Campaigns and Elections magazine. That same year he was investigated by the Tennessee State Bureau of Ethics and Campaign Finance for not registering as a lobbyist for the past three years. During that period he had been lobbying on behalf of an energy company to coal mine inside Tennessee’s Catoosa Wildlife Management Area.

In 2013 he was receiving undeclared payments for "PR” support for sitting TN Governor Haslem, a move Haslem defended under juicy headlines such as “Haslam Defends Secret Payments To Lobbyist”.

Tom Ingram was also instrumental in Jeb Bush’s low-energy bid for the presidency in 2016.

The Ingram Group was the inside-inside baseball team that Martha Ingram was putting together to represent “all” of the arts organizations when she was going to lobby the mayor, the Symphony’s former board member, for more money.

Jacky Christian from the Old-Time Music and Dance Foundation was skeptical that this team, which had never consulted with her group, two-time recipients of Metro Arts funds, was going to speak in her Old-Time Music and Dance’s best interest. As Alan Bostick tells it:

Soon Christian was on the attack, throwing out charges of elitism, unilateralism and other such things. In the course of a very few minutes, she opened up a festering old wound that the arts establishment here is snobbish, exclusive and the rest.

Well, at least that doesn’t describe the arts establishment here anymore.

After presenting the “perturbed” David side of the conversation, Bostick turned it over to Goliath.

Bostick: “The Rep's Mac Pirkle and Steven Greil, the symphony's executive director, were among those who attempted to apply the salve.”

Let’s hear what Greil has to say about how this isn’t all a bunch of trickle-down economics hoodoo and that he understands the importance of folk artists like her.

Bostick: “Greil observed that the very existence of the smaller groups largely depends on the health of the big ones.”

Oy vey.

Just like in 2024, there was some seemingly language barrier between the two parties. On the one side were grassroots artists who felt they weren’t being included in the decision-making. On the other side were large arts institutions, the wealthy donors who fund them, and the well-connected political class.

But despite even Alan Bostick defining the terms of this divide as “David vs. Goliath”, the was, as there is now, gaslighting that this divide even existed at all.

Bostick: “The irony is that Christian's comments come at a time when a new spirit of cooperation appears stronger than ever. Both the mayor and the Metro Arts Commission, to its continued credit, seem anxious to dispense with petty politics and work for everyone. (As evidenced by this very meeting, as a matter of fact).”

Jacky Christian, Bostick pontificated from his column in the Tennessean, should have been grateful there even was a meeting put together by the Mayor and Metro Arts.

Good vibes were abounding, why was she dragging everything down by talking about something vulgar like money?

Why was Christian resorting to petty politics when everything was coming up roses for arts organizations? The newly elected Bredesen was turning the hose on for arts orgs, it wasn’t a good look to be bickering about petty things like “who actually got that money.”

The thing that “perturbed” Christian was the proposal from the Ingram Group that the large arts organizations should receive 90% of all arts funding. That did not come true.

Large arts organizations only received 83% of the arts funding that year.

$135,000 of that went to Martha Ingram’s Nashville Symphony.

Jacky Christian’s group, The Old-Time Music and Dance Foundation, received $4,000. They would never receive funding from Metro Arts again.

The organization she established still exists. You can see their performances in Shelbyville.

The organization is still going strong, preserving and promoting “traditional acoustic string music and traditional folk dance associated with the early 1900's of Tennessee and Appalachia.”

But they don’t seem to have taken off financially, 32 years after Steven Greil told it’s founder, in so many words, that a rising tide raises all ships. Their tax returns show they haven’t earned over $50k a year recently, if ever.

The Fine Art of Buck Dancing

Let’s take a moment to give Jacky Christian her flowers. Here she is Buck Dancing with Emmylou Harris on TV in 1995.

Tapes of her folk dancing are archived in the “Archives of Appalachia” at East Tennessee State University.

A travelogue through Nashville in the Washington Post from 1986 namechecked her as a must-see local performer to get a sense of the real Nashville.

That night, one of Tennessee's best-known buck dancers, Jacky Christian, jumped into action. She's danced on the Grand Ole Opry stage and for Bill Monroe's band, and was taught by Robert Spicer, who's been teaching Tennesseans to buck dance for decades. If she's there, she'll try to persuade you to get out on the floor, where she'll teach you America's folk dance -- buck dancing.

Jackie Christian appears in many writings about the historical preservation of folk dances of Appalachia. She dedicated herself to preserving its legacy and bringing it the clout and recognition it deserved.

Part of that work was bringing recognition to her mentor, Robert Spicer. Two years before she was more or less told to shut up and sit down by the Nashville arts establishment, her personal efforts had gotten Spicer and their shared art form honored by the National Endowment for the Arts.

Charmaine Lanham tells the story of how Spicer and Christian struggled to preserve the legacy of Tennessee folk dance.

There is no way to measure the impact of the team of Robert Spicer and Jacky Christian. Old Time music and dance would have never gained the prominence, however modest, that it has today. Together they created the most successful traditional dance and teaching partnership in Tennessee’s history…

Robert’s elevation from local personality to nationally-known “Master Practitioner of the Traditional Arts” was due to the efforts of Alabama native Jacky Christian, a classical dancer who hit Nashville with unequaled enthusiasm for buck dancing. Jacky’s dance studies had not included old time country dance until she met Robert. In partnership with him, Jacky became a world-class instructor and taught for many years.

[Christian] founded the incredibly successful Old Time Music and Dance Foundation, preserving the history of the art while performing, and promoting. Using the Old Time Music and Dance Foundation as a platform, Jacky nominated Robert Spicer for the National Heritage Foundation Award. In 1990 Jacky’s efforts were rewarded when at age 69 Robert was given this highest honor which can be bestowed on a folk performer or artisan by the United States.

Spicer and Christian gained acclaim for preserving and teaching “buck dancing”, an Appalachian tradition that has its roots in African dance and Scottish and Irish step dancing. It emerged in the South’s Reconstruction era.

The percussive footwork and rhythmic patterns in buck dancing are rooted in African dance traditions. Kidnapped and enslaved Africans brought their cultural practices to America, including their dance and music. These traditions emphasized complex rhythms and improvisation, which became foundational elements of buck dancing. The use of the body as a percussive instrument, evident in buck dancing, mirrors African dance practices where the feet and hands create rhythmic sounds.

The blending of African and European dance traditions in Appalachia is an example of cultural syncretism, where different cultural elements merge to create something new. This process was facilitated by the close proximity and interactions between African American and European American communities.

Buck dancing emerged in the early 20th century, and under Jim Crow laws, was typically a segregated activity. But its cultural heritage preserved hidden moments of cultural exchange, furtive co-mingling of traditions and art forms, moments when enslaved or newly freed Black and ethnic working poor white Americans danced and celebrated together outside the racialized boundaries of southern society.

Like the banjo, an instrument based on the African akonting, buck dancing became synonymous with white music and dance. But its roots in African traditions reveal moments of hidden camaraderie and shared humanity across racial lines. Somewhere, sometime, during the hot Tennessee nights at the height of brutal chattel slavery in America, some poor white Scots-Irish danced with enslaved African brethren.

That’s what’s preserved in folk traditions like buck dancing. The power of art to capture human connection across time and space.

But the preservation of folk culture, the culture of the people, requires funding.

It requires us to be interested in all forms of art.

It requires us to grapple with questions of: “Who is an artist?”

What do we mean when we say “low” vs “high” art? And what is our responsibility, with limited public funds to preserve and grow artistry that has its origins in Tennessee?

What are our cultural goals? Are they to ape the aesthetics and artistic high water marks of 19th century Austria with its grand opera palaces? Perhaps that’s a “good to do.” But for any city, isn’t preserving the unique culture of a place a “must do?”

We are Nashville.

Our art is folk music and dance. It is country and blues. It is printmaking and rap music. Perhaps the most important question to ask is when it comes to deciding where we invest our limited social resources, and what heritage we preserve and grow, who is entitled to make those decisions?

I have avoided writing about Public and Community Safety issues since leaving the Nashville Mayor’s Office of Community Safety in winter of last year, in deference to those who took my place in that capacity. I break that silence to offer no criticism, just to give Council suggestions for how Metro Nashville may want to spend some extra money it’s now got.

Last night Metro Council voted down a $774,900 “FUSUS” contract, a highly controversial program that would allow citizens to expedite the transmission of privately recorded video footage to Metro Nashville Police. Advocates have touted its ability to speed up police receiving footage when a crime has allegedly taken place. Opponents have expressed concerns of over-policing of minorities, LGBTQIA+ folks, people who may or may not be undocumented, and lack of certainty on matters of oversight, and what the realities of a forth-coming militarized federal Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) means for the independence of local law enforcement.

The strong opinions of the public comment session of last night’s Metro Council meeting were overshadowed by Council Members themselves speaking passionately, sometimes tearfully, about their concerns over the proposed contract, while others steadfastly defended it. Some of the defense of the three quarter million plus dollar contract dismissed concerns as overblown and purely hypothetical, but more often than not the proponents on Council did their best to acknowledge the fears of the opposition.

No one was taking safety lightly. But there was a disagreement about what what a safe community looks like, and for whom.

What was unanimous across the Council floor was the acknowledgement that investing in law enforcement alone is not enough to make us safer.

A place where all of Council agreed, perhaps the only place, was an acknowledgement that we need to invest holistically in things that make us all safer. Things like mental health support, youth services, community policing, addiction recovery services.

Now that Metro Council has decided not to spend $774,900 on FUSUS, what are those programs everyone was so eager to fund? Will we be shifting those funds towards programs we actually agree on?

One thing I’ve noticed, when funding goes to things like law enforcement or the military: the return on investment isn’t that big of a concern. It’s almost as if the symbolic gesture of investing in “security” is the measure of worth of the monies spent. But when it comes to things like investing in communities, there’s much more anxiety about how we spend those tax payer dollars, and how we’re measuring the impact of those outlays.

Social services are nice, but they’re also kinda vague. It feels a whole lot “realer” to invest in police gear. You can see it. You can clank your knuckles against a riot helmet and say “dang, that’ll stop a rock!” But a restorative justice program? That feels pretty touchy feely. That kinda feels like homework. That feels like something I’d get called “soft on crime” for, and what good can I do the city if it doesn’t reelect me to serve it? We can’t really measure how many crimes don’t happen — can we?

Luckily for Nashville, people have been hard at work doing that very research and answering the question: What programs can local governments invest in that actually reduce violent crime and make communities thrive?

And how much money will those lives saved save us? (And we have to measure that because this is, after all, late capitalism, baby.)

Here’s one group that’s been looking into these questions: Community Justice is an organization that has studied what has been empirically demonstrated to reduce violence, and measured how much cities are doing it. They’ve taken a systems change approach and realized that there is no one thing that makes us safer.

There is no magic bullet that stops bullets. Rather, it is a scaffolding of services across the community and the life span of individuals that act together to reduce our rates of violence, and increase our likelihood of thriving.

Take one of those scaffolds out for a person though, and their life likely falls apart, like a game of Jenga with generational consequences.

Community Justice has been rating cities for how many of the vital community safety programs they fund. Nashville got a 40% for 2023. Pretty dismal — but that is up 9% from the year prior. We’re making progress.

And Mayor O’Connell’s Office of Youth Safety is going to get us further up that list. We actually are making progress. We have momentum in investing in the programs that make us safer.

But what are those programs? Why should we take this one organization’s assessment at face value? Are we all going to have to read reports like this and become subject matter experts on everything to do with community safety?

That seems very boring and honestly kind of a bummer!

But alternatively, maybe we just need to take the advice of Judith Clerjeune, the Advocacy Director of the Tennessee Immigrant and Refugee Rights Coalition who spoke last night at the Council Meeting.

She put it like this:

Instead of all the hours of back and forth on this software, what if we were to sit down with all partners, and create a true community safety plan?

I think she’s right.

I think she’s very, very right — we need to have a conversation about what we mean when we say “Community Safety.” There is no one contract that is going to solve our safety problems — and there’s no one department or person who has all the pieces of the puzzle that will lower our homicide rate and increase our collective thriving.

We need to work on this together. Not because its equitable or nice or because our DEI administrator said we have to, but because working on this together is the only thing that’ll work.

Providing safety is not the job of the police. It is the collective job of all of us. It’s the very point of why we huddle together in these weird things called towns and cities. Because together, even despite our ever more polarized contempt for each other, we are safer and happier together than we can ever be alone.

The purpose of government is to enable the people of a nation to live in safety and happiness.

-Thomas Jefferson

That’s a real Jefferson quote. Ain’t that wild? Very convenient for my argument.

If all the aspects of the government and society in general, not just the police and military and Ring Cameras, collectively bring about safety and happiness — perhaps the relevant question about what causes insecurity and misery is what, and who, are we not investing in?

That’s what we need to be talking about together: What do we need to be doing that’ll make our community feel safe for us all.

But what do we do at this meeting with “all the partners” that Judith proposed?

How do we have tough conversations that aren’t just call-out sessions that make us white people shut down and lash out? (Doing that is so 2020.) How do we make it be an actual dialogue that creates new bonds between “accidental adversaries”?

How do we look and think deeply at entrenched issues, without just funding expensive studies that no one reads and surveying the residents of 37208 to death?

Where can we start? Is it as simple as just making a list of:

  1. What do we want our city to be like?

  2. What works to get there?

  3. What are we doing to get there?

  4. What else do we need to be doing?

We need to start somewhere if we want to get anywhere.

Towards a Community Safety Plan for Nashville

View as Doc

Nashville should follow national best practices and perform a thorough assessment of its Violence Prevention Community Safety programming. The city should identify areas where we need deeper investment in services, performed by public and private organizations, which impact the outcomes relevant to community safety, and then set up mechanisms to fund and monitor the success of those programs.

There are 10 Categories and 35 program types that demonstrably lower crime and violence and enhance community resilience and life quality. Nashville must identify how much investment is in these programs and fund those programs appropriately to our size and need (preliminary efforts here).

Some core concepts about Community Safety

  • National Standards for Community Safety and Anti-Violence plans already exist.

    • We do not need new commissions telling us what we need to study and implement, we already know what we need to know and do to lower violence and enhance safety.

    • The Community Justice Action Fund’s Violence Prevention Index has already done the hard work of identifying what works to reduce violence.

  • Nashville scored very low on the Violence Prevention Index, only 31% in 2022.

  • There are three periods of time when you can invest in preventing violence, each require different types of programs.

    • 0-30 Years Before | Prevention Programs

    • 0-1 Year Before | Intervention Programs

    • Concurrent | Interruption Programs

  • Public Safety is different from Community Safety.

    • Community Safety programs are geared toward preventing violence and crises. Community Safety workers are perceived to be generally inside community spaces.

    • Public Safety programs are geared toward responding to violence and crises. Public safety workers are perceived to be generally outside community spaces.

    • Community safety work is not the responsibility of any one department or agency.

  • Just as public safety involves many agencies, community safety involves, e.g., Social Services, Health Department, Office of Family Safety, MNPS, Metro Action Commission, TN Department of Labor, and more, as well as many NGOs.

Towards an Complex and Effective Understanding of Community Safety

Mayor O’Connell is not the first to highlight a need for a comprehensive plan to address community safety. A draft of a letter to Former Mayor Cooper from the Healthy Nashville Leadership Council (HNLC) Community Safety Workgroup explored community safety as one of the community health improvement plan (CHIP) strategic issues, and provided the following preliminary feedback:

Following research of the local landscape on community safety to identify appropriate stakeholders and community safety plans, the workgroup members experienced challenges related to obtaining comprehensive information and plans related to Nashville’s community safety “systems”. It became apparent that while we have various resources directed at our community safety issues, a comprehensive community approach or plan was not evident.

The draft letter also proposed that since these efforts need “anchored leadership to coordinate the city-wide efforts and implement a strategic vision for community safety in Nashville,” that MNPD should house a department that would “focus on establishing and maintaining partnerships with community agencies and neighborhood groups, assist in the decision making regarding how funding is directed, and provide ongoing coordination to enhance and scale evidence-based community safety strategies.”

There’s a reason why law enforcement agencies are often seen as the proper place to produce and implement “Community Safety” plans, and that is the perceived synonymousness of the concepts of “Public Safety” and “Community Safety.” Often used interchangeably, these have different academic and practical definitions that have important differences in implementation and agenda. When applied to programs, these different perspectives have enormous impact on how they affect communities and are able to prevent crime.

Definitions of Public vs Community Safety

Public Safety

The protection of the general public from harm, is provided by a wide range of public workers that enter areas to respond to community needs as requested. Relevant programs include law enforcement, firefighting, emergency medical services, and public health.

Community Safety

The protection from the root causes of crime and violence, such as poverty, inequality, and lack of opportunity, is provided by those perceived to be within the community. Relevant workers include teachers, healthcare providers, and credible messengers.

To put it simply:

Public Safety programs are geared toward responding to violence and crises. Public safety workers are perceived to enter community spaces when needed.

Community Safety programs are geared toward preventing violence and crises. Community Safety workers are perceived to be already inside community spaces.

The theories of community safety and public safety can be viewed along a spectrum, with dialectical oppositions at the extremes and a need for balance between them.

A correlate to this framing, and something very much supported by research, is that Public Safety departments’ function is not to prevent crime and violence. This is well established in the literature1 and should not be perceived as a slight on the effectiveness of public safety departments. It is simply that despite any size of investment a municipality can make in public safety, the labor force and skills involved in public safety have little to do with things that empirically prevent and reduce crime.

The underinvestment in the actual social programs that prevent violence turns into a burden on police and other law enforcement. This not only leads to minimal violence prevention (since this is outside the skill set and remit of these agencies) but it also leads to burnout, stress, and mental health issues for public safety workers.

The Three Times You Can Stop Violence

Stopping violence requires a multi-faceted approach across different time horizons. This section outlines three key timeframes for violence prevention and the types of strategies used at each stage.

  • Prevention (0-30 Years Pre-Violence): These programs are designed to tackle the root causes of violence long before it occurs. They include services aimed at providing employment, educational support, services for youth and families, community health services, housing and food security, and firearm regulations. These are long-term strategies that aim to build a strong social foundation, thereby reducing the likelihood of violence.

  • Intervention (0-1 Years Pre-Violence): Closer to the potential event of violence, intervention strategies include Group Violence Intervention (GVI), programs with credible messengers who have influence within the community, restorative justice initiatives, diversion programs to steer individuals away from the criminal justice system, addiction support, survivor support services, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), partnerships in care, and emergency housing. These are more immediate, addressing imminent risks and helping to prevent violence before it starts.

  • Interruption (Post Violence): These are reactive measures taken after an incident of violence has occurred. They are designed to prevent further violence and include public safety agencies, violence interrupters, and hospital programs. These are typically where law enforcement and emergency medical services are most involved, responding to and managing the situation after violence has taken place.

Figures 1 & 2 show that there is a spectrum of anti-violence efforts, with various agencies and programs operating at different stages. It underscores the importance of a comprehensive approach to violence prevention, which includes long-term community-building efforts, targeted interventions to address immediate risks, and responsive measures to halt the cycle of violence once it has begun.

The model suggests that for effective violence reduction, communities need to invest in a wide array of programs that not only respond to violence but also prevent it by addressing systemic issues and providing support services. It also hints at the need for collaboration across different sectors and the importance of tailoring interventions to the specific time frame relative to the violence occurring.

Protective factors play a crucial role in preventing harm and fostering resilience in individuals. These factors can be classified into different categories based on whether they are individual, familial, or community-based.

  • Individual Protective Factors:

    • These include caregivers who establish safe, positive relationships with children, practice nurturing parenting skills, and provide emotional support. The ability of caregivers to meet basic needs like food, shelter, education, and health services is also significant. Higher educational attainment and steady employment among caregivers are also protective factors​​.

    • Mental health is closely related to personal strengths and weaknesses. Protective traits help individuals react resiliently to difficult situations, minimizing the impact of risk factors. Resilience, an aspect of positive psychology, is crucial for maintaining or improving mental health under stress. Examples of individual protective factors include strong social connections, hope for the future, positive goals, problem-solving skills, strong family relationships, positive experiences with parents, a safe environment, availability of counselors, healthy eating, and physical activity​​.

  • Family Protective Factors:

    • These include families with strong social support networks and stable, positive relationships. The presence and interest of caregivers in a child's life, enforcement of household rules, and engagement in child monitoring are important. Additionally, having caring adults outside the family who can serve as role models or mentors contributes to a protective environment​​.

  • Community Protective Factors:

    • Communities that provide access to safe, stable housing, high-quality preschool, nurturing and safe childcare, engaging after-school programs, medical care, mental health services, and economic and financial help are protective. Work opportunities with family-friendly policies and strong community partnerships also serve as protective factors​​​​.

The importance of these factors lies in their ability to reduce the likelihood of maltreatment and other harms. By identifying and enhancing protective factors, individuals can find resources, support, or coping strategies that allow them to deal effectively with stress and challenges. This approach is particularly effective in mitigating the impact of risk factors and promoting mental well-being, even when risk factors are present. Protective factors enhance resilience, allowing individuals to bounce back from difficult situations more adeptly. Recognizing and actively building upon these factors can start preventative steps to lessen the impact of risk factors on one's life and begin the path to maintaining mental balance​​​​.

Much of this aligns with the extensive, well researched and well funded work around “Adverse Childhood Experiences” (ACEs) The correlation between negative childhood conditions and events has been so well documented, that municipalities are increasingly building their social systems around preventing ACEs, and/or providing interventions to gain “resilience” to overcome the negative effects of them.

“Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) can have a tremendous impact on future violence victimization and perpetration, and lifelong health and opportunity. CDC works to understand ACEs and prevent them.”
CDC.gov

“The National Governors Association (NGA) has supported states in their efforts on ACEs, trauma and resiliency through multiple projects over the years.”
Nga.org

“Childhood adversity is associated with adult criminality. We suggest that to decrease criminal recidivism, treatment interventions must focus on the effects of early life experiences.”
Reavis JA, Looman J, Franco KA, Rojas B.(2013)

Protective factors across individual, family, and community levels play a pivotal role in harm prevention and resilience building. They provide a buffer against risk factors and are integral to fostering well-being and mental health.

The analogy between anti-violence programs and individual protective factors is based on the concept of creating environments and conditions that reduce the likelihood of harm and foster resilience.

  • Preventive Measures and Individual Protective Factors: Programs like housing and food security, community health services, and school-based services parallel individual protective factors such as a safe living environment, good health, and education. These elements create a foundation that helps prevent the onset of violence, similar to how individual protective factors in a person's life reduce the likelihood of negative outcomes.

  • Intervention Strategies and Crisis Support: Intervention programs like mental health co-response and survivor support are akin to personal crisis support and coping strategies. Just as individual protective factors help a person navigate through difficult times, these intervention programs provide immediate support and resources to individuals and communities facing imminent or recent violence

  • Interruption Services and Immediate Response: The interruption phase, involving hospital-based intervention and violence interrupters, is comparable to immediate personal response mechanisms in crisis situations. These services aim to swiftly address and mitigate the impact of violence as it occurs, much like how an individual's immediate coping strategies or support systems work to reduce harm and foster resilience in the face of a crisis.

The programs and services within these three categories work at a systemic level to create conditions and provide resources that parallel the protective factors at an individual level, aiming to prevent harm, provide timely intervention, and effectively interrupt ongoing violence, thereby fostering resilience and reducing trauma in the community.

The Interplay of Public vs Community Safety

Community Safety approaches, those towards the left side of Figs. 1 & 2, prioritize the “social determinants” of health and safety, focusing on preventative measures that address economic, social, and environmental conditions contributing to violence. This perspective argues for upstream interventions, investing in public health, economic opportunity, housing security, youth development, education, and institutional transformation to prevent violence​​.

This perspective also aligns with the perspective of violence in society as a public health crisis rather than a criminal justice issue to solve. Going back to the 1960s, research was undertaken focused on the circumstances that explored the links between social inequality, discrimination, and violence.

For the past two decades, extensive literature has explored “the social determinants of health” and how poverty, inequality, discrimination, and lack of access to education and healthcare, played a significant role in driving violence. This has culminated in an open letter to congress entitled “Community Safety Agenda: Public Safety Needs a Public Health Approach” undersigned by nearly 70 civil rights groups.

The Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights have lobbied congress to reevaluate community safety as a “Public Health” issue. Their open letter, with 69 undersigned civil rights organizations, asks congress to refocus their attention when it comes to community safety onto the categories of:

  • Community Health & Prevention Programs

  • Economic Opportunity & Housing Security

  • Youth, Families & Community Spaces

These three categories of work fall outside the purview of all law enforcement agencies. If you look at Figures 1 and 2, these action areas fall definitively in Column 1 (Prevention) and to some degree Column 2 (Intervention). The overburdening of these types of social welfare activities has been highlighted as a major burden on law enforcement agencies that prevents them from executing their primary functions.

Working towards other goals, “public safety” typically involves reactive measures such as law enforcement and emergency services. Unbalanced reliance on community safety alone could result in insufficient immediate response capabilities to crime and emergencies, while overemphasis on public safety can lead to over-policing and underinvestment in social services.

The integration of both community and public safety efforts is necessary to address both the symptoms and root causes of violence and harm​​.

Another lens that is useful is internal vs external protection, or as Shakey et al. (2017) would put it, “exogenous” vs “endogenous”. In large municipalities, Public Safety work presents most often as outsiders who enter a community to perform tasks (arresting perpetrators, putting out fires, resuscitating victims). Community safety workers present more often as insiders raising up the community from within (teachers, employers, youth group leaders, credible messengers).

Research, such as Sharkey et. al (2017) indicates that this latter form of self-intervention does more than just solve immediate problems, but strengthens what is known as “social cohesion,” a critical communal social trait that correlates with lower rates of violence. When communities have the resources internally to solve their problems, through sufficient funding and equitable access to social resources and capital, social cohesion is augmented.

“The degree of social cohesion and informal social control within communities arises from local networks of organizations, institutions, and residents who work together to uphold common values and shared expectations of behavior.” This may align with describing community violence as a “public health” issue, which can be “treated” by enhancing endogenous services, rather than increasing the exogenous solutions that come from a “Public Safety” approach.

The 10 Categories of Violence Prevention

The Community Justice Action (CJA) Fund has developed an index of 10 categories and 35 program types that demonstrably reduce violent crime and enhance community resilience. The categories of these programs are as follows:

  • Intervention

  • Risk Factor Reduction

  • Housing and Food Insecurity

  • Employment

  • Education

  • Health

  • Youth and Families Services

  • Local Firearm Laws and Regulations

  • Crime Reporting and Data

  • Local Office of Violence Prevention

After a concerted, collaborative effort to consolidate and document all of Metro Nashville Funded “Violence Prevention” programs undertaken by the Office of Community Safety, which included 36 programs across 44 public and private agencies, Nashville still only scored a 40% for 2023.

What this effort undertaken by the Office of Community Safety highlighted, which very much aligns the CHIP draft letters frustration in finding a lack of “comprehensive information and plans related to Nashville’s community safety ‘systems’” is that because these efforts are so wide reaching, and go across municipal departments with different raison d’etres, philosophies, value systems, KPIs and approaches towards marginalized communities and offenders, it is difficult to have constructive, goal-oriented conversations with empowered stakeholders.

A standalone office of Violence Prevention, itself one of the 10 categories of programs recommended to implement by the CJA, is likely the only way to oversee the implementation of these programs at scale.

This office would, as per suggest parameters from the CJA:

  • Fund and coordinating activities of community-based violence intervention and prevention programs.

  • Provide technical assistance to community violence intervention and prevention organizations and programs.

  • Oversee regular quality and efficacy evaluations of the strategic plan, local government violence prevention efforts, and community-based violence prevention programs.

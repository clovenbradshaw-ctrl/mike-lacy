Yesterday, Friday, September 20, Nashville Mayor Freddie O’Connell announced that his nominee for the Director of Finance, replacing the interim Kevin Crumbo, would be Jenneen Reed.

If she is approved by Metro Council, she would take office in January of ‘25.

Bottom Line Up Front:
Jenneen Reed, nominated as Nashville’s next Director of Finance, faces a challenging role shaped by past conflicts between Metro Finance, Metro Arts, and public officials.

Recent controversies involving various Metro Departments, including Metro Finance, have damaged public trust in public institutions, an issue generally in America and increasingly dire in Tennessee.

If Reed steps into this role, her success will depend on fostering open, collaborative decision-making and reforming outdated financial processes.

More than just managing the budget, her task will be to help restore faith in the city's institutions, rebuild trust, and ensure accountability in a system that has too often operated behind closed doors.

Like Crumbo before her, her recent professional credits include serving in a senior financial capacity at the Nashville Symphony and within Metro Finance. She served under Crumbo as Deputy Director of Finance before being the Chief Financial Officer at the Symphony. And up until 2021, she spent 24 years at the Tennessee Titans, the last dozen as their CFO.

What should she know about what she’s walking into?

What lessons have emerged over the year from the high-profile showdowns between the outgoing interim Finance Director and the former leader of the Arts Commission? From the conflict between Metro Arts’ commissioners and staff, and the artists that won settlements out of Metro’s Legal insistence on reversing a Commission vote?

At the bottom of this more or less thesis paper is my advice on what any incoming Finance Director may want to focus on. You can cut to the chase of that here. But I'd recommend working your way through all of this post eventually.

Even for those of us who feel like we have a good grasp of what's happened, it's important to grasp how much, about anything, we just can't know. And yet somehow we must, collectively, figure out how to lay down our blindspots, and build something better. Together.

How We Got Here

Ever since the Nashville Symphony averted bankruptcy with the help of $250,000 in 1988, delivered to it by the Metro Arts Commission (the first time it ever distributed operational funds to an arts org), Metro Arts has had a complex, crucial, and highly scrutinized role in the local arts world.

Mayor Phil Bredesen, who had used those first funds to close a contentious Symphony Union contract in ‘88, later said the Metro Arts grant monies were “put in the budget with the idea that the major arts groups would have a stable source of funding that they could more or less count on year after year."

Since then, mayors and priorities of constituents have changed. When Arts Commission Executive Director Jen Cole developed Thrive in 2014, it intentionally oriented funding to artists and arts organizations that didn’t fit within Bredesen’s narrow, trickle-down art-e-nomics view of Metro Arts funding.

Cole identified that individual artists needed to be funded, not just organizations. And this was possible if they were paid as contractors rather than receiving funds as grants. This methodology was supported by Metro Finance, and Cole’s vision of more equitable public funding of the arts was uncontroversial at the time.

But these were two very different ways of thinking about what Metro Arts funds were for.

The former was the brain child of Martha Ingram and Mayor Bill Boner, continued and vastly expanded upon by Phil Bredesen, and was designed to give large classical arts institutions a cushion year over year.

The latter was the advent of an Executive Director, Jen Cole, in the initial phase of the new civil rights reckonings of the past 10 years (beginning in earnest after the killing of Michael Brown in Ferguson), when social institutions the country over began reassessing the impact they were generating through a racial equity lens.

Neither of these raison d'etres for Metro Arts grants were made official in any capacity. Despite the City Charter and Code requiring that Metro Arts’ “criteria for the awarding of such funds shall be established by the Metropolitan Nashville Arts Commission and approved by resolution of the metropolitan council,” neither of these modes was articulated in resolution, debated in open session with public comment period, and voted on.

It’s my contention that it is because there was no place for us to have this public discussion of what these funds are for that led to these parallel visions colliding with one another, an inevitable event that was perhaps accelerated by former Director Singh, but was not set in motion by him.

Its possible, however, that with a potential upcoming revision of the Metro Code re: the Arts Commission, that this will change. An ordinance on the docket for Oct 1, BL2024-476, would modify the regulations for the Arts Commission such that the commission would have to present the criteria to Council “each year before funds are awarded.”

This creates a public forum where the intent of these funds can be debated. Where concerns about Supreme Court interpretations and divvying up of funds for large or small organization can be hashed out long before the checks come due.

This is a systemic change, a small tweak that can have large knock-on effects. Is it perfect? No, but few fixes are, they require compromise and to be politically efficient. It’s a far cry from what the original sponsor of this Ordinance, Council Member Styles had hoped for — but it is a step in the right direction. An incoming Director of Finance should take note that this is the type of systemic solutions that improve a city. And there are plenty of inefficiences in our city’s finances that need tweaking.

The Two Metro Arts

There didn’t seem to be much conflict between those visions for Metro Arts, until under Director Singh the equity-focused funding pool, “Thrive”, got priority over the operational grants for larger arts orgs.

36 years after that first grant to the Nashville Symphony, there hasn’t been much reconciling of these two conflicting visions, and little to no open public discourse as to “what is our vision for the arts in Nashville?”

There is a clear, and honestly impressively transparent, process at Metro Arts for deliberating which organizations receive funding. Former Director Singh received much-deserved acclaim for developing community-guided frameworks for how to evaluate groups to receive grants.

But what wasn’t operationalized well under Singh was a forum to deliberate what the overall purpose of this funding was for, and last-minute changes to the funding buckets in July 2023, while fully legal, clearly sewed chaos.

Perhaps Singh felt that opening a public forum and deliberation process for how these funds were meant to be used (not just who received them) would not, in the deceptively illiberal city of Nashville, lead to the outcomes he felt were needed to bring equity for the first time at Metro Arts. Or maybe he thought, with good evidence, that there was no precedent and therefore need, to hold such a discussion.

Given the vagueness of Metro Code regarding the purpose of these funds (a vagueness that arguably allowed them to flow to Phil Bredesen’s preferred few orgs with little to no oversight, reporting, or impact analysis), there was nothing wrong with Singh suggesting the Commission redirect funds towards Thrive.

Besides Bredesen’s statements to the Tennessean in the ‘90s, and very vague language in the Metro Code, there could be no clear legal objection to what Singh was advocating for as there was no codified language that this wasn’t what it was for.

But a year out, it seems like that was a tactical disaster by Singh. A Commissioner asked Metro Legal to investigate whether they had acted unconstitutionally by considering “BIPOC” impact of their votes in light of the recent Supreme Court ruling on affirmative action in higher education. At virtually the same time that Metro Health was “Quietly Reassigning [its] Health Equity Team”, Metro Legal told the Art Commission they had to re-vote lest the open themselves to possible lawsuit (if someone decided to sue for reverse racism about anti-racism).

Not only was the Art Commission encouraged to revote, but the payment methodology that “Thrive” had been using since 2014, long before Singh, was reanalyzed by Metro Finance and determined to be “illegal”.

Whatsmore, the “Metro Arts Dilemma” has been cited, I’ve been told, elsewhere in the state as proof that racial equity cannot be a consideration in funding decisions. Nashville’s arts situation has become an informal precedent of applying the Supreme Court’s ruling on affirmative action to tangential subjects like arts grants. This “precedent” has been made despite a Metro Human Relations Commission report asserting the converse, and a settlement being reached between the city and negatively impacted artists.

For the most part, we take the meaning we want from stories, and rarely have the courage to see what’s really there.

It would probably be wise for Reed to dig into the weeds on this all a bit, and take no single person’s word for what’s happened.

Mine included.

I’ve found that this situation with Metro is indeed a Rich Text that exemplifies the power of what are called “Bayesian Priors”.

The idea of that $10 phrase is that information, whether it’s data, stories, Tweets or Supreme Court Rulings, never comes into your brain unfiltered.

Your “priors”, the worldview you come to any situation with, your unconscious beliefs, prejudices, preferences and experiences, will change the way the information appears to you. And since for each of us the world is as it appears to us, we mostly all live in our own worlds.

I like this “Bayesian Priors” term. I’ve come across it in my unexpected binge of audiobooks that swirl around the 2008 financial collapse, books by people like Michael Lewis and Nate Silver.

Talking about our “biases and prejudices” feels a little captured by social justice perspectives, and while I think that’s a really important lens, it feels boxed in to particular notions of being discriminatory of other people because of various “-isms”. But there’s a lot more to what makes us see the world differently than just our conscious or unconscious animus towards other types of people.

Bayesian Priors get at a broader and deeper swath of preconceptions. These are the assumptions we approach any type of information and experiences with, not just assumptions or prejudices about MAGA people, liberals, guys named Travis, etc. These cover every type of assumption and underlying values that shape our reality.

Wall Street, in addition of course to being greedy (not really a critique of them, it’s literally their point) had some bad priors — very few people (namely the folks you see in The Big Short) believed that an all-out drop in the housing market was possible.

If you were a fringe participant in Occupy Wall Street, as I was, the easy analysis was that the finance types were “greedy”, and “greedy” was kind of a stand-in for evil, and that’s what caused our problems, and the analysis stopped there. But with the benefit of 16 years, the “greedy” critique falls a bit flat. Investment bankers are greedy every day. But only some days they do things that disrupt the world economy for the next quarter century.

Wall Street’s unwillingness to challenge their “priors” led to a unraveling of the global economy, an event whose economic and cultural impact is very much in the background radiation of much of our cultural tensions to this day.

What have been the Bayesian Priors at work in Metro Arts and the financial struggles around it, and how has that shaped how people interpret these events?

To some, this Metro Arts situation has been a story of a Metro Arts Director running wild, making promises he couldn’t keep and enemies he couldn’t contend with.

To others, it’s the story of powerful, mostly white elites reigning in progress and equity in ways that smacked of glad-handing, backroom deal-making and plantation mentalities.

There’s a version of things where unprofessional, illegitimate artists are trying to take the funds from the “real” arts organizations in the city so they can go “buy things at Kroger”.

There’s a version of events that considers the “traditional arts” (Martha Ingram’s preferred art forms of Symphony, Ballet and Opera) as pretensions to cosmopolitanism that come from an insecurity amongst the local elite, manifesting in a disdain of provincial art forms, like country music and folk dance, much less the blues. Through that lens, efforts to diversify funding are looking to raise up the arts that are actually indigenous to Nashville, rather than emulating the traditions of European art centres in past centuries.

Some view Nashville’s arts activists as being crusaders for economic fairness, moving beyond mere representation with “diversity initatives” and towards genuine financial equity.

Other see them as anarchic chaos-makers that just want to see the world burn — the Heath Ledger Jokers of Public Comment periods.

I don’t think the people who hold these different perspectives have access to more or different facts, per se. Rather they’re coming to facts with some preconceptions about whatever they uncover will mean.

In my writing on this over the past six or so months, I don’t honestly believe I’ve changed too many people’s deeper perceptions of what’s been going on. I’ve provided a lot of facts, but not often changed what those facts mean for people.

Folks likely leave my articles, just like after watching a local news report or a political debate, with their Bayesian Priors mostly intact, having gleaned whatever information helped confirm what they already predicted was the case.

What I’m discovering, begrudgingly as somewhat of a misanthrope and a person who is very good at dunking on others, is that the only way for us to change our priors is by building trusting relationship with those who we don’t agree with. Look to “Deep Canvassing” for evidence of this.

These scientists keep proving that reducing prejudice is possible. It’s just not easy.

Why is all this relevant for a candidate for Metro’s next Finance Director to think about?

Restoring faith in our public institutions, faith which nationally is near an all-time low, should be task number one of all public servants.

This lack of trust (often earned by those institutions acting unaccountably) is contributing to a decline in small “d” democratic norms and a slide towards authoritarianism.

The state of Tennessee, which has been ranked lowest for being an effective democracy, has much rebuilding of trust to be made across all our institutions, State and Local. That is if the goal is for Tennessee to be a democracy.

If we’re looking for TN to be a petrie dish for the type of inefficient, financially disasterous, and inhumane authoritarianism that liberal democracies out-competed throughout the 20th century, we’re headed in the right direction.

Democracy at Stake (dramatic, but true)

This is not a PR problem to be solved. There are no talking points to be memorized that will lower folks’ hackles and get things easily back on track toward a more perfect union.

Like every major problem we have, this is a systemic problem that must be worked on and operationalized, led by intelligent, openminded people working across the silos of government agencies, businesses and non-profits, public servants and constituents alike.

We must build better ways of relating and making decisions that build trust, transparency, and efficiency we will need to take on the unprecedented challenges on the horizon.

Making decisions behind closed doors, with opaque and potentially conflict-ridden processes is not bad because it is “undemocratic”.

It’s bad because it produces bad decisions.

Being “democratic” isn’t good in itself. There’s lots of bad things that are arrived at democratically. Green Book won best picture democratically.

Transparent decision-making is crucial because, when done effectively, it leads to more informed choices that offer a competitive edge over authoritarian approaches.

A functional liberal democracy is an often overlooked but essential requirement for a truly free market, as it establishes the necessary regulatory framework for businesses to compete and out innovate each other, which highlights why folks like Elon Musk and Peter Tiel are neither interested in legitimate free market competition or democracy.

Decisions made without transparent methodology and collaborative processes produce narrow-minded, short-term decisions that only “fix” anything if you ignore everything else they break.

Metro needs leadership at the highest level that understands this, and if my past writing is correct that the Director of Finance is the most powerful position in Metro (including the Mayor) then it seems like a good idea for them to get it.

The Metro Arts situation is important to learn from, not so an aspiring appointee can “get on the right side of history” or politically “know which way the wind is blowing” — but because it reveals vividly that the way we do things in public spaces is as important, if not more so, than what we decide to do.

The Quiet Part Out Loud

Singh and Metro Arts was not openly criticized for the intentions behind his funding models, except for in select highly conservative spaces, like in the Nashville Pamphleteer.

The Pampheteer is a small media outlet that mostly produces right wing reviews of movies and tv shows, but in January it published what it considered a bombshell article about the Executive Director Singh, revealing that the India-born Executive Director with years of work in diversity focused arts programming was… anti-colonialist??!

This is worth bringing up because it highlights the strange powder keg we sit on in Nashville. An increasingly emboldened ecosystem of far-right media personalities of the Ben Shapiro-ilk are clout chasing by attacking Metro leaders that were tasked with implementing equity initiatives for the efforts to implement those initiatives.

In my opinion, this makes textual that the aversion to DEI initiatives, like the kind Metro Arts was trying to implement, is interwoven with ambitions to turn back the clock democracy, all stemming from a single core anxiety — the fear that if others are not inferior, how can I ever be superior?

I'm not being hyperbolic. The origins of today's Project 2025 type agendas can be traced back to the Paleo Conservatism of the 1990s. Jon Ganz, in his new book “When The Clock Broke”, talks at length about how, with the ideological enemy of Soviet Socialism crushed, the far right conservative movement set it's eyes on its next enemy: Democracy. Emboldened by the political successes of former KKK Wizard David Duke, and given a clarified mission by Pat Buchanan, the marching orders of leveraging racial animus to deteriorate the representative democracy was embedded into the DNA of certain strain of conservatism.

This has taken root on Nashville.

And the Pamphleteer’s overage of Metro Arts’ attempt to implement the equity focused programming the former Executive Director was under the impression he was hired to implement is case in point.

The slide deck shared with the Pamphleteer, created by a consultant helping operationalize its equity programming, was leaked by someone who apparently found it objectionable. What issues that person had with it, we don’t know. But Davis Hunt’s writings are extraordinarily explicit about why he finds de-colonizing programs problematic.

In the first paragraph of Hunt’s essay on launching the Pamphleteer bemoans the modern era’s inability to provide him with “distant colonies to oversee”.

Without having access to opportunities of “wars worth fighting, land to conquer, untrammeled wilderness to civilize”, Hunt has turned to editing an online local media outlet “to actively engage in the life of the city and assert a vision of it against what I see as homogenizing and destructive forces.”

“It’s in the neglected interior of the country,” he writes, “where the real adventure may soon arise.”

Perhaps written as an apologia on why he returned to his hometown after living near the conservative hotbed of silicon valley, the “real adventure” coming soon is giving ominous rather than inspirational.

Hunt’s article on Metro Arts hits many alt-right talking points, seemingly holding up Metro Arts more equity-focused mission under Former Director Singh as some of these “homogenizing and destructive forces” assaulting Nashville.

The article’s subtitle reads: “Executive Director Daniel Singh has surrounded himself with a cadre of radical consultants and has pledged to re-envision Metro Arts as an anti-racist organization committed to decolonization”

An Aside About (or to) Davis Hunt & The Pamphleteer

Hunt’s unabashed longing for the era of colonialism, where humans could be subjugated, abused, raped, and killed by landed white people without repercussion is… upsetting.

Those particular actions are not what he cites as what he’d like to get out of living in that era, rather it seems to be the unfettered Will to Power that those time periods evoke, where white guys like he and I didn’t have to “subdue our greatness” and hide our Aristotelian magnanimity under a bushel basket.

Absent in these fond recollections for empires past is any meaningful comprehension of the trauma inflicted on others. There’s an obscene disinterest in what it would have been like to be a woman, a person of color, an indigenous person, a peasant or a sex slave in any of these eras. Like a person recounting their past lives, Hunt imagines he would have been Cortez, acquiring glory and gold for god and country.

One might argue that a good way to develop empathy towards other human beings, and comprehension of what’s hiding within the infinate space of nouminal reality, shielded behind our priors, is through art — specifically art focused on raising up the consciousness of the other into our field of perception. Art aimed at deconstructing the notion of otherness, not to destroy and homogenize humanity into a gray melange of trigger warnings, but to unlock our deepest potentials that require connection to that great terror: other people. Potentials that line not in the immature notions of domination and self glorification, which is just so 19th century, but in something new that we don’t know about yet, Davis.

There is an undiscovered country still, terrain to explore and conquor, but instead of physical land that other people already live on and lives we have to shatter to hollowly claim our dominion, it's a place of inner exploration and collective transcendence. It’s a landscape of shared experience and understanding that is boundless and uncolonized by the ego. It’s a place not mapped in with borders, resources, or power but through the deepening of human connection, empathy, and creativity.

I don’t know, man. For whaterver reason, my Bayesian Priors don’t have a problem with acknowledging that Western society and art is deeply flawed, just like how I’m deeply flawed. I wouldn’t be alive if not for colonialism, or for my parents being introduced by Senator Lindsey Graham. These are things I feel deeply weird about, but I don’t hate myself because of them either, and I don’t think anyone who is reasonable and knows what they’re talking when it comes to anti-racism or de-colonization is asking us to hate ourselves either.

I just have a hunch that the “neglected interior” of our souls is “where the real adventure may soon arise.” Why don’t you colonize the beast, the one within that craves warfare and domination, whose only ambition is to stave off the lurking sense of inferiority? What’s underneath that? That’s magnanimity, that’s true Will to Power. Can you discover that without making peace with the outside world? I don’t think so…

This Metro Arts story is chalk full of us chalky folks who don’t know how to reconcile our belief that we are good people with the information that we also need to understand our privilege if we want to continue to be perceived as good people. Even having the option to choose not to understand our privilege is a privilege.

I’ll say this for Davis Hunt of the Pamphleteer: at least he is honest about what he found problematic about changing the way we spend public money — he didn’t like it being decolonial, and he would like, in his own words, to run a colony.

Hunt’s critique did not get caught up in the pedantics of how commissioners were shown what funding models to vote on and if they were leveraging their delegated procurement authority within its original scope. He didn’t like what Metro Arts was doing because of what they were trying to do.

I don’t know why others reacted so strongly to what Metro Arts was doing. Was there a genuine fear of State intradiction after the SCOTUS ruling?

Was it Metro Arts originalism, keeping funds in line with Bredesen’s interpretation of their purpose?

Was it backscratching for the types of folks that bump into each other at The Swan Ball? (My condolences by the way.)

Is there deep alignment with Davis Hunt’s ambitions for an era when authority stemmed not from the people, but from a mandate from god, and a man’s worth was measured in how much blood and treasure he could pile up?

Was it an urge just to turn the clock back a few years? Back when making things more diverse was enough, a few dark faces in the board room sufficed as progress, but we weren’t actually changing where the money went — that’s just… anarchy, or worse, socialism…

Or was it from a place of real concern about “best practices” not being followed, boxes not being checked…

I suppose it doesn’t really matter. The way government is meant to work is that we shouldn’t have reason to ask these questions. They only arise when we’ve gone too far down the primrose path of winging it. It happens when we haven’t taken a step back and asked the fundamentals, like:

What is this money for?

How We Do Things

In the absence of a public forum for Metro Arts to hold those democratic deliberations as to its strategic vision, and for those decisions to be able to be implemented with fidelity, it’s understandable that arts orgs that feel passed over accuse systemic entities like Metro Finance and Legal, and large institutions of putting their thumb on the scale.

And with no formal place to promote their positions and preferences without receiving ad hominem critiques, it’s understandable (though highly unfortunate) that institutional actors return these types of barbs and double down in their positions, filling their ears with wax lest they go mad from the siren calls of “woke-ism”.

“Elitism,” “racism”, “classism”, “anti-Blackness”, “collusion” — these are the nicer versions of the terms thrown at folks on all sides of this issue, from the Directors of public agencies and large arts orgs, to the activists decrying iniquity and the public servants processing grant applications.

The way we engage with one another, our seemingly declining ability to assume the best of others, or at least to set aside the worst of them and see what we can do collectively — that’s not a problem any single leader, much less a Director of Finance, can solve.

But what they can do is operate like a public servant, not a politician. They must avoid tipping the scales of policy and process to accomplish the goal of a minority of Nashvillians. Rather, they should focus on a few critical infrastructural problems that have atrophied and set the stage for these chaotic scenes.

Unsolicited Advice for Any New Finance Director

Overhaul Metro Finance’s Accounting System

The current Metro Finance database is not a functional relational system. Department leaders, city council members, and even the Mayor can't generate a basic report—such as determining how much a department has invoiced for a program funded in a past resolution—without assistance from Finance employees. With Oracle potentially moving its global headquarters here and receiving up to $240M in tax breaks, upgrading the system shouldn’t be a big lift.

A lack of transparent accounting was a significant issue during the Metro Arts audit. It took almost a year for Metro to audit Metro Arts and clarify their spending, while I was able to draft an audit of 1,249 expenses within 48 hours. I am no financial genius—I simply know how to build a functional relational database (and so does Oracle).

Align Practices with Metro Charter’s Requirements for Metro Finance

A recurring issue raised by Finance during the Metro Arts audit was that they were unaware of how much Metro Arts had spent, to whom, and under what authority. This gave the impression that Metro Arts had unchecked access to funds. In reality, however, only Metro Finance can distribute funds to vendors, grantees, and anyone else involved in Metro government. These assertions reveal that Finance has not been fulfilling its obligations as outlined in the Metro Code and Charter.

According to the Metro Charter, the Director of Finance must:

Examine all contracts, purchase orders, and other documents involving financial obligations against the metropolitan government, ensuring there are unencumbered balances to cover such obligations.

Audit and approve all bills, invoices, payrolls, and claims before payment, confirming that they are legally authorized by appropriations or allotments.

While a $3.2B budget is complex, that complexity is no excuse for failing to perform legally binding duties. The lack of an efficient system for linking financial obligations, balances, charges, and authorized appropriations is precisely the problem. But this is exactly what relational databases—literally invented by Oracle—are designed to solve. Nashville is not the first large entity to need efficient, transparent accounting. When this inefficiency is weaponized against departments whose duties do not include such tasks, it feels like weaponized incompetence.

Stay In Your Lane

The Department of Finance has repeatedly been perceived as non-neutral in how Metro funds are spent. For example, during discussions with the ARP Committee about allocating COVID relief funds to a project for one HBCU (at the expense of another), or suddenly declaring a 10-year-old procurement process as “illegal,” it’s hard for many Nashvillians to see this as anything other than having a stake in where funding goes, rather than ensuring proper process.

There are plenty of politicians in Nashville whose job is to decide how our $3.2B annual budget is allocated. It’s not even terribly expensive to run against them if someone is interested in influencing those decisions. But the Director of Finance's job description, outlined in the Metro Code and Charter, is clear and sufficient for anyone who wants to help the city thrive.

Where We Go From Here

Anyone can see the fabric of our society is tattered. There is much work to be done in restoring faith in our institutions.

We all like complaining that people can’t talk to each other and work together anymore. That no one is collectivist enough; everyone is just out for themselves.

But what are we each doing that is making this worse? How are we all contributing to these problems? What are we willing to give up, be it ego, power, a sense of being the victim or the savior, to make things better?

That’s what I’d want to know from Jenneen Reed when shes in front of Council on October 1 and 15.

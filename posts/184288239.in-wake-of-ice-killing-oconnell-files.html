On January 9, 2026—as protests and vigils were held in Nashville and around the country for Renee Good, a US citizen killed by an ICE officer two days earlier in Minneapolis—Mayor Freddie O’Connell filed four resolutions to accept a total of $3.38 million in donations to MNPD for surveillance and policing equipment from the Nashville Downtown Partnership (NDP), the latest update to the so-called “Surveillance MOU” scandal.

The resolutions are an effort to acquire the direct permissions needed for Metro Nashville to “accept” the donations referenced in the proposed MOU the Mayor's Office submitted discretely the Friday before Thanksgiving. Indications are that they will not be pursuing an MOU anymore once the permission is obtained.

The MOU was purportedly being sought to “limit” NDP from using the grant for things the Mayor's Office was concerned about. However, the contents and working documents obtained via public records request revealed that the function was more clearly to require NDP to purchase specific surveillance and policing tech and donate it to MNPD with the grant funds.

The resolutions submitted on January 9 are as follows.

  • RS2026-13811: 15 Axis video cameras — $150,000

  • RS2026-13812: 1 Terradyne Gurkha armored rescue vehicle — $430,000

  • RS2026-13813: 2 ATC 48’ mobile command trailers + 2 Ford F-550s — $2,000,000

  • RS2026-13814: 1 International CV command/equipment truck — $800,000

On the same day they submitted the resolutions for the policing gear, O’Connell held a press conference about his new Community Safety Task Force wherein he declined to take the opportunity to denounce the killing of Renee Good. Instead, he attributed similar violence against innocent people at the hands of immigration agents to “federal authorities working without close local coordination.”

The problem isn’t ICE’s abject lack of accountability to meaningful use of force doctrines and constitutional due process norms, as O’Connell appears to view it, but that ICE isn’t working closely enough with local agencies.

The insight into his perspective on federal law enforcement comes as evidence is mounting that the equipment O’Connell is pursuing via the NDP donations is explicitly designed to help local law enforcement collaborate more effectively with ICE.

The Deep Irony: Then vs. Now

Mayor O’Connell won many supporters during his time on Metro Council speaking out in support of the unhoused, for the right to data privacy, and against the “surveillance state.”

Here is a post of his from 2021 decrying New Orleans’ adoption of a mass surveillance system:

“Strong communities are better for safety than a surveillance state,” he wrote. “We can build safer communities through smart, strategic investments that don’t involve spying on each other.”

When on Metro Council representing District 19, O’Connell “regularly publicly railed against surveillance in his social media posts” and even “advised his followers on Twitter to obtain memberships to the privacy watchdog Electronic Frontier Foundation.” He warned Nashville about the dangers of public-private surveillance partnerships like what has emerged in New Orleans through Project NOLA. That surveillance apparatus now includes more than 5,000 cameras across the city, with New Orleans purchasing cameras from vendors including Axis Communications.

One of the resolutions O’Connell has submitted to Council for approval on January 20: $150,000 for fifteen “Axis Video Cameras,” costing $10,000 per unit. Precisely the same equipment detailed in the article he cited.

The resolution describes these as “traditional video cameras, consistent with existing public safety cameras, for departmental use”—language that appears designed to sidestep the mandatory public hearing required for surveillance equipment.

The Legal End-Run

No such “traditional camera” exception exists in Metro Code Section 13.08.080. The ordinance defines “surveillance technology” to include “closed-circuit television cameras” and “video and audio monitoring and/or recording technology, such as surveillance cameras”—which would plainly cover the Axis cameras. The only exemption for cameras applies to “manually operated non-wearable handheld digital cameras... whose functionality is limited to manually capturing and manually downloading video and/or audio recordings”—not fixed surveillance cameras.

That public hearing requirement was authored by Councilman Dave Rosenberg in 2017 through ordinance BL2017-646. As the Nashville Scene reported, Rosenberg “wrote the city ordinance requiring council approval and a public hearing before Metro installs new surveillance technology.” Rosenberg is now O’Connell’s Director of Legislative Affairs—the same official who has been required to shred his credibility defending and deflecting the administrations push for this technology across multiple Council meeting.

The new resolutions do not invoke Metro Code 13.08.080. It is not clear if the Mayor’s Office will be requesting the required public hearing, or if they will somehow claim these purchases “don’t count” as “surveillance.” The ordinance Rosenberg wrote contains precise definitions, and by any reasonable reading, all four of the resolutions likely qualify.

Why the Mayor’s Office would not want the funding to align with direct feedback from the community members impacted by the funding is unclear.

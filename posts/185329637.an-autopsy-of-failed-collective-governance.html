A Framework for Understanding What Failed — and What an Actual Democracy Requires

What happened Tuesday night wasn’t just a political loss. It was a diagnostic test—and the results reveal something more fundamental than policy disagreement.

To understand what broke requires stepping back from the immediate fight over cameras and tactical gear to ask: what does functional governance actually look like? What capacities must a system have to navigate complexity, respond to legitimate concerns, and maintain democratic legitimacy under pressure?

There’s a body of research on cooperative governance and adaptive systems that identifies seven essential themes—seven capacities that resilient, legitimate governance systems demonstrate. These aren’t abstract ideals. They’re practical measures, patterns observed across successful organizations, communities, and movements that manage to coordinate action, process feedback, and adapt without collapsing into either chaos or authoritarianism.

Tuesday night, Metro Nashville was tested on all seven.

The Seven Themes of Cooperative Governance

Think of these as diagnostic criteria—the vital signs you’d check to know if a governance system is healthy:

  1. Governing without Coercion - Can authority maintain order through consent and alignment rather than force?

  2. Feedback & Truth-Telling - Can the system process uncomfortable truths and actually adjust course?

  3. Decision-Making Beyond Binaries - Can leaders find creative solutions beyond either/or frameworks?

  4. Contextual Leadership - Does leadership adapt to the actual situation rather than imposing predetermined approaches?

  5. Ritual & Myth - Do shared stories and practices create genuine cohesion, or just theater?

  6. Circulation of Power & Resources - Do influence and resources flow throughout the system, or concentrate in static centers?

  7. Structural & Planetary Care - Does the system protect both immediate needs and long-term wellbeing at multiple scales?

When governance systems succeed across these themes, they demonstrate what researchers call “antifragility”—they grow stronger under stress because they can adapt. When they fail, specific patterns reveal what’s broken and where.

Let’s see how Metro Nashville scored.

Theme 1: Governing without Coercion

The Standard: Legitimate authority emerges from what political theorist Mary Parker Follett called “power-with” rather than “power-over”—coordination through voluntary participation and aligned intentions, not imposed force. Systems that govern through coercion may achieve temporary compliance, but they become brittle. Those that govern through genuine consent become resilient.

The Test: Would Council hear the 22 speakers (against 2) representing diverse constituencies and respond to their concerns? Would the Mayor’s Office treat this input as valuable feedback or as an obstacle to manage?

The Result: Split. The surveillance cameras failed—but only those explicitly labeled “surveillance” and subject to mandatory public hearing. The tactical gear passed despite identical concerns raised by the same speakers, because it was categorized differently.

This reveals the mechanism: consent can be engineered around through categorization. Public input becomes a procedural checkbox rather than substantive engagement.

The Mayor’s Response: When confronted with 550 Nashvillians offering alternative uses for the same funds (all within approved state categories), the Legislative Affairs Director pleaded ignorance. When NOAH’s 50,000 congregants opposed the measures, the Mayor dismissed them as organizations “that have not invested in understanding the safety context.”

This is textbook “power-over”: “I have expertise you lack, therefore your concerns are invalid.”

What Failure Looks Like: The research on this theme is clear—when authority figures “insulate themselves from criticism,” they create what systems theorists call “closed-loop” systems. “Errors accumulate until a sudden crisis or collapse.” You can maintain order through force or dismissal, but you sacrifice the adaptive capacity that comes from genuinely incorporating diverse input.

The Mayor didn’t seek alignment with community concerns. He declared expertise and proceeded. Council didn’t facilitate emergence of solutions that could honor multiple perspectives—they categorized their way around the consent requirement.

The Verdict: Failed. What we witnessed was “dominating guided will”—a system that claims to guide the people while fundamentally dismissing their guidance.

Theme 2: Feedback & Truth-Telling

The Standard: Healthy systems treat truth-telling as essential infrastructure, not optional nicety. As one researcher put it: “A system’s ability to steer (govern) is wholly dependent on its feedback on current state. A government that insulates itself from criticism is essentially flying blind.”

This doesn’t just mean “listening” to feedback—it means creating safe channels for it, processing it seriously, closing the loop by responding to it, and visibly adjusting course when warranted.

The Test: Twenty-two speakers delivered substantiated concerns about surveillance technology, state collaboration, and community safety. Many explicitly cited their marginalized status—immigrants, people of color, LGBTQ+ community members—explaining why this equipment made them feel less safe, not more.

The framework is explicit: this is exactly the kind of feedback systems most need and most often reject. “Inconvenient truths” from those who will bear the consequences.

The Result: The feedback was heard but not metabolized. No one from the Mayor’s Office or Council majority engaged with the substance. No one explained how these specific concerns would be addressed. No one acknowledged the gap between intent (”public safety”) and reception (”we feel targeted”).

The Pattern: This isn’t isolated. The Community Oversight Board that might provide feedback on MNPD? Abolished, with Deputy Chief Gilder’s involvement. The community safety plan promised “day one”? Delivered 836 days late. Alternative uses for the funds? Never considered, per the Legislative Affairs Director’s own admission.

The system has eliminated or ignored every feedback mechanism that might force course correction.

The Mayor’s Translation: “Shut up. You don’t know what you’re talking about.”

What the Research Says: This is what’s called “exposed marginalized concealment”—the administration monitored the opposition (they knew we were coming), marginalized critical voices (dismissed NOAH’s legitimacy, questioned speakers’ expertise), yet concealed actual intentions (last-minute conversion to “grants,” undisclosed state information-sharing requirements).

Studies of organizational failure consistently show this pattern: “Systems that punish truth-telling (closed-loop) see errors accumulate until sudden crisis or collapse.”

The Specific Design Flaw: The surveillance ordinance requiring public hearings was written by Dave Rosenberg when he was on Council. It’s now administered by Dave Rosenberg in the Mayor’s Office, where he determines what counts as “surveillance.”

This is like letting the thermostat decide what counts as temperature.

The Verdict: Catastrophic failure. The feedback infrastructure exists on paper but is designed to be one-way. Truth flows up, but consequences don’t flow down.

Theme 3: Decision-Making Beyond Binaries

The Standard: Complex realities rarely fit either/or frames. Effective governance finds what’s called “third alternatives”—creative solutions that transcend initial options by reframing the problem itself.

The research here draws on everything from Hegel’s dialectics to indigenous consensus practices to modern conflict resolution: when you’re stuck between Option A and Option B, the answer is often Option C that nobody saw at first because the frame was too narrow.

The Test: Could Council find solutions that address both community safety concerns AND civil liberties? Both state compliance AND local autonomy? Both police capacity AND accountability?

The Alternative Offered: 550 Nashvillians submitted survey responses suggesting alternative uses for the same state funds—all within approved categories. These represented attempts to find solutions beyond “tactical gear or nothing.”

The Result: The Legislative Affairs Director hadn’t even considered the possibility. The question itself seemed novel to him. He promised to “get back” to the Council Member—a promise that, if fulfilled, would come after the vote, rendering it meaningless.

The Frame That Wasn’t Examined: The entire debate accepted the premise that “state offers equipment grants” requires “city accepts equipment grants.” But grants can be redirected within approved categories. That alternative wasn’t explored not because it’s impossible but because the frame wasn’t questioned.

The Timing Deception: The equipment proposals had been available since December 2024. The deliberative time existed to explore creative alternatives. The will to use it did not.

What Research Shows: Organizations that succeed in complexity “deliberately ask: is there a way to do both A and B, or something beyond them?” They use structured processes—citizens’ assemblies, deliberative polling, multi-criteria decision analysis—to escape binary traps.

Metro Nashville did the opposite: treated a months-old decision as requiring immediate either/or resolution, rejected alternatives without examination, and when asked if alternatives existed, responded with “I’ll get back to you.”

The Verdict: Refused. The system isn’t incapable of beyond-binary thinking—it’s unwilling to engage in it. That’s actually worse.

Theme 4: Contextual Leadership

The Standard: Leadership isn’t a fixed trait you impose on all situations—it’s a dynamic that emerges from reading the context correctly and adapting to it.

The research here spans from Sun Tzu (”know heaven, earth, and your own forces—adapt tactics to circumstance”) to modern adaptive leadership theory: effective leaders diagnose whether a challenge is technical or adaptive, stable or volatile, requiring expertise or requiring legitimacy, and adjust their approach accordingly.

The Test: What is the actual context we’re operating in?

The Mayor’s Reading: “Downtown safety” requiring expert technical intervention. Organizations questioning this “have not invested in understanding the safety context” and should defer to those who have.

The Community’s Reading: State-federal collaboration threatening immigrants and dissidents, in the immediate aftermath of an ICE-deputized officer murdering Renee Good, in the context of Tennessee’s immigration law retooling with Stephen Miller, in the context of Trump’s stated intentions toward blue cities, in the context of what’s happening in Minneapolis.

Who Read the Context Correctly?

The Mayor held his first community safety meeting 836 days after promising a “day one” plan. He called Renee Good’s killing a “complicated matter” while the Immigrant Caucus chair called it murder. He’s pushing surveillance he once opposed with no explanation for the shift.

These aren’t the actions of someone who’s reading the room. They’re the actions of someone who’s decided the room doesn’t matter.

The Framework’s Diagnosis: The Mayor is operating from “stable expert central leadership”—I’m the professional who understands what citizens don’t. But the actual context requires “adaptive stewardship”—recognizing that expertise alone isn’t legitimate authority when the community withdraws consent, and that the situation has fundamentally changed from when previous positions were formed.

The Research is Blunt: “A contextual leader has a permeable ego-boundary, able to let in information from the environment and let the needs of the context permeate their decision-making, rather than imposing a rigid agenda.”

The opposite happened. The agenda was rigid. The ego-boundary was impermeable. The context was misread or ignored.

The Verdict: Misdiagnosed context, rigidly applied predetermined approach. This is how systems optimized for normal times become brittle under pressure.

Theme 5: Ritual & Myth

The Standard: Every governance system operates within shared stories about what we’re doing and why. These aren’t optional decorations—they’re the meaning-making infrastructure that allows people to coordinate action and maintain commitment under stress.

The research here spans anthropology, organizational culture, and social movement theory: communities need myths (shared narratives about who we are) and rituals (repeated practices that enact those narratives) to function. The question isn’t whether you have them—it’s whether they’re genuine or hollow.

The Test: What story are we telling about who we are as a city? Does the ritual of “public hearing” actually mean what it claims to mean?

The Mayor’s Myth: Nashville as a pragmatic city that gets things done. Downtown as special, requiring expert management. Public safety as a technical problem solved by equipment and expertise. Progressive credentials maintained through rhetoric while making “tough necessary choices.”

The Opposition’s Myth: Nashville as a city that protects its most vulnerable. Democracy as collective decision-making where community voice shapes outcomes. Safety as emerging from community relationships and trust, not surveillance and enforcement.

The Ritual That Broke: The public hearing itself became contested ground. For those who spoke, it was democratic participation—the moment when community voice should matter. For the Mayor’s Office, it was apparently theater to be endured before proceeding with predetermined outcomes.

Twenty-two speakers. Five hundred fifty survey responses. NOAH’s 50,000 congregants. All participating in what they believed was a meaningful ritual of democratic input.

The outcome revealed what the ritual actually is: procedural compliance, not substantive engagement.

Council Member Suara’s Intervention: Reading Dr. King on the Council floor—”He who passively accepts evil is as much involved in it as he who helps to perpetrate it”—was an attempt to invoke a different myth, to call Council members into a different story about what this moment means.

It didn’t work. The myth of pragmatic governance—”we know better, we’ll handle this”—won.

What the Research Shows: When the gap between stated values and actual practice becomes too wide, “organizational rituals lose their meaning and become empty performance.” That’s what happened. The ritual of public input occurred. The meaning of public input did not.

The Warning: “Far from being opposites, logic and mythos are complementary aspects of a whole human system.” You can’t maintain legitimacy through purely technical-rational frames when the mythic layer—the story about what we’re doing and why—collapses.

The Verdict: Competing narratives, with the official myth increasingly hollow. The ritual performed, the meaning lost.

Theme 6: Circulation of Power & Resources

The Standard: Healthy systems keep power and resources moving. Concentration creates what the research calls “high entropy gradients that cause sudden failures”—too much accumulates in one place, creating instability that eventually corrects itself, often violently.

The principle here spans ecology, economics, and political science: “The Matthew effect—to those who have, more is given—is a pattern systems must actively counter or face collapse.”

The Test: Do resources and influence flow to where they’re needed, or do they accumulate in established power centers?

The Result: The Nashville Downtown Partnership—operating without required Council budget approval for 21 years, collecting tens of millions in special assessments—now gets to determine how millions in state surveillance funds are distributed.

Not NOAH’s 50,000 congregants. Not the 550 survey respondents. Not the 22 public speakers. Not the communities who will be surveilled.

The people who’ve avoided democratic oversight for two decades get to be the conduit for state surveillance money. That’s not circulation. That’s capture.

The Pattern: NDP already controls vast resources through special assessments. They’re now the vehicle for additional state funds. Meanwhile, the Mayor’s Office of Homeless Services has $3.2 million in questioned expenditures while remaining chronically underfunded.

Resources flow upward to established power centers. They don’t circulate downward to address identified needs.

The Moment That Crystallized It: Ben Simpson, NDP’s COO, sitting in the gallery as tactical gear was approved, visibly pleased. The framework has a term for this: “power-as-possession” rather than “power-as-flow.”

He possesses power. It doesn’t flow through him to serve community needs—it accumulates in the institution he represents.

My Parting Shot: “See you at budget season”—because the newly discovered Council authority over NDP’s budget represents a potential circulation mechanism. Whether Council will actually use it to redirect resources remains to be seen.

The Research Warning: “Systems that only take and don’t give back eventually exhaust the source.” NDP takes special assessments from downtown businesses and takes surveillance funds from the state. What does it give back to the community whose concerns it dismisses?

The Design Principle We’re Violating: “Each major flow should form a loop (even if long and complex). A person or entity that only takes resources and never contributes back is a sink that can drain the system.”

The Verdict: Captured. Resources and power concentrate in established centers while circulating away from community input or control.

Theme 7: Structural & Planetary Care

The Standard: Good governance considers impacts beyond the immediate. As the research puts it: “Structural care at one level must align with and feed into care at higher levels, and vice versa. Decisions should consider impacts on the next larger whole.”

This isn’t abstract—it’s about whether local decision-making accounts for state-level, federal-level, and long-term dynamics, or optimizes for short-term political management while ignoring larger forces.

The Test: Does Nashville’s decision account for the context we’re actually operating in?

The Context Available to Anyone Paying Attention:

  • Tennessee’s immigration law retooling in partnership with Stephen Miller, documented booster of white supremacist culture

  • Federal threats to “take over” local police departments that don’t comply

  • The killing of Renee Good by an ICE-deputized officer

  • Trump’s stated intentions toward blue cities

  • Minneapolis under what by any reasonable definition constitutes federal insurgency

  • A diverse crowd including immigrants explaining why tactical equipment makes them feel targeted

The Decision That Was Made: Give MNPD tactical equipment and an armored vehicle, to be used in coordination with state requirements, including “delivery of certain public information to the State of Tennessee”—the specifics of which remain vague despite direct questioning.

The Promise That Wasn’t Credible: Deputy Chief Gilder assured me only “de-identified information” would go to the state. But the Tennessee Information Protection Act already allows 3rd party data processors to share with law enforcement based on suspected violations of “rules”—not laws, rules.

And the Community Oversight Board that might provide accountability? Abolished, with Gilder’s involvement.

The Framework’s Language: “If a structure cares only for itself and not the larger context, it will not be resilient when that context shifts beyond thresholds.”

We’re not talking about abstract future thresholds. The threshold shifted when Trump was inaugurated. It shifted when Renee Good was killed. It shifted when Tennessee partnered with Stephen Miller.

Metro Nashville is making decisions as if we’re still in 2019. We’re not.

The Research Warns: “Each subsystem should have an internal intelligence function keeping an eye on the outside environment and future.” We have a Mayor who held his first community safety meeting after 836 days. We have a Council that defers to narrow technical definitions. We have a community screaming warnings that are dismissed as uninformed.

There is no intelligence function scanning the environment and adjusting. There’s only the predetermined plan, executed regardless of changing conditions.

What Care Would Look Like: The seventh-generation principle from indigenous governance—every decision considers seven generations forward. We’re not even considering seven weeks forward. The equipment approved Tuesday night will be in service for years, used in whatever context emerges, accountable to whatever forces demand compliance.

The Verdict: Externalized harm. Local decisions made without regard for state-federal dynamics, long-term consequences, or the credible threats identified by those who will bear the costs.

The Overall Diagnosis

Across all seven themes, a pattern emerges that’s more troubling than any single vote: Metro Nashville’s governance system is optimized for normal times and brittle under pressure.

This isn’t a question of left versus right, progressive versus conservative. It’s a question of capacity. Does the system have what it needs to:

  • Maintain legitimacy when consent is challenged? (Theme 1: No)

  • Process uncomfortable feedback and adjust? (Theme 2: No)

  • Find creative solutions beyond predetermined options? (Theme 3: No)

  • Read changing contexts and adapt? (Theme 4: No)

  • Maintain meaningful shared purpose? (Theme 5: No)

  • Prevent concentration of power and resources? (Theme 6: No)

  • Account for larger forces and longer timeframes? (Theme 7: No)

Seven diagnostic criteria. Seven failures.

Tuesday night wasn’t one bad vote. It was a systems test that revealed governance incapacity across every essential dimension.

The framework teaches: “Systems with open truth channels exhibit homeostasis—they detect problems early and adjust. Conversely, in systems that punish truth-telling, errors accumulate until sudden crisis or collapse.”

Mayor O’Connell and Metro Council just demonstrated they’re flying blind into a storm they refuse to acknowledge exists.

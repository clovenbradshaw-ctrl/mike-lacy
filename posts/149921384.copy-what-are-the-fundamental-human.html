Abstract

This essay explores fundamental human needs driving behavior across cultures, acknowledging tensions between individualistic and collectivist perspectives without asserting one as more correct. Recognizing that we lack an objective understanding of the underlying needs and desires of the human organism, and that frameworks like the Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) focus on collective external needs without grounding in a comprehensive theory, we aim to understand what we genuinely desire without cultural bias. Inspired by personality trait mapping methods like the lexical hypothesis used in the Big Five and HEXACO models—particularly the discovery of the Honesty-Humility trait—we consider using artificial intelligence to analyze language across cultures to identify universal human needs. However, as Ludwig Wittgenstein argued, words have inherent limitations in capturing the full essence of meaning and set the boundaries of our understanding. Therefore, perhaps the truest way to represent these needs is as statistical centers in high-dimensional semantic vector space. By understanding these shared drives and applying frameworks like Ken Wilber's quadrants, we aim to develop a foundation for more culturally universal models of human thriving.

If you want to get to the meat of my idea, you can jump down to the methodology proposal, or “the recipe”.

Preface

The Limits of Popular Needs Models — like the Social Determinants of Health

I’m working on a project that is mapping complex systems of human interactions, and what emerges from that is a the need to categorize, in a taxonomically complete way, all human needs.

The idea is that if we are tracking the activities of public and private institutions, in order to understand how effectively they’re working together and if they’re achieving their collective goals, its necessary to understand what needs they’re fulfilling.

This is, at first glance, not a wheel that needs to be reinvented. If you are trying to map the interactions between, e.g. The Health Department and Community Food Access Non-Profits, there are definitely some existing frameworks that can help map this out, in variously useful ways.

Here is, for example, a perfectly useful “systems” map that ChatGPT can spin up outlaying the interactions between the Health Department, Food Access Non-Profits, and the Community members — mediated by the human needs they are fulfilling: economic stability, food security, and healthcare access.

This example is particularly robust because a lot of these things are framed by the terms “Social Determinants of Health” (SDOH). The hexagons above are factors that research has shown have an impact on overall health, things that are often overlooked.

One conclusion that a Health Department Official might have looking at this map is “if we are tasked with improving the overall health of our population, and increasing food security is a requirement for that, perhaps we should partner with or help coordinate food distribution with these community groups?”

And indeed, addressing food security for overall health benefits is something that the National Institute of Health and local health departments do focus on increasingly.

The SDOH is a great example of applying a system-level lens to addressing human needs, understanding that concepts like “health” are not improved by one or a few interactions, but have a multitude of things underlying them on both the personal and collective level, across both our interior and physical embodiments.

In the past, especially in the West, health was often discussed as just the intersection between your personal choices, your genetic predispositions, and things that just “happen” to you. Now health is increasingly seen as emerging from a complex interplay of factors across different aspects of our lives.

When we divide the SDOH into these four sections (known as Wilber’s Quadrants) we can see that there’s much more than just that narrow lens, which would only show up in the individual focused Upper Right and Upper Left quadrants.

The SDOH, however, has factors across all of four spaces: our personal inner lives, our collective inner lives, are exterior personal selves and bodies, and our collective exterior systems. The SDOH is particularly interested in the Lower-Right, our collective exterior, our collective systems, processes, and access to the things that make positive health outcomes more likely.

This is huge because health outcomes have historically been obscured in class-based and race-based arguments that implicitly or explicitly linked negative health outcomes exclusively to personal choice or “bad genes.” We now have a lens that supports what many of us intuitively know: it’s more complicated than that.

But the SDOH isn’t without its limits, and this gets at the kinds of things my project is looking to overcome. Let’s look at some of the literature criticizing the SDOH. Little of it says that it’s just straight up wrong or unhelpful, most of the challenges arise with its limits, and it's inability to really do much about its diagnoses.

  • Revisiting the social determinants of health agenda from the global South

  • A critique of the English national policy from a social determinants of health perspective using a realist and problem representation approach: the ‘Childhood Obesity: a plan for action’ (2016, 2018, 2019)

  • Re-politicizing the WHO’s social determinants of health framework

  • Health in global context; beyond the social determinants of health?

  • The Social Determinants of Health: Time to Re-Think?

The critiques follow similar lines of analysis. The SDOH, as they’re often used and discussed, are kind of just a list of human needs that emerged from a long lineage of mostly Western analyses about what causes good and bad health. The needs focused on are robust, but don't have any real internal consistency or taxonomical completion — they're kind of just, common sense.

People who have less access to healthcare, transportation, public safety, etc. tend to have worse health outcomes. The real question is (to get ahead of myself) why are we ever convinced or deluded as a society that it would ever be otherwise?

The last article makes four specific observations about the limitations of the SDOH.

  • Emerging global issues, such as obesity patterns and climate change, challenge the traditional SDoH framework.

  • Difficulties in translating SDoH theory into effective policy hinder implementation.

  • Theoretical and methodological shortcomings limit SDoH research's impact on practical health improvements.

  • Broader issues in modern public health research, such as insufficient focus on political contexts, need to be addressed.

  • The SDoH framework remains valuable but requires evolution to address new complexities and improve health equity.

I’m not really trying to pick on the SDOH. As the last bullet says, it's very valuable, but frameworks like it just aren't able to comprehend the complexities of human systems in any way that really moves the needle.

The SDOH and other need frameworks are not grounded in any more fundamental theories of how individual and collective human needs and social forces intersect, it's diagnoses and prescriptions really land more like observation and commentary. It's analyses are more or less a systematized, medical focused form of sociology — the branch of the academy that's always having to defend itself as more than pseudoscience, or essentially just structured non-fiction observations.

Medical researchers are increasingly recognizing how external cultural forces—like systemic racism—shape poor health outcomes. While this acknowledgment is both brave and long overdue, it doesn’t yet bring us to systemic solutions that can create real, lasting change. We’re diagnosing the problems, but often stopping short of offering a prescription that addresses the root causes.

Some of the more daring researchers take the risk of naming these root causes, even in the face of professional and social risk — identifying white supremacy, colonialist patterns, and classism among the social diseases causes these symptoms.

It can feel like the conversation ends with naming the issue, as if identifying the problem is enough. It’s similar to the days when people believed illness came from bad air—there’s truth in it, but we wouldn’t have created vaccines if we stopped there. And right now, when it comes to social issues, we’re stopping at bad air.

To make real progress, we need to dig deeper and understand the fundamental structure of human systems, and those systems emerge in a large way from the deep needs that we have and the interplay of those instincts with the emergent needs of complex human systems (not necessarily one and the same).

In medicine, you need to understand the body at the cellular level, the organ level, and how those organs interact to maintain overall health. Human systems are no different—there are fundamental layers, but we need to identify what those “cells” are when it comes to society.

Lessons from the Lexical Hypothesis

We can learn a lot from the how we’ve come to understand human traits and behaviors in the field of Personality Psychology. The lexical hypothesis has played a significant role in the study of personality, suggesting that the most important individual differences are encoded in language. This approach has driven the study of personality for over a century, evolving into models like the Big Five personality traits and the HEXACO model, the only validated forms of segmenting essential human personality characteristics.

Researchers began with a vast list of traits derived from everyday language, then gradually condensed this list by examining the terms that people consistently used to describe themselves and others.

The idea was that all descriptors of human beings personality would fit into the resultant categories, or be emergent properties between multiples of them.

This process of condensing terms was no small feat—it required a painstaking effort to sift through thousands of descriptive words, looking for common themes and paring these down to yet more fundamental themes. Researchers essentially relied on people to function as semantic analysis machines, providing the linguistic input and feedback necessary to refine and categorize personality traits. The result was more than just a well informed hunch, but aimed to be a taxonomically complete list of all human traits. And out came The Big Five.

The Big Five—Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism—became the dominant model for understanding personality. It provided a framework that is both empirically robust and widely applicable across different cultures. It’s been effective because it seems to reflect underlying physiological and genetic factors that shape human behavior. Research has linked these traits to certain brain structures, neurotransmitter levels, and even genetic markers, making them more than just theoretical constructs.

However, the robustness of these models has been tested across different languages and cultural contexts, revealing some interesting dynamics. The Big Five and later the six trait HEXACO model have been studied in non-Western societies, sometimes requiring adjustments to better fit the local context. For example, the HEXACO model's addition of Honesty-Humility—capturing qualities like sincerity, fairness, modesty, and a lack of greed—emerged partly because the Big Five’s focus didn’t fully capture aspects of personality that are emphasized in some cultures outside the West.

The inclusion of Honesty-Humility in HEXACO was a breakthrough, revealing a trait that had been “hidden” through the original Big Five lens. It’s a trait that speaks to core human concerns about fairness and integrity, but it took a shift in perspective and methodology to recognize its importance.

The discovery of this trait was significant because it highlighted how Bayesian priors—our preconceived assumptions—can obscure certain aspects of reality. The researchers behind the Big Five weren’t necessarily ignoring honesty; rather, their focus, cultural conditioning, and the methodologies they employed didn’t reveal it as a distinct, fundamental trait.

Applying This to Understanding Human Needs

The lexical hypothesis and the evolution of personality models provide a roadmap for how we might approach understanding human needs. Just as language revealed core dimensions of personality, it might also help us uncover fundamental human needs. But it’s important to remember that we’re not aiming for a one-size-fits-all solution or trying to create a homogenized view of human nature.

We know that all needs are culturally, even individually specific in how they’re expressed, yet our goal is to approximate the underlying genetic drives that manifest differently across societies. This is not about erasing cultural differences, but understanding the universal urges beneath them. If we can identify these deeper drives, we might be able to create a kind of preventative cultural model—a way of providing cultural nourishment that sustains well-being that is less hemmed in by our ideological apertures.

Ideological Apertures and Systemic Solutions

It’s not just about understanding needs—how we perceive and prioritize these needs is crucial. This is where my concept of ideological apertures comes in. Ideological apertures are the lenses or frameworks through which we see the world, and they often shape how we pursue certain needs while making others harder to recognize or meet. For example, a society that values hyper-individualism may prioritize self-actualization but struggle with meeting collective needs like community or belonging.

These apertures aren’t just cultural; they’re influenced by personal levels of mental, emotional, and relational development. How we understand and prioritize needs is shaped by where we are in our own growth.

This also makes it exceptionally difficult to come to alignment on what our fundamental needs are and what their boundary lines appear to be. Just as the Big 5 had “Honesty-Humility” lying latent in its categories, and bring out of this trait reshaped the limits and texture of all the other traits, how we divide up the more abstract human needs can shade our conception of human nature and society greatly. And throughout that, our impulses and assumptions of what “makes sense” are the Bayesian Priors we most need to be weary of — it's the things that seem obvious and a priori that may be coming from the very ideological apertures we are trying to widen.

The Power of Language in Mapping Human Needs

Language is the tool we use to articulate needs. And it’s where much of the complexity lies. Wittgenstein famously argued that the limits of our language are the limits of our world. The words we have for human needs—or don’t have—shape how we understand and meet them. That’s why it’s so important to study how different cultures talk about needs—because in that linguistic diversity, we can begin to see the deeper human drives at play.

Using tools like AI and cross-linguistic analysis, we can gather and analyze terms for needs across languages and cultures. By doing so, we can map out the semantic spaces where these needs exist and find centroids—the central points where all these culturally specific terms converge. These centroids won’t give us the full picture, but they’ll help us approximate the genetic, underlying drives that shape human behavior across societies.

From Diagnosis to Cultural Nourishment

Ultimately, our goal isn’t just to understand these needs for academic curiosity. The reason we need to understand fundamental human needs is so that we can create a model of cultural nourishment—a way of supporting societies and individuals by addressing the deeper, universal drives that shape human behavior, and acknowledging the ideological apertures that are nearly always at work limiting what needs are met and/or who gets to meet them.

But it’s important to acknowledge that we’ll never fully get to the core of these needs in any permanent or unchanging way. Just as DNA is constantly evolving, our understanding of human needs will continue to evolve as our cultures and language change. We can get closer to the genetic and physiological roots of these needs—much like evolutionary psychology helps us understand the basic drivers of behavior—but we have to recognize that language itself is constantly evolving to meet our needs in the moment.

Any AI system we use is just a snapshot of a limited view at a particular time and place. Like any framework—whether psychological, spiritual, emotional, or social—what we’re building is simply a map. It’s a way to help us come into contact with these deeper needs, but it’s not the territory itself. Real understanding comes from engagement, from living through the messiness of life and navigating our constantly shifting needs.

In the deeply complex world we inhabit, where individual needs intersect with collective goals, having a shared, cross-cultural understanding of human drives is critical. The centroids we identify through language analysis won’t give us a complete, static picture of what we fundamentally need. But they can help us get closer to a shared understanding of those underlying urges, making it easier to build systems that align human needs across diverse cultures.

We’re not looking to create a world where everyone meets their needs in the same way. Instead, we’re trying to develop cultural nourishment—structures that respect the diversity of human experience while being rooted in the universal patterns of human nature. This approach allows us to move through our ideological apertures and recognize that while our expressions of needs are culturally specific, the deeper drives behind them are shared. This understanding is the key to creating systems where everyone is more likely to have their needs met—a world that allows each of us to thrive, within our own contexts, while remaining connected to the broader human story.

And here’s how we might try to do this…

Proposed Methodology

My proposed methodology builds on an initial version of this approach, which has already shown promising results in identifying commonalities among human needs across cultures. The process involves several key steps:

  1. Data Collection: Begin by compiling a comprehensive list of terms and phrases that various cultures use to describe human needs. This involves gathering words and phrases that address “human needs” across a wide selection of languages.

    1. The easiest, though not without additional room for bias, route to this is by using an AI model. Sources of error include: the limited source texts of non-western languages; potential semantic biases in the structuring of non-western languages in vector space; terms with multiple meanings in the same or other languages that would throw off the vector analysis.

    2. My current dataset is split fairly evenly from languages labelled as “Global South” and “Global North” languages, as well as “dead” languages including Ancient Greek and Latin.

  2. Optional: Quadrant Segmentation: Building on this initial analysis, I’d recommend segmenting of the data using Ken Wilber’s quadrant framework.

    1. This means categorizing terms into four key areas:

      • Interior-Individual (needs related to personal inner experiences and self-perception)

      • Interior-Collective (needs connected to shared cultural beliefs and values)

      • Exterior-Individual (needs related to personal physical conditions and actions)

      • Exterior-Collective (needs tied to societal systems and structures)

    2. This segmentation helps isolate the ways different needs are understood and expressed at these different levels, making it easier to discern underlying universal patterns amidst cultural variations.

    3. While there’s an exploration for whether Wilber’s quadrants are philosophically valid and exist across cultures, this segmentation has advantage of splitting terms apart that perform grammatically different functions. Words and phrases might have different semantic meaning when describing a need of an individual vs a collective.

    4. However, this segmentation might lose the very nuance we’re trying to uncover. Consider the Zulu term “Ubuntu”, often translated to “I am because we are.” This is precisely the kind of term we want to explore semantically because of how it is at odds with many individualist terms from Western culture, yet if you were to place it on the Wilberian quadrants it might sit directly in the middle.

  3. Initial Analysis: Using AI models, perform an initial semantic mapping via “embedding” of these terms, which involves plotting them in a high-dimensional space to identify how different cultures articulate needs. What this means practically is that an AI system is fed a file that’s likely less than 1 MB, with a term on every row. The AI then processes this and adds to each row a whole bunch of numbers representing where it exists in “high dimensional space” compared to other terms, giving basically a crazy version of a latitude and longitude of this set of character.

  4. This can be accomplished via fairly affordable AI models with APIs from OpenAI, Anthropic, and more.

    1. Optionally reduce “noise” by focusing only on prominent features or reducing the “dimensionality” of the data for easier clustering. Setting some high-end, low-end limits could help remove outliers in the dataset.

  5. Cluster Analysis and Identification of Centroids: Use AI to perform cluster analysis on these segmented data sets, identifying where the terms overlap in meaning and converge into central clusters, or centroids. These centroids represent the core expressions of certain needs, pointing toward the underlying genetic and physiological drives that are common across cultures, yet expressed differently due to linguistic and cultural diversity.

  6. Validation and Iteration: Compare these centroids with established frameworks of human needs and personality, like Maslow’s hierarchy, Spiral Dynamics, and even HEXACO traits, to ensure alignment with existing psychological and sociological theories. This step includes refining the AI model based on feedback from subject-matter experts and cross-referencing with anthropological insights to improve the cultural accuracy of the results.

The goal is to use these findings to develop a flexible, adaptive model of cultural nourishment that respects cultural diversity while understanding the shared human urges that drive behavior across societies.

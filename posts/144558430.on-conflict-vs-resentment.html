While walking to the graduation from my parent’s house, a stroll through wide sidewalks dividing commuter roads from replanted deciduous forests, she talked about this cognitive limitation that her American colleagues seem to hold. This “us vs them” of it all, something that often didn’t resonate with the actual citizens of the country.

I imagine it goes something like this:

US Officials: And we’ll hold elections next year, they’ll be open to all citizens, and we’ll distribute candidate applications in the allied held regions in the North and East.

Country Officials: Well, yes but how will the insurrectionists in the South and West put up their candidates?

US Officials: Well why would you want that?

Country Officials: Because we said it was for all citizens.

US Officials: We’ll we didn’t mean THEM!

Country Officials: Good idea…

It seems like there’s a level of black and white thinking that we impose and bring to these situations that isn’t always already present in those societies. I’m taking this a step further than she did, but it makes me wonder if our most strategic international export isn’t weapons, but rather zero sum, WIN/LOSE, binary thinking.

Two Perspectives on The Rule Of Law as a concept

Francis Fukuyama: Fukuyama views the rule of law as a cornerstone of modern liberal democracies and a crucial element of what he describes as "getting to Denmark," a metaphor for stable, prosperous, democratic states with strong institutions. In his works, particularly in "The Origins of Political Order" and "Political Order and Political Decay," Fukuyama argues that a strong state, rule of law, and accountable government are fundamental to achieving political stability and development. He sees the rule of law as not merely the existence of laws but their impartial enforcement and the assurance that no one, not even leaders, is above the law. This framework supports predictable and enforceable contracts, which are essential for economic activity and investment, leading to broader societal prosperity.

Michel Foucault: Contrasting sharply with Fukuyama, Foucault's exploration of the rule of law isn’t centered around the virtues of legal frameworks but rather on how these frameworks serve as instruments of power and control. In works like "Discipline and Punish," Foucault examines how laws and institutions are tools used by those in power to maintain control and normalize social order through surveillance and discipline. Foucault would likely interpret the rule of law as a method for imposing specific norms and behaviors on society, contributing to what he calls "governmentality," or the art of governing beyond just state politics to include control over the self and others within societal norms.

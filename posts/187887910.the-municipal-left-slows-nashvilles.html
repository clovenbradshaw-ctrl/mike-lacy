Firstly, a highlight from Nashville where increased awareness of the brutal efficiency of our eviction pipeline has led to more intentional systems design in the wake of last month’s weather state of emergency. With nearly half a million people without power and losing paychecks, the logical outcome is large numbers of people being served with eviction paperwork when their February and March rent comes due.

With no official dashboard tracking these numbers, its left to pet projects like the one I posted about (and more professionalized ones from The Eviction Lab and Legal Services Corporation) to see which way the wind is blowing.

That is why it is all the more impressive and important that Nashville today announced a “Winter Storm Housing Support Docket.” As Council Member Porterfield posted today, “the purpose of this docket is to help tenants whose inability to pay rent was affected by the winter storm to complete applications for community-based rental assistance in order to keep as many people in their homes as possible.”

More will be publicized in a various forums about what those community-based resources are (and if they actually are distributed efficiently and equitably), but legal experts embedded in the heart of our eviction system in Nashville tell me that this special docket “is a really big deal” and an immensely important breakthrough we need to appreciate. It is an important step in the marathon Nashville needs to take to accept that housing is a human right and that the the only way to end homelessness is de-commodified housing.

The announcement of a special eviction docket was not a predetermined outcome from the declared state of emergency last month. It was a something that had to be contemplated, researched, advocated for and rallied behind. I was not very involved in the process. But I was a fly on the wall for much of it to see how change like this happens.

What I saw was an informal network of advocates and allies across priorities and focus areas uniting with a laser focus. The core of this movement is an as yet unnamed political coalition, though I have heard a term for it floated that aligns with what we see in NYC, Portland and elsewhere: the Municipal Left.

The Municipal Left is distinct from both the establishment Democratic center, which manages existing elite power structures with progressive branding, and the purity left, which refuses to engage with those structures at all — it plays the game in order to change its rules.

The work product of the Municipal Left is not primarily digital activism nor “we’re not playing the game”. The thesis of the Municipal Left is, if you ask me, game change. You cannot change the rules if you do not play the game.

When people participate in the unending work of improving government, they’re often criticized by those on the purity left for allegedly endorsing its injustices by engaging with the system. This critique is a form of purity politics that, aside from actively enabling far worse outcomes for the sake of ideological purity, is an extremely boring and uncool way to be a Leftist.

This is the work of the Municipal Left — accepting that yes, that the world is in a perpetual state of collapse and the universe, as a law, moves towards entropy. But through intentional, grounded work, through what Aristotle would have called democracy’s Entelecheia, the consistent activity of a collectivist polity being-at-work-staying-itself, the arc of moral universe bends towards justice.

All of the judges who voluntarily voted for this deserve the credit for it, as do the Council Members (whose district’s eviction rates are now being publicly tracked) who understood that it was in the best interest of their constituents to not be facing mass eviction.

But I want to highlight a particular community member who was, to my eyes, the primary champion of this and deserves the lion’s share of the credit for making this happen: India Pungarcher.

Ms. Pungarcher, Associate Director of Advocacy at Open Table Nashville, and a member of Metro’s Homelessness Planning Council, was the selfless center hub of this tremendous effort that is going to help keep literally thousands of Nashvillians stay housed.

Systemic change is a team sport, but sometimes there are team captains. And in this race against the clock of eviction filings that would mean hundreds or more Nashvillians likely sliding into homelessness, India Pungarcher was a model for what change looks like for the Municipal Left.

Surveillance Push Back Has Super Bowl Ratings

In case anyone thought a repudiation of mass surveillance was something only people like me care about, a little experiment was run at the Super Bowl this past weekend. The Amazon-owned Ring Doorbell firm ran an ad advertising that it had a new feature that would allow you to search for a lost dog across the cameras of everyone in your neighborhood using AI.

I had a lot of people forward me these articles, including a Metro Council Member who jokingly tagged it with “FUSUS for dogs?” I’m glad that average Americans are taking privacy seriously even if some of our elected officials who are in consistent communication with lobbyists for mass surveillance firms are not taking it seriously.

Its unclear what Amazon thought the reaction was going to be to try to acclimate its users to ubiquitous mass surveillance, the type of privacy nihilism that has led Nashville Mayor Freddie O’Connell to tell his constituents to “throw their phone in the river” if they’re worried about privacy and the CTO and co-founder of Oracle to say “the privacy you’re concerned about is largely an illusion. All you have to give up is your illusions, not any of your privacy.”

But it seems like the massive clap back was not what they expected.

The response has had real impacts, including Amazon ending its partnership with the highly controversial Flock corporation that has been installing unremovable tracking devices in American cities, refusing to turn them off and remove them even once contracts are ended.

And the Flock cameras, surveilling residents across the country, are anything but secure as this popular YouTuber has documented.

And speaking of doorbell cameras — the disappearance of Nancy Guthrie, mother of NBC’s Savannah Guthrie, from her Tucson home on February 1st has inadvertently become a live stress test of every claim these companies make about your data.

When investigators first examined Guthrie’s doorbell camera, Pima County Sheriff Chris Nanos said the camera had detected motion at 1:47 a.m. but that no video was recoverable because Guthrie didn’t have a paid subscription. The camera — initially misidentified as a Ring but actually a Google Nest — had recorded something. But without a subscription, that recording was supposed to be gone within three to six hours.

Except it wasn’t gone.

On Tuesday, FBI Director Kash Patel released images and video showing a masked, armed individual at Guthrie’s front door, extracted from what the FBI described as “residual data located in backend systems.”

Hm.

Google engineers reportedly worked with the Bureau over several days to recover it. A company told its users — and initially told law enforcement — that video from non-subscribers isn’t stored. Then, when the stakes were high enough, the data materialized from backend systems never part of the consumer-facing product description.

Cybersecurity experts are now saying the quiet part out loud. One analyst told CBS News that Google may flag certain events — like tamper detection — for longer retention, a capability potentially never disclosed to users. Another told NPR that even without a subscription, data likely still gets pushed to the cloud — “deleted” just means moved somewhere else until it’s overwritten, or until the FBI comes calling.

Ring founder Jamie Siminoff went on Fox Business to clarify that Ring doesn’t retain non-subscriber footage, while carefully noting that “everybody builds their systems differently” — which only underscores that consumers have no way of knowing what’s actually happening to their video.

In this case, the undisclosed retention may help bring someone home, and that’s good — but we know this will be used to justify side stepping fourth amendment protections at any available opportunity.

The broader implication is the one that should concern everyone with one of these devices on their front door: the cameras are always recording, the data is always going somewhere, and the companies’ statements about what happens to it are, at best, incomplete. And in TN at least thanks to the Tennessee Information Protection Act, there does not need to be any warrant or subpoena for the data processor to collaborate with law enforcement based on what they see in that data.

"Citizens will be on their best behavior, because we're constantly recording and reporting everything that is going on."

The Metro Arts Commission casually announced on Tuesday that it would not be doing Thrive funding this fiscal year — waiting until at least September 2025 to revisit the funding pool that has given arts diverse arts organizations and individuals arts roughly $1.6M in its decade of existence.

It came the same week as the reconciliation agreement between Metro and the with defunded artists filed under Title VI of the Civil Rights Act is being signed.

Those artists in the Title Vi complaint had their funding cut in half last year when an initial allocation was ruled by Metro Legal to be “unconstitutional” because, they asserted, the funding vote was race-conscious. This was, Metro Legal ruled, unconstitutional under a SCOTUS ruling regarding Affirmative Action in Higher Education. In an unusual move, Metro Legal held a closed-door session with the Arts Commissioners and insisted they revote.

The resulting vote stripped the diverse arts pool, Thrive, of half its funding, giving the funding to large arts organizations — a reversal that outside counsel hired by the Metro Human Relations Commission determined was itself a potential civil rights violation.

The Title VI complainants are getting as much of a victory as one could expect in a situation like this, with full funding for all artists promised funding last year, including winning funding for a contested pool of “muralist” grants that was nearly not included.

But it’s unclear how many steps forward and backward have been taken with progressive arts funding in the city, a year into this mess.

One metric does seem clear — despite the work of all the activists, artists, public comments, bloggers (hi!), and elected officials, it seems that the Metro Arts’ funding for intentionally diverse, independent artists in FY2025 will be $0.

The intent of the Thrive awards has always been explicitly more inclusive than the recipients of Metro Arts general operations grants, a funding schema which, as I’ve written about, was developed to help the Nashville Symphony stay solvent in 1988.

The bulk of the Metro Arts funding has always gone to large art institutions, which arts groups and activists for decades pointed out were comprised of leadership, staff, and art programming that did not demographically represent all of Nashville.

The embattled art allocations were not always controversial. They only became so after a vote last July when, in the wake of a budget shortfall, the now former Director Singh oversaw an increase to the Thrive pool and a corresponding decrease to the large institution's operational grants.

Thrive was established in 2014 under Metro Arts Executive Director Jen Cole, whose husband Erik Cole was serving in Mayor Carl Dean’s office as Metro’s Director of Financial Empowerment at the time. The “power couple” left Nashville in 2018, but not before leaving a clear paper trail of what the point of this funding was, and demonstrating the institutional support it had from Metro agencies including the Mayor’s Office, Legal and Finance, all of whom have questioned whether this funding is even “legal”.

A WPLN interview with Cole in 2015 coincided with the publication of a report from the Metro Arts Commission entitled “Holding the mirror up: Institutional reflections on driving a more equitable city through the arts” which painted a stark vision of the city’s art scene.

“We’re never going to dismantle racism unless we first start with awareness and then behavior, and then behavior gets to institutional behavior, and we really kind of believe someone has to opt into that pathway,” Cole told WPLN’s Tony Gonzalez.

The report featured some pretty harrowing quotes that don’t seem like they could come from a 21st-century metropolis, passages like:

One leader suggested that artists of color simply might not have the skills to uphold the level of excellence that is demanded by the organization or industry. Further, this entity equated being African- American with not having the resources and social norms (ability to adhere to the rules of field) to pursue a professional career in the field. The equation of equity or multiculturalism as diluting excellence must be interrogated by the arts sector…

Speaking about the Nashville Ballet, one leader noted that some Southern audiences are made more uncomfortable when the dancers are of different racial or ethnic backgrounds.

The report could mostly be summarized by the sentence “While steps are being taken to address overt discrimination and exclusion, implicit bias and structural inequity remain significant challenges to realizing a vision of equity.”

Thrive was created to do something about this situation.

It remained mostly uncontroversial in this function until 2023 when a budget crunch required a funding deficit required one of the funding buckets to take a hit. Executive Director Singh proposed several plans to proceed, and the one approved by the Arts Commissioners (all of whom were nominated by the Mayor’s Office and approved by the City Council) gave the shortfall to the larger organizations.

In the wake of this decision, criticism of the Arts Commission’s Executive Director came from many sides, inside and outside the government and deeply politically connected art community. Media circulated identifying him as a dangerous, radical anti-colonialist liberal. A total freeze of the department’s grants was put into place, with scrutiny put on his department’s expenditures backed up by hearsay, and HR complaints about his workplace demeanor raised in public meetings without his presence.

Metro Legal ruled that the decision to intentionally fund Thrive because of its focus on diversity was unconstitutional now that the Supreme Court had banned Affirmative Action in higher education, a decision refuted by outside counsel hired by the Metro Human Relations Commission.

After month of battling with his own commissioners and Metro’s Legal and Finance Department, Singh agreed to walk away from his position in exchange for dropping a TN Human Relation’s Commission Complaint for the sum of $200,000.

A week after his exit, Metro’s Internal audit department completed a long-awaited audit of the processes of the Metro Arts Commission. Rather than an audit of who received what monies, the process audit was about the operational mechanics of the department. It very much found the department wanting in its standard operating procedures, with 34 areas of “high risk” to be addressed to prevent liability for Metro. Many of these issues predated Singh. They also continued under him. And a few were unique to her tenure.

But one of the issues that wasn’t flagged as “high risk”, but was just “medium” was over a question over the way Thrive payments were being made. The passage from the internal audit reads:

In 2014, Metro Arts obtained permission from the Metropolitan Procurement Agent to allow individual artists to be treated as a procurement activity for the establishment of the Thrive program. Allowable procurement activity in the expressed context was to apply in limited circumstances. Specifically, allowable activity was to engage artists in a variety of venues on very small engagements. Small engagements were defined as an item not exceeding $4,000 in cost. The Thrive program has expanded to no longer conform with the Procurement Agent’s approval. As award amounts have increased over the years, additional approval from the Procurement Agent was not sought.

Additionally, the nature and type of activities conducted by Thrive recipients varies significantly. The nature of some activities is more in line with a procurement while others are more in line with a grant. For example, grants award funding to a recipient for the recipient to achieve its own goals and objectives consistent with Metro Arts. Procurements typically would be used for the direct benefit of Metro Arts and seek best value.

pp. 20

Metro Arts had been operating within the rules that Metro Finance had laid out for some period, but, without the apparent notice of Metro Finance (who is responsible for ensuring the proper spending authority is attributable to all outgoing payments), Metro Arts’ allocations for Thrive began to increase in size. It wasn’t indicated when this started happening, under whose Executive Directorship, Mayorship, Finance Directorship, etc., but just this was an ongoing problem.

The gist was, that since some Thrive grants were over $4k, the original agreed-upon max, and since the general public was the recipient of the benefits of the services of artists through Thrive and not Metro Nashville, by their new interpretation, these monies should have been awarded as grants, and not individual payments to artists. And since individuals cannot receive grants in Nashville, Thrive funding for individual artists was deemed “improper.”

The recommended remedy for this “medium risk” issue was not to dissolve Thrive, nor to say categorically that paying individual artists was something that Metro could not do. The recommendation was:

Work with the Metropolitan Nashville Council, Department of Finance, Department of Law, and other stakeholders to establish a framework where the categorization of Thrive conforms to the Metropolitan Nashville Code, resolutions, and the intent of lawmakers.

pp. 21

The chatter about the appropriateness of how Metro Finance was cutting Thrive checks is not, from what I can see, something that concerned Metro authorities prior to the July 2023 vote that shifted funding away from large arts orgnaization.

At some point in year since the initial ruling to fully fund Thrive and to pass the budget cut to the larger arts institutions, questions arose about Thrive’s funding methodology. The questions stemmed from this:

Metro Nashville is not able to give non 501(c)(3)s grants, and that includes individual artists. Since the advent of Thrive in 2014 under Jen Cole, individual artists appear to have been paid as vendors, providing a service to Metro in their creation of public art. A 2014 Tennessean article about the first Thrive allocations very much spells this out.

Current Vice-Mayor Angie Henderson happened to have been interviewed by (then at the Tennessean) Tony Gonzalez. She summed up the public-private possibilities that came from funding artists succinctly:

A plain parking garage never lifted anyone’s spirits, but a colorful, artistic parking garage that the community planned together can. There may be artists in the community that have seen a particular wall or place that they want to improve. (THRIVE) gives people the feeling that something can happen here.

All of it raises some questions I haven’t heard expressed clearly.

To take the Metro Audit Internal Committee and various Metro Departments like Legal and Finance at their word, that the way individual Thrive artists were being paid was “improper”, why was this only brought to light within the last year?

It appears to be without contention that Metro Arts had been spending money in this way for several years before Singh’s tenure began in 2022, so why did it only come to the fore after outrage stewed amongst many in the high-profile art world when funding was cut for large institutions like the Symphony and Cheekwood?

As I’ve pointed out previously, the Department of Finance has a Metro Charter-obligated responsibility to ensure that all outgoing funds have a valid funding source.

(e) Examine all contracts, purchase orders and other documents which would result in or involve financial obligations against the metropolitan government, and approve the same only upon ascertaining that there is an unexpended, unencumbered and unimpounded balance in each such appropriation and allotment to which they are applicable, sufficient to cover such potential obligation.

(f) Audit before payment all bills, invoices, payrolls and other claims, demands or charges against the metropolitan government and approve the same only if proper, legal and correct, and duly authorized by appropriations or allotments of appropriations.

Metro Charter, Powers & Duties of Department of Finance, Sec. 8.103

The Metro Internal Audit of the Arts Commission raises some salient concerns about how they were keeping their books and tracking their grants and other SOPs that were being neglected. I would love to see similar audits of all departments, and not to make Metro Arts look better, but to give Nashville the information it needs to know how to improve.

But I find it conspicuous that the audit laid full blame for the perceived malfeasance re: Thrive funding at the feet of Metro Arts. Delegating procurement does not delegated away chartered responsibility, and if Metro Arts’ books were so out-of-wack, where is the remediation plan for the Department of Finance that allowed this to happen. After all, the charter requires of them to:

(c) Prescribe the accounts to be kept by all departments, offices and agencies of the metropolitan government, the form of receipts, vouchers, bills or claims, warrants, requisitions, purchase orders or any financial stationery to be used, and provide suitable instructions for the use thereof, and to review and approve the forms and procedures of the trustee and the tax assessor.

Six months into watching this closely, and while I feel that there is more accountability to go around than I first perceived — I am not unconvinced of some of my first impressions.

Metro Arts as a department has been flying by the seat of its pants for decades, its allocations not aligning with any coherent strategy for enhancing the arts in a sustainable, meaningful, or locally relevant way. Since the 1988 its primary function has been to supplement the budgets of large arts organizations, and in this capacity, it was given much autonomy to operate as it saw fit. When calls for more equitable funding were too loud to be ignored in 2015, the year after the killing of Michael Brown, the beginning of the Black Lives Matter movement

I had a back-and-forth with the Mayor on this a few months back in a journalistic email exchange that I’ll share excerpts of below. I had asked him if he agreed that the City Council had a right to oversee the funding criteria of the Metro Arts Commission (as stated in law). My line of questioning to him was about how the City Council, through this power, can provide clear guidance about what the point of these funds are, and who has the power to decide who receives them and why.

Mayor O’Connell agreed they had this authority (with some caveats) but also brought up the interpretation that this funding mechanism, implemented under the Mayorships of Karl Dean, Megan Barry, David Briley, and John Cooper, and overseen by various Legal and Finance Directors (including Kevin Crumbo in his previous stint in the role from 2019-2021) was illegal.

It’s a not insignificant allegation of Metro against itself, potentially putting Nashville under the crosshairs of the State comptroller, at least according to Mayor O’Connell.

O’Connell: Complicating the scenario, however, is that it appears that Thrive funding has been awarded unlawfully since the program’s inception in 2014. We’re exploring ways to ensure compliance with TCA on that front, too, to avoid Comptroller intervention. I’m open to suggestions if you have any.

Lacy: The "illegality" of Thrive is related to the "Delegated Procurement" of it all I believe? And the recent determination from Metro Finance that the type of services exchange that procurement is meant for does not cover artists creating works that the city would not own?

I had several reactions to that when I heard it in a Committee meeting I was present for, and no easy fix from how I see it right now, besides of course just getting a fiscal sponsor operating under clear guidelines to simply process payment for Commission awardees. Metro Arts did this is first year in 1988, pretty standard solution for this kinda thing. You gotta just be sure you retain Commission control of who gets awarded, with the same democratic process as now, or you'll be dragged for privatization of public funds and outsourcing equitable funding while keeping the funding in place for large arts orgs -- not a good look.

The ruling itself

As a philosophy grad from a family of lawyers, that ruling from Metro Finance felt more like a philosophy paper than a legal brief, but that's not to invalidate Finance's authority to make this ruling. Talking about this topic, you quickly get into cul-de-sacs of "what is 'The City'?" and "who owns art?" and what is a "public service"? Counter-arguments to Finance's current POV could cite existing contracts for cultural enrichment activities the city provides, like the Library hiring storytellers for a children's hour. Is "Metro Nashville" the recipient of these storytelling services, or is it the populace? But I'm not going to claim it's not a gray area that needs a ruling, and in this case, it's Metro Finance's remit to determine our procurement boundaries. 

Other cities, under different financial & legal leadership, and with different institutional preferences and values, find legal justifications for this type of activity (here's a sample legal justification they could put forth to the Comptroller). Our city's financial leadership clearly does not have this inclination -- and that's just the current state of play. I suppose this is an example of why Jonathan Saad (former Metro Grants Manager & Managing Director of Nashville Opera) confided in me that his experience made it clear that the Metro Finance Director "had much more power than anyone else in the city" which is what prompted my writing on all this in the first place.

Questions about the ruling

The obvious question is, if this is not a permissible way to pay artists, why was this allowed to happen for a decade unchecked? 

There is an extremely important discussion to be had about when Finance and Legal decide to take a hard look at how we spend money and find the rules that make something unallowable. If Finance is right that Thrive has not been funded properly since its inception, why was this not "caught" when it start in 2014? The Executive Director at that time was four years into her Metro tenure (with a husband who was a city council member and later Mayor's Office employee) -- this was leadership that should have been very familiar with procurement policies. 

Given that Thrive was being paid out so differently than previous grants, I'd be surprised if former Arts Director Cole didn't get something in writing in 2014 saying this was permissible according to Metro Finance.

But, if I'm honest, my experience with Metro Finance and Legal has shown me that they frequently allow things (I think almost always unintentionally) to happen that shouldn't have happened. It's fully believable to me that they only recently looked closely at this situation and determined it wasn't aligned with their existing interpretation of procurement rules.

When we were giving out Community Safety grants, virtually every time we submitted a new grant for approval, Legal and Finance found rules and processes that they'd more or less "forgotten" about, and we'd have to adjust our grant submission process. When we'd review those ordinances or Council Rules or State laws (because it naturally led us to be skeptical of their assessment of these things) we'd find that they were almost always correct. But we were confused on why they didn't know this before. It felt like a lot of on-the-job learning was happening on their side. I wound up making a manual for how to pass grants because Finance and Legal's SOPs were so up in the air.

I also saw hundreds of thousands of dollars of grants get passed through the City Council, through the hands of Metro Finance, Metro Legal, the Budget Committee, other Council committees, get approved and signed by the Mayor -- with no contract completed and attached to the resolution, something the charter says is required to be submitted with the resolution. (This happened again very recently actually, a grant went through to a non-profit, but they were never required to sign a contract.) In some cases, the approved recipients didn't even have valid 501(c)(3)s and were never eligible in the first place for grant funding, yet it was approved by the Council. 

I was never sure what to make of this situation. Was the resolution and allocation invalid? Were these recipients entitled to these funds if the Council and Metro Legal and Finance had incorrectly approved them? There are still some orgs that were approved for funds in 2021 that never got a contract signed and haven't gotten any money. Can they come back indefinitely and get paid out from that resolution? Lotta questions I never had answered.

I don't present any of this as a "gotcha" to point fingers. I liked everyone I worked with in Finance and Legal and thought they were doing their best. But it's a clear flag of an information gap on a few crucial things that have come to light with the Metro Arts ordeal. 

There’s a lot of anxiety about what’s next, if anything, for Thrive or funding like it in the city. It seems unimaginable that in 2024 Metro Nashville has no city funding for independent artists. The term “privatization” has come up a lot amongst Metro Arts watchers, a concept I discussed at length in a previous post.

While the primary fear of those who have come to rely on, or at least see the social import of, public funding for local arts is that it goes away entirely, the next anxiety is about if the determination of the administration is for it to allocate a yearly lump-sum for a 3rd party to allocate and distribute through some sort of general “DEI” best practices.

By using a 3rd party non-profit, individual artists could be funded as there is no restriction against a grant recipient passing along funds to individuals under 1099 contracts. This possibility has raised very valid concerns, and questions of why it’s necessary, pointing back to the ten years of successful payments to individual artists. “Can’t Metro Finance just reconsider their opinion, or tweak the arrangements with artists so Metro Nashville is more clearly the beneficiary?”

Anxiety over this type of “privatization” and skepticism over Public/Private partnerships in general has been percolating for decades. Leaders who see this as a future for programs like Thrive need to be aware of very warranted anxieties that exist about taking public funds and placing into organizations that are less accountable in how they are spent (and the routes to maintaining oversight that can be in place to alleviate these concerns to some degree).

In a past article I wrote:

Chiara Cordelli in The Boston Review writes: “If the twentieth century was the age of the bureaucratization of the modern state… the twenty-first century has been the age of its privatization.”

The impact of Neoliberalism and the privatization of global governments is stark, Cordelli writes, bringing:

“a renewed feudal order within which political power is increasingly exercised on the basis of privately negotiated obligations, nonpublic purposes, and, ultimately, unilateral determinations.”

Cordelli is saying that without the public’s real awareness or consent, the power structure we live under has been fundamentally changed since the 1980s.

A thing I point out is that “privatization” is not one thing, there is a spectrum. Strictly speaking, the government using any 3rd party vendors is to some degree “privatization.” Indeed, in the article I wrote on the topic, I argue that the origin of Metro Arts giving out grants to anyone is a form of privatization. Prior to the arrangement of grant funding that began with the Symphony, the department’s role was partly to put on arts festivals and generate revenue for the arts. By shifting to a grant model, it effectively outsourced the cultural activities of the government to 3rd parties like the Nashville Symphony, which is a form of privatization.

And indeed, it is still privatization when those funds go to organizations and people of color that do not work for the government, because the government could always just hire artists to make our city more beautiful (which not even a loony socialist like FDR would do… oh wait, he did.)

But, a third-party non-profit or foundation can certainly serve a useful purpose in distributing monies to individual artists who are performing a social good for Nashville through their creation of art.

But delegating the responsibility of choosing what artists receive this funding to a 3rd party, that is unaccountable to the voting populace, is at the far end of privatization models that Cordelli warns us about.

As I discussed with the Mayor in my email, the systemic entity in Nashville that likely has the most power to ensure this arrangement is democratic, the entity that has control over both Metro Code (which dictates the purpose and powers of the Metro Arts Commission), is the Metro City Council.

The Metro Code is quite clear on the powers of the Council to determine how this Commission spends its funds.

[The Metro Arts Commission shall:] Award funds appropriated to it by the metropolitan council to deserving nonprofit civic and nonprofit charitable organizations. Criteria for the awarding of such funds shall be established by the Metropolitan Nashville Arts Commission and approved by resolution of the metropolitan council.

2.112.040 - [Metro Arts Commission] Powers and duties.

The Metro Council has every ability to pass ordinances that would, for example, designate what percentage of funding each year goes to what types of organizations, and if funding were to be transferred to a 3rd party for allocation, what role the Metro Arts Commission would maintain in determining who receives that funding.

Again, “privatization” is not one thing. If Metro honestly believes it does not have the legal right to cut checks to individual artists, and that the last four Mayors and prior Legal and Finance Directors (who include the same ones in those roles now) were in violation of the law in doing that — that’s fine.

But it doesn’t mean Metro has to give up determination on who receives that funding and these decisions fall into a black box of undemocratic accountability.

How would you do this, though? What does this look like?

I’m not a big believer in pointing out problems without pitching solutions. Here’s what I suggested to the Mayor via email back in June.

I don’t know if he read it. Maybe you’ll take someone from it that is useful.

[How to Resolve the Metro Arts Predicament]

Roadmap

  1. Clearly map out the stakeholders who are involved in all the steps of funding distribution, starting from yearly city budget allocation & other sources to final invoice approval.

  2. Draw out the stages of Commission funding, from city budget allocations & other income sources, through to final distribution, with an eye to these three things types of decisions:

    1. Tatical: Who exactly is receiving funds? Who is involved in this determination?

    2. Procedural: What is the process we use for selecting who receives funds? How do we assess potential recipients? How do we modify this process?

    3. Strategic: What is the overall vision of this funding mechanism? How do we get input from the proper stakeholders to make this determination, and how to we evolve it based on public will and democratic norms? This is the stage that needs to have the most public input to build legitimacy back into the government.

  3. Define with precise language the scope of each stakeholders' powers and accountabilities. Citations must be provided for all of these, with a delination of what is self-determined policy, and what is binding law, ordinance, or other form of authority.

  4. Identify clearly the public venue to discuss the Strategic goals of Commissions, with opportunity for competing visions for the purpose of a given Commission to debate with one another. This is where different "classes" of organizations and recipients would have a voice to lobby for their vision of funding decisions. This is a more appropriate place in the chain of events than to have this debate at the tail end of allocations.

If I was hired to consult with Metro Arts this past year for systems design and change management work (knowing what I know now), I would have told them they had the precedent to allow them to alter the funding scheme as they did in July. But I'd also tell them to expect very violent systemic pushback because. By changing which of the competing agendas of Metro Arts funding they were prioritizing, they were executing what would be perceived as a "Stategic" decision (changing the perception of "who these funds primarily are for") rather than just a Tactical decision (who precisely is getting funded within an agreed upon Strategy and Process). This would not be the appropriate time for effective change management to implement such a change, and an attack on the Commissions' authority to make such a decision would likely occur. 

But it's not simply that they made a Stategic move at the wrong time, the nature of the Stategic move, to change the prioritization of the different agendas within Metro Arts, was not going to sit well with groups with a lot of positional authority. 

Metro Arts Commissioners have voted innumerable times for Strategy changes to how we use these funds without getting these updates approved by the City Council. But this was the first time those strategy changes explicitly de-priorized the Bredesen agenda of using these funds for "the major arts groups" as "a stable source of funding that they could more or less count on year after year."

So you gotta define when and where the Stategic vision of Metro Arts funding gets publicly debated, and leverage democracy to sort out these competing agendas.

This is how I'd use the city council's review of the funding criteria to do the hard work for me. This should be where Metro Art's strategy for funding is set in stone until the next time it is reviewed. It should contain information like this:

For FY2025, Metro Arts shall allocate its General Funds monies as such:

  • [e.g.] 25% of funding will go to organizations with over $3M yearly budget

  • [e.g.] 8% of funding will go to organizations with [focus] on performance art

  • [e.g.] 20% of funding will go to the Thrive program, whose parameters are defined [here]. 

    • Funds will be paid to individual artists via a 3rd part 501(c)(3) acting as a fiscal sponsor.

    • Said fiscal sponsor shall change no more than 1% for processing invoices on behalf of metro and the Arts Commission retains all authority on allocations of funds. 1% overhead shall not be deducted from the funding Thrive recipients receive.

In the event of a shortfall (or windfall) for the department, these percentages would be maintained, keeping the approved Stategy, but having the autonomy to make the tactical decisions of which of these types of recipients get funded.

So then funding process would work like this:

  1. Metro Arts staff takes the temperature of the arts community about the most desirable and effective funding strategy, detailing what types of recipients there should be and in what proportions, and they writes a proposed funding strategy document.

  2. Metro Arts Commissioners debate (and revise if needed) this Stategy document and provide to city council for approval. Public debate is available here for different cohorts of the public to give feedback.

  3. Metro Council received the approved version from the Arts Commision, moving through the Public Arts & Culture committee to the broader council, with all the requisite space for public comment, emailing your council person, tweeting and press conferencing.

  4. The Metro Arts funding strategy is approved in a democratic manner where different parties each have their opportunity to lobby for particular strategic visions of "what this funding is for."

  5. Mayor and City Council debate and approve a yearly budget allocating a total amount of funding for Metro Arts via the City Budget.

  6. Metro Arts Staff review applications and submit recommendation to the Commission that fall within the approved framework of the strategy document approved by the City Council.

  7. Commission reviews, amends if needed, and approves the grant recipients.

The downside of making a process more transparent and democratic is its harder for individuals to make off hand promises to organizations for funds, and then lean on board members commissioners to steer it in their direction. Democracy and autocracy kinda run at cross purposes here, so you gotta figure out what kind of leader you wanna be I suppose.

Sorry for the novel, but I think it could be useful.

I’ve been killing time while looking for a new job by attending public meetings on the Metro Arts debacle here in Nashville. It’s been an exercise in empathy, critical thinking, and searching for public parking.

You don’t have to look far in this city, state, or country and see bright lines drawn down cultural hot button issues, and when it’s come to theories of funding the arts with public monies, we’ve got a microcosm of the culture war all our own.

For a recap, read the first section of the above.

To me, this is a classic communication breakdown across lines of differing worldviews, something I don’t think we’ve ever been great at overcoming as a species.

The Metro Arts program, as an experiment in how we can evolve our values and our funding priorities as a city, is something that could meaningfully be called progress in a state that has all but outlawed the concept — if its allowed to happen.

But the process will require us to do the thing we feel least inclined to do: experience each other’s humanity and negotiate with folks we’d rather not have to deal with.

But that’s democracy, baby.

I was drawn to the flames of this controversy because the situation was reminiscent to a many of the issues I tried to work on at the Mayor’s Office. I wasn’t previously much invested in where public monies for arts go before this, aside from being a lifelong PBS dork who escapes depression by watching this video of Mr. Rogers testifying before the US Senate:

One thing about the Metro Arts situation that caught my attention last week was something that a lawyer hired by Metro Legal said at an Metro Arts Oversight meeting.

“There is a distinction between Anti-Racism work, and Diversity, Equity and Inclusion and Accessibility work. And it was apparent to me… that from Director Singhs perspective and the perspective of certain staff members and probably certain commissioners, he was tasked with operationalizing Anti-Racism.

But it was also clear to me that from the perspective of some Commissioners, and perhaps, even some staff members, that he was tasked with a Diversity Equity, Inclusion and Accessibility mandate to improve historic dysfunctionality.

And that starts the tension that i think the confusion around all of these issues that I think led us to this point where it was even necessary for me to conduct an investigation.”

Luther Wright Jr., Esq., Ogletree Deakins

I would categorize the distinction that Mr. Wright is making as between valuing “equality” vs valuing “equity,” something that’s been highlighted a lot the last half decade, but also the last half century.

…it’s important to distinguish between equity and equality. To build equity into your DEI initiatives, you must realize that each person comes to their role with a different set of needs. While equality means giving everyone the same resources, equity means giving individual employees the resources and opportunities specific to their needs so that they can reach the same level of success as their colleagues.

TechCrunch (2021), Candice Bristow

This image was making the rounds a few years ago. It’s intent is to demonstrate that equity is about actually getting folks to be able to have access to the same experiences. The same box does not give everyone the ability to watch the game. This view of what equity means is saying: “it’s not enough to just give boxes equally to everyone, but what barriers do different people have keeping them from the opportunity to truly thrive?”

But what’s underlying the image’s comparisons is an implicit value judgement — the judgement that one should care who has access to see this apparently two player soccer game in an enormous stadium. Anyone can see that there is a difference between these images, and that when we put in the work to give people the ability to achieve their dreams, God rewards us with a rainbow and fewer clouds. But it doesn’t get to the heart of the issue, because it’s not a linguistic or technical issue we’re actually grappling with. This isn’t a carpentry problem

A person could say:

“But why do I care if these kids can see this soccer game or not? Soccer is dumb! Also, they all seem to be stealing access to this game, can we talk about that? Should this even be allowed?”

I tried to write about this years ago, back when I still had a Facebook, and my feed was filled with polarized attacks from disparate from parts of my social life that had no business knowing each other existed. The question I asked then was, in the face of horrific tragedies that seemed to be coming one after another:

For Whom’st Must We Give A Fuck?

I was writing about people’s reactions to the migrant border crisis (the ‘kids in cages’ era, something which hasn’t substantially improved despite our attention going elsewhere). I was very distressed by it. And pretty overwhelmed when feuds happened in the comments about whether this was actually a crisis we should care about.

The debate really didn’t seem to be about if this was a bad situation for these children. That was pretty clear. But the debate was if it was our problem to care about the well-being of these children.

The view was: “They aren’t Americans. Their families made choices to leave their homeland. What’s it go to do with us?!”

The whole, “well, we destabilized Central and South America for the better part of a century…” thing didn’t seem like a satisfactory answer at the time.

But the subtext was: “I’m not feeling the same way you claim you’re feeling about these people — and I don’t believe you actually feel that way either.”

When expressed in these extremes, there’s a coldness and an inhumanity that few people would condone. It’s easy to ascribe an antisocial personality diagnosis on folks who say things like that, which is probably sometimes warranted. Indeed the guy posting this on my wall back then seemed to be having a mental breakdown of sorts and was prone to sending rabid DMs about Jewish conspiracies and “the globalists”.

But when we look at how we structure society, and indeed, not just western and modern society, but across history and culture, there are inevitably lines drawn in the collective values of that group over “whom’st they must give a fuck about”, and to what degree.

I bring this up in the context of Metro Arts because while I think we can all agree that when you look at the numbers over the past three plus decades of funding, it is unambiguous to see that the primary beneficiaries have disproportionately been white organizations, passed on to white employees, and what efforts have been made to remedy this have been minimal (and often aren’t addressing the historic imbalance, but rather pledging to balance the equation a bit better in the future).

I would argue that reasonable minds would agree on that (but I’ll also concede that there are many minds, when engaging on topics like this, have little ability to be reasonable).

The next question that an honest conversationalist who, saw this situation and wasn’t feeling the same outage as the person who is outraged might say: “yeah, but so what?”

“I’m not feeling the same way you claim you’re feeling about these people.”

That is rarely said aloud. I think folks mostly all know that it is shameful to articulate such flippancy on “social justice issues”, but it might be helpful if folks actually said it, because that’s the discussion that needs to be had.

Many folks are quite triggered at the assertion that there is inequity in something they’re attached to — and I think the reason for this is more complicated than that folks are just “self serving” or acting racistly, or lashing out from white fragility.

There’s too often people of color who have these negative reactions when confronted with complicity in inequity against other people of color to chalk it all up to white supremacy and internalized racism.

I think there is a deeper, more existential anxiety when these topics are brought up. The fear of misalignment on the “scope of services” within our social contract: the unanswered question of: “For Whoms’t Must We Give Fuck?”

I think this is what undergirds our culture war and the rise of global fascism in the face of liberal progress with impending global climate catastrophe on the horizon — we’re unclear on Whom’st We Must Give Fuck about, and that’s only going to be more and more important to get right.

The tricky and nuanced thing is that it’s not a single circle drawn around us of “whom’st we must give a fuck about” (and to what degree). We have multiple spheres drawn around us all, growing and shrinking based on how we are doing mentally, emotionally, spiritually, and there are different themes to the values that we have on how we treat those that are in those different spheres.

Why and how we draw those lines is a really complex issue, I’ve grappled with it the better part of 15 years now. It’s been hard even articulating what fields I’ve needed to study to get some perspectives on it. My undergrad, exclusively and exhaustively focused on the Western Canon, going from Homer to Heidegger, only had traces of answer the question of “For Whomst Must We Give Fuck?” and why do we see to disagree on who that is.

This may be a thing that was never fully discussed openly with Metro Arts — why should we care

I had some conversations after this meeting where Mr. Wright spoke, and some people had harsh words for his articulation of this dichotomy. The criticism was that Mr. Wright didn’t go far enough to say definitively that the version of DEIA that only pursues equality (and stops short of genuinely implementing equity focused Anti-Racism) is basically bunk and can actually make things worse.

Their take was basically, “he’s saying people were confused between one thing and another, as if that’s understandable, but one of those things isn’t a real thing.”

To me though, this gets into a non-trivial philosophical space where we have to ask, what is it for a thing to be a real thing? I don’t think equality without equity is a real possible, but my observations tell me that other people do think that — and that’s, if nothing else, interesting.



How and why do people think that, and how do you get them to move past that? I’ve had this very conversation with some primary figures in this situation, and the response is often to advise people to bone up on their anti-racism reading.

I have a friend Chris I met in Amsterdam at a conference for a thing called Holacracy. He was a change consultant and his job was to transition teams at a major shoe distributor to an non-hierarchical self-governance work structure. People had to unlearn everything they’d seen and heard about work structure, hierarchy, and fully step into self-accountability and autonomy while also respecting the boundaries of everyone else around them. He was one of the best change consultants in that field. I asked him how he taught people this system, filled with complex words and jargon and byzantine process, so quickly and effectively.

He told me: “I would never teach them any new words. I would work with them and study what they were doing, and say things like ‘lets do a little more of this, and less of that.’ If I acted as if I knew something they didn’t, they would never feel empowered, it’d be like I had all wisdom and they needed to get it from me. But I knew they all had the will and desire to be like this anyway, I just needed to help nurture it.”

He believed less in the system’s rules, and more in the spirit of what he was professing, and believed that it aligned with how people wanted to behave. And he wasn’t surprised when his clients, freed of the blockages they had from behaving that way, started behaving that way.

When looking to have a deeper understanding of where we are, I think we all need to turn to whatever transformative perspectives resonate with us already. All are valid in their own ways. You’re probably familiar with at least one of several I favor — Spiral Dynamics, Metamodernism, Shakespeare, the The Judeo-Christian Bible, The Tao Te Ching, Hindu Advaita Vedanta, and when I’m feeling freaky, Dune (the books).

As Sri Ramakrishna put it: “Many are the paths to truth, and they all lead to the same goal. Just as rainwater falls on many fields and flows by many paths to the same ocean, so all these paths lead to God."

Let’s lay out the situation as we know it, and do some analysis:

Characters and Tensions:

  1. Metro Government Officials

    • Role: Central figures in the distribution of arts funding, holding the power to withhold funds based on legal and financial oversight concerns.

    • Tensions: The citing of allegedly non-relevant Supreme Court rulings and withholding of $2 million in arts funding, citing financial prudence amid potential deficits, raises questions about equitable treatment of smaller arts organizations versus larger institutions, especially when these institutions are seen to have ties with government officials.

  2. Metro Arts Commission

    • Role: Body responsible for the allocation of arts funding, aiming to diversify and equitize funding across the arts community.

    • Tensions: Faced significant pushback and legal scrutiny from Metro Legal and other entities after attempting to redistribute funding to favor smaller organizations and individual artists. This includes accusations of unconstitutional actions and potentially violating civil rights provisions, reflecting the broader struggle to balance equity and equality in funding distribution against established interests.

  3. Larger Arts Institutions

    • Role: Historically significant beneficiaries of Metro Arts funding, with substantial budgets and influence within the community.

    • Tensions: Their opposition to funding redistribution underscores the conflict between maintaining traditional funding streams and the push for a more equitable allocation of resources. This resistance highlights the challenge of transforming the funding landscape in a way that supports both established and emerging arts entities.

  4. Community and Smaller Arts Organizations

    • Role: Advocates for more equitable funding practices, aiming to increase support for diverse and grassroots arts initiatives.

    • Tensions: Struggle against systemic barriers and the dominance of larger institutions in funding allocations. Their push for change is met with legal and procedural hurdles, exemplifying the broader debate on how to achieve fairness and equality in the distribution of public arts funding.

Overarching Tensions:

  • Financial Oversight vs. Equity: The Finance Director's role in financial oversight introduces a tension between fiscal responsibility and the equitable distribution of arts funding, particularly when potential conflicts of interest are considered.

  • Tradition vs. Transformation: The struggle between larger arts institutions accustomed to significant funding and the desire to transform the funding landscape to support a broader spectrum of the arts community.

  • Legal Scrutiny vs. Inclusive Policies: Legal challenges to funding decisions reflect a deeper tension between adhering to traditional legal interpretations and pursuing more inclusive, equitable policies.

  • Systemic Barriers vs. Grassroots Advocacy: The efforts of smaller arts organizations to navigate systemic barriers and advocate for more equitable funding practices highlight the broader challenges in achieving systemic change within entrenched funding structures.

  • Ethical Conduct vs. Perceived Immunity: The concerns raised about the Finance Director's potential conflicts of interest and the adequacy of his ethical conduct underscore tensions between individual actions and systemic ethical standards within municipal governance.

SD is a simple system for describing the different values at play in human interactions, and indeed within individual humans. Within all of us, and manifesting in varied combinations across any group of people or organization, lie diverse arrangements of value sets at play. All of them have a rational, evolutionarily valuable purpose for existing. Putting these values into balance, and plugging them into situations to the right degree and in the right context is difficult. It’s not the role of everyone to balance them, that’s the role of leaders, to integrate these values, unlocking human potentials in the process.

These value sets are called “vMemes” for a reason that doesn’t have to do with the internet (and is bit too much to explain right now). For shorthand, they’re labelled by colors, and they go in a certain order, like the rainbow. And like the rainbow, all the colors are necessary.

I think they all lead to the same realizations.

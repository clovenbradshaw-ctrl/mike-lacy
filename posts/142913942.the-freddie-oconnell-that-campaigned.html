What Even is Progressivism?

Progressivism “is a political philosophy that holds that it is possible to improve human societies through political reform or through government mandates.” It is a willingness to spend public money to alter the underlying iniquities of society, and a willingness of its leaders to stand up to the political and business forces who benefit from these imbalances.

Emerging in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Progressivism aimed to rectify the social inequities and economic disparities of the Gilded Age, a period marked by rapid industrialization, urbanization, and unfathomable imbalances of wealth and power. It was a period of extreme income inequality that is unparalleled in human history, until, of course, now.

Leaders like Theodore Roosevelt and Woodrow Wilson championed reforms to curb corporate power, improve labor conditions, and enhance democratic governance.

Progressivism has a complicated relationship with racial equity concepts. From its inception in the United States, there were “Progressive” leaders from the Black liberation movement and intelligentsia, such as W.E.B. DuBois. His founding of the NAACP is rightly situated as a movement within the broader Progressive Era.

But some of the most prominent pre-war Progressive leaders, like Woodrow Wilson, had no interest in racial equality, much less equity. Wilson is defined as an unequivocal champion of Progressivism, implementing constraints on big business, instituting the eight hour work day, implemented the first child labor law, and eventually welcomed women’s suffrage. But he was far from woke.

A virulent racist (as his own Presidential Library attests), he reinstated segregation in government agencies, promoted discrimination in government policy, and is reported to have hearlded the film “Birth of Nation”, a glorification of the KKK and demonization of Black Americans as mindless, raping hordes, as “History written in lighting.”

Bringing the Progressive Era to a close, Wilson’s legacy goes to show that being a “Progressive” is a mantle that takes some defining. Let’s assume, with fingers crossed, that people like Mayor O’Connell sees themselves in the mold of what we’ll call a “Modern Progressive” that have integrated an equality and equity focus into their Weltanschauung.

The legacy of the Progressive Era set the stage for the New Deal programs of the 1930s under Franklin D. Roosevelt, which sought to further address the economic and social challenges highlighted by the Progressives and were exacerbated by the Great Depression.

And the last major push of progressive movement was in Lyndon Johnson’s Great Society policies, the passing of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Voting Rights Act of 1965. It’s at this point that Progressivism is unavoidably intertwined with the cause of social equality for Black americans and all other oppressed minorities.

Progressivism arguably left the White House at the end of Johnson’s term, though it’s tent expanded and welcomed important victories for new Progressive causes include protections for the environment, women’s rights, and gay rights.

The ‘80s and ‘90s led to Progressivism decreasing in prominence in the Democratic Party, and the rise of Neoliberalism. Neoliberalism, still the prevailing valence of moderate and centrist Democrats in the United States, focused on the privatization of public resources and the dissolution of the services that help the socially vulnerable.

All that might sound familiar from notes we copied off Hillary Berkowitz in AP US History, but is any of this applicable to city politics? Afterall, isn’t there “no Democratic or Republican way to clean the streets?”

While these different terms rarely get discussed in local campaign ads and debates, there are certainly differences in Neoliberal and Progressive ways to be a Mayor.

Neoliberal Mayors look like this:

  • Privatizing previously public services (e.g., utilities, waste management, public transportation).

  • Implementing policies that favor business and real estate development, often at the expense of affordable housing or public spaces.

  • Reducing municipal spending on social services, such as public education, healthcare, and welfare programs, in favor of encouraging private sector solutions.

  • Offering tax incentives to attract corporations or to stimulate economic growth, sometimes at the cost of public revenues.

Progressive Mayor focus on these types of things:

  • Housing: Actively combating segregation and gentrification, protecting against discriminatory lending, investing in affordable housing, and supporting land trusts.

  • Education: Ensuring equitable public school funding, actively closing achievement gaps, and promoting culturally relevant curricula.

  • Economic Inclusion: Uplifting minority businesses, ensuring job access, and addressing wealth gaps with workforce programs and fair development.

  • Health Equity: Eliminating health disparities, supporting community health centers, addressing environmental injustices, and providing culturally competent care.

  • Criminal Justice Reform: Reforming policing, reducing racial profiling and violence, ending cash bail, and supporting non-incarceration alternatives.

  • Civic Engagement: Enhancing minority representation in policymaking, establishing advisory councils, and encouraging civic participation.

  • Racial Equity Lens: Implementing systemic approaches to equity, conducting impact assessments, providing anti-racism training, and engaging with communities of color to aim for a more inclusive, equitable city.

There is a a nearly 1:1 match with O’Connell’s “15 Fixes on Day One”. Except the racial equity lens that is. This is something that, as we continue to watch the administration, we shall “put a pin in” and see how it goes. But from a philosophical angle, let us ask:

Can you be a Modern Progressive, but not anti-racist?

In this author’s opinion, anti-racism is an unavoidable subcategory of a modern Progressive agenda, which sits alongside mitigating other social vulnerabilities, such as lack of access to affordable housing, literacy, transportation, and fresh food.

There will, and likely should be, considerable debate as the relative hierarchy of different forms of disenfranchisement. As has been elucidated thoroughly by far smarter people than you and I, 400 years of systemic social, psychological and economic oppression against Black americans is one of the greatest forms of sustained disenfranchisement in world history.

Government’s role in addressing systemic racism as a causative factor in social inequity came to the fore 60 years ago with the passing of the Civil Rights Act. It’s obligation will never go away until humanity stops essentializing people based on the socially constructed concept of race — which has not, as of this article’s publication, happened yet.

Addressing inequities, including systemic racism, is now at the core of what it means to be a “Progressive”. And since a government’s primary role is the balancing of public monies into different places, doing so equitably is the primary preoccupation of Progressives in power.

We have more nuanced ways of talking about who needs social support from the government than we did under Lyndon Johnson. The federal government has developed a tool called “The Social Vulnerability Index” that tracks, down to census tracts, which Americans are most at risk in times of crisis, natural disaster, community violence, and other forms of catastrophe.

The term “social vulnerability” includes 14 factors, including socioeconomic status, household composition, housing/transportation, ethnicity, language and race, which are ranked to help identify those who are most at risk in times of emergency or crisis.

Initiatives like Transit Referendums could in theory have Progressive valences to them, but they could also regress to being Neoliberal solutions that simply transfer public money to private organizations for construction and service delivery without really enhancing the transportation needs of those who need the most assistance.

To be Progressive, projects must have, in planning, implementation, and outcomes, methods to reduce the economic and social burden of the most socially vulnerable members of society.

In 2024, Just as in 1964, this is where the rubber of Progressivism hits the road.

It is not enough to “prioritize” Progressive ideas, you actually have to get them implemented with fidelity.

What A Progressive Legal Team Looks Like

Lyndon Johnson was not a lawyer. He was a weird Texas Hill Country teacher who had unusual needs for his pants who bullied legislation through congress with an encyclopedic knowledge of his colleagues desires and weaknesses. Yet he changed history by passing Progressive legislation that has stood for the better part of a century.

LBJ accomplished his Progressive policies because he had the requisite legal apparatus beneath him. His legal team understood how to implement his agenda with fidelity and legitimacy such that it would be extraordinarily difficult to deconstruct and return society to its racial stratification.

The architect of his legal strategy was Nicholas Katzenbach. He was key in designing and implementing the legal strategy for the Civil Rights and Voting Rights Acts.

Here he is confronting Governor George Wallace in 1963 to force the legally mandated integration of the University of Alabama.

Katzenbach understood that implementing these society-changing policies would take more than drafting LBJ’s policy proposals and submitting them to Congress. He understood the resistance they would face in implementing these policies with fidelity, and what he would need to do to overcome that.

Katzenbach saw that overt white supremacy, that was the norm in America from 1619 to the Civil War, was no longer the overt ideology they were up against - it something more subtle, harder to pin down.

The fact [was] that nobody in the South, no elected official in the South believed that you should deny blacks the right to vote formally. You could do it by every other means that you wanted. You could have poll taxes, you could have literacy tests, you could keep them from voting. But if you asked them to defend [it] —if you asked them if the 13th Amendment was wrong they’d say no.

Nicholas Katzenbach, Oral History

Katzenbach saw that the policies which claimed to be “race neutral” had a very clear dispropriate impact, and that many things that were on the surface “not racist” were effectively so.

Katzenbach negotiated with moderate and conservative congressional leaders in ways that seemed impossible. His team understood how to use legal readings, political frameworks, and the underlying motivations of their opponents to take the mounting waves of progress stirred up by centuries of Black activism, and turn the tide of history.

Nicholas Katzenbach's helped draft the legal theory for the Civil Rights Act, including Title VI, which savvily leveraged federal funding as a tool to enforce non-discrimination, compelling institutions to comply with civil rights objectives to receive government support.

This is what a legal team is supposed to do for a Progressive administration. Breaking down the obstacles that are in the way of policies. Deconstructing the legalese doublespeak of your opponents. Placing you on firm, defensible ground to build the society you want to see.

We don’t have a legal team like that in Metro Nashville.

“And you sir, are no Nicholas Katzenbach.”

We have a Legal Department that is siding, with minimal consideration of the facts, with dubiously applicable Supreme Court rulings that are aimed at deconstructing core Progressive policy items that are directly tied to LBJ’s Great Society initiatives.

We have a Legal Department that is aligning themselves with TN Attorney General Skrmetti’s agenda anti-equity agenda.

We have a Legal Department that is plausibly committing Title VI violations of the very Civil Rights Act of which I’ve been speaking, and continuing to double down on its actions.

Here is what Metro Legal told Metro Arts after they voted to equitably fund arts last July:

At the last Arts Commission Meeting, the Department of Law was asked about the effect of the recent U.S. Supreme Court ruling on race-based decision-making. Here is a summary of the ruling and how it would apply to local government grants.

Race may not be used as a way of distinguishing between applicants. The Equal Protection Clause forbids treating people differently because of the color of their skin.

Preferences for one race over another may not be used to remedy the effects of societal discrimination. Racial balancing is patently unconstitutional.

People may be treated differently on individual skills, differences, or unique abilities. They may be treated differently based on where they live. Grant-makers may consider an applicant’s discussion of how race or other societal issues affected his/her life or experiences as an individual – but a decision may not be made on the basis of race.

Metro Legal memo, July 25, 2023.

This is not a real legal analysis. For one, it is 9 sentences long. It also has, and please forgive the legalese, a lack of citation to binding precedent, insufficient analysis under existing legal standards, overgeneralization of constitutional provisions, absence of distinction between de jure and de facto discrimination, lack of consideration for nuanced remedies to societal discrimination, and deficient reasoning on racial balancing.

It also makes fundamental misrepresentations of the role that race played in the Metro Arts vote which, interviews by the MHRC found, was not a determining factor by any of the commissioners.

If Metro Legal wanted to dash off a quick memo to keep Metro Nashville out of any potential trouble, this is all they needed to say:

Following the Supreme Court's ruling on Affirmative Action, it is imperative that our funding frameworks reflect a commitment to equity without making race a determinative factor.

We advise employing the CDC’s Social Vulnerability Index, a method recognized by the State of Tennessee, which incorporates factors including race and ethnicity within a broader, intersectional assessment of equity issues. This approach aligns with precedents in both federal and state funding practices, ensuring compliance with the Equal Protection Clause.

Departments and commissions are encouraged to revise their funding criteria, ensuring that decisions are not race-determinate but encompass a wide array of vulnerability factors. Utilizing tools like the Social Vulnerability Index will help in making informed, equitable funding decisions that stand up to legal scrutiny.

That’s what a memo to department leaders looks like when you’re watching out for the city’s liability, but not trying to intentionally derail a Progressive program for any number of motivations.

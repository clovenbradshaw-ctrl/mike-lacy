The last few weeks have left me at a loss for words. Unusual for me, I know. I have felt my aperture narrow, not able to take much in. A profundity of bad things are coming towards us in fast motion, too much to comprehend. Our forthcoming catastrophes are foreshadowed by one carnivalesque cabinet appointment after another.

My life, and my family’s life choices, have already been impacted by things like Supreme Court rulings these past few years. I’m near paralyzed about what is coming for communities I support, like the immigrant laborers at Workers’ Dignity that I volunteer with each week.

I have an immigration lawyer in my family. They’ve told me that while there’s nothing certain, the only debate is whether the planned policies of rounding up immigrants indiscriminately are going to be terrible, or unfathomably barbaric.

In this time of upheaval, let’s take stock of who is most vulnerable from the collective choices we have made.

Please consider supporting my cause of choice, Workers’ Dignity, who I have worked with first hand for the last six months.

I cannot think of people more worthy to support than the laborers that are victims of wage theft that Workers’ Dignity supports. They are primarily Spanish speaking, first generation immigrants, because that is who most often has their wages stolen.

Wage theft—when employers deny workers their rightful pay—costs U.S. workers an estimated $15 to $50 billion every year, disproportionately impacting low-wage and immigrant workers.

Workers’ Dignity was my first gig after I left the Mayor’s Office last year. I started there at the same time as their new Executive Director, Rev. RJ Robles, who I’ve had the honor of learning the ropes from these past few months.

I switched to volunteering a while back because I believe deeply in its mission and didn’t want to draw down on their limited budget. Their small team has, this year alone, recovered over $200,0001 in lost wages through a three step, restorative justice informed process — money that goes 100% to the workers who earned it.

Here are three quick stories of victories they’ve had the past few months, and some details about the folks Ted Cruz thinks you should be afraid of. These are real people I’ve met, helped through a process I’ve seen first hand, a process that is, despite what people who want you to be afraid of your neighbors say, precisely what democracy looks like.

Carla’s Two-Year Fight for Justice
Carla, a divorced housekeeper in her 50s, spent two years waiting for the $6,000 her employer kept from her. With her pregnant daughter unable to work and rent bills piling up, she turned to Workers’ Dignity. With only a few months before the two year statue of limitations on wage theft was up, she couldn't afford a lawyer, but she desperately needed what she was owed.

Through their advocacy and restorative justice-informed process, Carla finally received the money she was owed—just in time to welcome her grandchild into the world.

Antony and Rosa’s Fight for Their Son’s Health
Antony and Rosa were reeling after their 7-year-old son required three surgeries—first for an appendectomy, then two more to address complications. As their medical bills mounted, they were being denied thousands in wages from construction work they had already done.

With Workers’ Dignity’s support, they reclaimed $6,250 of their stolen wages—giving them the financial stability to begin paying down their son’s medical debt and move forward with dignity.

Joaquin and a Victory for Many
When Joaquin wasn’t paid for his construction work, he thought he was alone. But Workers’ Dignity showed him he wasn’t. After holding a public protest and mapping the power dynamics at his job site, he received the $2,530 he was owed.

But the story didn’t stop there. Workers Dignity uncovered wage theft affecting more workers on the same project, and to date, they have recovered over $7,000—with more cases underway.

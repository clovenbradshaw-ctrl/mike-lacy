I write to break things. Not people, illusions. But I know that for people who’ve grounded their identity in illusions, this can feel threatening.

I write to break silence. The consensus. The collective lie that says staying quiet is what keeps us safe. I break things by naming names, because it is the type of thing that can only exist while it is unspoken.

My latest article

The title of this piece says “resurrect”. That word means something specific: you cannot resurrect what is still alive. Something has to die first. And the soul of Nashville, because it has stopped learning, is dead.

Let's revive it.

The culture I am describing in this piece — the one that governs Nashville from both parties, at both levels of government, through an honor-shame system older than either — that culture has killed our soul. Honor-shame is a specific type of culture, different than a “guilt” culture. It has particularities from being in the American south. Its roots are deep in the soil of slavery.

This piece is not a funeral. It is not an autopsy. It is an invitation to the people — on both sides, at every level — doing the Weekend at Bernie's of our democracy, to clear the ground for a rebirth.

It's an invitation to stop, to look at what the performance has cost, and to participate in what is already growing underneath our feet.

This is a piece about Tennessee politics. It is also a piece about what grows in the dark.

If you came here to find out which side is right, you will leave disappointed. If you came here to find out what’s alive in the shadows and what needs to be reborn, keep reading.

A note on what prompted this piece.

On March 19, 2026, Bill Carey had a cover story published by the Nashville Scene: “The Forgotten History of the People Nashville Enslaved.” Thirty-three human beings, owned by Nashville’s taxpayers. The city has, as of that article’s writing, done nothing to acknowledge them.

It's a staggering piece of journalistic integrity.

The Nashville Scene is published by FW Publishing, owned by Bob Freeman — a sitting Democratic state legislator, the largest of Nashville’s apartment managers, and a board member of the Nashville Downtown Partnership, the private nonprofit at the center of the accountability failures described in this piece.

Freeman sits on the board of the organization that funds a secret private police force — staffed by off-duty state troopers, accountable to no elected body — that arrests homeless people on the same downtown streets where Nashville once ran slave patrols.

Pastor Lindsey Krinks spoke to a crowd gathered in front of the Nashville Downtown Library last week, reopened after its garage was exploded under the watch of the Nashville Downtown Partnership, an event they have never apologized for.

“This was the road that the sit-in movement, in the late fifties, early sixties protested at,” Krinks told the high school robotics team that had donated twenty handmade benches to their neighbors, “because they were being told they couldn't sit down because of the color of their skin.

So this is a really important area for the history of civil rights in Nashville. When those arrests happen, they are not done by Metro Police officers or on-duty highway patrol officer. It is the private police force employed by the Nashville Downtown Partnership. And as I explained in my last post, they funds they use have not been properly authorized for 22 years.

Bob Freeman’s Nashville Scene excavated an ancient history of oppression, and it often does vital investigative work. But it has been silent about the role of the Board of Directors of the Nashville Downtown Partnership that in victimizing today’s underclass.

Many I've spoken to see this as the dark irony one should expect from liberals that preach tolerance from Instagram but execute oppression from the board room.

I see a path.

Bob Freeman can read this piece and see an attack. Or he can read it and see what it actually is: a description of a structure he is part of. And also a path forward. The same path that is open to every person named or implied in what follows.

The path pushes through shame, it meets at the crossroads of guilt, and it’s destination is communion with one’s neighbors. But few with power have the balls to walk it.

I know why people don’t walk this path. Far more powerful than greed, the fear of losing honor and living in shame rules Nashville. The logic of shame says: if what you did is revealed, you will be excluded, and exclusion is, or might as well be, death. That logic is the entire engine of the silence.

And the calculation is not entirely wrong — there are real consequences to admission, real relationships that will strain, real professional costs to saying aloud what everyone knows but does not speak.

I know because I have paid them.

But the calculation has a flaw in it, and the flaw is this: the people who would permanently exclude you after you have shown genuine contrition, after you have done the work to make amends, after you have walked through the fire of honesty to repair what has been broken — there is a technical term for those who won’t forgive you. The term is “assholes.” They are not the moral center of community. They are the guards at the tomb. And you do not owe your life to the opinion of assholes.

There is another vocab word we need to relearn — a word for someone who lives in your community, at times commits wrongdoing, acknowledges it, makes amends, and moves forward, stronger for it. The technical term for this is a neighbor.

We are going to resurrect the soul of Nashville. Not with revivalist church services or even with hand-painted benches. We are going to do it by reminding the most powerful people in our community that deeper than license plate readers, deeper than rent exploitation, deeper than surveillance budgets and backdated contracts and twenty two years of unauthorized transfers — the thing they most want, because it is what we all want, is to feel safe. To feel part of something. To have, and to be, neighbors.

And we very much do want you to be our neighbor. But you playing.

I. The Two Silences

Here is what you think is happening in Tennessee politics: a conservative supermajority at war with progressive cities. Here is what is actually happening: both sides are using each other’s playbook, and neither side’s voters are benefiting.

The supermajority — the party of limited government — governs through consensus enforcement, passing bills along party lines with the procedural smoothness of a single-party system. The irony should sting: the most vocally anti-communist legislative body in the country governs like a politburo.

In Nashville — the party of transparency and accountability — allows at least sixty million dollars in unauthorized payments to a private nonprofit for over twenty years, routes surveillance equipment through that nonprofit to bypass its own procurement oversight, and lets an office created to serve the homeless operate with millions in questioned expenditures while people sleep on the streets.

Both strategies produce the same outcome: unaccountability. And both serve the same class of beneficiaries — not voters on either side of the culture war, but the people who operate in the gap between both systems. The developers, contractors, consultants, and administrators who need the supermajority to preempt local regulation and need the city to keep routing money without oversight.

This is not a conspiracy. It’s a structure. The pattern running across both is suppression-through-consensus: the social cost of disclosing institutional failure is made higher than the social cost of institutional failure itself.

When it is more socially dangerous to acknowledge wrong doing than to do immoral things, doing right becomes a moral hazard. This is deeply, viscerally connected to a culture grounded in slavery. But it doesn't show up today in the same Messianic rhetoric that legitimized the breaking of Black bodies for the benefit of the white body politic. Now it's about professionalism.

This is the honor-shame structure in secular dress: don't rock the boat. The offense is never the act — it is the exposure. Not secrecy — the information is usually available. The suppression occurs at the level of speakability: what can be said aloud without violating the social contract that binds participants to the consensus.

It works through a self-stabilizing loop. The consensus frame makes the cost structure invisible (”that’s just how things work here”). The cost structure prevents disclosure (”why would you make trouble?”). The absence of disclosure preserves the consensus frame (”see, nobody’s complaining”). Round and round. Year after year. In a blue city and under a red dome.

II. The Red Silence

When the supermajority dissolved TSU’s Board of Trustees, its own $2 million forensic audit found no fraud. It dissolved the board anyway, the same day the audit was released. Meanwhile, federal data showed Tennessee underfunded TSU by $2.1 billion relative to UT. UT had the same dormitory problems — but called it “Volunteer Inn” and everybody celebrated.

The preemption pattern extends further. The state abolished Nashville’s voter-created Community Oversight Board, banned inclusionary zoning, preempted minimum wage laws, blocked gig economy regulation, and in 2024 passed a law prohibiting local governments from implementing ordinances that increase police accountability. The state codified unaccountability into statute. If you are a conservative voter who believes in local control, your own supermajority is governing like the progressive bureaucratic state you say you oppose.

III. The Blue Silence

Metro Nashville transferred over sixty million dollars to the Nashville Downtown Partnership without the legally required Metro Council budget approval. The last Council-approved CBID budget was FY 2003-2004. NDP CEO Tom Turner acknowledged to the Budget Committee in July 2025 that the group “had sent budgets to the Metro Finance Department and had assumed that submission satisfied their obligations.” It did not. Over $42 million in transfers since 2019 alone — processed without anyone flagging the missing authorization. Not the Finance Director, not the Comptroller, not Metro Legal. For over two decades.

Then the surveillance money — routed from state funds through the District Management Corporation to NDP to purchase cameras, software, an armored vehicle, a mobile command post, all bypassing Council oversight. The office created to serve the homeless: at least $350,000 paid to landlords with no contracts, $500,000 to a national nonprofit with no contract. Backdated contracts. People placed in housing, then abandoned — rent payments stopping, eviction notices arriving.

The system runs on the complicity of the compassionate. It is powered by the people who care the most and can afford to risk the least. This is honor-shame’s progressive variant: the community performance matters more than the community outcome, and the shame attaches to anyone who names the gap. NDP’s security contractor was cited for sixty-two counts of impersonating police officers. Its off-duty troopers arrested over two hundred homeless people — with explicit instructions to leave tourists alone. When the city removed benches and replaced them with concrete spheres, people started building their own. The mantra: “For every seat removed, two shall take its place.”

IV. The Convergence

There is a popular dichotomy between guilt cultures and shame cultures that misses the point of both. Guilt is not the mature version of shame. Shame is not the primitive version of guilt. They operate on entirely different dimensions. Guilt tracks an act against a standard — you violated a rule, you know it whether or not anyone saw, and there is a repair pathway. The self survives the evaluation. Shame tracks being — something about what you are has been exposed, and the exposure cannot be undone. Every culture runs both systems simultaneously; the question is never which type you are but which system dominates, in which domains, and what happens when they collide.

And here is what no developmental model will tell you: without the active capacity for self-correction — without the ability to let what you learn about yourself rewrite what you believe about yourself — every system, no matter how sophisticated, gravitates toward honor-shame. Guilt requires maintenance. It requires confession, repair, a community that can absorb the admission and keep the person in the room. That infrastructure is expensive and fragile. Shame requires nothing. It is the default. It is what you get when a system can define itself but cannot revise the definition — when the cost of admitting error becomes higher than the cost of continuing it. The theological traditions understood this as the problem that grace solves: the structural capacity of a system to let the truth in without collapsing.

Without it, every community becomes a court, every movement becomes a purity test, every democracy becomes a performance. And when two systems that have both lost this capacity face each other — one calling itself conservative, one calling itself progressive, neither able to admit what it has become — they stop being opponents. They become the same system with two uniforms. The personnel become interchangeable. The silences merge. And that is exactly what has happened in Nashville.

The off-duty state troopers who patrol downtown under a private security contract funded by the city are from the same pool who conducted immigration sweeps in Latinx neighborhoods — six hundred traffic stops over six nights, seventy-five percent with no criminal record. The same personnel, serving two different silences, paid by two different systems, accountable to neither.

Nashville rolled out the red carpet for Oracle, whose co-founder Larry Ellison told financial analysts his vision for AI is a world where “citizens will be on their best behavior because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that’s going on.” In the hands of an administration that already routed surveillance through a private nonprofit, these are the infrastructure of a list-making apparatus. And Vanderbilt — Nashville’s most prestigious medical center — paused gender-affirming surgeries under political pressure, turned over unredacted patient records to a hostile attorney general, and withdrew from Pride. A liberal institution, kowtowing to a conservative anti-trans agenda with one hand, building surveillance infrastructure with the other.

Both sides have stolen each other’s honor codes. The supermajority’s code says: don’t break ranks. Nashville’s code says: don’t break the brand. Same structure, different content. Both punish the person who speaks, not the person who acts.

V. What It Feels Like Inside

You file a records request. You read the documents. You find that the law was not followed — not arguably, not in some gray area, but plainly, for twenty two years. You publish what you found. And the response is not “thank you” or even “you’re wrong.” The response is that you are now the problem.

It feels like being a wild animal. Someone who cannot be trusted. As if you are vindictive and wrathful for asking people to follow the law. It is dissociating. It creates a cognitive dissonance so total that it becomes atmospheric — you breathe it. You start to wonder: Am I crazy? And underneath the dissonance, a voice: Wouldn’t it just be easier if we didn’t talk about this?

That voice is the honor-shame system working as designed. In a guilt-innocence system, the question is: did you do it? In an honor-shame system, the question is: did anyone see? The person who exposes wrongdoing inherits the shame that should have belonged to the act. You are now carrying what you found, because the system needs it to live somewhere, and it cannot live with the people who did it — they are still in the room, still honored.

This is the operating phenomenology of Nashville in 2026. It is also the operating phenomenology of Nashville in 1835, when Amos Dresser was publicly whipped for possessing abolitionist pamphlets — charged not with violence but with having the wrong literature. The committee published its proceedings as civic virtue. And fifty-seven years later, when Ephraim Grizzard was lynched while a white man in the same jail — charged with raping an eight-year-old Black girl named Maggie Reese — was not touched, Ida B. Wells saw the structure clearly. The punishment was never about the crime. It was about who gets to be exposed and who gets to remain hidden.

The abolitionists endured this. They were told they were dangerous, radical, treasonous for claiming that all people are, in fact, people. And still they continued — not because they were brave in some abstract way, but because once you have seen the lie, you cannot unsee it. The enslaved people who built communication networks under total surveillance — the operational intelligence system that became the Underground Railroad — they left the blueprint for what is growing now.

VI. What Is Already Growing

Something is already happening. Something that has already produced measurable results. Something that cannot be reduced to its parts, does not have a name, and cannot be joined but is always welcoming.

A surveillance contract dies from a thousand small acts — records requests, vendor cross-references, forwarded documents, public comment from people who know more than they should. An unauthorized twenty-year payment stream is questioned because someone asks “why hasn’t the council been approving these budgets?” and the council’s own legal counsel cannot find an answer. A judge creates a special eviction docket during an ice storm using data that is now available because someone built a public database the city has to answer for. A pastor texts two friends and thirty people show up with power tools to build benches. The liturgy: “For every seat removed, two shall take its place.”

There is no name. There is no 501(c)(3). If you want to understand what it looks like, think about mycelium — the vast root network of fungi beneath the forest floor, the largest organism on earth, invisible. When a tree is dying, it doesn’t mourn — it consumes the decay and routes the nutrients to new growth. It is not sentimental about what was. It is only faithful to what is becoming.

Here is what makes this different from the system it’s replacing: it can be wrong. It can investigate something and find outs it's assumptions we're wrong. It can pursue a lead that doesn’t pan out and say so. It has no brand to protect, no consensus to maintain, no funder to keep happy.

And it needs broken things. Mycelium does not attach to perfection. It attaches to decay. The fibrous hyphae seek out dead wood, failed structures, things that didn’t work. A movement that has no room for people who got it wrong is a movement building on bare rock. It will not grow.

This is where both the megachurch and the social justice warring version of purity get it exactly wrong. Both demand perfection — moral or ideological — as the price of belonging. Both are worshipping a golden calf: an ontological fallacy elevated to the sublime object of ideology. They have mistaken the performance for the thing, the purity of the container for the quality of what it holds. And both produce the same outcome: people who cannot admit error, because error means exile, and exile means death. It is honor-shame all the way down. The content differs. The structure is identical.

The mycelium does not require purity. It requires honesty. The people on the real path are not clean. They are the ones who got dirt under their fingernails because they were digging, and some of what they dug up was their own rot, and they kept digging anyway.

VII. The Offer

I am not writing from a position of purity. I have made mistakes in this work — published things I later had to correct, burned relationships I should have handled better, let my own anger write sentences that should have been written by my patience. I am in my own purgatory. Nobody doing this work is finished.

So when I say what I’m about to say, understand that it is not coming from above. There is no group to join. There is no gatekeeper. There is only the path — and the path is not something anyone owns or controls or can define precisely. It is, in the oldest theological language, something you can only gesture toward. Only define through negation: “Not this, not this.” You can only point at what it is not, and what it is not is everything the current system is: closed, performative, and terrified of its own reflection.

But the path needs what you know.

If you are a Council Member who looked the other way — the work needs you, because you know which questions the budget committee stopped asking and when and why. If you are a mayor who inherited a system you didn’t build — the work needs you, because you know what “impossible” actually looked like from the inside. If you are a lobbyist who won everything your client wanted and then drove home and couldn’t stop thinking about what winning looked like for the people on the other side — the work needs that drive home. Nobody else has it.

The path is not asking you to join despite your broken past. It is asking you to join because of it. That is the operational reality. Your insider knowledge is not your shame. It is your contribution. Without it, the repair is being built in the dark.

The records requests will keep coming. Accountability is not negotiable. But accountability is not cancellation. Cancellation is spiritually immature authoritarian liberalism using the master’s tools to add another floor on the master’s house. It produces fear. Fear produces concealment. Concealment is exactly what got us here.

What is being built is not a guillotine. It is a table. But to sit at it, you have to walk through the door with honesty — not because anyone needs your confession, but because you need to say it, out loud, in order to stop being the only one carrying it.

I will not lie to you about what this looks like. It is not a homecoming. It is purgatory.

But purgatory is not hell. This is the thing the Catholic tradition understood that both the megachurch and the puritanical activist space have forgotten: there is a place between damnation and paradise, and all of the real work happens there. You are not being destroyed. You are being refined.

And here is what matters most: if you are not at peace, and if you are not at work with your soul, you are already in hell. You have been in it for years. That is what the silence feels like from the inside — the weight of knowing what you know and not being able to say it, the grinding cognitive dissonance of performing competence over a system you know is broken. You are already burning. The only question is whether you name the fire and let it do its work, or keep pretending you are not on fire until there is nothing left to save.

If you feel like you are being crucified right now — reading this, seeing yourself in it, feeling the nails go in — it is because you are. Welcome to the purgative work of self-discovery. Welcome to the other side of the looking glass, the glass you once only saw through darkly. All souls, to find peace, to become true neighbors, must pass through these stations of the cross. There is no shortcut. There is no version of resurrection that skips the death.

And the word that waits on the other side — neighbor — is not a sentimental abstraction. I don’t think there will ever be enough statistics to convince someone who doesn’t want to hear it that their actions are harming someone else. The way is not to hear more about their suffering and weigh it against your own anxiety. The way is to realize that you are your neighbors. The edict to “love your neighbor as yourself” is ontologically precise. It is not a syllogism. It is to dissolve the boundary between you and them — not to deny the difference, but to follow that incommensurability all the way to the asymptotic limit, the point where the distinction between your wellbeing and theirs becomes so thin it can no longer bear weight, and what holds you up is the same ground that holds them.

If you’re sharing this to perform awareness without changing behavior, you’re feeding the pattern. The supermajority is worse and Nashville is culpable for its mismanagement. Hold both.

Neither party can walk this path. Both are locked in honor-shame’s terminal bind: the cost of admitting error feels higher than the cost of continuing the error. That is how these systems die — not by being defeated, but by becoming unable to learn. A system that cannot incorporate new information about itself is already dead. It just doesn’t know it yet.

But mycelium always learns.

To see everything you must give up knowing anything. To know everything you must give up being anybody.

You want to join and be a part of it, but you can’t join what you’re already inside of.

You want to lead the revolution, but there’s nowhere to lead to if everyone is already present.

Every false leader is a hollow shell. Every true leader is an empty serving vessel.

People want to know what the movement is called, but as soon as it’s named it’s no longer that.

Never abandon your beliefs. Transcend them.

In the world of the mute, those with voice are prophets.

In a world where all can speak, there is no injustice.

Enemies are only defeated when they join community.

The world is made whole when we realize it isn’t broken: it is forever becoming what it has always been.

The way is dark, perilous and fraught when the goal is narrow. The way is easy, joyful and clear when the goal is wide.

The only souls that cannot attain enlightenment in a prison are the guards.

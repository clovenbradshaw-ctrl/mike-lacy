Two Tales of The Gordian Knot

With the authority of having a Western Classics degree, I’ll say the the story of the Gordian Knot is not a very good parable. In fact, it’s kinda dumb. But it’s the two different versions of the legend that have profound significance to me. They feel like vivid metaphors for potential futures for the Metro Arts entanglement, stemming from two different versions of who our Mayor is, or may become.

In both tellings of the “Gordian Knot” story, Alexander the Great, during his rise to power in year 333 BC, comes across a portentous challenge and overcomes it: a literal big ol’ knot he has to untie.

In the city of Gordium in Phrygia, within modern-day Turkey, an omen-filled rope tied a chariot to a post in a sacred temple of Zeus (separate long story of how it got there). The knot on the rope, tied so complexly that it foiled all who tried to detangle it, became embedded with prodigious meaning. An oracle prophesied that he who was able to undo the knot was destined to rule, not only Gordium and Phrygia, but all of Asia.

Entering Gordium with his armies, Alexander indeed fulfilled the prophecy: he undid the knot, and, history shows, conquered an empire reaching from the Balkans to India.

But accounts differ in how the knot was undone.

Version One: The Ancient Roman writer Rufus reported that, faced with the solemn stringy mystery, the conqueror showed his boldness by taking a sword and simply sliced through the knot. SNIKT! Like an ancient hellenic Wolverine, bits of severed rope flying everywhere, he “solved” the knot with force. Feels like a bit of a “first draft” idea, but sure, it’s evocative, and shows his DGAFness for a culture’s rites and mysteries, but technically, yes, no more knot.

Version Two: Aristobulus, as later retold by Plutarch in his genuinely page turning biographical essay on Alexander the Great, tells a different version, one that portrays him as savvy rather than brash. Staring upon the chariot, with the rope looped around it’s wagon pole and tied in a cryptic knot to a post, Alexander had an epiphany: “By simply taking out the so-called ‘hestor’, or pin, of the wagon-pole, by which the yoke-fastening was held together, and then drawing away the yoke” the knot just fell away.

What’s the big difference? In either case, the knot is no longer there, and Alexander still fulfilled the prophesied colonialist marauding across all of the Asian subcontinent. But I think it’s significant which version resonates most with us.

Do we value cutting through things and walking away with the tatters of our problems behind us? To me. it’s giving less problem solving and more “You happy? Which way’s Delhi?”

Solution Two seems more worthy of the educated Alexander who studied under Aristotle. He took primary pride in his intellect, saying, according to Plutarch, “I had rather excel others in the knowledge of what is excellent, than in the extent of my power and dominion"​. Solution Two celebrates critical thinking and elegancy in action as leadership qualities. It’s core principle is: “What is the simplest thing that could lead to the most complete resolution?”

So. Which type of solution with Mayor Freddie employ when it comes to Metro Arts?

Freddie’ll Fix It

We’re heading towards some sort of resolution for this Metro Arts mess. It didn’t start under O’Connell, and it won’t “end” per se, but there will be some sort of new norm under his auspices.

But — will his solution be one where he slashes through the problems, leaving fibrous bits of resentment amongst arts community members, political apparatchiks, and those of us concerned about representative democracy’s longevity in a boom town for authoritarianism?

Or can Freddie implement an elegant solution, which, while not perhaps untangling everything, will remove the tension from this rope.

As has been said many times in public forums, the labor of equity-centered advocates and employees, very often unacknowledged or discredited women of color, have been loosening the cords on this knot for years, if not decades. If the Mayor is able to elegantly able to detangle this mess, it will be with the benefit for their labor.

Let’s lay out out each of the issues making up this rope. I count at least six different tangled up cords, made up of many strings, all knotted together in the form of unresolved questions. Here’s what I’m seeing:

  1. Funding Models

    1. What will the funding models be for future years? How will this affect budgets of legacy arts institutions that have “gotten used” to this funding, and for medium and smaller groups that need city support to grow?

    2. How will community-generated grant funding models, developed over the past few years by the Metro Arts Commission, be preserved in future funding cycles?

    3. What will be the distribution of funding between large, and smaller (more heavily BIPOC) organizations moving forward?

    4. How will individual arts, rather than non-profit art orgs continue to be funded, as they have been since 2014, and why is funding of individual artists only be raised as an issue after 10 years of funding?

    5. If individual artists are funding via a fiscal sponsor, how will that sponsor be selected with transparency and funding continue with democratic integrity?

  2. Metro Arts Commissioners

    1. What onboarding and operational support can be offered to a commission that has had about 50% turnover in 2 years?

    2. How can best practices for public meetings be strengthened to enhance public trust in this commission and all Metro boards and commissions?

    3. How can further public trust be put into the nominees put forth for this commission which is now the highest profile board or commission in the city?

  3. Metro Arts Staffing Questions

    1. When and how will the status of the executive director be resolved?

    2. What is the role of Metro Legal in Human Resources issues, such as Family and Medical Leave requests?

    3. Has there been a change in the workplace conditions since accusations of anti-Black behaviors were reported under the prior executive director?

  4. Departmental Oversight

    1. What role does the City Council play in overseeing the funding methodology of the Arts Commission?

    2. How is it possible for departments, which must have their obligations and invoices approved by Metro Finance, spend into “the red”? How often does this happen and what system wide changes are being implemented to prevent this?

    3. When will Metro Finance release its administrative hold on the funding of the department?

    4. What policy documents can be generated that explain the processes of if and when an administrative takeover of a department occurs by Metro Finance and/or Legal?

  5. MHRC Report & Title VI Civil Rights Claim

    1. What role will the Metro Human Relations Commission (MRHC) play in remedying the underlying causes of the Title VI complaint and potential lawsuit brought on behalf of artists who had their funding overturned in 2023?

    2. What type of reconciliation can be had with the Title VI complainants such that their perceived need for a federal lawsuit is minimized?

  6. Healing and Reconciliation

    1. What efforts will be made to implement restorative justice practices to help mend the rifts created within the city’s arts community?

    2. Will there be an acknowledgement of the role of systemic racism and inequity in the historic patterns of arts funding and in the rescinding of last July’s vote?

    3. What systemic changes will be made to prevent these from happening again, and what space will be made for voices of dissent to raise these concerns before problems arise to this level again?

The Metro Arts Commissioners, the Metro Arts Executive Director, the Metro Arts Staff, the Large Arts Organizations, the Smaller Arts Organizations, the Individual Artists, the City Council, Metro Finance, Metro Legal, Metro Human Relations Commission (MRHC), the Title VI Complainants, and probably more parties than even that.

To many who are deeply versed in this ordeal, this seems like an impossibility. There’s a pervasive assumption that in this situation, like in most complex human entanglements, that people simply want different things and there’s no way give everyone what they want. “The world is a win-lose game and you’re a sucker if you see it different.”

I met a social philosopher in Ukraine in 2019 that sees the world differently. Hanzi Freinacht sees the world as a game whose rules we all make up, but one in which we are able to change those rules. Those rules can, if we so want, include ones wherein we all win. If Mayor O’Connell is successful in his effort to cutting the Metro Arts Gordian Knot, it’ll be by adopting a mentality like Hanzi’s. Hanzi talks about a way of resolving conflict for mutual benefit, win-win or “both-and”:

Resolving the conflict for mutual benefit, through a higher synthesis or “both and” that in fact refines the perceived interests of the parties involved and gets more at the core of what they truly want, just in a way they hadn’t necessarily thought of — often redefining the roles and the nature of the relationship itself.

Hanzi Freinacht

What this means is O’Connell has to listen not just to what people are saying and asking for, but to understand what the underlying needs are that are causing them to ask for those things. People always feel they have very particular wants that must be met, but as good negotiators know, for the most part those wants stem from a very small list of needs:

Safety and Survival.
Love and Belonging.
Power and Control.
Order and Rule of Law.
Personal Advancement.
Community and Equity.
(In that order.)

O’Connell may not be able to give everyone what they want. But he can get a lot closer to giving everyone what they actually need.

OVERVIEW OF FY24 GRANTS -- WHAT HAPPENED

This one-page overview provides a summary of what occurred leading up to the FY24 grant cycle and the decisions made in July and August 2023.

Arts staff led a number of community-engaged planning activities in Fall 2022 to develop recommendations that would increase access to Metro Arts funding opportunities. Primary among them were Community Editing Panels in which 27 representatives from arts organizations and independent artists provided direct input that led to a number of policy recommendations, examples including shortening the grant applications and removing various eligibility barriers, increasing the maximum amount for Thrive grants, and creating a funding model based on organization size that shifted some funding to organizations with smaller budgets. This included a tiered budget structure that determined funding amounts based on organization budget sizes categorized as micro, small, medium, midsize, and large. These and other changes were approved by the Arts Commission in December 2022. Meanwhile and throughout Spring 2023, Arts Commissioners and Arts staff participated in a number of trainings, planning, and capacity building work toward incorporating anti-racist and equity-focused initiatives.

By the mid-April 2023 grants deadline, 92 organizations and 155 individuals had applied for Operating and Thrive grants. The arts community mobilized to advocate for a budget allocation that would meet this demand, but then-Mayor Cooper’s budget fell short. The Council approved a $2M amendment for cultural support, which the sponsor described as intended to support equity and Thrive projects. Still, the total budget was insufficient to meet the full requests of all Thrive applicants and all organization requests, which left the Commission in a position to determine how to allocate funds in alignment with their equity goals and in support of the changes approved through the Community Editing Process.

Director Singh and contractor Dana Parsons developed multiple “funding scenarios” based on the budget structure approved in December (2022). Combined with data about organization sizes, included in informational materials and Commission deliberation were “BIPOC impact” and “white impact” dollar amounts that showed the result, in dollars, of each funding scenario for each category. The Grants and Funding Committee recommended approval of two scenarios that prioritized fully funding Thrive awards and prioritizing organizations with smaller budgets. In July (2023) the Commission approved one of those recommendations with an 8 (yea) -3 (nay) -1 (abstain) vote.

Commissioner Cheek expressed concerns over the constitutionality of the vote directly to Law Director Wallace Dietz. Metro Legal issued a memo and called for an Executive Session at which they recommended the Commission rescind its vote due to the Supreme Court’s recent ruling in Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard against affirmative action, implying that arts grants were race-based programs. In August (2023) the Commission rescinded and revoted, this time approving a scenario that partially funded Thrive projects, maintained funding levels from the previous year to midsize and large budget organizations, and funded micro, small, and medium organizations at lower amounts than they would have received under the July vote. There has been considerable Commissioner turnover and staff changes in the months since these controversial votes. This report provides details and our findings about the actions that led to the approval of an equitable funding model and its abrupt reversal.

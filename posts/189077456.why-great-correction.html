One of my best friends in the world, Whitley Watson (On Substack as), an NYC-based writer, filmmaker and comedian, is also low key one of our eras great philosophers.

She’s only people I’ve talked to in the past few years who’s been talking about what happens after the catastrophic now, and she has a half serious, fully plausible fantasy called “the Great Correction.”

I hit her up Sunday night to riff on the “Great Correction”, her idea that systemic corruption and inequality have become so unsustainable that a genuine cultural and political shift is inevitable. Not a pendulum swing to the opposite extreme, but something new: more community-oriented, more participatory, more honest about what actually works.

From there we got into the cynicism trap on the left — where identifying that “they’re all corrupt” becomes an excuse to disengage rather than a call to act — and the arrested development of millennial politics, with boomers still gripping power while younger generations oscillate between disillusionment and the occasional spark of real agency (see: Mayor Mamdani).

We talked about my theory that consultant class is the modern priesthood — the McKinsey-ification of governance and how top-down hierarchical management empirically fails at managing shared resources, per Elinor Ostrom’s Nobel-winning research. The library doesn’t run for profit, why must the subway?

The back half of the conversation touches on AI, convenience, and learned helplessness — the trade of dependency for convenience, AI bots emailing AI bots with no conscious center, and what it means to actually be present in your own life. Plus: oligarchs as deeply unwell people crowd-surfed to the top precisely because of their sociopathic traits, the ketamine mind as the drug of our current political moment, and Robert Maxwell as an underappreciated architect of financial strip-mining.

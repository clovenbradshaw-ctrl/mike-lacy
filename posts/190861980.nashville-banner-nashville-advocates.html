Tensions have flared again between the Nashville Department of Transportation and community advocates after the removal of wooden benches that had been built, painted and placed at bus stops around town. The bench confiscation came to light Thursday by way of a widely circulated photo showing more than a dozen of them in a heap at a downtown Metro facility.

The guerilla bench movement in Nashville, similar to efforts in Berkeley, Calif. and Chattanooga, began last year, and picked up steam after Metro removed eight metal benches along Korean Veterans Boulevard in August. The city benches had regularly been used by unhoused people and were replaced with large concrete spheres. In the months since, a growing coalition of community advocates has placed dozens of benches, painted with art and various slogans, at bus stops where there is nowhere else to sit.

On Thursday, those advocates started sounding the alarm that NDOT had removed 15 to 20 of the benches without warning.

“This is a disappointing betrayal of the trust we had been building with NDOT,” Robin Lovett Owen, a leader of the bench-building group To Nashville With Love, said in a statement. “In a meeting on November 21, 2025, NDOT assured us that they would not remove our benches and wanted to work with us to meet our demands. We now believe that they had already begun removing benches at that time.”

Asked when and why the benches had been removed, NDOT spokesperson Brendan Scully responded to the Banner by email on Thursday night.

“The benches currently held at NDOT were collected last year due to their placement obstructing the right-of-way,” he wrote. “We must always ensure that the right-of-way is safe and accessible and we’re committed to working with partners to keep that the priority. We’ve been in communication with the organization that made and placed the benches, and we’re working to return the property.”

Scully said NDOT has been working with the group “to permit the installation of wooden benches in the right-of-way through our Tactical Urbanism Program.”

Lovett Owen said the bench builders were “disappointed” by NDOT’s response.

“As we told them in our meeting, our goal is always to make our public spaces more accessible, not less,” she wrote in an email to the Banner Friday morning. “This means that we take blocking the right of way or ADA concerns very seriously. We take a lot of care to place our benches in places where they will add to a space, not take away from it. Previously, we expressed to NDOT that if they had concerns with any specific bench, we would be happy to move it to a more appropriate location. NDOT removing our benches and storing them was never part of the conversation, and we were unaware that NDOT had removed 15-20 of them.”

Late Friday morning, a volunteer with the group told the Banner that four of the benches had been retrieved from NDOT custody. Online, advocates shared a flyer advertising a bench building party at Riverside Commons on March 21 from 9 a.m. to 12 p.m.

“For every seat removed,” the flyer reads, “two shall take its place.”

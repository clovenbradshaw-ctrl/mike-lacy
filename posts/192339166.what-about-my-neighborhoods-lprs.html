I got a nice email after my last article talking about MNPD’s push for License Plate Reader (Recognition) devices (LPRs). It was from an acquaintance I’ve have coffee with a handful of times and always have good conversations with.

They asked a very natural question and I thought our exchange would be helpful for the broader discussion around these devices and similar tools for surveilling our neighbors.

Here's our exchange, with their name redacted. And if your reaction is “wow Mike’s email style is extra,” just imagine how insufferable I was straight out of Liberal Arts College.

Here is their email to me.

Good morning Mike,

Talk about spooky! Oh well, to me the over-arching issue is what you identified: whether Council will use its authority to oversee procurement. But a question to counter what you wrote: “there is no evidence that surveillance technology makes any meaningful positive impact on community safety.”

You’re likely as aware as I am of the countless communities nationwide using LPRs. The one sticking in my memory is Mt Juliet because of their success catching criminals using LPRs. Do you not accept their experience as valid?

[Nashville Neighbor]

Here was my response:

Hi [Nashville Neighbor]!

I like the way you framed that, “do I accept their experience as valid?” I do accept that their experience has been that some people have seen arrested tied to these systems.

My response would be: “Yes, but do you also accept the experience of people who are being stalked by rogue police officers for personal reasons using these systems, targeted by ICE, monitored for accessing reproductive healthcare, and tracked after going to protests as valid experiences?”

What I think is the unfortunate thing is that the examples of the latter far outweigh the former, and so by any reasonable measure these tools are a net negative on community safety.

So the question becomes “whose experience is considered ‘valid’ in conversations like this?”

Quite often it is white property owners whose opinions “matter” the most in these conversations, the same people that tend to be less likely to be targeted by the abusive sides of these systems.

So my overall response would be yes, I think their experience is valid. It is just not a very representative experience in the larger context of things, though I know that is not something people like to hear.

Truth is asymptotic. We approach it ever closer as a limit, never able to fully touch it, but always able to get closer. We get closer the more perspectives we integrate, the more we put things in context and see the whole picture. And the experience of the people putting these systems in, seeing a few “criminals” be arrested and saying “this is making my community safer”, is a real experience.

It is just a very limited, narrow experience.

It is seeing the world through a keyhole and claiming you’re omniscient. And it also draws very narrow lines as to whom you consider your community — and I know the circle you draw for who matters in your community is actually very broad, and encompasses everyone.

Here’s an important caveat: I do not think it is inherently harmful for license plates to be recorded, if that data is only accessible to law enforcement in the process of actual investigations of crime. This is not much different than someone sitting on their porch writing down license plates as a pass time and the cops asking them “who did you see on the 15th? There was a robbery near by.”

But that is not what these systems really are. They are rolling systems of monitoring the movements of people that can be accessed by all sorts of actors in many places, very often in violation of the explicit terms of service the purchasers signed.

If your system is integrated with a national system like Flock, I would say that you are doing grave harm to your neighbors by providing that data to unregulatable 3rd parties that are using that to track people for all kinds of things, increasingly for doing things like accessing reproductive healthcare and going to protests.

I would say that if your system is sending data to anywhere but a computer that exists in a place that you have seen, and you consider violence inflicted by police officers or the government as problematic and similarly harmful to what is done by the “criminals” you mentioned, you should cease using that tool immediately.

You and I have talked at length about how much of the worst harm in our society is done by people wearing uniforms and white collars. I think pushing for the removal of this system in your community is very much where those values would show up in actions more than just words.

And in truth, I would advise you to get rid of the system even if it does record things locally, does not proactively share data with law enforcement, and has been useful to stop a couple carjackers.

To quote a former Metro Council Member: “We can build safer communities through smart, strategic investments that don’t involve spying on one another.”

I hope that helps,

Mike

Here was their response

That was a mouthful worth reading more than once, which I’ve done. I will add Zeno and his paradox to the mix, as it paints a neat picture of how public policy debate progresses on issues. It’s rarely black and white, although I’m nervous about accepting your assertion that “white property owners” have the upper hand. I don’t think that approach helps.

I continue to fall on the side of using LPRs etc because of their potential positive impact vs how misusing these technologies causes personal pain and suffering. I’ve not been convinced yet that the knee-jerk reactions and their litany of abuses are as prominent as many claim. I’m not asking for you or anyone to barrage me with statistics and anecdotal commentary. I’m just not there yet.

But your email has been helpful because, as usual, you prompt me to think further.

[Nashville Neighbor]

And my final response to them yesterday.

Zeno’s Paradox is precisely the right metaphor. Binary thinking, the crossing of half into the other side unto infinity, never gets us there.

Binary thinking does not allow us to cross the threshold of seeing each other’s needs and taking others’ lived experiences as equally valid as our own. And this, I think—not to pontificate on the genealogy of political movements—is the failure of “Liberalism” in a nutshell. Liberalism is grounded in the idea that if we just meet in the marketplace of ideas and express our needs, the “best” results will out. But it doesn’t take into account (intentionally, I’d argue) that many of the “needs” people express are the results of millions of dollars of marketing telling people they “need” something like license plate readers to keep them safe. And so those “needs” show up in budgets that places like the Nashville Downtown Partnership put in wish lists of how they’d like to spend taxpayer grant money.

Zeno presents the paradox to show the limits of reason, and this is precisely why geometric logic had to be transcended to understand how the world actually works (see Newtonian physics). Geometric logic can create new things, new wholes that can’t be broken back down into unchanged parts. But it doesn’t explain how we cross thresholds, and the fundamental threshold in a society is the experience of other people. How do we build a society where the powerful do not trample the less lucky? Liberalism says “go state your case in the public forum, go vote every four years, never mind the gold tipping the scales in the favor of the same few every time, more and more. Have you tried asking extra politely?”

Some people see this and turn to nihilism, anarchy, or violent uprising.

I turn to transcendentalism. Not in a vague, ephemeral, Emersonian way (I’ve actually never read Emerson, now that I think about it). I mean transcendental like calculus. Calculus is fundamentally about transcending limits, about taking two differently sized lines and going to the transcendental point beyond geometric logic where the difference is so negligible that they are the same. And this, to me, is the way.

I don’t think there will ever be enough statistics to convince someone who doesn’t want to hear it that their actions are harming someone else. The only way to care about your neighbors is not to hear more about their suffering and weigh it against your own anxiety and make rational choices. The way is to realize that you are your neighbors. That the edict to “love your neighbor as yourself” is ontologically precise. It’s not a syllogism, “as you love yourself, so should you love your neighbor.” It is to dissolve the boundary between you and them, not to deny the difference between you and them, but to follow that incommensurability all the way to the asymptotic limit.

I think then we see the world differently. Not as atomic units of self-interest bumping off each other forming molecules of special interest groups. What we see is a community, a whole that generates itself, whose movements are not propelled by competition and mutual self-interest, but by something like “Entelechia”, Aristotle’s concept of “being-at-work-staying-itself.”

There’s a Pre-Socratic philosopher, Heraclitus, and between him and the Tao Te Ching, I don’t think anything else needs to be read. He has a famous fragment that’s often translated “nature loves to hide.” It’s a bad translation and I’ve been working on a better one. I’ve landed on “Emergence loves to be private.”

We should not spy on each other — and not just because we ought to feel empathy for the bad things that happen to each other when it goes wrong (and therefore, in a binary way, feel bad for doing it). Spying on each other is bad because it is unhealthy for the collective organism of society.

Emergence, the evolutionary agency needed for new forms of life to grow, requires people to have a reasonable expectation of privacy. We need the ability to move with some degree of privacy to make new friends, to plan a surprise party, to go to a protest, to talk to a doctor about what to do with our pregnancy — so many things that we take for granted are based on the ability to not have them be observed, and not because we are doing anything wrong.

When you move beyond black and white thinking, you see that not all good things grow in daylight. Fungi are some of the most abundant and miraculous life on earth, and they thrive in the darkness. Not because they are evil, but because Emergence Loves to be Private.

So I don’t ask anyone to have “empathy” for their neighbor. I’d ask them to try, on their better days, to be a neighbor, a part of a larger whole, and to think about what is needed for this precious organism called “society” to grow new forms and adapt. Because anything that is not allowed to adapt, anything that overly controls its ability to take feedback, anything that sends its immune system to monitor its own activity too much, becomes sick and dies.

Spying on our neighbors is a form of sickness. It’s a social autoimmune disorder. I’ll admit, it’s very profitable for a few people. And sometimes it does help find a stolen car. But I would rather trade my home for a society that cared to ask “why are people stealing cars?” than one that could track every hotwired Hyundai.

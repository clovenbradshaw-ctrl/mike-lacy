THEM

There’s a way some people rail about white supremacy as being the underlying evil behind all ills of capitalism, bigotry, and oppression that it can go too far. It so villifies white supremacist thinking that it leans towards glorifying the power of white identity.

Personally, I firmly have faith in people of all creeds and colors to, in the paraphrased words of Mary J. Blige, “do bad all by themselves.”

It’s almost like explicitly white supremacist thinking is on the same wavelength as seeing white supremacists or Western colonialialism behind all evils.

One says: “White men rule the world and all evil comes from that.”

And the dialectical opposite says: “White men should rule the world and all good comes from that.”

They both smack of oversimplification and a level of magical thinking, the type Homer encountered when the Stonecutters asked rhetorically: “♬ Who holds back the electric car? / ♬ Who makes Steve Gutenberg a star?♬”

However, in specific cases, we know that it was Western, imperialistic cognitive patterns, much more than our weapons exports, that led to horrific disasters.

The academic consensus on the Rwandan Genocide is that the Hutu/Tutsi distinction, which played a critical role in the deaths of approximately 800,000 to 1,000,000 people within about 100 days, was transformed into a rigid racial divide with genocidal implications primarily due to the actions of Belgian colonists in the early 20th century.2

Within only two generations, starting in 1919, the Belgians took a loose, pseudo-caste distinction and turned it into a firm racial boundary that divided the nation firmly in an Us vs Them divide. This division, which served their colonial interests (literally dividing and conquering) led to the most rapid and efficient genocide since the era of Ghengis Kahn.

But for a counter-example, we only need to cite Ghengis, the Great Khan, who existed half a millennium before the concept of “whiteness” was invented. Ruler of the Mongolian horde, he controlled the largest contiguous land empire in history and directly ended the lives of 40,000,000 people, and famously is the progenitor of least .5% of all living male humans.

It’s perhaps wrong to say his victims were “ethnically cleansed” because there wasn’t really a concept of ethnicity at that time (of types of people with some admixture of culture/genetic difference). Rather they had one thing wrong with them. They were “Them” rather than “Us.”

But mostly, our historical memory is only leveraged for brand caché, with historical landmarks being flipped into 2nd tier theatre venues that perpetually present Cirque du Soleil by-way-of Dave & Busters.

Twain said history didn’t repeat itself, but it does “often rhyme.”

What would Twain, who died before there was broadcast radio, think about history’s habits if knew of social media and the cultural microbiome it is generating? These mini worlds-within-worlds that have their own alternative histories.

What about when we butt up against people with belief systems, or rather, algorithmically curated worldviews, entirely different than us, living right next door? Or lying in bed next to us?

I quit social media for my mental health. I’ll provide Zuck & Elon with a doctor’s note if I need it. But even from the periphery of it, and increasingly in person in public spaces, I’m aware I’m operating in a different cosmos than those around me.

It feels as if we’re growing fully distinct cultures, colonizing our little spots on the Petrie disk like competing cybernetic mold specimens.

What can there possibly be in common between US and THEM?

It’s a strong belief of mine that every culture is asymptotically3 different from one another. That means there’s some difference between cultures that we can never fully account for, a uniqueness that makes the emigree to any place not their own forever a stranger in a strange place (even if only microscopically). The history of one culture won’t ever rhyme with another culture. But it may, if we squint at the point past infinity, see that they are just different musical modes or scales played on the same cosmic chord.

Two Perspectives on The Rule Of Law as a Concept

Francis Fukuyama: Fukuyama views the rule of law as a cornerstone of modern liberal democracies and a crucial element of what he describes as "getting to Denmark," a metaphor for stable, prosperous, democratic states with strong institutions. In his works, particularly in "The Origins of Political Order" and "Political Order and Political Decay," Fukuyama argues that a strong state, rule of law, and accountable government are fundamental to achieving political stability and development. He sees the rule of law as not merely the existence of laws but their impartial enforcement and the assurance that no one, not even leaders, is above the law. This framework supports predictable and enforceable contracts, which are essential for economic activity and investment, leading to broader societal prosperity.

Michel Foucault: Contrasting sharply with Fukuyama, Foucault's exploration of the rule of law isn’t centered around the virtues of legal frameworks but rather on how these frameworks serve as instruments of power and control. In works like "Discipline and Punish," Foucault examines how laws and institutions are tools used by those in power to maintain control and normalize social order through surveillance and discipline. Foucault would likely interpret the rule of law as a method for imposing specific norms and behaviors on society, contributing to what he calls "governmentality," or the art of governing beyond just state politics to include control over the self and others within societal norms.

The Rwandan genocide was significantly influenced by colonial ideas introduced by Belgian colonists. These colonial constructions of Hutu and Tutsi as distinct racial categories instilled a racist hatred of Tutsis among the Hutu population Luft (2023). The Belgian colonial policies, including the institutionalization of the Hutu/Tutsi divide as racial hatred through state apparatuses like censuses and identity papers, played a crucial role in shaping the identities and perceptions of the Rwandan population Nsabimana (2023). The colonial regime's emphasis on ethnic divisions and the sociocultural dominance of the Tutsi over the Hutu exacerbated ethnic rivalries and contributed to the genocide Holden (2021).

Moreover, the precolonial legacies and colonial policies in Rwanda created a state with an increasingly autocratic and unpopular government, which resorted to genocide as a last-ditch attempt at survival Hintjens (1999). The colonial history of Rwanda, marked by ethnic divisionism and the manipulation of identities, laid the groundwork for the violence that erupted in 1994 McMillan (2012). Mamdani highlights the issue of racism and the inculcation of racial consciousness as core features of both the colonial and post-colonial environments in Rwanda McNamee (2007).

The genocide in Rwanda was not merely a spontaneous outbreak of violence but rather a culmination of historical processes influenced by colonial legacies. The colonial constructions of ethnicity, racial hierarchies, and divisive policies created a fertile ground for the genocide to unfold, emphasizing the significant impact of colonial ideas on the tragic events in Rwanda.

A term from calculus, and about as much as I took away from calculus, it describes the nature of two line, down curving down towards the other. The one that’s curving is reaching closer and closer but as long as you keep measuring, it is never at the same point as the other line. But if we take a leap to that point infinitely small, beyond which we could ever measure, the lines join. It’s that post-rational leap that makes calculus possible and is perhaps is the type of transpersonal leap we need to make to solve our most intractable problems.

The political turmoil surrounding the reversal of a funding vote by the Arts Commission last July has tied the arts community and city government up in knots. As new policies and legislation are moving forward to untangle the city's arts funds and determine how they are used in the future, a history lesson in "how we got here" is crucial.

It's a long, winding story not simply about an institution in the Nashville arts community that has received an outside portion of public monies despite it's track record of financial mismanagement. It's also a lens into the values, personality types, priorities, and deal-making that have shaped the city as we know it.

The history of the Metro Arts Commission and the Nashville Symphony's financial troubles are inextricably linked. Public records show that the Symphony, twice near bankrupt in 25 years, was the intended, first, and primary recipient of Metro Arts funding. 

In the past year, artists, activists, politicians, and career bureaucrats have collided in ways that on the surface appear unprecedented, stemming from recent social trends towards concepts like “equity” and “anti-racism” and decisions made by various leaders over the past few years.

The truth is that this collision was set in motion from the very beginning of Metro Arts funding.

In its original form, the Metro Arts Commission mostly oversaw truly “public art” – murals, public festivals, and the beautification of public buildings and spaces. All that changed in 1988.

That year, the Symphony Board faced a tense contract negotiation with their Orchestra Union. It came the same fiscal season as a global financial crisis (the $1.71T shock to the stock market known as “Black Monday”) that dried up corporate donations. 

The 1988 season was gearing up to have a $900k shortfall according to the board, though Orchestra leaders thought this figure was exaggerated.

With the deficit seen by some as a negotiation tactic to lower the Orchestra Union’s demands, a Nashville Symphony board member drove a hard bargain: reduce the performer numbers and sign the contract, or the Symphony goes bankrupt.

The businessman that drove that bargain was Symphony board member, and soon-to-be Mayor, Phil Bredesen. He offered the union a carrot, a show of good faith from the city that would help make up for the fundraising shortfalls of the board and management.

The Orchestra union was lured back to the negotiating table with a promise that there was a new type of funding mechanism coming from the city which, in this first year alone, would add $250,000 to the Symphony’s coffers. 

Negotiated by Board Chair Martha Ingram, Mayor Bill Boner offered the symphony a quarter million dollars that would be distributed by the Metro Arts Commission in exchange for free public concerts. 

Dedicated arts funding of this size had never happened in Nashville, and it would change the financial realities of the arts forever.

There were immediate complications. Perhaps to not show favoritism or normalize municipal “bailouts”, Mayor Boner pledged an additional $250,000 be distributed to unspecified “other” arts organizations who could apply alongside the Symphony’s earmarked money. 

This likely came as a surprise to Arts Commission Executive Director Anne Brown who was not equipped for the drastic mission pivot of her department.

Brown told the Tennessean in 1988: “We are still dealing with a city that has never made arts funds available before… Basically, the Metro Arts Commission has very little money to operate. We have a staff of three with a budget from Metro of about $160,000,” and $50k of that budget was dedicated to the now defunct “Summer Lights Festival.” 

Metro Arts, as a funding institution, was not a well-conceived plan implemented with integrity and foresight. Instead, it emerged as a solution to a problem caused by insufficient fundraising efforts by salaried development executives and affluent board members of the Nashville Symphony. Nonetheless, Nashville was off to the races, and this new funding mechanism provided much-needed support for the city's artists.

By 1992, there was already discord between the large and small arts organizations about how these grants were being distributed. Phil Bredesen, now Mayor, had resigned from the Symphony Board. To date the Symphony had received a total of $650,972, a full 47% of what all arts organizations had gotten.

On May 21, 1992, The Tennessean published Alan Bostick’s commentary, entitled: “Showdown looming for arts funds?” It ominously led with a line that could have come from 2024: “A forum titled ‘Metro Funding for the Arts’ would not appear to be another way of saying ‘Blood Bath.’”

Bostick detailed how the meeting discussing grant figures devolved into a bickering match. “It came down to an old-fashioned stand-off between 'big' arts groups and 'little' ones, a contemporary version of David and Goliath.” 

He had an interesting take on the parable, however.

He proclaimed: “Goliath’s the good guy here.”

Bostick would later serve as Senior Communications Director for the Nashville Symphony from 2006 to 2010. 

Bostick’s acerbic recounting of the 1992 meeting records for us the moment when: “A group of local folk music artists became, well, perturbed after hearing a proposal that the ‘biggies’ like the Nashville Symphony and Tennessee Repertory Theatre should receive about 90% of the grant money, the rest being divided among smaller organizations.”

Bostick goes on to detail when “Steven Greil, the symphony's executive director” gave his take on the debate between funding the large organizations vs. the smaller ones. “The very existence of the smaller groups,” Greil told the distressed small arts org leaders, “largely depends on the health of the big ones.” 

The Symphony believed in a trickle-down, rising tide approach as it were.

The next dust-up was in 1998 when the Symphony, which to date had received $2,088,472, and 26% of all arts money ever allocated, went from being ranked #1 to number #2 on the Metro Arts grants list. 

The then and current Symphony Executive Director, Alan Valentine, appealed the ruling directly to the Arts Commission and Mayor, calling it “punitive” and called the reduction from $290k to $207 (both less than 5% of the Symphony’s annual budget) “devastating.”

Steven Greil, now the Executive Director at TPAC, also had his funding diminished and demanded the vote be voided. He alleged Arts Commissioners were being discriminatory.

"I think the commission is biased against groups that end the year in the black," Greil said. "Specifically, I think there's a bias against the symphony and TPAC."

To date, the two organizations have collectively received $2.1M in the nine years of grants, 32% of all Metro Arts funding.

Kevin Nance on the same page in the Tennessean gave his two cents for why there was such an extreme reaction to a relatively small change in grant size.

“The symphony's dismay over this year's grant is related, it seems clear, to matters of symbolism and hierarchy. The orchestra has always received the largest grant, and to be toppled from the summit (by the Tennessee Repertory Theatre, the year's biggest money winner) obviously came as a shock. The cosmology of Nashville's arts universe had suddenly shifted for a moment, leaving the orchestra in a state of organizational vertigo.”

Phil Bredesen, former Symphony board member and still Mayor, took the issue directly to the Executive Director of the Arts Commission, Tom Turk. 

Bredesen relayed the conversation to the Tennessean: “I told Tom that I had some concerns... I respected the process that the commission had gone through, but that I didn't agree with the outcome.”

Bredesen explained to the Executive Director of The Arts Commission that although this arts funding was technically being voted on by an independent, democratic commission, the commission was not actually meant to decide on the best use of these funds.

“That money,” Bredesen explained, “was originally put in the budget with the idea that the major arts groups would have a stable source of funding they could more or less count on year after year."

The following year the Symphony was back at #1 with $300,000 of Metro Arts money, nearly double the second-highest recipient.

As Phil Bredesen was becoming Governor of Tennessee, Kevin Crumbo was rising in the ranks of the city, becoming the Board Treasurer of the Nashville Symphony in 2003. That year was also the peak of Metro Arts’ investment in the Symphony, capping out at $500,000.

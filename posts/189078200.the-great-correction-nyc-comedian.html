One of my best friends in the world, Whitley Watson (On Substack as ), an NYC-based writer, filmmaker and comedian is also low key one of our era’s great philosophers (not kidding).

She is the funniest and smartest person I've ever met, and I'm forever sad we never got to do our live comedy show planned for March 12, 2020. Something came up, trying to remember what it was…

Whitley is one of the only people I’ve talked to in the past few years who’s been talking coherently about what happens after the catastrophic now, and she has a half-serious, fully plausible fantasy called “the Great Correction” about what may come next.

I hit her up Sunday night to riff on the “Great Correction”, the idea that systemic corruption and inequality have become so unsustainable that a genuine cultural and political shift is inevitable. Not a pendulum swing to the opposite extreme, but something new: more community-oriented, more participatory, more honest about what actually works.

From there we got into the cynicism trap on the left, where identifying that “they’re all corrupt” becomes an excuse to either disengage entirely or criticize those doing the real work of caretaking our neighbors. We talked about how this shows up in the arrested development of millennial politics, with boomers still gripping power while younger generations oscillate between disillusionment and the occasional spark of real agency (see the potentials placed onto Mayor Mamdani).

I got some shit off my chest about the consultant class being the modern priesthood — the McKinseyification of governance and how top-down hierarchical management empirically fails at managing shared resources, per Elinor Ostrom’s Nobel-winning research. The library doesn’t need to be profitable, and the subway doesn’t need to either!

The back half of the conversation hits on AI, convenience culture, learned helplessness, AI bots emailing AI bots to send spam to AI bots, and what it means to actually be present in your own life. Plus! Oligarchs as deeply unwell people crowd-surfed to the top precisely because of their sociopathic traits, the ketamine mind as the drug of our current political moment, and Robert Maxwell (as in Ghislaine Maxwell’s dad) as the unsung architect of financial strip-mining the world and the reason why everything you like has been enshittified.

Our convo from Sunday resonated a lot with a post I was forwarded yesterday.

Written during the waning days of COVID (oh THAT’S what happened with our comedy show) and already four (4!) calendar years old, it’s more relevant as ever. Remember when we referred to authoritarianism as “rising”?

And we couldn’t not end without talking about our reigning DGAF-kween Alysa Liu, and how her casual vibe of just wanting to share her art with the world is showing us all how to skate backwards into greatness by not giving a fuck about external validation.

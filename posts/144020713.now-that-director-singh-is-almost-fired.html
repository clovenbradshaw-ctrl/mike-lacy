Yesterday, the Metro Arts Commission

chose an interim Executive Director to oversee departmental activities, heading into a crucial budget cycle. With a department that has had its assets frozen since a July 2023 vote reduced funding for large arts institutions (and increased them for small and more diverse recipients categorized under the “Thrive” program), and an Executive Director on leave going for nine weeks — some leadership seems like a good idea.

Executive Director Singh is currently on simultaneous FMLA and Administrative leave. The Commission did not mince words, with an apparent majority of present Commissioners making it clear that their intention was to fire him at the soonest allowable Commission meeting.

His ouster has been described as retaliation for his successful efforts at implementing anti-racist policies that had not just pretty language, but financial realities. Others say it is because he “made promises to all the arts orgs he couldn’t keep” and the arts budget is awry. This author leans heavily towards the former, but this article isn’t about that. It’s about the content of the work he came here to do — challenge not just the language of how we describe arts funding, but who actually receives that funding.

The special commission meeting yesterday started less than an hour after receiving an email from Executive Director Singh’s lawyer, Jamie Hollin, Esq., which responded to two HR complaints filed against his client. Mr. Hollin’s letter included “context” he wanted to provide before proceeding to the formal response.

Nashville Arts historically operated as a proverbial washing machine. Taxpayer money was put in the proposed budgets by various administrations and appropriated by the Metro Council to Nashville Arts to flow to legacy institutions historically receiving taxpayer funding—according to the MHRC Report—over 71% of funding to Nashville Arts from Metro taxpayers went to institutions with annual operating budgets exceeding $1M. Until Director Singh was hired as Director and led the Commission to commit to equitable funding, no one in the Department of Finance cared much about the functions of the Arts. In other words, it was working as intended—taxpayer subsidies to wealthy institutions on the backs of others who were compelled to pay.

Jamie Hollin, Esq., Re: Summary response to report of Luther Wright on behalf Daniel Singh

It appears to be the final chapter for Director Singh and Metro Arts. His short tenure at Metro led to much well warranted attention to the inequitable funding practices of the Arts Commission in its third decade of grant making, but just as much attention was paid to critiques of his leadership style and the business practices (many dating back decades) of the commission he oversaw since mid 2022.

The candidate selected to replace him on an interim basis, decided upon after an extremely convoluted nomination process, is Dr. Paulette Coleman.

Dr. Coleman currently serves on the MDHA board, which she will need to step down from if she accepts the position.

Her qualifications include being one of the commissioners on the Arts Commission in 2014 when it first established the equity centric Thrive grant program. While some commissioners yesterday saw this experience as an asset to help shepherd the commission to fulfill its mission as an equity-centered arts granting organization, Commissioner Nefflen asked if being on the board when Thrive was developed created a conflict of interest for her.

Nefflens's objection to a connection with Thrive stemmed from a prior Commission meeting discussion on the legality of Metro Arts' practice of awarding individual artists direct funds, rather than providing grants to 501(c)(3). This direct funding requires a"delegated procurement" process which has been in place since 2014. The Commission was advised that this is actually not appropriate, a Metro Finance Department employee explained at a commission meeting last week.

This payment methodology, the Finance representative explained, is meant to be used for direct purchases for goods or services rather than for donations towards things that do not "belong" to the city, and Metro Arts should not have been doing this.

Reasonable minds can differ on the interpretation of delegated spending authority code, and if Metro Arts was acting outside their remit in doing this, but what is undebatable is that Metro Arts was doing this since 2014 and the advent of Thrive, but this only became an issue in recent months.

A proposal was discussed at the commission meeting last week to resolve the issue of direct appropriations to artists by leveraging a 3rd party non-profit to distribute these funds. It is unclear how this 3rd party would be held accountable to any of the equity standards that the Arts Commission developed in 2022 during a community grant review.

Skeptics of this plan identify this as a classic attempt to privatize public funds in the face of moves to redistribute funds from large organizations and intentionally puts them in the hands of smaller organizations with more diverse leaders and staff, and even individual artists.

Another non-standard practice of Metro Arts that has gone unnoticed was that Director Singh, and no commissioner before him going back to 2001, had submitted their grant criteria to the city council1 for approval. Highlighted first by the Metro Human Relations Commission2, this practice seems more relevant than anything brought by Metro Finance or Legal for resolving future issues of this nature.

The City Charter’s outlay of the Metro Arts Commission (which was amended by a 2015 state law) gives the Arts Commission final spending authority on funding recipients, but it retains oversight of its funding criteria by the City Council.

If the grant criteria for Metro Arts had been reviewed and approved by Council, as the City Charter stipulates, the controversial July 2023 "scenario" would almost certainly not have been overturned. The funding methodology, which would have outlined percentage funding to organizations of different sizes and criteria for selecting recipients, would have been pre-debated through official channels and not subject to simple overturning via an email correspondence from Metro Legal.

It is unclear why the Arts commission has been allowed to operate without bringing it's funding criteria to council for 23 years, including when it brought forth a net new category of funding through delegated procurement with the advent of Thrive in 2014.

Perhaps now that director Singh’s fate is all but sealed, and new leadership is on its way for Metro Arts, if but on an interim basis, we can have the discussion that everyone has done their best to avoid.

As Jesse Ross, Public Arts Coordinator for the Metro Arts Commission, commented on the scrutiny his Department has been under, which only began with the July vote of the Commission. He said yesterday in the commission meeting:

Before July, there was no complaints, no chaos. Not from the staff or anybody. After July, and after a huge conflict of interest and calls to the top on speed dial, chaos ensured…The problem with scapegoating is when you send the person out, the can that keeps getting kicked down the road, I don’t personally think this commission understands what equity is, what it looks like. It’s on the top of our agenda but we don’t talk about it. And how is this next person going to prioritize that? I know there’s different opinions. But for me, congratulations to Dr. Singh for actually for the first time in 30 years prioritizing a funding model that was not 100% to legacy organizations. It was going to artists and people on the ground.”

Jesse Ross, Public Arts Coordinator, Metro Arts Commission

There is, amongst certain influential figures in the city, unequivocally, an unwritten understanding of "who these funds are for" and the distributions of the July vote last year are NOT who they had in mind.

Be it for reasons of genuine concern for the financial well being of legacy art orgs, or for reasons of class or racial discrimination, or because as Will Cheek bluntly put it the large orgs were just “used to it”, or because of old school cronyism, or genuine differences in opinion of what "is" and "is not" art, or what the role of government is in distributing funds in ways that reverse historical harm — there is a Gordian knot of conflicting values surrounding the question: "who should get Arts funding?"

Reasonable minds can disagree about some of those conflicts. Some of those conflicts are unreasonable and should be paid no mind.

It takes a lot to sort through those issues. Parsing through those in public with grace, accountability, financial prudence, people management acumen, vision, and (perhaps most importantly for radically changing systems) disarming charm, is a tall order.

A leader in this space will have to take the job knowing their assignment from the powers that be will be to deconstruct the equity-based funding programs Singh implemented, but do so in the name of “equity” (a Shibboleth that in public spaces has lost all meaning).

But the real task of the Metro Arts leader (and this is a secret don’t tell anyone), will be to work on a transpersonal level to disarmingly lead Metro Officials and powerful arts organizations to compromise and see that their bizarro version of “equity” without equitable funding is not in their best interest either.

The real thing that would allow the large institutions to thrive is a vibrant art scene, with publicly supported independent artists creating a new vision for our city's artistic future, unmediated through the hands of middle managers at larger institutions.3 A thriving arts scene will drive interest in the arts generally and trickle up to increased revenues for the large arts institutions, with a higher ROI for the legacy orgs than just receiving the city funds.4

I hope the next Arts Commission Executive Director is up to this task.

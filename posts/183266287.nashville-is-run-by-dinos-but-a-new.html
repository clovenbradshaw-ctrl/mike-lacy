This weekend I wrote a piece about Nashville’s political landscape that was, as is my wont, quite didactic and hectoring. I scrapped it. It’s in my drafts because Metro Nashville Council Member Quin Evans Segall recently published something that crystallized what I was trying to articulate, with far more grace than I was able. She wrote:

Radical honesty in governance is the first step in making sure that we save this system of governance. Only then can we work together, or against one another, to try and capture attention, move ideas forward, and continue to mold and evolve our current system to be the perfectly imperfect system we crave.

Deep, abiding, and consistent radical honesty is my plea for 2026, both for me and also for all those working to move Nashville forward through this age of deep skepticism and within our attention economy. Honesty must be our group project, and we cannot afford any missing members. Trust in government only comes if everyone in our government can be trusted.

This is it exactly.

And yet, I think we all know that people rarely begin to provide radical honesty out of elegant exhortation. In politics, honesty arrives only when power is exercised in a way that makes it a requirement. Quinn’s call is necessary — but it won’t be sufficient until there are structural consequences for opacity. That’s the work that follows from naming the principle.

But naming it matters. I think we should consider the call for radical honesty not so much a naive West Wingean cry out to the angels of our better nature, but the benchmark of a performance improvement plan (PIP) for our mayoral administration. The point of a PIP is to set a reasonable goal that someone worthy of retaining their position should be able to attain. When assigning a PIP, you may or may not have confidence that a person can rise to occasion. But ethics, HR policies, and election cycles such as they are, it behooves us to let them make a good faith effort to perform their tasks at the level requisite for the position they have been hired for.

We’re putting it in writing now so that no one can later claim they didn’t know what the target was. Our leaders can rise to meet it or fail to meet it, but the standard is no longer ambiguous. Radical honesty isn’t an aspiration we hope they’ll stumble into. It’s the minimum expectation, clearly stated, against which they will be measured.

The anger I felt last year wasn’t really about bad decisions — politicians make those everywhere everyday. It was about the refusal to be honest about what those decisions were, who made them, and why they were made. And Quinn’s framing of what we should demand instead — radical honesty about limits, process, and accountability — feels like the right foundation for the work ahead.

What she named isn’t just good politics. It’s not just some touchy-feely HR nice to have that makes the workplace more palatable. It’s one of the seven pillars of Cooperative Power-Sharing required for sustaining democracy.

We cannot share power if we do not tell the truth about how it is used. And when people do not tell the truth about their power, the natural assumption is that it is because they do not want to share their power.

We’ll be exploring the other pillars this year, across different issues and from different angles. But I’m glad she surfaced this one so clearly, because it has to come first.

A Serious Matter That Was Unmoored from Serious Discourse

Last month a public fight erupted across Council meetings, headlines, and public comment periods about surveillance. What you saw from sources like me was lengthy analysis linking to source documents and a clear delineation between:

  • What we think we know

  • Why we think we know it

  • What we’re assuming

  • What we think should happen

I took getting the facts right seriously enough that I approached a national media outlet to see if they wanted to do a story on it. Instead of giving it to one of their staff writers, I was teamed with a national researcher on surveillance and we co-wrote an article that was professionally fact-checked and edited.

The other side of the debate did not engage on similar terms. What they projected was:

  • Their interpretation: “This is guardrails, you’re not against oversight, are you?”

  • Their authority: “Trust us [or else].”

  • Their intentions: “We are good people. So what we want is good. So voting against what we want is bad.”

When it comes to the surveillance MOU, I don’t think I was the only one who noticed a lack of candor from the local administration.

Truth cannot harm a democracy. But it can really burn when autocracy has been allowed to metastasize.

CM Evans Segall wrote the following on Substack on January 2, 2026. She did not directly cite the surveillance MOU, though it seems safe to say it was on her mind. The Mayor’s Office handed out “fact sheets” as she alludes to and her depiction of hand waving at serious matters aligns with the behavior of the Mayor’s Office last month.

Trust has to come before we can effectuate change, and we cannot assume that the change we seek to effectuate will always succeed. Instead, we have to establish trust before we achieve results. This will require a radical honesty.

We must start with every single part of a law or policy or process from the beginning and work through it wherever it is in a fully open way. It is not enough to say “here is my fact sheet and why I think this policy is good.” We must instead say “this is why we’re talking about it, how we got to this point, where we could have done things differently, and why we didn’t.” When we mess up, we have to say that. When we’re late to a problem, we have to cop to that. When we have a limitation that affects our desired result, we have to share that. When we don’t know, we have to admit that. When we succeed, we have to proclaim that. When we disagree, we have to say with specificity why we disagree.

This is what radical honesty actually looks like in practice: government modeling the pillar of Feedback and Truth Telling. It means being transparent about process, limits, and mistakes, not just outcomes. It’s the government’s part to play in a system that depends on shared truth.

Discussion of politics and government almost totally ignores the concept of training people to update their opinions in response to new evidence - i.e adapt to feedback.

Dominic Cummings
Former Chief Adviser to the Prime Minister of the United Kingdom

Information Warfare vs Collective Governance

I’ve gotten compliments from the other side of the aisle for the pro-privacy work I’ve been a part of. “You guys had a head start,” one person very much on the other team told me. “You hit the ground running with messaging. We were really on the back foot.”

Thanks. But that misses the point.

I turned to them and said: “No, I don’t think that’s why we’re winning. It’s because, based on all the evidence that’s been presented thus far, we are right. This is a bad deal, and Metro Council can get a better one. This isn’t about PR — it’s about what is true and what is not. But am I off base? What did I get wrong?”

I’m still waiting for an answer.

To many of our current leaders, politics is nothing but information warfare. There is no reality to be observed — only narratives to be managed. The facts are never given; they are always meant.

But reality? It has a way of reasserting itself. And this year, we’re going to work in terrain where narrative management cannot survive.

Over and over throughout 2025, I heard some version of “you shouldn’t be asking these questions” because “we need to be united,” or “these are just personality conflicts and ad hominem attacks.” But what I’ve been pointing to isn’t rhetorical or personal. It’s in black and white — often provided by the city itself through public records and public testimony.

Calls for unity are easy when they ask nothing of power. They become corrosive when they are used to dismiss evidence, flatten disagreement, or recast accountability as disloyalty.

What it feels like to be retaliated against

I think a shift is happening in Trump’s second term similar to his first. The #MeToo movement was a direct repudiation of the inability to hold Trump to account for his sexual improprieties. I believe that, perhaps in a less viral but similarly decentralized way, Trump’s blatant cronyism and autocratic “it’s my way, OR ELSE” approach is going to cause a political clap back that will fall evenly against Democrats and Republicans alike.

Just like how sexual misconduct was once so pervasive it seemed implausible that anyone could be held to account, so too does political retribution, cronyism, and financial misconduct seem so normalized that no one in power need worry about it. Even short of those extreme examples, the micro-aggressions of democratic opacity and anti-oversight behavior are becoming so Trump-coded that it will be increasingly hard to justify them as necessary or benign.

When I resigned from Mayor O’Connell’s Office, I sent an email to his Chief of Staff on November 17, 2023. I had a gut feeling that made me offer some unsolicited advice (a guilty habit of mine). I wrote:

Please be good to each other, and yourselves. I think spaces like this bring out the best and worst of us all — I know it has for me. If you’re new in this space, expect to encounter, in yourself and others, equal parts idealism and cynicism. Some of my proudest and lowest moments happened in this job. It’s going to get hard and ugly. Put in the work on building positive relationships early so you have the solidarity backlogged… If you build trust (and don’t confuse building trust for building “loyalty”), there’s nothing you folks can’t do.

Thanks for everything,
Mike.

About a year later, the person I sent that resignation message to told my employer at a non-profit to terminate my contract because of things I was writing about Metro that she didn’t like. I don’t know that I’ve fully processed what kind of violation that is — of my personhood, of my sense of safety, of what it means to participate honestly in a democracy. A democracy run by Democrats, no less. On some level, I’ve just brushed it aside. Eh. That’s just part of the game.

Video of my public comment where I discussed for the first time the retaliation I received for critiquing the O’Connell administration.

I know this happened because my then employer called and told me right after it happened. And because the Mayor later apologized to me in person for it, and said that his chief of staff was told she was “never to do that again,” as if just the one time was okay. As if this was the only time something like that had happened. It was really surreal. It still is.

Since then, I’ve reflected back to the retaliation I faced for my early blogging — writing that was far less incisive and never even targeted at our dear mayor. If I’m honest, what’s surprised me most is my own reaction to being retaliated against. There was a part of me that shrugged and thought, yeah, that tracks. I had a “I probably had this coming” feeling that seems unhealthy in hindsight — like I don’t expect better treatment for myself, or worse, that I’ve come to expect so little of our Democratic leaders that retaliation feels like a normal cost of telling the truth.

I’ve caught myself thinking that I got off easy. How dare I think I can blog without consequence?

It would be one thing if I was spreading lies and character assassination pieces. But if you’re reading this far, I hope you know that isn’t my M.O.

I’m sure, in some twisted way, that the story of me being retaliated against has been used to discredit my work.

Well, we tried to get him fired for the way he exercised his First Amendment rights, so clearly all the stuff he’s saying about us acting unethically must be unfounded.

If they’re saying that, I don’t think that’s the defense they think it is.

For one thing, public record shows me voicing my concerns about the O’Connell administration long before I was punished for speaking up. I’m not one to hold a grudge, not out of Christian indifference, but more out of neglect for my own well being. I’m much more apt to speak up in fear of future regret, regret of not saying something that could help others avoid harm. This is its own form of self-protection that isn’t necessarily noble. It’s protection of my own ego, and ego that wants to see itself as someone who is doing their best to help others at any cost.

As far back as February 2024 I tried to help the current administration course correct with public commentary. It didn’t seem to work.

What worries me most isn’t what happened to me. I have a strange pluckiness that helps me land on my feet. But in the course of doing this work, I’ve come across many people who have infinitely more harrowing stories than me. People who’ve suffered consequences that rebounded on themselves and their families — all paying the price for telling the truth at the hands of various people in positions of power over them.

There are rules and at times even laws against this type of behavior, but so often the people who’d be responsible for investigating these types of things report to the people who would be investigated — and so retaliation goes unaddressed, built into the fabric of our failing democracy, with truth always being the first and foremost victim.

As I’ve come to terms with how I’ve been retaliated against for speaking the truth as best I knew it (and still waiting on any fact check for where I errored), it alarms me how readily calls for “unity” are used to excuse retaliatory behavior that should be disqualifying from public office. If this is just “the game,” then the game is broken. And it is for the people playing it to change the rules.

And that’s why radical honesty isn’t a luxury. It’s not tone policing or personal preference. It’s one of the structural conditions a democracy needs to remain a democracy at all.

What Happens When You Lose Truth Telling

When honesty and truth-telling break down, democracy doesn’t collapse overnight. It degrades.

The first thing to go is shared reality. Decisions stop being argued on their merits and start being defended as narratives. Evidence no longer constrains power; it becomes something to be managed, selectively cited, or ignored. Process turns into theater.

This degradation doesn’t always look the same. What we’re seeing in Washington right now is an overt attempt to concentrate power around a single figure — a personalist assault on constitutional norms. What we see in places like Nashville is different, and far more familiar: the slower, quieter erosion that comes from treating democracy as a management problem rather than a political one.

Once shared reality fractures, accountability becomes impossible. If there is no agreed-upon truth, there is nothing to be held accountable to. Leaders are judged on whether their stories survive, not whether they are accurate. Errors are never admitted, because admitting error would require acknowledging an external standard of truth.

In this environment, power doesn’t so much seize control as abstract itself. Through the influence of corporate consultants, the financial class, and well-funded philanthropic intermediaries — Bloomberg Philanthropies being a prominent example that embeds corporate leaders directly into government to corporatize their operations — democratic institutions are steadily reframed as professionalized management offices tasked with “removing the politics from government.” Decisions are cast as technical. Outcomes are treated as inevitable. Disagreement is recoded as inefficiency.

One of the predictable consequences of this shift is the nullification of dissent through structure. Boards and commissions are consolidated, sidelined, stripped of authority, or bypassed entirely. Public participation is reframed as a liability — something that could invite populist racism, capture, or disorder — rather than as a democratic safeguard. In trying to preempt hypothetical threats, institutions remove the very mechanisms that surface real ones, leaving government increasingly blind to the public’s lived reality.

In a city like this, the leaders who flourish are skilled managers of ambiguity. They speak in partial truths, deferred explanations, and process-heavy justifications that are difficult to contest but impossible to interrogate. Because they avoid overt confrontation, they are often described as pragmatic, even responsible. But what distinguishes them most is their ability to operate without meaningful feedback—insulated from public consequence, shielded by process, and rewarded not for telling the truth, but for keeping the machinery running smoothly no matter how disconnected it becomes from the people it governs.

Without trust, power hardens. Consent gives way to compliance. Scrutiny is recast as hostility. Dissent becomes disloyalty. Calls for “unity” are used not to bring people together, but to silence those who insist on asking questions.

In systems like this, truth-tellers become liabilities. People who surface facts are labeled divisive. People who document reality are accused of bad faith. The lesson is subtle but clear: honesty is risky, silence is safe.

Over time, people adjust. Expectations drop. Retaliation feels normal. Cynicism replaces engagement — not because people don’t care, but because caring without honesty is exhausting.

A democracy can survive bad decisions. It can survive conflict. These are, in fact, built into the hardware of democracy. What it cannot survive is the replacement of truth with narrative and accountability with loyalty.

That’s why radical honesty isn’t a style choice. It’s a structural requirement.

A Bitter Medicine — for now

I know, as CM Segall clearly does, that truth-telling is an essential part of a healthy democracy. Last year, I was called “fake news,” “misinformation,” and accused of sowing division where there was none. I didn’t mind it, too much. I saw it for what it was: the swarming of political antibodies in an organism that has learned to treat dissent as danger, not as diagnostic information.

Truth is a bitter medicine. When it arrives late — after years of avoidance and insulation — it can feel harsh, even brutal, like emergency treatment rather than routine care. It hurts because the system refused to listen earlier. But the pain isn’t evidence that truth is destructive; it’s evidence the medicine is working.

Truth cannot harm a democracy. But it can really burn when autocracy has been allowed to metastasize.

In healthy bodies, cells are always growing beyond their proper boundaries, and most of the time we never notice — because feedback is constant and integrated, regulating growth back into balance before it becomes disease. Like us, the body politic is never technically “cancer free”. Power, like capital, has a tendency to accumulate. What matters is whether the system has built-in ways to notice and correct that accumulation early.

A healthy democracy doesn’t experience truth as an external assault. It absorbs it naturally. Honest feedback is not managed, sanitized, or punished; it shapes decisions. Successes and shared joys are part of that healing process, not as distractions from truth, but as proof that honesty leads somewhere better.

But when feedback is rejected — when dissent is treated as pathology rather than signal — truth can only arrive from the outside, late and forcefully. Until institutions learn to integrate honest feedback into how they function, there will always be a need for people willing to administer the medicine anyway. Not because we enjoy the discomfort, but because pretending the illness isn't there guarantees it worsens — and unfortunately elegant exhortation rarely makes anyone honest who isn’t already. Consequences do.

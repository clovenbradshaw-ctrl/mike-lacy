I’ve been trying to articulate why the Metro Arts quagmire has hooked me so hard. There’s a few obvious things that are clearly causing the equations: Metro Arts Scandal = flame, me = moth.

Tennessee is seemingly in a race with Florida and Alabama to see who will have more real estate in the future textbook chapters on the fall of democracy.

Yes, I’m a big proponent of giving public funding to the organizations most connected to the communities that need the most support. And while I love shrieking Froy-deh shurn-er Guh-tter-funken back at Symphony Choir during Beethoven’s 9th, the fact they have white-tie galas as fundraising opportunities puts them not on the top of my most-in-need Metro Arts March Madness Bracket.

And yes, I think bringing an anti-racism lens to all our institutions is not only a good idea, but inevitable. I believe our country, or whatever reboot of America we have in this longitude and latitude by the time we allow ourselves to be a bit more chill, will eventually embrace anti-racist policies — it’s just a matter of how long we want to weigh down the arc of history by stacking copies of Atlas Shrugged and The Turner Diaries atop it.

I think it’s got a more visceral, and yet boring seeming draw to me. The city just didn’t follow the rules, and that goddamn bugs me.

In July 2023, a legal, “small d” democratic vote gave more funding to small arts organizations than to large organizations, for the first time ever. But after the single commissioner who voted against this signaled his dissatisfaction to the Metro Department of Law Director, the Department of Law said they had to reverse their vote on constitutional grounds.

And that’s just not how the rules work. And by rules, I mean the City Charter of our fair city, and the small d democratic norms that hold our local democracy together. The Department of Law has no authority to tell any commission how they must vote, even if their vote was unconstitutional (which outside lawyers are saying it wasn’t).

We’re a city that is under perpetual attack by undemocratic norms by the State Government that is cheese grating away the autonomy of local governance, something that has caught the eyes of the world, it’s a bad look to to have the very leaders of the #resistance pull the same moves as this authoritarianism they’re allegedly resisting.

And it’s not just Metro Law that is doing democratic process dirty. The Finance Department has also been leaving the charter on “read”, withholding funds from all arts organizations until if and when the Finance Director is satisfied by the staffing and grant making decisions of the Metro Arts Department. The Finance Director does have particular financial oversight power over Metro Arts, and all Metro Departments — but these ain’t it.

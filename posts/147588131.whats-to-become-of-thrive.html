The Metro Arts Commission casually announced on Tuesday that it would not be allocating “Thrive” funding this fiscal year — waiting until at least September 2025 to revisit the funding pool that has given arts diverse arts organizations and individuals arts roughly $1.6M in its decade of existence.

Metro may have some very real advantages in using a 3rd party non-profit to cut checks to individual artists. But it should not abdicate its responsibility to have a transparent, democratic process of allocating those funds.

The result is a disheartening, if not fully unexpected, result for supporters of the 10-year-old Metro program designed to increase equitable access to government funds for the arts. Supporters of the program have perceived institutional support for the program waning in the wake of conflagrations around the allocation of FY24’s funding by The Metro Arts Commission.

The announcement came the same week as the reconciliation agreement was signed between Metro and artists defunded in 2024, filed with the Metro Human Relations Commission working under Title VI of the Civil Rights Act. A group representing affected artists, Arts Equity Nashville, announced the finalization of an agreement in an email on Friday.

If we don’t change those policies, rules, laws and norms, our criticisms haven’t redesigned anything – and more often than not, once the dust settles from our feuding and cajoling, things stay the same – or get even worse.

The artists in the Title VI complaint had their funding cut in half last year when Metro Legal ruled that an initial allocation that prioritized this diverse pool over established art institutions was “unconstitutional.”

Metro Legal ruled that the initial funding vote, that took into consideration the racial impact of different funding models, opened Metro up to potential litigation and had to be revised. The intentionally equitable funding, Metro Legal ruled, was newly “unconstitutional” under a 2024 Supreme Court of the United States (SCOTUS) ruling regarding Affirmative Action in higher education. 

This opinion was refuted by outside Council hired by the Metro human relations commission which also gave the opinion that the reversal of this equitable vote itself was potentially a federal Civil Rights Title VI violation.

It’s unclear how many steps forward and backward have been taken for progressive arts funding in the city, a year into this mess.

One metric does seem clear — despite the work of all the activists, artists, public comments, bloggers, and elected officials, it seems that the Metro Arts’ funding for intentionally diverse, independent artists in FY2025 will be $0. At least that is $0 from Metro Arts itself. 

Speculation amongst activists is that funding for diverse and individual artists may continue from Metro, but be “privatized” in some way moving forward – potentially with a 3rd party engaged to allocate funds. The term “privatization” has come up a lot amongst Metro Arts watchers, a concept I discussed at length in a previous post.

One thing that is clear, by using a 3rd party non-profit, individual artists could be funded with no reasonable objection – there is no restriction against a non-profit grant recipient passing along funds to individuals under 1099 contracts. The funding of individual artists under Thrive has come under scrutiny, a methodology that began under Thrive’s creator, former Metro Arts E.D. Jen Cole – though Metro Finance and Legal have recently ruled that this procurement-based allocation to individual artists is improper and must stop.

This possibility of “privatizing” diversity-focused arts funding has raised very valid concerns, and questions of why it’s necessary, pointing back to the ten years of successful payments to individual artists. “Can’t Metro Finance just reconsider their interpretation of Metro Code, or tweak the arrangements with artists so Metro Nashville is in compliance?”

Global anxiety over this type of “privatization” and skepticism over unregulated Public/Private partnerships in general has been percolating for decades. 

Leaders who see using a private 3rd party as a future for programs like Thrive need to be aware of very warranted anxieties that exist about taking public funds and placing into organizations that are less accountable in how they are spent (and the routes to maintaining oversight that can be in place to alleviate these concerns to some degree).

In a past article, I wrote:

Chiara Cordelli in The Boston Review writes: “If the twentieth century was the age of the bureaucratization of the modern state… the twenty-first century has been the age of its privatization.”

The impact of Neoliberalism and the privatization of global governments is stark, Cordelli writes, bringing:

“a renewed feudal order within which political power is increasingly exercised on the basis of privately negotiated obligations, nonpublic purposes, and, ultimately, unilateral determinations.”

Cordelli is saying that without the public’s real awareness or consent, the power structure we live under has been fundamentally changed since the 1980s.

A thing I point out is that “privatization” is not one thing, there is a spectrum of privatization, and checks and balances that can be applied to prevent it from being undemocratic, unregulated, and an obfuscation of maintaining the status quo dressed up as “progress”. 

Broadly speaking, the government using any 3rd party vendors is to some degree “privatization.”

Narrowly speaking, privatization occurs when the government transfers “ownership” of a service or utility that was once public and generates revenue from it.

I’ve argued in the past that the transfer of Metro Arts monies (that once had been used to put on art events for the public) to non-profit organizations like the Nashville Symphony was a form of privatization. 

This isn’t a traditional interpretation of “privatization” since organizations like The Nashville Symphony don’t “own” the general domain of arts provisioning – but there needs to be a way of acknowledging that this is a transfer away from the government previously performing the task of providing culture to the public.

Prior to 1988, Metro itself used Metro Arts funds to provide art experiences to the public with events like the revenue-generating Summer Lights Festival. Now, without stipulations on the use of general operating grants, organizations like the Nashville Symphony can use public monies to pay staff, put on events, or pay for fundraising and gain revenue. This is a different type of investment in the arts than directly putting on festivals for public benefit, and I think it’s appropriate to call this a form of privatization, though not terribly far on the spectrum of it compared to using private prisons, selling off water rights, or strip mining the public school system.

But applying this logic holds that arts funding for diverse recipients would also be a form of privatization. While smaller grants tend to, interestingly, have much more restrictions on how they may be used, this is public money going to non-public entities to create art. 

But I’m fine with that conclusion, that all arts funding moved outside the government is a mild form of privatization, because I see this as a broad spectrum rather than a sharp binary. What’s important to focus on, in my opinion, is not merely who is profiting in these arrangements, but who has the decision-making authority of where and how funds are used.

As a side note, what would “non-privatized” arts funding look like by my definition? It would be the government hiring artists directly to create art and beautification projects, as it did under FDR with the WPA’s Federal Art Project.

It’s worth noting how limited our imagination is these days about how the government can improves our lives and make them more beautiful. And how much we all, across the political spectrum, have been trained to view the government with distrust, and be eager for our tax dollars to get back into the private marketplace — where its uses cannot be democratically accounted for.

Our political and economic systems have been redesigned under our noses over the last 45 years and many of our preconceptions of our institutions have been shaped by this redesign. The major throughline of that redesign is to strip the democratically accountable government of its ability to decide where and how funds are used, field by the religious belief that the invisible hand of the market will make better choices.

And simultaneously, the culture of critique and activism has been polarized and “purity-pilled.” There's such animosity within progressive movements, both towards those perceived to be outside and inside their movements, that nuanced analysis and strategic tweaking of our systems, seem incredibly difficult, if not impossible to implement. Progressives have been trained to have a “learned helplessness” that creates a feedback loop to further villainess their opponents, and too often, even their allies.

It's hard to build a coalition of the willing when you've been incentivized to tear down all potential teammates.

I can see a critic saying that by showing nuance in my understanding of “privatization”, and expanding its definition to include art grants, I am defanging a dirty word, perhaps giving license, intentionally or not, to inequitable practices. I am “equating” inherently good practices (arts funding) with inherently evil ones (public resource privatization) by placing them on a spectrum, and for that I should be cancelled. But I don’t think that reaction is on the positive end of the helpfulness spectrum.

I think that the hard thing to realize in our lives is that all binaries, public vs private, good vs evil, male vs female, individual vs collective, really only exist in our minds. The world is made of infinite intersecting spectrums and relationships, and the best we can do is tweak the rules of how they intersect. Labelling people as good vs evil in this fight for a nuanced redesign of how we live together is on the negative end of the helpfulness spectrum.

I think practically speaking, we’re always more empowered when we bring more nuance and precision to our critiques. This allows us to create more effective guard rails against what we don’t want to see, and more agency to get more of what we want.

I believe, but I could be wrong, that what art equity activists in Nashville most strongly don’t want to see is: an undemocratic process of allocating tax dollars to arts organizations in the city by a non-government entity that is not accountable to taxpayers. That, I would argue, would be a form of art funding privatization too far along the spectrum of privatization.

Metro may have some very real advantages in using a 3rd party non-profit to cut checks to individual artists. But it should not abdicate its responsibility to have a transparent, democratic process of allocating those funds.

The art world is already full of black-box art grants that lack transparency and accountability. The entire purpose of public funding is that it is the public democratically allocating its resources based on expertise, best practices, and public benefit.

As I discussed with the Mayor in an email a few months ago, the systemic entity in Nashville that likely has the most power to ensure this arrangement is democratic, the entity that has control over both Metro Code (which dictates the purpose and powers of the Metro Arts Commission), is the Metro City Council.

The Metro Code is quite clear on the powers of the Council to determine how this Commission spends its funds.

[The Metro Arts Commission shall:] Award funds appropriated to it by the metropolitan council to deserving nonprofit civic and nonprofit charitable organizations. Criteria for the awarding of such funds shall be established by the Metropolitan Nashville Arts Commission and approved by resolution of the metropolitan council.

2.112.040 - [Metro Arts Commission] Powers and duties.

The Metro Council has every ability to pass resolutions that would, for example, designate what percentage of funding each year goes to what types of organizations, and if funding were to be transferred to a 3rd party for allocation, what role the Metro Arts Commission would maintain in determining who receives that funding.

Again, “privatization” is not one thing. If Metro honestly believes it does not have the legal right to cut checks to individual artists, and that the last four Mayors and prior Legal and Finance Directors (who include the same ones in those roles now) were in violation of the law in doing that — that’s fine.

But it doesn’t mean Metro has to give up determination on who receives that funding, allowing these decisions fall into a black box of undemocratic accountability.

Metro Council can pass resolutions that oversee the allocation levels of Metro Arts recipient categories and the process of allocating their funds. It could look like this:

CITY OF NASHVILLE RESOLUTION NO. [2025X]

A RESOLUTION OF THE METRO COUNCIL OF NASHVILLE AND DAVIDSON COUNTY, TENNESSEE, ESTABLISHING FUNDING ALLOCATIONS FOR METRO ARTS FOR THE FISCAL YEAR 2025.

WHEREAS, the Metro Arts Commission plays a critical role in fostering the growth and sustainability of the arts within Nashville and Davidson County; and

WHEREAS, it is necessary to allocate funding in a manner that supports a diverse range of arts organizations and individual artists across the community; and

WHEREAS, the Metro Arts Commission has developed a funding strategy to ensure that public funds are distributed equitably and effectively;

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED BY THE METRO COUNCIL OF NASHVILLE AND DAVIDSON COUNTY:

Allocation of Funds:

  • For Fiscal Year 2025, Metro Arts shall allocate its General Funds as follows:

    • X% of funding shall be allocated to organizations with an annual budget exceeding $3 million.

    • X% of funding shall be allocated to organizations with a primary focus on performance art.

X% of funding shall be allocated to the Thrive program, with parameters as defined in the [attached document/link].

Payment to Individual Artists:

  • Funds designated for individual artists shall be disbursed via a third-party 501(c)(3) organization acting as a fiscal sponsor.

  • The fiscal sponsor shall charge no more than 1% for processing invoices on behalf of Metro Arts.

  • The Metro Arts Commission shall retain all authority over the allocation of these funds.

  • The 1% overhead charge shall not be deducted from the funding allocated to Thrive program recipients.

Contingency Provisions:

  • In the event of a budget shortfall or windfall for the department, the specified funding percentages shall be maintained.

  • While maintaining the approved strategic allocations, the Metro Arts Commission shall have the autonomy to make tactical decisions regarding which recipients within these categories receive funding.

BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that this resolution shall take effect immediately upon its adoption, the welfare of The Metropolitan Government of Nashville and Davidson County requiring it.

As Antoinette Carroll of Creative Reaction Lab says: "Like all systems, systems of oppression, inequality, and inequity are by design. Therefore, they can be redesigned."

But redesigning of public systems doesn’t end with critiquing them, it only starts there.

Systems are built on the interplay of people, programs, agencies, and organizations, and that interplay is governed by policies, rules, laws and norms.

If we don’t change those policies, rules, laws and norms, our criticisms haven’t redesigned anything – and more often than not, once the dust settles from our feuding and cajoling, things stay the same – or get even worse.

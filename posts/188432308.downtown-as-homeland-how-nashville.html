On a January night at Metro Council, as members debated whether to accept policing and surveillance equipment tied to a $15 million state grant being sourced through the Nashville Downtown Partnership, Council Member Jordan Huffman offered a blunt warning:

“We are one incident away from having the money turn off in Nashville.”

That debate is now over. The equipment has been accepted. The city has taken the gear. The Mayor’s office is no longer trying to obligate the Downtown Partnership to anything in exchange, and the questions that were briefly raised—about procurement, oversight, long-term costs, civil liberties, and what exactly this infrastructure is being built to do—have been, its boosters hope, put to bed.

What, if anything, all this had to do with the outstanding question of liability for the library parking garage that unexpectedly exploded last year while under contractual management by the Nashville Downtown Partnership, is anyone’s guess.

But Huffman’s line remains. It remains because it was not a throwaway comment or a moment of candor that slipped out by accident. It was an unusually clear statement of the governing logic that has increasingly shaped Nashville’s decisions about downtown: the point is not merely to prevent harm, but to prevent disruption. Not primarily to protect residents, but to protect revenue. Not to treat downtown as a neighborhood, but to treat it as an economic engine whose uninterrupted operation must be guaranteed.

That sentence clarifies what this entire debate was really about. It clarifies was formerly only subtext: many Tennessee leaders are not primarily concerned with safety in the broad sense—housing stability, public health, mental illness, addiction treatment, neighborhood infrastructure, or community well-being. They are concerned with protecting the economic conditions that make the state's most profitable district function smoothly.

They are concerned, first and foremost, with protecting confidence, protecting investment, protecting the perception of financial stability, and protecting the flow of money through a downtown that has been transformed into a high-yield asset. And there seems to be no cost to public safety from credible threats of state violence that they are unwilling to pay to do it.

Mayor O’Connell offered his own version of this logic while defending expanded surveillance downtown:

“We’re continuing to solicit feedback… but we want to make sure that everyone understands the context of how different the downtown safety environment is from other parts of the city.”

Downtown is not treated as a neighborhood. It is treated as an asset: dense with tourism, hotel taxes, convention bookings, alcohol sales, property value, and corporate investment. When the Mayor insisted downtown was “different,” he was not describing crime patterns. He was describing capital. Downtown was “different” because disruption there threatened the city’s most valuable economic district. It was “different” because it is where Nashville’s reputation is monetized, where its brand is manufactured, and where its future development model is most concentrated. It was “different” because instability there is not merely inconvenient. It is costly.

Of course, defenders of the surveillance expansion can point to facts that are not imaginary. Downtown does have uniquely dense foot traffic. It hosts large-scale events. It concentrates nightlife, alcohol, and tourist crowds in a way most neighborhoods do not. A violent incident on Broadway has ripple effects that reach beyond the people immediately harmed: it becomes national news, it threatens convention bookings, it alarms investors, and it shapes the city’s reputation. In that sense, officials are not wrong to argue that downtown carries a different risk profile.

But that reality does not resolve the deeper question. It sharpens it. The issue is not whether downtown has safety needs. The issue is why those needs generate rapid political urgency and millions in available funding, while the slow violence of housing instability, unaffordable healthcare, underfunded schools, and neighborhood disinvestment is treated as background noise. Downtown’s “different” status is not simply about public safety. It is about what kinds of danger the city treats as unacceptable: not the everyday harms that shorten lives quietly, but the visible disruptions that threaten economic confidence and revenue flow.

An overly simplistic narrative says this is about appeasing “downtown interests”—the businesses on Broadway, the bar owners, the hotel groups. That story is not entirely false. Those constituencies have access, they have money, and they have political leverage. But it misses the deeper point. It treats downtown power as a matter of individual personalities, individual establishments, and local business operators, when the reality is far more abstract and far more entrenched.

Recent comments have made clear that when we talk about local leadership’s commitment to “business interests,” it is not really local business leaders at all. The commitment is to the abstract power they are beholden to: the financial institutions that often have even regional elites over a barrel, for whom the worldview of “the market knows best” is the iron clad law of the jungle.

Downtown is not defended because of affection for particular businesses or any sort of local pride. It is defended because it is the part of the city most legible to outside capital, the part most integrated into national investment networks, and the part whose stability is most directly tied to the city’s ability to attract large-scale development deals, corporate relocations, and the constant churn of tourism dollars.

It is the part of the city that, if you fuck up, you’re liable to be excluded from the secret boys club of deal makers that most politicians hope to retire into after their tour of duty in public service.

To the financial masters of the universe, the operators of downtown establishments are interchangeable.

In booming districts, even property owners are often embedded in a larger financial structure. Debt, investment expectations, and rising valuations can leave them constrained by capital markets that reward growth and punish stagnation.

They can be swapped out. If one bar closes, another opens. If one brand becomes unprofitable, another replaces it. If one local owner cannot survive a tax hike, a national chain can.

This structure does not incentivize long-term community or cultural depth. It incentivizes churn. It incentivizes homogenization. It rewards whatever is most scalable, most profitable, most insurable, and most easily replicated. It breeds disposable places: districts engineered for throughput rather than belonging, consumption rather than community, and investor confidence rather than resident stability.

What is being defended is not a brand name, a culture, or even a class of people. It is an asset class. And in the language of finance, assets must be “secured” — stabilized, insured, and protected against volatility.

For financial instruments, that means hedging against risk, diversifying exposure, insulating revenue streams, and minimizing uncertainty.

For a business improvement district, it means surveillance infrastructure, rapid-response policing, behavioral regulation, and the removal of anything that introduces visible instability — whether that instability is crime, protest, poverty, or simply unpredictability.

In both cases, the objective is the same: reduce volatility to preserve value.

This logic did not originate in Nashville. It is what happens when decades of neo-liberal “financialzation” meets with the national security apparatus, and we are watching it unfold in real time in Tennessee.

Over the past two decades, cities across the country were promised economic windfalls from hosting NFL drafts, conventions, and championships. In exchange, they were expected to harden their security infrastructure: surveillance networks, integrated camera systems, command centers, mobile tactical units, real-time data platforms. The spectacle came and went. The infrastructure remained. And that infrastructure was never neutral. It was never simply “safety equipment.” It was the physical embodiment of a new governing logic that treats public space as a managed environment, treats human behavior as something to be monitored, and treats instability as a threat to economic continuity.

Surveillance is never a one-time purchase. It is a permanent operating expense. Cameras require storage, maintenance, upgrades, and staffing. Data requires analysts. Analysts generate enforcement. Enforcement generates arrests, court processing, and jail bookings. Each step feeds an expanding criminal legal system that must continually justify its scale. Surveillance does not simply observe. It produces a pipeline. It creates a constant stream of incidents, classifications, and actionable targets that can be used to justify more funding, more staffing, more technology, and more authority. The city builds the infrastructure, and then the infrastructure demands to be used. It is not a passive tool. It is an engine that generates its own necessity.

In Nashville, that downstream system was never abstract. CoreCivic—one of the largest private prison operators in the United States—was headquartered here. Incarceration was not merely a policy outcome; it was part of the city’s corporate landscape. When surveillance expanded, it did so in a place where confinement itself intersected with economic power. This does not require a conspiracy. It requires incentives. It requires the alignment of political convenience with economic interests. It requires a governing culture that treats policing and confinement as normal administrative functions rather than extraordinary acts of state violence.

After 9/11, federal doctrine formally fused economic continuity with national security. “Critical infrastructure” came to include not only systems whose disruption would threaten lives, but systems whose disruption would threaten economic stability. Tennessee adopted that framing. The state’s Office of Homeland Security explicitly included protecting “property and the economy” within its mission. That language mattered because it reframed volatility as threat. It elevated revenue streams into protected infrastructure. Once economic stability became a security objective, protecting a downtown revenue district was no longer ordinary municipal governance. It was cast as homeland defense.

And that shift is not merely rhetorical. It changes how leaders interpret reality. It changes what gets funded. It changes what becomes urgent. It changes what gets treated as “unacceptable.” A city that frames its economy as critical infrastructure begins to treat dissenting views as sabotage. It begins to treat protest for social justice as instability. It begins to treat visible poverty as a security threat. It begins to treat public space as something to manage rather than something to share. The result is a city whose official language becomes saturated with the vocabulary of mitigation, resilience, preparedness, and precision policing—terms that sound benign, even responsible, but which increasingly function as a moral alibi for expanding surveillance and enforcement capacity.

In 2025, the federal government extended this trajectory through National Security Presidential Memorandum-7 (NSPM-7), directing agencies to coordinate efforts to counter domestic terrorism and organized political violence, including tracing networks and financial support structures. Civil liberties advocates, including the ACLU, warned that the memorandum’s broad language risked sweeping nonprofits, activists, donors, and lawful political advocacy into a domestic security framework. When economic continuity and political dissent are both pulled into a security paradigm, disruption becomes crisis. Protest becomes risk. Democratic debate becomes something to manage rather than something to welcome. And when dissent is framed as a security risk rather than a democratic right, the expansion of surveillance stops being an emergency measure and becomes a permanent administrative function.

This is the policy environment in which Nashville’s surveillance expansion is taking shape. But the deeper reality is simpler. Surveillance is not primarily about safety. Surveillance is about risk management.

Risk for whom?

You may have come across the phrase “risk management” in this Substack and elsewhere while reading about the expanding ecosystem of surveillance, policing, and private security. It shows up in the names of a surprising number of organizations that seem, at first glance, to have very little in common. One of the most visible examples in Nashville is Solaren Risk Management, the private security vendor that helps enforce the Nashville Downtown Partnership’s control over Lower Broadway, including by harassing and arresting unhoused people.

It would be easy to assume the word “risk” here refers to risky behavior in the ordinary sense—preventing fights, keeping intoxicated tourists from falling off balconies, making sure the stairs aren’t slippery, or ensuring nobody gets food poisoning from undercooked fish. But that is not what “risk” means in this context. The term is almost certainly chosen because it resonates with the values of the financial class, where “risk” does not mean danger to human beings so much as danger to revenue streams. It refers to the risk of disruption, the risk of volatility, the risk that something might interfere with the smooth functioning of a profitable district. In other words, “risk management” is not really about safety. It is about ensuring that nothing interrupts the flow of money.

Risk management is the ideology linking municipal governance to finance—the language of insurers, bond markets, and asset managers. It treats stability as the highest good and disruption as the highest danger. That’s why leaders default to sterile terms like resilience, continuity, preparedness, and mitigation: not moral language, but market language. Downtown Nashville is governed like a portfolio. And a portfolio doesn’t have neighbors—it has liabilities.

Once a city frames its problems as “risk,” the solutions become automatic. Risk management doesn’t ask what people need; it asks what outcomes must be prevented, what volatility must be suppressed, what disruptions must be neutralized. In Nashville—where downtown functions as a revenue engine—“disruption” includes anything that unsettles tourists, scares off conventions, produces viral footage, interrupts consumption, or makes the district appear unstable.

This is the natural, inevitable consequence of the political agenda that elite democrats and republicans alike have been spreading across American cities and the globe, a framework that “financializes” everything that is known as “neo-liberalism.” It is not “liberal” in the normal sense, its political parents were Ronald Reagan and Margaret. It reached its peak with Barack Obama. And with Donlad Trump, we have tipped into a violent populist backlash against it, unsure many of us even what it is we are reacting against.

We have become illiterate of how actually to help a society and what being citizens even looks like.

This is why the political reflex becomes surveillance and enforcement instead of supportive housing and care. If leadership genuinely wanted to reduce dangerous encounters downtown, the most effective interventions would be permanent supportive housing, addiction treatment, mental healthcare, and material stability. You can’t police instability out of existence—you can only displace it, treating symptoms while reproducing causes.

And this approach isn’t even efficient on its own terms. It creates churn: displacement, arrest, instability, repeat. The city then absorbs the downstream costs—ER visits, jail bookings, court processing, bureaucratic labor—turning predictable social problems into recurring crises. It’s not efficiency. It’s political convenience: performing control without solving anything.

That’s why, without any direct coordination, Solaren Risk Management and LexisNexis Risk Solutions belong to the same ecosystem. Solaren is the visible enforcement arm: uniforms pushing bodies out of sight. LexisNexis is the abstracted infrastructure, selling “information” and “analytics” that make enforcement broader and more permanent. One removes people from the sidewalk. The other helps decide which people are treated as problems in the first place.

The city didn’t build this system alone. It worked with private intermediaries and data vendors whose business models depend on collecting and monetizing information at scale. Kristin Wilson, Mayor O’Connell’s Chief of Operations and Performance, coordinated the proposed $15 million surveillance agreement between MNPD and the Nashville Downtown Partnership. Before public service, she worked at LexisNexis Risk Solutions and oversaw the integration of ChoicePoint after its $4.1 billion acquisition. LexisNexis Risk Solutions is not a neutral research platform—it’s a worldview. “Risk solutions” means sorting people into categories of threat, volatility, and liability.

ChoicePoint’s history makes that clear. Before being absorbed into LexisNexis, it sold government databases for enforcement, including contracts with the INS. It acquired Mexico’s voter registry—legally restricted to elections—and sold it to U.S. immigration authorities, prompting Mexican prosecutors to presume a crime and oversee deletion of the files. That logic didn’t disappear; it scaled. LexisNexis later held a $22.1 million ICE contract explicitly designed to bypass jurisdictions that refused to share incarceration data. When democratic constraints appear, the market supplies an end-run.

This is how the modern surveillance state expands: through adaptation and redundancy. When communities resist, data brokers provide alternatives. When officials hesitate, grant funding provides a back door. When procurement slows, public-private partnerships become the workaround. When one vendor becomes toxic, another fills the gap. The infrastructure arrives piece by piece, until permanence feels normal.

That’s why the downtown surveillance package matters even if the MOU fight is moot. The gear is here. The pipeline is being built. And the lesson has been taught: when capital demands stability, money appears. The Downtown Partnership could have been required to invest in outreach, housing, and community interventions that reduce conflict at the root—but instead the city prioritized surveillance and policing. This is what “public safety” looks like when safety is defined as the absence of disruption rather than the presence of well-being.

And then there’s Oracle in the room.

The All Seeing 👁️

Nashville’s East Bank plan isn’t just real estate—it ties the city’s future to one of the world’s most powerful data infrastructure companies. Oracle is a major federal contractor whose cloud systems support sensitive government workloads. That matters because Nashville is simultaneously expanding surveillance capacity downtown: more cameras, more integration, more centralized command. Separately, each move can be framed as pragmatic. Together, they form a pattern: a city reorganizing itself around the collection, management, and monetization of data.

Oracle co-founder Larry Ellison has been explicit about his vision: a society blanketed in surveillance so people stay on their “best behavior.” Oracle’s role in government isn’t incidental—it is embedded in the machinery of the state. So Nashville hardening downtown while anchoring development to a data-governance giant isn’t coincidence. It’s convergence: the city as asset portfolio, disruption as security threat.

The point isn’t that any one official designed this system. It’s that municipal surveillance procurement, private data brokerage, federal enforcement, and public-private contracting increasingly operate as a seamless ecosystem. Nashville abandoned the Fusus platform in 2025 after public opposition, yet surveillance expansion reappeared through the Nashville Downtown Partnership, outside normal procurement channels. Even when vendors change, the logic persists. Systems reproduce themselves through new containers.

This was never only a debate about cameras. It was a debate about what Nashville believes downtown is.

That belief was visible in the ACME Feed & Seed controversy. Asked about a reported property tax increase (from roughly $129,000 to about $600,000), Mayor O’Connell emphasized market valuation under state oversight and suggested it was up to the owner to “capture the value” in the property. The response wasn’t careless—it was doctrinal. The city does not exist to preserve a particular version of Nashville; it exists to steward market conditions. If long-standing businesses can’t absorb rising valuations, that isn’t framed as policy failure. It’s framed as demand.

The administration later posted a “fact check,” a stunning admission of political weakness these comments created for the mayor. But the expanded context did nothing to indicate that the Mayor is anything but a staunch adherent to the market-as-god economic philosophy that has led to the right-wing populist backlash against liberal elites for the past decade. The defensive clapback served only to crystallize that Mayor O'Connell is fully and unapologetically committed to being a neoliberal in his approach to governing Music City And that he genuinely does not understand why he is being criticized for his comments.

I know every landlord has a different approach to this as commercial property owners. Some of them have, you know, triple net leases. They're [sic] a bunch of different ways for this to work out. I think this is a tough one because you look at businesses all along Broadway, all along 2nd Avenue. You look at the volume of transactions that are there and the prices people are willing to pay for property, and it's tough. It's competitive. It's a place where a lot of people want to be, and the market evolves. And, you know, we see new businesses starting even as old businesses -- in some cases, beloved old businesses -- are closing.

In the “fact check” rebuttal, the philosophy remained: market displacement is treated as natural evolution, while market interruption—anything threatening tourism revenue, conventions, investor confidence, or downtown throughput—is treated as emergency.

When the market prices out a business, it’s “inevitable.” When protest or economic instability from years for financial mismanagement on a state and federal and the wholesale gutting of the social safety threaten to disrupts downtown as a financal asset, millions appear for surveillance and tactical readiness. That asymmetry is the point. Downtown is treated less as a lived place than as a critical node to stabilize, harden, and insure.

This is where “public safety” blurs into asset protection. Surveillance and rapid-response infrastructure are justified not only to prevent harm, but to prevent disruption. The feared outcome isn’t only violence—it’s volatility. Not only injury, but interruption. Governance shifts from care to continuity, from community to risk mitigation, from human needs to investor fears.

If downtown feels “different,” it may not be because it is uniquely dangerous. It may be because it is uniquely profitable. And once profitability defines a place, policy begins to resemble the defense of an asset rather than the care of a community.

The question Nashville must answer isn’t whether it values safety. It’s whether, in protecting certain people’s profits, no price is too high—not even the steady erosion of everyone else’s civil liberties.

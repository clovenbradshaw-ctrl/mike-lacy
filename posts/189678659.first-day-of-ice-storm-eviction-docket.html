I wrote recently about a victory for those potentially facing eviction.

But the success of a “special eviction docket” is not in its creation.

It’s in its actual ability to deliver the justice it is designed to enable. And for the winter eviction docket to work, money needs to flow into the system where it will do the most good.

This, I’m told from people on the ground at eviction court today, has not happened.

The delay created by the docket gave ample time for organizations such as the Metro Action Commission, the United Way, Metro Social Services, Rooftop TN, and the Tennessee Titans (who opened their wallets for this purpose) to identify who was being evicted and to proactively support them.

Today was the first day of the special winter storm eviction docket. According to one person present with intimate professional knowledge of the situation and the logistics around evictions: “It’s a fucking shit show.”

Another with similar experience says: “I think the storm docket is mostly going to be to negotiate move-out agreements.”

It is not a secret who is being evicted. It is available on caselink.nashville.gov. For $25, you can search for people being served evictions or “detainer warrants” in the wake of the winter storm.

I even built a demo of an eviction tracker that makes all of this infinitely easier.

The truth is, it has never been a secret who is being evicted.

What has been harder to keep track of is which landlords are doing it the most, which law firms are profiting from this practice, which Council Members are allowing this to happen in their districts the most, and which organizations are sitting on money and hiding behind excuses about “paperwork” while Nashvillians are being turned out on the street.

That is no longer the case.

When it comes to that last part, about the support funding getting out the door, the problems are the classic non-profit-industrial complex problems, primarily focused on making success legible to funders rather than on actually getting money to where it needs to go.

The 2020 tornado relief scandal should have been a watershed moment for Nashville's disaster response infrastructure. More than $12.5 million was raised; millions couldn't be accounted for; checks sat untouched in bank accounts for years; and the resulting audit revealed an organization that was fundamentally unprepared for the job it had volunteered to do. The response to all of this was not to build systems that could identify and reach vulnerable people faster — it was to shuffle the responsibility from one large nonprofit to another and add more tracking mechanisms designed to protect the institutions, not the people they ostensibly serve.

And so here we are again: a winter storm hits, a special docket is created to buy time, money is pledged, and the organizations tasked with distributing it are turning away working-class immigrant families because they don't have an email address. The only lesson that appears to have been learned from the tornado fund fiasco is not how to get money to people more efficiently, but how to build a longer paper trail of reasons for why you didn't.

The United Way is claiming that nearly half a million dollars has been “deployed to 576” families for winter storm relief.

But delays, sources tell me, are coming upstream, with the United Way requiring things like email addresses that the most vulnerable residents often do not have. And while virtually no one wasn’t in some way impacted by the winter storm, it appears like everyone must pro-actively request assistance and the wherewithal to jump through the bureaucratic hoops that come with getting help inside of neoliberalism.

To my knowledge, it does not appear that any recipient orgs of the United Way funding are proactively checking caselink.nashville.gov to see who is being evicted and offering support (despite the fact that the addresses of evictees are almost always available).

Recipient orgs of winter relief as per United Way.

To those who are concerned about blowback to how relief money is being spent, your anxiety is justified. But the context is different than in 2020. Now, it will be the stalling, the hemming and hawing, the refusal to understand context and get out of your own way and let community experts lead, that will lead to public scrutiny.

And also, the day of putting the labor on those being served is waning. In the era of AI, vibe-coding, Whatsapp API integrations and free, reliable translation tools along with armies of real human volunteers that want to help, pretending its “all so complicated” doesn’t cut it anymore.

The thesis is this: institutions like the United Way aren't designed to solve problems — they're designed to mediate access to resources in ways that keep vulnerable communities and the grassroots organizations serving them dependent on institutional donors.

Those donors, in turn, shape what gets funded. And when the donor class is composed primarily of wealthy individuals and corporations, funding rarely flows where it's needed most. Not by accident, but by design — because genuinely lifting people out of poverty threatens the very inequalities that concentrate wealth and power in the hands of donors in the first place.

If this thesis is wrong, prove me wrong. Get money to where its needed most. And in this case, that’s people in the active process of being evicted. It’s not a mystery of who they are, and they are not expected to go find you, your website, and fill out a form, and wait. You make a salary. You can access caselink.nashville.gov. Figure it out.

The rules of the game are changing, and notice has been given. If you want to stay in the game, note the updates to the rule book. Or prepare to get a red card.

The Stakes

In case the outcome of families needlessly losing their homes is not immediately concerning to you, please, if nothing else, think of the tax burden.

Once Nashvillians slip into homelessness, the public investments in policing and surveillance technology (money that Council did figure out how to get out the door against public wishes) will be turned on them, and they dramatically increase their likelihood of being swept up into the penal system, where they will cost taxpayers vastly more money than if they were helped to stay housed, all to the profit of private prison corporations like the Brentwood-based Corrections Corporation of America (CCA).

And it should be a reminder that a shockingly small number of attorneys represent that vast majority of all evictions.

More information on the Council Districts where this is happening most is coming soon, along with whether those Council Members are helping their constituents access relief funding or are acting as if this has all been sorted.

I will also follow up on whether Nashville’s Office of Homeless Services is taking any concrete steps to keep people housed — or whether they will continue allowing people to slide into homelessness and then using “MRRF” funds to pay motel conversion landlords to temporarily shelter them. These are, in many cases, the same landlords who evict high volumes of residents every year, including tenants placed there with Metro support.

This shouldn’t be a surprise, I wrote an open letter to Director Calvin in November about how evictions are now being tracked and that this will be, regardless how her director supervisors judge her performance, part of the public key performance indicators (KPIs) that she will be judged on.

The same KPI should be of concern to who she reports to, Kristin Wilson in the Mayor’s Office of Operations Performance and Mayor O’Connell, as well as Council Members and the Judges who oversee evictions.

It all raises a question: if we are spending millions on homelessness, but barely lifting a finger to stop one of its primary causes, evictions, where, precisely, is that money going? And why, time and time again, does it look like into the pockets of the people doing the evictions?

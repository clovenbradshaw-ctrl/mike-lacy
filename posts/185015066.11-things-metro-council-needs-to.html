The Question Before Council: The Mayor's Office is asking Council to accept $3.38 million in law enforcement equipment from the Nashville Downtown Partnership, funded by the state's Downtown Public Safety Grant— released in the same batch of funding that is supporting ICE operations in Memphis.

Before voting, Council has some very import questions that need to be answered.

Funding Source Clarification

Earlier versions of our analysis of these funds incorrectly stated that Nashville’s $15 million comes from the Violent Crime Intervention Fund (VCIF). After performing fact checking for this post, we found that this was incorrect. Nashville’s $15M of funding comes from the Downtown Public Safety Grant Fund (DPSG)—a separate, yet explicitly connected pool of $100 million announced at the same time last year with overlapping stated purposes.

Both funds were announced together by Governor Lee on October 2, 2025 as a combined $175 million package “to strengthen downtown public safety and support local law enforcement’s work to reduce violent crime in communities.”

Here’s the exact breakdown:

  • VCIF ($75M): Created FY23, formula-based distribution to law enforcement agencies statewide based on violent crime data

  • DPSG ($100M): Created FY26, application-based grants limited to Memphis, Nashville, Knoxville, and Chattanooga

We regret the error. Precision matters in accountability journalism—especially when asking elected officials to scrutinize funding streams. If we expect Council to understand exactly where this money comes from, we must get it right ourselves.

Part I: Financial Questions

Is this “donation” really free? And does accepting it create complications?

1. The “Donor” May Have Unresolved Liability Exposure to Metro

The Nashville Downtown Partnership isn’t a neutral pass-through for state funds. It’s an entity with active, unresolved liability questions involving Metro Nashville.

June 10, 2025: A fire broke out in the Downtown Library Parking Garage—owned by NDOT and operated by the Nashville Downtown Partnership. As WSMV reported, the fire started in a maintenance area the Downtown Partnership’s vendor Block by Block used to store and service its streetsweepers, ATVs and other equipment. The blaze collapsed the ramp between the fourth and fifth floors.

Seven months later: Six vehicles remain trapped on the upper floors. One garage customer paid over $1,500 in auto loan payments and insurance premiums while waiting for answers.

The liability question remains open. City officials have stated there has been “no final determination of legal liability for the fire or damage” and that “liability may be contested in future proceedings.”

The city is evaluating the relationship. Officials confirmed the city “is not required to maintain its current partnership with the garage operator and is evaluating next steps.”

This raises a question Council should consider: When one party may owe another party money, and that question is being actively litigated, entering into a separate “donation” agreement during that period can create the appearance of a conflict — even if none exists.

Nashvillians have a right to expect that Metro will pursue fair and equitable recovery for the damage to the Downtown Library Parking Garage, which has now been closed for seven months. Accepting a $3.38 million “donation” from the same entity while liability remains undetermined could create the unintended appearance that the two matters are connected — that one is being exchanged for the other.

We are not suggesting any such arrangement exists. But good governance requires avoiding even the appearance of impropriety. Council should ask whether accepting this donation at this time — before the liability question is resolved — serves the public interest, and whether the timing is appropriate.

It is worth flagging as well that NDP has been receiving funding from Metro Nashville since 2004 without any authorizations of its budget, as required by Metro Code. That has totalled $40M since 2019, as per public records requests obtained by {Rich Text}.

2. The Crime Statistics Don’t Suggest a Policing Equipment Emergency

Violent crime fell 14% in 2025 to its lowest level since 2013. Homicides dropped 27.5% to 74—lowest since 2014. Robberies fell to lowest level since 1969.

What’s the justification for prioritizing $3.38M in surveillance equipment over other potential uses of these funds?

The surveillance ordinance contemplates a legal analysis that “no alternative with a lesser economic cost or impact upon civil rights or civil liberties would be as effective.” What is the emergency that warrants this invasive technology?

Council must ask these questions, it is their job to do so.

3. MNPD Already Received Significant New Funding — And the Grant Allowed Much Broader Uses

MNPD’s FY2026 budget included an additional $24.7 million in new funding, including $4.5 million for the Alternative Police Response Program, Mobile Field Operations, and D-Detail. The department isn’t starved for resources.

The state grant allowed much more than surveillance equipment. The Downtown Public Safety Grant that funded this $15 million allocation explicitly allows:

  • Resident assistance (housing, transportation, clothing, food vouchers, job/career assistance)

  • Unhoused outreach services

  • Drug/alcohol rehabilitation

  • Lighting and electrical upgrades (”Hot-Spot” street-lighting)

  • Training for staff, partners, and community stakeholders

  • Traffic-calming and protective hardware

  • Renovation/rehabilitation of existing downtown spaces

The Nashville Downtown Partnership’s grant application checked boxes for all of these categories — including “Outreach & Recovery Navigation” as a program structure.

What’s being brought to Council: Cameras. An armored vehicle. Mobile command centers.

What’s not being brought to Council: Housing assistance. Job programs. Lighting upgrades. Outreach services. Drug rehabilitation.

Council should ask why surveillance equipment is the priority when the grant explicitly funded alternatives?

Part II: What the Surveillance Ordinance Requires

Metro Code 13.08.080 exists specifically to ensure public oversight of surveillance technology acquisitions. Here’s what it says and how it applies.

4. The Surveillance Ordinance Requires a Public Hearing

Metro Code 13.08.080, authored by then Council Member Dave Rosenberg requires acquisitions of “surveillance technology” receive “approval by the Metropolitan Council, by a resolution adopted after a public hearing“ and a determination that “no alternative with a lesser economic cost or impact upon civil rights or civil liberties would be as effective.”

The ordinance defines “surveillance technology” to include:

  • Cameras with network integration, AI analytics, or system integration capabilities

  • “Vehicles equipped with electronic surveillance devices”

  • Equipment designed for multi-agency data sharing

The resolutions filed do not reference this ordinance. Whether public hearings will be scheduled is unclear. But Council should know: if this equipment meets the ordinance’s definitions—and the pricing strongly suggests it does—a public hearing is required before approval.

5. The Equipment Pricing Indicates Capabilities That Likely Trigger the Ordinance

Resolution RS2026-1733: Cameras at $10,000 Each

Equipment: 15 Axis cameras at $10,000 each ($150,000 total)

Stated Purpose: “Traditional video cameras”

The ordinance exempts only:

“(iii) manually operated non-wearable handheld digital cameras, audio recorders, and video recorders that are not designed to be used surreptitiously and whose functionality is limited to manually capturing and manually downloading video and/or audio recordings”

Why this exemption does not apply:

  1. Not handheld: The exemption covers “manually operated non-wearable handheld” devices. Fixed Axis cameras mounted in public rights-of-way are neither handheld nor manually operated.

  2. Not manually downloaded: The exemption requires functionality “limited to manually capturing and manually downloading” recordings. At $10,000 per unit, these are networked cameras with remote access capabilities—not standalone devices requiring physical download.

  3. Price indicates prohibited capabilities: Traditional fixed surveillance cameras retail for $500–$1,200; high-end PTZ models max out around $5,500. The $10,000 price point indicates cameras equipped with network integration, AI analytics, or Real Time Crime Center connectivity—precisely the capabilities the ordinance was designed to require Council approval for.

Resolution RS2026-1734: Armored Vehicle at $430,000

Equipment: Terradyne Gurkha MPV at $430,000

Recent comparable purchases by other agencies: $315,145-$399,745. Nashville’s price is $85,000-$115,000 higher.

Elm Grove, WI quote documents show surveillance options available at this price tier: roof PTZ cameras with 30x zoom ($9,800), thermal imaging ($9,800-$48,000), 4G LTE video transmission ($4,800).

If this vehicle includes such equipment, it meets the ordinance definition of “vehicles equipped with electronic surveillance devices.”

Resolution RS2026-1735: Mobile Command Centers at $2,000,000

Equipment: Two ATC 48’ trailers + Two Ford F-550s at $2,000,000

The stated configuration raises immediate questions. A 48-foot command trailer fully equipped with slide-outs, generators, HVAC, and workstations weighs 20,000-30,000+ lbs - firmly in semi-trailer territory. Yet Ford F-550s max out at 18,500 lbs conventional towing (31,600 lbs gooseneck with diesel and high-capacity package). Industry examples show 44-53 foot command trailers pulled by semi tractors, Freightliner M2 tandem-axle trucks, or commercial coach chassis - not F-550s. The largest trailer-and-F-550 pairing documented is 36 feet (Newton, KS). Either the specifications are inaccurate, the vehicles serve separate purposes, or the donation documentation requires closer scrutiny.

After trucks and upfit, approximately $1.8 million remains for two trailers ($900K each). Industry specifications from LDV, Farber, and Frontline show mobile command centers at this price point typically include pneumatic camera masts, video walls, 6-10 workstations, and integration with “drone feeds, pole cameras, body cameras, traffic cameras.”

These are “vehicles equipped with electronic surveillance devices” under the ordinance.

Resolution RS2026-1736: Tactical Support Post at $800,000

Equipment: International CV at $800,000

After chassis, $705,000-$735,000 remains for upfit. MNPD documents call this a “Command/Equipment Truck” despite the resolution describing it as “equipment storage.”

The bottom line: Council should request itemized specifications. If the equipment includes the capabilities the pricing suggests, the ordinance requires a public hearing before approval.

6. There is no way to safeguard resident privacy

What Mayor’s Office documents reveal about NDP’s actual interests: Despite claims that NDP had no interest in surveillance and policing, Mayor’s Office working documents published the night after the deferral show NDP’s own priority list in the grant application included:

  • LPR Technology (License Plate Readers) — $1,500,000

  • Off Duty Law Enforcement — $4,249,500 (Solaren-style private policing)

  • Noise Camera Technology — $900,000

This contradicts any suggestion that NDP was a neutral pass-through with no interest in surveillance or expanded policing capabilities.

Why this matters for accountability: Without an MOU or other binding document on file, there is no mechanism to hold NDP accountable for how these funds are used. The state grant allows broad discretion. If Council wants any enforceable limits on NDP’s use of these funds—whether for surveillance technology, private security expansion, or other purposes—those limits must be documented in a formal agreement.

Why promises about data sharing are legally unenforceable: Even if MNPD or the Mayor’s Office promises that surveillance data won’t be shared with federal agencies, Tennessee state law overrides any such commitment. The Tennessee Information Protection Act (TIPA), which took effect July 1, 2025, explicitly states that nothing in the law restricts a data controller or processor’s ability to:

  • “Cooperate with law enforcement agencies concerning conduct or activity that the controller or processor believes may violate federal, state, or local laws, rules, or regulations”

  • “Comply with a civil, criminal, or regulatory inquiry, investigation, subpoena, or summons by federal, state, local, or other governmental authorities”

This means any third-party data processor—Axis cameras with cloud connectivity, mobile command centers with integrated video systems, vendors operating surveillance platforms—is explicitly permitted under state law to share data with any law enforcement agency, including federal agencies like ICE and HSI. Local ordinances, resolutions, or verbal commitments cannot override state law. The only meaningful protection is to not acquire the capability in the first place.

Council members who had concerns about the December MOU should examine whether these resolutions raise similar questions.

Part IV: What Council Should Do

11. The Questions That Remain Unanswered

Before voting, Council should have answers to:

  1. What are the itemized specifications? What capabilities do these cameras, vehicles, and command centers actually include?

  2. Does this equipment trigger the surveillance ordinance? If so, has a public hearing been scheduled?

  3. What systems will this equipment integrate with? Local only? State? Federal?

  4. Why surveillance equipment over other eligible uses? The grant funded housing assistance, job programs, outreach services, drug rehabilitation, and lighting upgrades. Why are cameras and armored vehicles the priority?

  5. Does the timing of this donation create an appearance problem? With NDP’s liability for the garage fire unresolved, does accepting a $3.38 million “donation” from them now risk the appearance of a quid pro quo — even if none exists?

  7. Why is there not an MOU that binds NDP to only purchase things the community would support? If the community’s priorities are not being considered, whose priorities are? Bill Lee’s? Cameron Sexton’s? Stephen Miller’s? Donald Trump’s? Why is Mayor O’Connell prioritizing their preferences over the people who elected him?

The vote is January 20, 2026.

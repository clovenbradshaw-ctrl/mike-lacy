y2="14"></line></svg></button></div></div></div></a></figure></div><h2>How the Tracker Works</h2><p><a href="https://www.groundtruthnashville.org/evictions">The Ground Truth Nashville Eviction Tracker</a> draws from Nashville General Sessions Court records accessed through CaseLink, the public court database. An automated system captures all “detainer warrant” filings — Tennessee’s legal term for evictions proceedings—scrubbing backwards through the ancient internet 1.0 database and enriches each record with geographic data for classification.</p><p><em>Note on data: All defendant names have been redacted from public-facing versions of the tracker to protect tenant privacy, though the underlying court records remain public.</em></p><p>Every property address obtained from CaseLink is geocoded via Google’s Geocoding API service and cross-referenced with <a href="https://www.nashville.gov/departments/information-technology-services/maps-and-gis">Nashville Metro GIS</a> to assign each filing to its Metro Council District. This geographic enrichment is then integrated with Metro Nashville’s district-level demographic data, enabling apples-to-apples comparisons that account for population differences and homeownership rates.</p><p>The analysis focuses on evictions [filings] <strong>per 1,000</strong> <strong>renters</strong> rather than raw population, since districts vary dramatically in [clarification edit 1/2/26 9:48 pm] renter proportion] size and housing composition. A high raw eviction count in a district may simply indicate that more rental housing exists there, not that individual renters face higher displacement risk.</p><p>[<em>Update: Feb 2, 2026, 11:27 AM:</em><br>A “detainer warrant” aka “eviction filing” is not the same as an eviction—it's the start of a legal process that may end in dismissal, settlement, or a ruling in the tenant's favor, and this tracker cannot yet distinguish outcomes. But the filing itself often causes harm in itself: it typically appears on tenant screening reports used by future landlords, meaning a single detainer warrant—even one that's dismissed—can follow a renter for years and lock them out of housing they'd otherwise qualify for.]</p><p>Advanced features of the tracker include report generation that can be pivoted by property, attorney or other variables. And select trusted users will have the ability to search for tenants, and do so in bulk. This can be useful for case managers who want to keep track of their clients placed in housing, allowing them to search a long list of names in case any come up. the city has actively chosen policing and surveillance over prevention. They enthusiastically did it the week before the ice storm. While much well-placed blame is being placed on NES for not preparing us for the storm, that same accountability must be placed on leaders who choose swords over plough shares every time and, in turn, are not preparing us for the storm of economic turmoil at our gates.</p><p>When the Metro Council passed a non-binding resolution for the Office of Homeless Services to develop a plan to use its $4 million budget increase to keep people housed, the Mayor’s Office fought it. Meanwhile, that same administration lobbied for a $15 million state grant to fund policing that could have been used for housing assistance, outreach, traffic calming, infrastructure, as permitted under the grant.</p><p>The math is clarifying. Nashville allocated $3 million to monitor people downtown from the warm safety of an armored SWAT truck and tactical command posts (it was never stated who they would be deploying these <em>tactics</em> against). But the Mayor’s Office could not stomach the indignity of a Metro Department with the word “homelessness” in its name to be asked to develop a plan to keep people housed.</p><p>This is the context in which conversations about eviction moratoriums are resurfacing—driven not by ideology but by the brutal reality that half a million people just lost power during the coldest week of the year, many of them hourly workers who missed a week of pay precisely when rent came due. The question isn’t whether Nashville can afford to act. The question is whether it has the will.</p><p>And if anyone remains who can feel shame. </p><p>Shame, bluntly used, is shunning. But we’re not interested in shunning. It’s a tool of oppression and people who are tools. We’re interested in redirecting political energy. Too often, those who hold power in systems like ours do not respond well to corrective nudges, feedback, and reminders about common sense. To them, that type of collective governing is not simpatico with the irrational Jenga games of elitist self-interest that our society has been built on. </p><p>But if there are people who are still capable of feeling shame for how we have failed so many Nashvillians for so long, people who are mature enough to use those emotions for the greater good, there are many good options on the table.</p><p>The tools exist. </p><p>Option one: Confront the problem head-on. Pursue every available means to pause evictions during this crisis—and by crisis, I mean not just the ice storm but the catastrophic dismantling of our nation’s social safety net. This is the only moral response to a deeply immoral system of wealth extraction that has been exacerbated since last January, but dates back more than a few dozen administrations. This can take many forms: </p><ul><li><p>Legislative action stopping evictions where legally viable, </p></li><li><p>Judicial discretion from judges who are willing participants in this system and who remember that they (like the Sheriff who has a significant role to play in the eviction process), must win elections,</p></li><li><p>Public demand in the forms we'll discuss more soon—including coordinated rent strikes and general work strikes.</p></li></ul><p>But there are also tweaks. Adjustments that, while they run the risk of convincing us we’ve fixed things, should be seen as a form of <em>harm reduction</em> inside of a dying system of exploitation that is driving us off a cliff with our turn blinker on. Here are some good options there:</p><ul><li><p>The relatively new Eviction Right to Counsel has had very positive initial results—tenants with legal representation stay housed at dramatically higher rates. Supporting this with additional funding is a sound move. </p></li><li><p>Emergency rental assistance, when administered without bureaucratic gatekeeping, prevents the cascade that turns a missed paycheck into a court filing into a shelter bed into the street into a statistic. </p></li><li><p>Metro Action Commission should be overhauled to be proactive rather than reactive, reaching households before they reach crisis rather than processing them after, with funding for <em>the need</em>s they have, not the programming <em>we want</em> them to have.</p></li><li><p>The Office of Homeless Services needs a more fundamental rethinking. As currently structured, OHS primarily relocates unhoused people from politically inconvenient locations and funnels public money to private landlords through rapid rehousing funds outside formal contracts. A genuine agency that worked to reduce homelessness would look very, very different:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Prevention over displacement:</strong> Use eviction data to identify at-risk households before they lose housing, and intervene at or before the court-filing stage rather than at shelter intake.</p></li><li><p><strong>Public acquisition over landlord subsidies:</strong> Stop paying market-rate rent to private landlords and acquire property directly—building permanent public housing stock rather than subsidizing the market, which can cause displacement.</p></li><li><p><strong>Cash over case management:</strong> Direct financial assistance with minimal gatekeeping, trusting people to solve their own problems.</p></li><li><p><strong>Separation from police:</strong> Outreach that isn’t triggered by displacement requests or conducted jointly with MNPD.</p></li><li><p><strong>Transparency over opacity:</strong> Public dashboards on every dollar spent and outcome achieved, with independent oversight rather than self-reported metrics.</p></li></ul></li></ul><p>And many of the people designing these systems should be the people who’ve survived them—lived-experience experts who know exactly where the gaps are because they fell through them, rather than consultants with pre-existing relationships whose studies reliably align with the priorities of property owners rather than the tenants being displaced. They should not be brought into these spaces because it is equitable, or because diversity is good for its own sake, or because it’s <em>woke</em>.</p><p>They need to be brought in because, to quote (for the 100th time on this Substack) the Nobel Prize Winner for Economics Elinor Ostrom:</p><blockquote><p>There is no reason to believe that bureaucrats and politicians, no matter how well meaning, are better at solving problems than the people on the spot, who have the strongest incentive to get the solution right.</p></blockquote><p>I hope that it gives a solid ground for activists and tenants fighting for their rights to stand on. Framing arguments not just with moral clarity, but also with a greater understanding of what is actually happening than those you are trying to overcome, that can be quite a one-two punch. Demonstrating that your opponents quite literally do not know what they're talking about does not instantly confer unto you their political power. </p><p>But it undercuts the political legitimacy they need to dismiss contrary opinions as “uninformed,” and it goes a way to show the emperor's lack of clothes. It reveals who is trying their best to do the right thing with what information they have (and to adjust when new data is provided), and who is cosplaying as a person who cares about reality, but is truly there to serve power, unmoored from the ground of given experience, twisting the facts with, at best, a truthful-esque patina.</p><p> The insights of this tracker are not, to me, “look at these problematic outcomes they're hiding from us!” I doubt there were reports and assessments generated on this at any point to even get buried. It is much more “look what they never even bothered to keep track of.” </p><p>Consultants and dead end reports with predetermined outcomes give metrics a bad name, and this sours change makers on the idea that gaining clarity actually helps improve outcomes. And that's a shame </p><p>All systems that can self-regulate, even the unicellular, require information to evolve their behavior. So now that we have a way to acquire information about our behavior, namely who and how we evict people in Nashville, the question is, are we evolved enough to regulate ourselves? </p><h2>Comments Welcome</h2><p>Please reach out to me if you have comments, corrections, concerns, or any other c-words. The dataset includes all detainer warrant filings—the legal instrument landlords use to initiate eviction proceedings—from January 1, 2024 through January 29, 2026, a span of 760 days. CaseLink is the official court records system for Davidson County; the data reflects filings, not outcomes. A filing does not mean a tenant was evicted—cases may be dismissed, settled, or ruled in the tenant’s favor. However, filings themselves carry consequences: they appear on tenant screening reports and can affect future housing access regardless of outcome.</code></p><p><code>Demographic data comes from Metropolitan Social Services’ Know Your Community report (December 2023), which draws on the American Community Survey 2017-2021 five-year estimates. Variables include median household income, poverty rate, unemployment rate, racial composition, renter cost burden, and renter household counts by council district. Because the demographic snapshot precedes the eviction data by approximately three years, neighborhood composition may have shifted in rapidly changing areas—a limitation worth noting, though district-level demographic patterns tend to be relatively stable over short periods.</code></p><p><code>Geographic Assignment</code></p><p><code>Defendant addresses were geocoded using Nominatim, an open-source geocoding service, and assigned to Nashville’s 35 council districts using boundary files from Nashville’s open GIS portal. Addresses that could not be geocoded or fell outside Davidson County were excluded from geographic analysis but remain in aggregate totals. Approximately 85% of records were successfully geocoded to a council district, with the non-matching ones stemming primarily from Case Link not providing this information.</code></p><p><code>Rate Calculations</code></p><p><code>Eviction rates are expressed as filings per 1,000 renter households to allow comparison across districts with different populations. Raw filing counts were annualized to account for the 760-day data window, then divided by the number of renter households in each district and multiplied by 1,000. The formula:</code></p><blockquote><p><code>(Total Filings ÷ Days of Data × 365) ÷ Renter Households × 1,000</code></p></blockquote><p><code>This matches the methodology used in the district profile displays and allows direct comparison to annualized rates reported in housing research literature.</code></p><p><code>Correlation Analysis</code></p><p><code>Correlations between eviction rates and demographic variables were calculated using Pearson’s r, which measures the strength and direction of linear relationships between two variables. Values range from -1 (perfect negative correlation) to +1 (perfect positive correlation), with 0 indicating no linear relationship. Conventional interpretation:</code></p><ul><li><p><code>|r| &lt; 0.1 — negligible</code></p></li><li><p><code>|r| = 0.1–0.3 — weak</code></p></li><li><p><code>|r| = 0.3–0.5 — moderate</code></p></li><li><p><code>|r| &gt; 0.5 — strong</code></p></li></ul><p><code>With a sample size of 35 districts, correlations of approximately |r| ≥ 0.33 reach conventional statistical significance (p &lt; 0.05). Correlations reported here as “moderate” (r ≈ 0.30–0.31) are at the threshold of significance—suggestive but not definitive. Correlation does not establish causation; these relationships may be mediated by confounding variables not measured in this analysis.</code></p><p><code>Plaintiff and Attorney Analysis</code></p><p><code>Landlord and attorney names were extracted directly from court filings as recorded in CaseLink. Names were aggregated as-is without normalization for minor spelling variations or corporate entity disambiguation—meaning the same landlord operating under multiple LLCs may appear as separate entries. The counts therefore represent a conservative estimate of concentration; actual consolidation of ownership may be higher than reported.</code></p><p><code>Limitations</code></p><p><code>This analysis has several important limitations:</code></p><ol><li><p><code>Filings vs. outcomes: The data captures eviction filings, not completed evictions. Dismissals, settlements, and tenant victories are not distinguished from judgments for the landlord.</code></p></li><li><p><code>Demographic lag: The demographic data is 3-5 years older than the eviction data. Rapidly gentrifying or changing neighborhoods may not be accurately characterized.</code></p></li><li><p><code>Geocoding failures: Addresses that could not be geocoded are excluded from district-level analysis, potentially undercounting evictions in areas with non-standard addressing.</code></p></li><li><p><code>Entity disambiguation: Corporate landlords operating through multiple LLCs may be undercounted in concentration analysis.</code></p></li><li><p><code>Ecological fallacy: District-level correlations describe relationships between aggregate characteristics, not individual-level causation. A correlation between district poverty rates and eviction rates does not mean poor individuals are being evicted—it means evictions concentrate in higher-poverty areas.</code></p></li></ol><p><code>Reproducibility</code></p><p><code>All intermediate calculations for the correlation analysis are displayed in the tracker’s “Audit the Math” panel, including raw data points, sums, means, and formula applications. The methodology follows standard practices for Pearson correlation. Source data is publicly available: eviction records through CaseLink (caselink.nashville.gov) and demographic data through Metro Social Services’ Know Your Community report (nashville.gov).</code></p><div class="subscription-widget-wrap-editor" data-attrs="{&quot;url&quot;:&quot;https://readrichtext.substack.com/subscribe?&quot;,&quot;text&quot;:&quot;Subscribe&quot;,&quot;language&quot;:&quot;en&quot;}" data-component-name="SubscribeWidgetToDOM"><div class="subscription-widget show-subscribe"><div class="preamble"><p class="cta-caption">{Rich Text} is a reader-supported publication.

Quick Timeline:

  • 1988-2018: The Nashville Symphony received $7,615,708 in funding from the Metro Arts Commission, amounting to 16% of all allocations. The second-highest recipient, TPAC, received $3,657,097, or 7.69% of total allocations.

  • Pre-1988: Metro Arts focused on public art, festivals, and beautification projects.

  • October 19, 1987: Black Monday financial crash resulted in a $1.71 trillion loss, impacting corporate philanthropy.

  • January 1988: The Nashville Symphony announced it needed a $400,000 cash infusion to avoid bankruptcy due to poor fundraising efforts.

  • 1988: A deal struck by Symphony Board Chair Martha Ingram with Mayor Bill Boner secured $250,000 in public bailout funds from the Metro Arts Commission to the Symphony. The mayor expanded the pool to $500,000, allowing other organizations to submit for funding.

  • 1998: The Metro Arts Commission reduced Symphony funding, allocating $207,000 to the Symphony and $252,000 to the TN Repertory Theatre. Mayor Phil Bredesen, a former Symphony Board Member, intervened but was unsuccessful in reversing the decision for that year.

  • 2013: Despite receiving $6,899,713 in operational funding from the Metro Arts Commission over 25 years, the Symphony faced bankruptcy again. It received $139,800 in Metro Arts support that year and $151,750 the following year.

  • 2023: Mayor Cooper, discussing hardships with major downtown arts organizations during the pandemic, pledged $2 for capex for large arts orgs, including the Nashville Symphony.

  • July 2023: The Metro Arts Commission reallocated funds to smaller organizations due to a budget shortfall, sparking controversy among larger institutions.

  • August 2023: The Commission rescinded and revoted on funding allocations after legal concerns regarding race-based programs, resulting in reduced funding for small organizations and maintained levels for midsize and large ones.

The story of Metro Arts and the Nashville Symphony are inextricably linked. Over the years, the Symphony has received, by an extremely wide margin, the highest amount of Metro Arts Commission funding since it began this type of grant making in 1988.

According to official Metro Data, between 1988 and 2018, the Symphony received $7,615,708 in City funding via the Arts Commission, a full 16% of all allocations. The second place recipient, TPAC, received less than half as much, $3,657,097.00 or 7.69% of total allocations. (See pivot table here.)

This may seem shocking to some who might assume that The Metro Arts Commission was chartered and tasked with equitably funding art programs around the city based on concepts like merit, social impact, artistic vision, etc.

But it’s a financial reality that is well known and unsurprising to those who’ve paid attention to the Commission and its relationship to the Nashville arts community prior to this current debacle.

Prior to 1988, the Arts Commission mostly oversaw “public art”, mostly murals, public festivals, and the beautification of public buildings and spaces. All that changed in 1988.

In the wake of Black Monday, a $1.71T overnight financial shock to the global financial system, corporate philanthropy buttoned up, and many organizations, including Nashville’s large arts institutions that were counting on a continuing easy flow of tax write-offs after a 5-year bull market were blindsided.

The timing couldn’t have been worse for Nashville’s symphony. In January 1988, with contract negotiations for the Orchestra Union looming, the Nashville Symphony announced that without a $400,000 cash infusion, the Symphony would go under.

The cause? While Symphony attendance wasn’t steller, it was increasing. Salary negotiations for the Orchestra would be a burden, but not insurmountable or unpredictable. The real issue: poorly organized and implemented fundraising on behalf of the symphony’s board and development team.

Even before Black Monday, the writing had been on the wall that receiving corporate donations was going to take more than holding a gala or two as had been the norm in the high-tax era the mid century. But beginning with Reagan’s corporate tax breaks in the mid ‘80s, culminating in the tax reform act of 1986, any decent donor development team could have seen that without as much tax-incentive to donate, corporate donations were going to be harder to come by.

It doesn’t appear like the Symphony was adjusting their strategy to the financial relatives of the times, however. The Tennessean reported that all of the Symphony’s budget lines in 1988 looked healthy, except for one.

Symphony President Matthew Maddin seemed to be talking out of both sides of his mouth when it came to the fundraising efforts this board and development staff were undertaking.

But how bad was it for the Symphony? The report from the Tennessean shows that even at that time there were some suspicions the Board was playing hardball with the Orchestra union.

The Symphony had an option to dip into ticket sales for future concerts (as it had in previous years) to remain solvent, but it newly found this practice untenable and “too risky.” The board developed an alternate proposal that would bring the musicians to the table, and get funds flowing in the door again. Sign a contract, or the Symphony will file for bankruptcy.

The Union didn’t cave. The board called their bluff. Board member and future Nashville Mayor and later TN Governor Phil Bredesen drafted a letter to the union making condition clears: sign the deal, and we’ll withdraw the bankruptcy request.

He told them: “If it's not accepted our options are reorganization or liquidation."

The union took the deal, which included stripping the Orchestra down to 76 members for the duration of the ‘88 season.

There was some sugar that helped bitter negotiations go down. Bredesen talked about some of the financial incentives that brought the orchestra to the table:

This $250,000 would be delivered to the Symphony through a department that had never cut checks for this type of funds to public entities — the The Metro Arts Commission.

‘Complex situation’ From the Start

In 1988, Symphony Board Chair Martha Ingram struck a deal with Nashville Mayor Bill Boner for $250,000 to go to the symphony. This public bailout helped secure a contract with the Orchestra union, and resulted in the Symphony emerging from federal bankruptcy.

Mayor Boner, perhaps out of patronic beneficence, or perhaps to avoid the impropriety of using public funds bailing out a single public institution that didn’t meet its fundraising goals, expanded the pool to $500,000 and let other organizations submit for funding.

The process didn’t go smoothly from the jump. I’m gonna copy a good chunk of this article because it feels bizarrely familiar to the current situation.

What a skeptic might see here is $250,000 that was meant to go to a particular group, through a “verbal agreement” between Martha Ingram and the Mayor, and then another $250,000 tossed off in a “and give some to other art folks” hand wave that was easier said than done.

The city had never given private orgs arts before. The Arts Commission, previously tasked with things like the now defunct “Summer Lights Festival”, were forms of public art, rather than the public sponsoring art — a subtle, but significant difference.

Arts Commission Executive Director Anne Brown explained the difficulties that came with this expanded purpose being dumped on her Commission with little notice.

The Commission was not set up to succeed in their expanded remit from the beginning, highlighting that the Department was solving a problem for the donor class of Nashville, rather than being an intentional investment in Nashville’s art community.

1998 Controversy

The closest parallel to the current controversy I can find is a 1998 issue where the Metro Arts Commission reduced the funding for the Symphony. Not for race conscious equity reasons, but simply because the independent commission determined this made the most sense for arts funding, and gave more of it the TN Repertory Theatre.

This didn’t fly with Mayor, and former Nashville Symphony Board Member (and hardline union contract deal maker) Phil Bredesen. The following is from a Tennessean article where Bredesen recounts his phone call with Tom Turk, Arts Commission Executive Director.

"I told Tom that I had some concerns that there were significant changes downward and upward, which is contrary to the idea of stable funding," Bredesen said. "I said I respected the process that the commission had gone through, but that I didn't agree with the outcome."

Bredesen did not seem to be advocating for the Symphony, simply out of loyalty for the institution. As the Tennessean says, “the mayor could see a storm on the way.” And indeed it was.

The Nashville Symphony and the Tennessee Performing Arts Center, which received 28% and 44% less than the previous year, respectively, described the cuts as "devastating" even though last year's grants were only 5% of the symphony's total budget and less than 1% of TPAC's.

In his letter of appeal to the arts commission, Nashville Symphony executive director Alan Valentine described this year's grant reduction as "punitive," although in an interview he struck a conciliatory tone. "We didn't mean to say the commission was punishing us, just that the cut felt that way."

TPAC president and CEO Steven J. Greil, on the other hand, was taking no prisoners. He went nuclear, drafting a letter (and asking other arts groups to sign it) that called for Mayor Phil Bredesen to void this year's allocations and start from scratch. The letter was never sent, but Greil kept up his attacks on the arts commissioners, saying he had "no confidence in them as a governing body" and accusing them of ignoring their mission.

Kevin Nance, Tennessean, Sunday, August 16, 1998

It appears Bredesen’s intervention were not successful for the 1998 season, and Metro records show the Symphony received $207,000 to the Rep Theater’s $252,000. The Symphony was back on top the next year, with $300,000 to the Rep’s $161,798.

2013 Bankruptcy

In 2013 the Symphony, despite receiving $6,899,713 in operational funding from the Metro Arts Commission over the past 25 years, went bankrupt again.

The explanation of the near bankruptcy, reported in The Scene in 2013, came from a familiar voice in the Metro Arts conundrum, current Metro Finance Director Kevin Crumbo, then Treasurer, and later Board Chair, of the Nashville Symphony Orchestra:

Kevin Crumbo, a board member and treasurer for the NSO, said that worst-case scenario would likely be a last resort. For now, the NSO expects no interruptions in its operation. "What happened was our original business plan did not work out the way we thought, so there was only a slim chance that we would have been able to pay back the entire debt on schedule," Crumbo says. "We felt it would have been irresponsible for us to run out of cash later, so we decided we needed to renegotiate the debt right now. We need to deal with that debt as part of a comprehensive financial restructuring."

Aside from the 1998 dustup, I do not see, at least in public records, major concern across Metro leaders with the actions of the Metro Arts Commission until the July 2023 vote which reduced funding for large institutions and increased it for smaller organizations because of limited funding.

The budget shortfall came as a result of Cooper’s last budget, which was insufficient to fund the amount of requests that came into the Arts Commission without cutting some group short. To make up for this, Council approved $2M to supplement the existing budget, but even then, as a result of the dramatic increase in artist applications because of the success of the Arts Commision outreach under Singh, there was a shortfall, and choices would have to be made.

Reasonable people can different as to how binding Council’s intent for the $2M of funds matters legally, though the sponsors are clear that it was smaller organs and THRIVE recipients.

It’s unclear if that ship came in for those orgs, because, as the MHRC details, “there is not a transparent way to track public funds distributed through the CVC.” What the public can put together is that attention pivoted by those organizations in this $2M appropriation by Council, and that became the apparent method by which Cooper would be keeping his promise.

This seems to come as a surprise to the City Council whose intentions for these funds were for small groups, nor to most commissioners who, according to the MHRC report, were not considering these funds as making-good on any promises the lame-duck Mayor had made to any arts organizations.

When the Arts Commission, reviewing the options set forth by Metro Arts staff, considered their options, they chose, with their democratically enshrined authority, to use the funds they were legally allocated, to fund small orgs rather than larger ones.

This clearly sent shockwaves through the establishment arts community, coming as somewhat of a shock to at least one commissioner, Will Cheek, who signaled to Law Director Wally Dietz that he felt this needed to be undone.

It is after this point that Director Crumbo began looking into the Finances of Metro Arts, freezing the arts funding, citing first an excess of contractor expenditures, then later claiming the Metro Arts Executive Director had obligated the Commission for grant expenditures, creating an untenable financial situation.1

OVERVIEW OF FY24 GRANTS -- WHAT HAPPENED

This one-page overview provides a summary of what occurred leading up to the FY24 grant cycle and the decisions made in July and August 2023.

Arts staff led a number of community-engaged planning activities in Fall 2022 to develop recommendations that would increase access to Metro Arts funding opportunities. Primary among them were Community Editing Panels in which 27 representatives from arts organizations and independent artists provided direct input that led to a number of policy recommendations, examples including shortening the grant applications and removing various eligibility barriers, increasing the maximum amount for Thrive grants, and creating a funding model based on organization size that shifted some funding to organizations with smaller budgets. This included a tiered budget structure that determined funding amounts based on organization budget sizes categorized as micro, small, medium, midsize, and large. These and other changes were approved by the Arts Commission in December 2022. Meanwhile and throughout Spring 2023, Arts Commissioners and Arts staff participated in a number of trainings, planning, and capacity building work toward incorporating anti-racist and equity-focused initiatives.

By the mid-April 2023 grants deadline, 92 organizations and 155 individuals had applied for Operating and Thrive grants. The arts community mobilized to advocate for a budget allocation that would meet this demand, but then-Mayor Cooper’s budget fell short. The Council approved a $2M amendment for cultural support, which the sponsor described as intended to support equity and Thrive projects. Still, the total budget was insufficient to meet the full requests of all Thrive applicants and all organization requests, which left the Commission in a position to determine how to allocate funds in alignment with their equity goals and in support of the changes approved through the Community Editing Process.

Director Singh and contractor Dana Parsons developed multiple “funding scenarios” based on the budget structure approved in December (2022). Combined with data about organization sizes, included in informational materials and Commission deliberation were “BIPOC impact” and “white impact” dollar amounts that showed the result, in dollars, of each funding scenario for each category. The Grants and Funding Committee recommended approval of two scenarios that prioritized fully funding Thrive awards and prioritizing organizations with smaller budgets. In July (2023) the Commission approved one of those recommendations with an 8 (yea) -3 (nay) -1 (abstain) vote.

Commissioner Cheek expressed concerns over the constitutionality of the vote directly to Law Director Wallace Dietz. Metro Legal issued a memo and called for an Executive Session at which they recommended the Commission rescind its vote due to the Supreme Court’s recent ruling in Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard against affirmative action, implying that arts grants were race-based programs. In August (2023) the Commission rescinded and revoted, this time approving a scenario that partially funded Thrive projects, maintained funding levels from the previous year to midsize and large budget organizations, and funded micro, small, and medium organizations at lower amounts than they would have received under the July vote. There has been considerable Commissioner turnover and staff changes in the months since these controversial votes. This report provides details and our findings about the actions that led to the approval of an equitable funding model and its abrupt reversal.

What Really Killed FUSUS

Late last year, FUSUS failed. First on the floor, then early this year in discussions off the floor that led to it not being resubmitted (until a very similar product was put into the MOU from a start-up run by former FUSUS exec that signed the initial contract with MNPD). It was not politically feasible for it to return, but not primarily because of community opposition. It was because Council Members couldn’t get straight answers about the third-party data control of images and videos of their constituents.

Despite how activists might like to imagine their influence over Council (or how lobbyists and enthusiasts for surveillance have attempted to blame them for the failure), it was not a matter of “woke” privacy hawks centering the dangers of surveillance of vulnerable populations that made it ultimately untenable.

Protecting constituents from a federal government with an explicitly racist, xenophobic, anti-woman, anti-LGBTQ agenda is usually not enough to get a majority in Council. There are usually enough lobbyist-generated talking points provided to convince Council to not worry about these things, that these fears are overblown and just come from an “anti-cop” agenda from people who don’t understand how the world really works.

But with FUSUS, the tipping point came when it it was discovered that this arrangement, where businesses would give their data to a third party under contracts that had never been made public, and then that data would be shared with MNPD under a contract that was public, put these businesses at risk.

Metro leaders went around and around on the terms for what MNPD would be able to do with the data. But no one could answer what the multi-billion dollar surveillance and weapons corporation Axon would be able to do with petabytes of customer data it obtained before it transferred that data to MNPD.

Council sought answers to these questions, not primarily about MNPD and Metro’s involvement, but about what Fusus/Axon could do with the data.

There were no substantive answers.

This was when “guardrails” for Fusus were first being discussed. But the guardrails could only limit what MNPD would be able to do with that data. It did nothing to protect the customers that MNPD and the Mayor’s Office were encouraging to purchase products from this corporation with the assurance that this was a “safe arrangement.”

Could they sell that data, potentially tracking business’ employees and customers, to 3rd parties? Were they planning on giving it to law enforcement other than MNPD? Under what conditions? There were no good answers. And there was enough vagueness and risk for pro-business supporters to loss enthusiasm for the product. It was this coalition of interests, people who were worried about vulnerable populations AND business interests that had liability and customer privacy and intellectual property concerns that make Fusus not worth the risk.

What was clear is that with Fusus, the proposed guardrails weren’t just insufficient—they fundamentally couldn’t reach the actual concern. Private corporations operating as data processors would exist beyond Metro’s contractual authority, and no framework was offered to address this jurisdictional gap. That’s why the votes couldn’t be whipped.

Since then, the legal landscape changed in a way that makes this far worse.

What TIPA Changed: A Legal Trap for Municipalities

The Tennessee Information Protection Act (TIPA) took effect July 1, 2025, and creates a legal impossibility for municipalities trying to regulate surveillance technology.

First, there’s a preemption clause. TIPA Section 4 explicitly states:

“This act supersedes and preempts any conflicting provisions... adopted by a home rule municipality, county, including a metropolitan government, or city regarding the processing of personal data by controllers or processors.”

Any local ordinance Nashville passes about how surveillance contractors process or share data will be preempted if it “conflicts” with state law.

Second, there’s a law enforcement exemption. TIPA § 47-18-3208(a)(3) allows companies to:

“Cooperate with law enforcement agencies concerning conduct or activity that the controller or processor reasonably and in good faith believes may violate federal, state, or local laws, rules, or regulations”

No warrant required. No municipal oversight. Just the company’s “reasonable belief” that a law or even a regulatory “rule” might be violated. The company decides what’s “reasonable.”

These create a legal impossibility. For guardrails to actually work, they’d need to restrict how surveillance contractors share data with law enforcement. But any such restriction would “conflict” with the exemption in § 47-18-3208(a)(3), which means it would be preempted under Section 4.

This is a trap built into the statute. The very provisions that would make guardrails enforceable are the ones that trigger preemption.

Why This Matters: The Surveillance Business Model

I speak from experience—I was a global manager of an enterprise “software as a service” (SaaS) video platform that sold analytics derived from our users, whether corporate or public sector workers. The economics on things like this are clear to anyone who has worked in this industry: selling surveillance tech to the government is profitable (you can usually do it at a huge markup). Selling the data generated by that tech is vastly more profitable.

TIPA allows corporate data sharing with law enforcement if the data controller or processor unilaterally believes this is warranted. This includes data sharing with federal agencies that are willing and able to pay for this exchange, or leverage their existing contracts with surveillance tech brokers to incentivize this proactive sharing. This is not theoretical, as I'll discuss below, this is happening every day on a massive scale.

Law enforcement relationships are standard in this industry. TIPA now explicitly protects this business model—companies have the legal right and financial incentive to monetize municipal data through sharing with state and federal agencies. Whether or not there is a formal data sharing agreement between a corporation and law enforcement is not the point: the point is that there is precisely nothing a local government can do in TN to prevent this from happening as soon as the data is passed into private hands.

What Other Cities Have Learned: Vendor “Guardrails” Are Meaningless

Cities across the country are discovering that vendor promises about data control are fundamentally unreliable. The most instructive example is Flock Safety, the leading automated license plate reader (ALPR) vendor that many cities initially adopted with assurances about “restricted access” and “privacy protections.”

Federal Immigration Access Without Disclosure

In May 2025, the 404 Media revealed that Flock Safety provided U.S. Customs and Border Protection with direct accounts giving 1:1 access to local police data—without informing any of the thousands of law enforcement agencies it works with. This wasn’t a data breach; it was Flock’s business model.

Analysis of Virginia’s Flock network showed nearly 3,000 searches with immigration-related terms like “ICE,” “ERO,” and “deportee” over 12 months. Warren County Sheriff’s Office confirmed it conducted searches because federal immigration authorities requested them. Public records nationwide revealed over 4,000 lookups conducted by local police at the behest of federal immigration enforcement—giving ICE “side-door access” without formal contracts.

Illinois Secretary of State determined this violated state law prohibiting such sharing for immigration enforcement. But the damage was done—cities that thought they had “guardrails” discovered ICE had been accessing their surveillance data all along.

Constitutional Challenges

Federal courts are now questioning the constitutional validity of this surveillance model. In Norfolk, Virginia, Chief Judge Mark Davis ruled a Fourth Amendment lawsuit could proceed, citing Carpenter v. United States and noting the system “secretly monitoring and cataloguing the whole of tens of thousands of individuals’ movements over an extended period.”

Discovery revealed one plaintiff was tracked 526 times in four months (about four times daily), while another was logged 849 times—more than six times per day. This is the reality of “limited” surveillance with “guardrails.”

Source: NBC News, September 18, 2025

The ACLU and EFF have sued San Jose, California over nearly 500 Flock cameras, arguing warrantless searches violate the California Constitution, particularly around highly sensitive locations including clinics, immigration centers, and places of worship.

The Pattern Is Clear

Cities that adopted surveillance technology with vendor assurances about “guardrails” have discovered:

  1. Federal agencies access data without direct contracts—they partner with local agencies who are customers

  2. “Restricted access” is meaningless when the restriction is just “we only share with our law enforcement customers”

  3. Security practices are inadequate to protect sensitive surveillance data

  4. Once infrastructure is in place, usage expands far beyond original justification

  5. Contract provisions can’t prevent what state and federal law explicitly permits

The Current Danger for Metro Council

When the Mayor’s Office offers “enforceable guardrails” as a compromise, they’re either unaware of TIPA and this national pattern, or they’re offering something they know can’t be delivered.

This creates multiple problems for Council:

  • Legal Impossibility: Any ordinance that would meaningfully restrict data sharing with law enforcement would conflict with TIPA § 47-18-3208(a)(3)’s exemption and would therefore be preempted under Section 4. The very provisions that would make guardrails enforceable are the ones that trigger preemption.

  • Credibility Risk: Organizations that adopt “enforceable guardrails” language will be perceived as endorsing surveillance while believing they’ve secured protections that don’t legally exist under Tennessee law.

  • Liability Exposure: If Metro presents legislation with “enforceable guardrails” language knowing these guardrails can’t restrict law enforcement data sharing, and data obtained through these systems leads to civil rights violations, Council could face exposure. The statute of limitations on civil rights matters extends beyond the current federal administration.

Reputational Harm: Nashville would be repeating the same mistake dozens of other cities have made—adopting surveillance technology with vendor assurances, then discovering those assurances were meaningless.

Council’s Actual Authority: Use It

There’s a leverage issue being obscured in these discussions. Metro Council has 100% budgetary oversight over the District Management Corporation (the entity that received the state grant)—authority inscribed in Metro Code since 1998. The Mayor’s Office has zero such authority. The DMC’s budget must be reviewed and approved by Council annually.

When the Mayor’s Office suggests that certain surveillance purchases are non-negotiable, they’re speaking from a position of less authority than Council actually holds.

The Ask: A Clean MOU With No Surveillance Technology

Council should use its statutory authority over the DMC to negotiate a memorandum of understanding that prohibits the use of grant funds for surveillance technology purchases.

This is not only legally prudent given TIPA’s preemption—it’s the only honest approach given what we now know:

  • No surveillance technology can be purchased with effective guardrails under current Tennessee law

  • Vendor assurances about data control have proven unreliable in dozens of other cities

  • Federal agencies access local surveillance data regardless of municipal contracts

  • Security practices are inadequate to protect sensitive data from breaches and unauthorized access

  • Constitutional challenges are pending across the country

The $15M grant can support public safety through many proven interventions that don’t create these legal, constitutional, and practical problems:

  • Street lighting improvements

  • Traffic calming infrastructure

  • Violence interruption programs

  • Community-based safety initiatives

  • Emergency response improvements

The Honest Conversation

Council should have an honest conversation with constituents about what the law is:

Tennessee has chosen to prioritize corporate data sharing rights over municipal authority to protect residents. Nashville Metro cannot legally prevent surveillance contractors from sharing data with law enforcement agencies when those companies believe—in their sole discretion—that sharing is justified. Any vendor promises to the contrary are either uninformed or deceptive.

Given this reality, the responsible choice is not to purchase surveillance technology that creates risks Council cannot legally mitigate.

Other Cities Are Making This Choice

You would not be alone in this decision. Cities across the country are rejecting surveillance technology expansion after learning these lessons:

  • Chicago decommissioned its $49 million ShotSpotter system in September 2024 after the Inspector General found only 9% of alerts were linked to evidence of gun crime

  • California agencies face mounting legal challenges over Flock data sharing with federal immigration authorities, with Oakland initially rejecting a contract renewal before approving with strict anti-ICE amendments

  • Cities including Durham, Winston-Salem, San Antonio, and New Orleans have ended ShotSpotter contracts citing cost-effectiveness concerns

  • More than two dozen cities have adopted Community Control Over Police Surveillance (CCOPS) ordinances requiring public hearings and council approval before police acquire new surveillance technology

Metro Council can lead by learning from other cities’ expensive mistakes rather than repeating them.

The Integral philosopher Ken Wilber would say, of truth, that it is different at different levels of development. He’d say that within a person and a society it can spiral up or down to different stages.

He would describe “truth” like this:

  • At the Magical/Clan-based stage: Truth is what the powerful say.

  • At the Mythic/Traditional stage: Truth is what the sacred text says.

  • At the Rational/Modern stage: Truth is what can be empirically verified.

  • At the Pluralistic/Postmodern stage: Truth is what your perspective makes available.

  • At the Integral/Metamodern stage: Truth is what survives the intersection of grounded perspectives.

From this lens, there is no such thing as “post-truth.” That concept is, at best, the danger that comes from a toxic form of postmodernism—one that correctly sees the perspectival nature of meaning but refuses to pass through the final gate: the requirement that perspectives stay grounded. “Your truth, my truth” is a halfway house. You can’t stay there.

The Ramakrishna Paramahamsa said often:

God can be realized through all paths. All religions are true. The important thing is to reach the roof. You can reach it by stone stairs or by wooden stairs or by bamboo steps or by a rope. You can also climb up by a bamboo pole.

But notice: there's still one roof. The paths differ, but they have to actually go somewhere. You have to climb. Standing on the ground and declaring yourself already at the top isn't a different path—it's refusing to take one.

I think we need three dimensions to analyze truth. There are three questions you have to ask about anything to know what kind of falsehood you're dealing with.

  • What kind of claim is being made?

  • What is it grounded in?

  • What is the speaker’s orientation to truth?

Three questions, three options each, twenty-seven positions in the phase space of untruth. Not a ladder you climb. Not a quadrant you sit in. A coordinate system for locating exactly how something is failing to be true.

I present to you: the Periodic Table of Lying.

How do you use it?

Ask the three questions, then find where your answers intersect on the chart.

1. What kind of claim is being made?

  • Descriptive — claiming to report what is

  • Prescriptive — claiming to direct what should be

  • Constitutive — claiming to create what becomes

Your uncle at Thanksgiving saying “everyone agrees the election was stolen” is trying to pass off constitutive work—manufacturing consensus—as descriptive reporting. No, Gary, “everyone” is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence.

2. What is it grounded in?

  • Grounded — anchored in given experience (documents, observations, records)

  • Fabricated — conjured from nothing

  • Constructed — built from other interpretations

“I read somewhere that...” is the mating call of the fabricated claim. Read where? When? Written by whom? Your cousin’s boyfriend’s podcast doesn’t count as peer review, Stephanie.

3. What is the speaker’s orientation to truth?

  • Sincere — genuinely trying to communicate accurately

  • Deceptive — deliberately misleading

  • Performative — indifferent to truth, playing a role

Then find your element:

  • Your answer to Question 1 picks the color (blue / orange / purple)

  • Your answer to Question 2 picks the column within that color

  • Your answer to Question 3 picks the row

Where they meet is the species of untruth you’re dealing with.

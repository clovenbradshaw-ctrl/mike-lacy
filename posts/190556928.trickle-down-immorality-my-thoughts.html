“What happened at eviction court Tuesday?” I’ve been asked. The world was not saved, the worst did not happen. Like so often in local politics, a herculean effort to get public attention led to some people behaving mildly more reasonably. But for how long? It’s a well-known phenomenon in the philanthropic space that people give money for incidents, they shrug and say “I gave at the office” for issues.

Winter Storm Fern has helped turn evictions into an incident, it gives conceptual handles people can grab a hold of. The real problem is that we have an eviction issue in Nashville, and no short-term storm docket will hold back the rising tide.

My other thoughts on the day are less reporterly, I leave that to Eli from the Scene and Mikeie from the Banner who were there too.

One could almost be forgiven for believing that, with a slumlord in office (and anyone familiar with slum lords will remind you that Donald Trump is, above all else, a slum lord) that ethics and morality do not apply in our society anymore.

And because there is not, at this time, a cogent path to relief from federal officials for all sorts of malfeasance, that the rules do not apply.

With the foxes fully moved into the federal henhouse, State-level authorities, as long as their subterranean pyramid schemes do not run afoul of the big man, have carte blanche.

With the State being nothing more than a craven viper’s nest of lawless womb regulators, many Metro-level officials think their jobs are nothing more than to green light kleptocracy, no longer even bothering to pretend it’s in the name of neoliberal, market-driven good governance, but just vague hand waves at “innovation” while accepting campaign donations from familiar faces with the other hand.

Immorality, unlike the economy, trickles down.

The low determines the possible. The high determines the probable. And when people in power leave their morality at the door, they make it more likely that others will too.

There are many paychecks tied to cycling people in and out of sub-par housing. It takes a village to ruin a family’s credit score. We’ve been talking a lot about lawyers and apartment owners, but they are only one element here. The role of judges, sheriffs, and bureaucrats at times all need to be examined.

I don’t want to talk about “good people” or “bad people” — but I’d like to talk about anxiously paranoid people. The kind I tend to piss off.

The kind who say things to me like “don’t ever fuck with my business like that again” and call things “fake news” without being able to cite anything specifically untrue. There’s a certain toxic anxiety that wafts off the person who know they’re up to some bullshit and who fidget incessantly to appear otherwise.

I am a non-dualist. I do not believe in the simplistic, Manichean dualism of ultimate good vs ultimate evil. (Though as a non-dualist, I must stick up for the Christian apologist’s perpetual straw man: the pluralist Manicheans weren’t wrong about everything.) At the end of the day, I do not really think people who harm others go to hell.

But I do believe 98% of them are already in hell, a hell of their own making.

It hurts your brain and your heart to hurt people. And no matter how much we’re told from on high that “get yo’ bag” is the all of the law, the vast majority of people don’t process harming others very well.

Lt. Col. Dave Grossman of the US Army wrote a book called “On Killing.” In it he found that in combat, the people who were most likely to get PTSD were those on the side of the aggressors. Not the defending army. Not the civilians caught in the crossfire. It was those who enlist or are conscripted to harm other people that consistently have highest rates of psychological casualties.

Grossman’s diagnosis is simple: harming others is against our nature. It is the warping of society that tells us we should do it. And it is the cognitive dissonance that breaks our brains.

Few people who engage in the abuse of the less fortunate have the good fortune of being genuine, amoral sociopaths. Most oppressors, the every day types you and I have certainly interacted with at length, are anxious messes, rife with mental bird’s nests of paranoid delusions, assured that everyone is out to get them.

The complicating factor to these delusions, since we do live in a society, and since humans do in fact have worth beyond just their resale price in eviction court or as jail cell occupants, is people often are after you — this is called being held accountable.

The fear of the oppressor is that they will be held to account with the same level of inhumanity that they showed to their victims. But that is not justice in a humane society. Justice for the abused is not served by further dehumanizing society.

The oppressor has often inherited their epigenetic worldview from their “past lives” as victims. For those grown in the soil of oppression, there are two natural roles: victim and oppressor. And the only way to not be a victim, the logic goes, is to be an oppressor.

All victims, all marks, all human grist for the eviction mill, deportation machine, penal sausage factory — they are all guilty, in the deep mind of the oppressor. Not guilty of mundane things like crimes or immigration violations or late rent payments. They are guilty, the oppressor’s dark consciousness whispers, of not choosing to become an oppressor.

This is not a modern problem. It is the problem of society, of what happens when we invent a neutral technology to solve for when “we” is made up of more people than we can actually know. Humans the world over created a technology to preserve what we owe to each other, it is called debt. And the sicknesses that comes with it, its pathologies and co-morbidities, are well known.

Debt is the abstraction of control. Securitized and structured, it abstracts away the messiness that is turning bodies out into the streets. And it has existed, if you ask David Graeber, long before currency ever did.

Debt is a tool that can leverage the far future for a better tomorrow. But debt that is unforgivable, debt that creates market bubbles, debt that props up fiefdoms of inequity managed by anxious hummingbird bureaucrats, is how many great city states in history fell.

Repeatedly, throughout placetimes the world over, debt is stacked, stacked by humans that themselves become embodied chattel tokens of the very concept of wealth. And all stacking eventually builds pyramids.

The thing about pyramid schemes is they are the same as the pyramids in Egypt and Central America. Many innocent lives are destroyed in their making, and the person it was built to immortalize for just dies anyway. But not before the person who tried to outpace death had to live a life, an anxious life, a life distracted by the 10,000 things to keep them from the transmogrifying, perennial wisdom the universe breaks open in every cycle, the eternal recurrence of the dark co-constituting the light.

The still small voice, the λόγος at the center of existence, it speaks out to every ear that listens. It says:

Don’t make money off the suffering of others. It’s fucking lame. It makes you a nervous wreck because your body knows its wrong but your mind tries to convince you it’s right. That is why you feel insane. Because your choices are breaking your brain. You will never have “enough” because there already is “enough” — but only if we share it.

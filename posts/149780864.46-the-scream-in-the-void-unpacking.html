Chapter 4: The Collapse of Collective Meaning

As we look back on the forces that shaped the modern world, one voice that looms large in recent decades is that of Francis Fukuyama. In 1992, Fukuyama famously proclaimed the end of history in his landmark book The End of History and the Last Man. In it, he argued that with the collapse of the Soviet Union and the rise of liberal democracies, humanity had arrived at the final form of governance—liberal democracy—which, combined with capitalism, would usher in an era of relative peace and stability. The ideological battles of the 20th century, he claimed, were over.

For a time, it seemed like Fukuyama was right. The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 symbolized the triumph of the Western model of liberal democracy, and the 1990s were marked by the expansion of free markets, democratic institutions, and globalization. Many believed that this was the inevitable course of human progress—that societies would continue to develop in the direction of liberal democracy, a system that would finally balance the needs of the individual and the collective.

But by the early 21st century, it was clear that Fukuyama’s optimism was premature. The new millennium brought with it waves of nationalism, authoritarianism, and tribalism. The financial crises of 2008, the rise of populist movements, and the growing threat of climate change exposed the fragility of the global system. Far from a stable “end of history,” the world had plunged into existential uncertainty. The promises of liberal democracy and capitalism had not delivered the meaningful, cohesive society Fukuyama envisioned. In fact, they seemed to be contributing to a growing crisis of meaning—a sense that modern life, with all its freedoms and wealth, had left people disconnected, alienated, and uncertain about the future.

Fukuyama himself recognized this. In his later works, including The Origins of Political Order (2011) and Political Order and Political Decay (2014), Fukuyama shifted his focus from triumphalism to an exploration of the underlying forces that drive political systems and human societies. In these books, he argued that the development of political order is always a delicate balancing act between competing forces, particularly the needs of the individual and the collective.

For Fukuyama, civilization is never static. It is always a negotiation between the individual’s desire for autonomy, freedom, and self-expression, and the collective’s need for order, stability, and cohesion. Every society must find ways to reconcile these forces, but the tension between them never goes away. It is, at its core, the defining feature of political life.

This tension is not new. As we explored earlier, human societies have always wrestled with how to balance personal freedom with the common good. But what makes the modern era different is the scale and complexity of the systems in which we now live. In the past, humans lived in small tribes or tight-knit communities where the connection between the individual and the collective was immediate and personal. In those settings, the balance between personal autonomy and social cohesion was managed through direct relationships—face-to-face interactions, shared rituals, and clear social hierarchies.

But as societies grew and became more complex, that balance became harder to maintain. Large, impersonal systems like nation-states, global economies, and multinational corporations now govern our lives, and the individual’s role within these systems is often unclear. The result is a growing sense of alienation—a feeling that the systems we live in are too large, too abstract, and too indifferent to our personal needs and values.

The Predictive Mind and the Limits of Intuition

One reason why this tension has become so difficult to navigate in the modern world is that the human brain evolved to handle the kinds of problems that exist in small, tribal settings—where relationships are immediate and intuitive—not the complex, abstract systems of modern civilization. This is where the work of Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, particularly in the book Thinking, Fast and Slow, becomes crucial in understanding why we struggle to make sense of the larger, more complex systems that govern our lives.

Kahneman and Tversky’s research on cognitive biases and the two modes of thinking—System 1 and System 2—helps explain why humans often struggle to navigate complex societies. System 1 thinking is fast, intuitive, and emotional. It’s the kind of thinking that helped our ancestors survive in small groups, allowing them to make quick decisions based on gut feelings and immediate sensory input. This mode of thinking is extremely efficient when dealing with simple, immediate problems—like deciding whether someone is a friend or foe or whether it’s safe to eat a particular food.

But System 1 is poorly suited for the kinds of problems that define modern life. These are problems that require System 2 thinking—slow, deliberate, and analytical reasoning that can account for the complexities of large, interconnected systems. System 2 thinking is what we use when we engage in long-term planning, cost-benefit analysis, or try to understand abstract concepts like financial markets, climate models, or political institutions. It’s the kind of thinking that requires effort, patience, and a willingness to engage with uncertainty and complexity.

The problem is that System 2 is cognitively taxing. It requires more energy and attention than System 1, and because of that, we often default to the quicker, easier mode of thinking. This is especially true when we’re overwhelmed by information or faced with decisions that are too complex to fully understand. In these situations, we rely on heuristics—mental shortcuts that allow us to make decisions without fully analyzing all the available data.

These shortcuts worked well in tribal societies, where problems were immediate and easy to grasp. But in today’s world, where the decisions we make often have global consequences or require us to think in abstract, statistical terms, these heuristics can lead us astray. Kahneman and Tversky showed that humans are prone to a variety of cognitive biases that distort our understanding of reality, leading us to overestimate our ability to predict outcomes, underestimate risks, and gravitate toward simplistic narratives in the face of complexity.

This is one of the reasons why modern life feels so overwhelming. Our intuitive predictive abilities—which evolved to help us navigate small, immediate problems—are mismatched with the complex, globalized systems we now live in. We’re trying to apply tribal instincts to planetary-scale problems, and it’s not working.

The Crisis of Meaning in an Overwhelming World

This cognitive mismatch contributes to the crisis of meaning that so many people are experiencing today. In a world where decisions are made by algorithms, where economies are driven by financial markets that operate on principles most people don’t understand, and where political power is concentrated in institutions that feel distant and unresponsive, it’s no wonder that people feel disconnected from the systems that govern their lives.

For someone like Rachel, the overwhelming flood of information—about climate change, social justice, global conflict—leaves her feeling paralyzed. The issues are too big, too complex, and too urgent for her to process through the fast, intuitive System 1 thinking that comes naturally to her. But engaging with these problems in the slow, deliberate System 2 mode requires more cognitive energy than she can muster, especially when every day brings new crises demanding her attention.

For John, the disorientation comes from the collapse of the simple, intuitive world he grew up in. The factory job he once held, the values of hard work and family that defined his life, have been undermined by economic systems that are too large and abstract for him to influence. He knows something has gone wrong, but the reasons are too complex for him to fully grasp. Instead, he falls back on System 1 thinking, relying on tribal loyalty to his community and political identity to provide a sense of stability in a world that feels increasingly alien.

For Ahmed, the complexity of global climate systems and international refugee policies offers no solace. His life, once governed by the rhythms of the land and the seasons, has been upended by forces beyond his understanding. The abstract discussions of climate accords and international agreements are distant echoes of decisions made far away, by people he’ll never meet. Like John, he is trapped in a world where the complexity of modern systems renders individual agency nearly meaningless.

And Marta? Her desperation for order in a chaotic, multicultural world leads her into the arms of a movement that promises to restore simplicity and clarity. She doesn’t need to understand the nuances of global economics or political systems; she needs a tribal identity that makes her feel secure, one that offers simple answers to complex problems.

In all of these cases, the complexity of modern life exacerbates the tension between individual desires and collective needs. The systems we’ve built are too large, too abstract, and too impersonal to engage with intuitively, but they’re also too vast to fully grasp through reason alone. This leaves individuals feeling adrift, searching for meaning in a world that no longer makes sense to them.

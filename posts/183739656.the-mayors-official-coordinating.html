Managing an integration of this scale typically involves consolidating systems, eliminating redundancies, and aligning business operations to extract value from the merged entity—the '$150M in annual synergy value creation' her resume highlights.

Whether Wilson made strategic decisions about which business lines to retain is unclear from public records. But at minimum, she helped make the apparatus more efficient: the same databases, the same contracts, the same customers—all built for speed and now delivering data on tap without warrants to ICE and other law enforcement agencies.

Wilson did not create ChoicePoint's data brokering for immigration enforcement business. But she helped fold it into something larger and more durable.

At the point of the LexisNexis merger, ChoicePoint was also already in the immigration enforcement business: FOIA documents obtained in 2002 revealed contracts with the Immigration and Naturalization Service to provide citizen registry and motor vehicle data from Mexico, Brazil, Argentina, Colombia, and Costa Rica—prompting Mexico’s Special Prosecutor for Electoral Crimes to declare that “there is a presumption that a crime has been committed.”

Wilson joined LexisNexis in 2005 and according to her LinkedIn her tasks included “managing the integration… of ChoicePoint by LexisNexis.” It appears she was crucial to helping fold ChoicePoint’s data operations into the larger entity.

That merged company, LexisNexis Risk Solutions, now holds a $22.1 million contract with ICE. Agency budget documents state it needs this contract because local governments increasingly “do not share information about real time incarceration of foreign-born nationals with ICE.”

Wilson did not create ChoicePoint’s immigration enforcement business. But she integrated it into LexisNexis Risk Solutions, the corporate entity that still profits from it today—and she is now directing the procurement of surveillance technology for a city that has said, over and over, “no means no” when it comes to more surveillance.

Preface: Why Professional History Matters in Public Service

When we enter public service, we bring our professional history with us. The networks we built, the problems we learned to solve, the metrics we learned to optimize—these shape how we see the world. It’s the very reason we have resumes.

But looking at who holds influential positions in our city, I think we forget this. Or maybe people are being hired for their experience, and we just don’t understand what job they’re being hired to do.

Kristin Wilson, Nashville’s Chief of Operations and Performance for two Nashville Mayors running, is a subject matter expert on merging surveillance technology at colossal scales—a credential that propelled her rise in municipal government.

But she likely knows nothing about surveillance from the other side. She helped build the infrastructure that determines who gets flagged, who gets detained—almost certainly never from the wrong side of the database.

Here’s what privacy advocates struggle to understand: those who broker in surveillance never see the product they’re dealing with. Car salesmen, realtors, drug dealers—they all have a relationship with what they sell. But when you traffick in data, you’re often abstracted from the goods you’re pushing.

Your address history, license plate records, phone data—its likely been sold to a bank, and it might get you rejected for a loan. Bad. But not life ruining. But that same data, in other hands, is far more dangerous. That same data sold to ICE? It could separate your family. Data used to shave “bad debt” off a ledger becomes surveillance data the moment a different buyer signs the contract.

The surveillance data business model requires finding enough criminality to justify the surveillance—not reducing it. If crime actually went down, they'd have nothing left to sell. It's lead generation for law enforcement: cast the net wide, catch more 'criminals.' Running low on criminals? Expand the definition to include anyone that might be an immigrant.

ProPublica documented more than 170 U.S. citizens detained by immigration agents in the first nine months of the current administration—a tally the reporters acknowledge is “almost certainly incomplete.”

Among those detained: nearly 20 children, including two with cancer. More than 50 Americans were held after agents questioned their citizenship; they were almost all Latinx. The problem has grown so acute that Wikipedia now has a dedicated article tracking individual cases.

This is not a data problem. And it is not going to be solved by putting an AI in charge of it. It is a due process problem. No amount of data can introduce accountability into systems that are insulated from oversight.

That Mayor O’Connell’s Chief of Operations and Performance made her career packaging together data aggregation firms that sell that information to ICE does not permanently disqualify her from public service. Nor does it make her a bad person.

I had the pleasure of serving in government under the guidance of a reformed felon whose crimes were classified as “drug kingpin” status and had served time in federal prison.

He was qualified for working in Community Safety because of his lived experience — but importantly, he shared with others why his former life path was not a road others should go down. I believe in nothing if not second chances for those who have seen the errors of their ways.

But it is unclear if Ms. Wilson has left the surveillance industry behind her when she joined public office. And the results of hiring someone with this background are so clear and predictable, that it makes one wonder: is it what she was hired to do?

Wilson’s Role in the Surveillance MOU

The Nashville Scene reported in December 2025 that “the mayor’s office, at the direction of chief of operations and performance Kristin Wilson, had coordinated with the NDP to receive $9 million in ‘grant-funded gifts’” as part of the proposed surveillance MOU. Repeatedly the Mayor’s Office framed the MOU as a form of “guardrails” against the Nashville Downtown Partnership’s alleged attempts to purchase extensive surveillance equipment. NDP representatives have denied the allegations, and public records show that it was MNPD who requested the surveillance and policing software—including an armored SWAT truck that would be purchased for them by NDP.

Email records I obtained via public records requests show that MNPD’s wish list was marshaled together by Kristin Wilson.

The original MOU Exhibit A itemized surveillance and policing technology including:

  • LeoSight and Fivecast — surveillance software platforms

  • Security cameras

  • Noise camera technology

  • A centralized video wall

  • “Threat identification” software for monitoring online activity

  • A virtual command post

  • An armored rescue vehicle

  • A mobile command post

  • A tactical support post

LeoSight, a software earmarked by name in the original MOU, is led by the former Fusus executive who personally signed contracts with MNPD Chief Drake—and founded the company (a rebrand of an existing firm) in March 2025, at the very same time Nashville killed Fusus.

Public records and Council testimony affirm that MNPD added the line items for LeoSight, and it was Ms. Wilson who finalized the MOU, signed by the Mayor and the Downtown Partnership’s Tom Turner.

A frequent refrain is that some sort of “guardrails” can be put in place to protect from problematic data sharing.

Through data brokers like LexisNexis Risk Solutions—the company that emerged from the integration Wilson coordinated—agencies like DHS and ICE are able to access much of the information that local jurisdictions have chosen not to provide, nullifying the effect of any guardrails on data sharing.

Additionally, any locally obtained data that uses a 3rd party cannot have its data restricted through local government action.

Tennessee's Information Protection Act (TIPA), effective July 1, 2025, preempts local governments from enacting stronger privacy protections—and authorizes data controllers to share information with law enforcement whenever they "reasonably and in good faith" believe someone may have violated any law, including federal immigration law. No warrant required. No subpoena. Nashville has no mechanism to prevent surveillance vendors from sharing data with ICE, and state law now prohibits the city from creating one.

What Wilson Integrated: ChoicePoint’s History

Wilson was given a plum position in the Atlanta Mayor’s Office because of her experience “managing multi-million dollar acquisitions and strategic projects.” Chief among those accomplishments appears to be the absorption of the company ChoicePoint by LexisNexis Risk Solutions.

ChoicePoint was a data aggregation giant spun off from Equifax in 1997, compiling criminal records, employment history, address data, and more on virtually every adult in America. The industry calls this “risk protection”—better data means fewer loan defaults, less insurance fraud, more reliable hires. Neutral, mathematical, clean. Right?

But “risk” encodes history. If your zip code was redlined, if you were arrested for something a white kid got a warning for, you carry that in your data. Credit scores reflect racist housing policy. Background checks perpetuate mass incarceration. “Risk protection” protects institutions from people already harmed by systemic exclusion—then harms them again.

Even the benign applications sort people into tiers of worthiness. And ChoicePoint was never content with benign applications.

ChoicePoint and its subsidiaries were at the heart of some of the wildest data scandals of the early 21st century—including the Florida voter purge lists that may have tipped the 2000 election to George W. Bush.

The 2000 Election Connection

A subsidiary of ChoicePoint, Database Technology (DBT Online), provided Florida with the voter purge lists used in the 2000 presidential election. The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights found that state officials instructed DBT to use loose matching criteria—accepting 80 percent name matches rather than exact matches—over the company’s objections. DBT vice president George Bruder testified that supervisors of elections present “wanted to be as exacting as possible on the matches,” but was overruled.

Approximately 8,000 people who committed misdemeanors in Texas were incorrectly identified as Florida felons and stripped of the right to vote. George W. Bush’s certified margin of victory was 537 votes.

ChoicePoint acquired DBT Online on May 16, 2000—mere months after Florida contracted for the purge lists but before Election Day. The $440 million deal made the voter file contract ChoicePoint’s responsibility through the contested election and subsequent congressional investigations.

This was one part of the portfolio Wilson helped consolidate into LexisNexis.

An International Data Heist

In April 2002, FOIA documents obtained by the Electronic Privacy Information Center revealed that ChoicePoint held contracts with the Immigration and Naturalization Service (INS) to provide “citizen registry, motor vehicle, and other information for Brazil, Argentina, Mexico, Colombia, and Costa Rica.”

The revelation triggered investigations across Latin America. According to the Chicago Tribune, ChoicePoint purchased the information for $250,000 in 2001 from a Mexican consumer information company called Bases de Datos. Bases de Datos claimed it bought the information from another company—whose representative “apparently has gone into hiding” before investigators could question him.

ChoicePoint returned 10 disks containing voter information to Mexican authorities, but, as the Tribune reported:

“Mexican prosecutors want proof–beyond a company news release–that ChoicePoint destroyed its original copy of the suspected voter files. The Mexicans sent a delegation to company offices in Alpharetta, Ga., to witness the erasing of the files, but the company first requested a statement from Mexico exonerating its employees of any wrongdoing.”

Mexico’s Special Prosecutor for Electoral Crimes, María de los Ángeles Fromow, told the Tribune: “Because of the simple fact that [the list] has left the electoral arena and has been used in a commercial form, for other ends, there is a presumption that a crime has been committed.”

In response, the U.S. Department of Homeland Security stopped using ChoicePoint’s data on Mexican citizens. A department spokesman said: “It was an extremely useful database, but it was [only] one of the tools we used. We have a lot of other, different databases.”

The Pinkerton Connection

The surveillance apparatus Wilson helped integrate into LexisNexis had roots stretching back to one of the most notorious names in American labor history.

The Pinkerton National Detective Agency, founded in 1850, became synonymous with violent strikebreaking. The 1892 Homestead Strike—where 300 Pinkerton agents battled Carnegie steelworkers in a firefight that left 16 dead and 23 wounded—prompted Congress to pass the Anti-Pinkerton Act of 1893, banning federal agencies from hiring the company. The La Follette Committee hearings (1936–1941) later exposed how the agency had pivoted from armed force to industrial espionage, revealing that Pinkerton maintained operatives “in practically every union in the country.”

By the 1990s, Pinkerton had rebranded as a security and risk management firm. In 1999, Swedish security giant Securitas AB acquired Pinkerton for $384 million. But the agency’s employee screening division—the descendant of its labor surveillance apparatus—took a different path.

In July 2001, ChoicePoint acquired Pinkerton’s pre-employment screening and drug testing business. The deal included Esteem, described in ChoicePoint’s own announcement as “a contributory database of retail employees who have admitted to workplace theft”—in practice, a corporate blacklist. Subscribing retailers would submit “admission statements“ extracted during questioning by store loss prevention officers. Workers—often coerced into signing under threat of arrest without understanding the consequences—would be flagged and locked out of retail employment industry-wide for seven years, even when no criminal charges were ever filed.

When LexisNexis acquired ChoicePoint in 2008, Esteem came with it. LexisNexis eventually settled the resulting class action in 2014 and suspended the database, with a class action suit alleged that Esteem “wrongfully labels job seekers as criminals even if they have not been convicted of a crime”—but not before it contributed to the “$150M in annual synergy value creation” Ms. Wilson’s resume highlights. Along with the profits came the Pinkerton surveillance model, absorbed into the data broker that now contracts with ICE.

The Broader Context: Post-9/11 Surveillance Infrastructure

The LexisNexis acquisition of ChoicePoint occurred during a specific moment in American history: the post-9/11 expansion of domestic surveillance infrastructure. When lawmakers defunded Total Information Awareness in 2003, similar capabilities migrated to private data brokers and intelligence agencies.

The 2008 merger consolidated two significant nodes in this privatized data network:

  • Seisint, Inc., which had built the MATRIX system for the Florida Department of Law Enforcement (and which LexisNexis acquired for $775 million in September 2004)

  • ChoicePoint, which had been selling Latin American government databases to U.S. immigration authorities since before DHS existed

By September 2009, ChoicePoint was fully integrated into LexisNexis Risk Solutions—the same entity that now holds ICE’s $22.1 million contract. Through mergers and acquisitions that created for shareholders tremendous “synergy value”, the consolidated data broker that helped determine who could vote in Florida in 2000 is now helping determine who gets deported in 2025.

LexisNexis Risk Solutions Today

Despite access to troves of data, civil rights are routinely violated. The problem is not insufficient data collection—it’s the absence of accountability for those wielding it.

The numbers make this clear. A 2021 Government Accountability Office report found that ICE arrested 674, detained 121, and removed 70 potential U.S. citizens between fiscal year 2015 and March 2020—people who cannot legally be deported. Data analyzed by the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse found that ICE wrongly identified at least 2,840 U.S. citizens as potentially eligible for removal between 2002 and 2017.

Research by Northwestern University political scientist Jacqueline Stevens estimates that up to 1% of all ICE detainees are U.S. citizens—a figure that would have meant over 4,000 citizens in detention in 2010 alone. The GAO found that neither ICE nor CBP maintain adequate records to even determine the full scope of citizens wrongfully targeted.

These violations don’t happen despite the surveillance infrastructure—they happen through it.

LexisNexis Risk Solutions, the corporate entity that absorbed ChoicePoint, now holds a $22.1 million contract with ICE running through 2026. ICE agents ran over 1.2 million searches in LexisNexis databases over just a seven-month period.

According to contract documents cited in the Colorado Law Review:

“ICE’s express intentions in forming this contract were to subvert local democratic lawmaking: ICE contracted with LexisNexis for the purpose of subverting sanctuary laws. According to contract documents, ICE required access to the Justice Intelligence database (a database containing incarceration records and jail booking data) due to ‘policy or legislative changes’ that have resulted in an ‘increase in the number of law enforcement agencies and state or local governments that do not share information about real time incarceration of foreign-born nationals with ICE.’”

Data aggregation without accountability can only lead to further violations of civil liberties, never delivering on its promise of public safety while causing real harm and violence. Its continued failures to deliver on peace and crime reduction are used as further evidence for the need of more surveillance, and those who profit off monitoring the bodies of vulnerable populations are only too happy to provide the product for law enforcement’s addiction, so long as there is synergy value to be created.

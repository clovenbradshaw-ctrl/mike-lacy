Fact-Checking Some Tweets

First, some tweets (“Xs”? I hate deadnaming poor Elon’s platform, I know he respects things like that so much) caught my eye. They come from Virgil Davis Hunt, the Founder & Editor of The Pamphleteer, an alt-daily online publication with, we might say, an explicitly "anti-woke-mind-virus” point of view.

Virgil (now a subscriber, hi, Virgil!) a past commentator on the Metro Arts crisis, has framed its soon-to-be ex-director as a singular force in derailing the commission and, somehow unilaterally steering it toward an anti-colonialist, anti-racist, pro-DEI agenda (which, the implication goes, is quite a bad thing).

Those things are **spoiler alert** not true, and in past and future writings I’ll provide you with facts that will help you evaluate the truth-value of what Virgil is saying for yourself. But people talk out of their ass on the internet all the time, big deal? Why signal boost him?

Well, I think there’s something useful about unpacking what he’s saying on Twitter and in other places about this situation because he’s saying aloud what many people must be reserving for private. And it’s actually that conversation that’s more genuinely the source of the conflict here, in my opinion.

The Pamphleteer, an also-ran in the Peter Thiel school of “new right” conservative media, is likely still a ways off from accomplishing Virgil’s stated goal for the outlet:

The Pamphleteer is my small expression of this impulse: to actively engage in the life of the city and assert a vision of it against what I see as homogenizing and destructive forces. It’s not even my vision of the city. I share it with many others, potentially even you.

As is, the site is mostly reviews of TV shows and movies with a conservative skew, but a few articles hedge more specifically towards ax-griding, geared at fearmongering for those fretting about cancel culture and anti-racist agendas.

An article’s subtitle from April 25, 2024, reads: “Alex Jones may be the target now, but you could be next”. And then there was the Metro Arts article which which accused the Director of hiring “a cadre of radical consultants and has pledged to re-envision Metro Arts as an anti-racist organization committed to decolonization”.

Virgil’s perspective, I’ve gathered, is that:

  1. A single person is responsible for this situation — there is a singular “bad guy” to blame, and that person is the soon-to-be-ex Executive Director.

  2. Nothing like this had happened before, there had never been serious delays of funding, nor feuds between potential recipients that drove deep wedges within the arts community.

  3. This situation came about primarily because of recent DEI initiatives, which he argues don’t have a genuine societal benefit and just serve to elevate inferior people to positions of power.

I don’t agree with any of that, and my work in this series is an attempt to deconstruct that narrative through public records and dank memes. But there is something accurate in his writing about Metro Arts funding that needs to be acknowledged, and I think it’s been underplayed by a lot of advocates for equitable funding.

In the original design of Metro Arts funding, Virgil is correct that “The primary purpose of Metro Arts is to provide operating grants to local arts institutions, such as the Frist, the Belcourt Theatre, the Country Music Hall of Fame, TPAC, and a variety of other cultural staples.”

This is thoroughly documented in the public record, including statements by Phil Bredesen, the symphony board member who used the first Metro Arts monies to negotiate with the Orchestra Union and who later oversaw the distribution of funds as Mayor from 1991 to 1999.

Bredesen provides no illusions in public statements that this funding was first and primarily for the Symphony, and secondly other large arts institutions like the ones Virgil mentioned. Funding for individual artists and with a focus on racial equity came later — but long before Singh.

Where Virgil strays into alternative facts is with his next statement, “However, under current Executive Director Daniel Singh—appointed by the Metro Arts Commission in August 2022—the organization has attempted to redistribute money from these purportedly ‘white’ institutions and apply an equity framework to give it back to minorities.”

An internal timeline from Metro Arts predating Singh’s tenure by three years tracked Metro Arts’ efforts to address racial disparities in their funding going back certainly to 2014 with the equity-focused Thrive funding program, and perhaps you could go back to the appointment of Dr. Paulette Coleman in 1999 as commission chair. A Ph.D. in Urban Planning from MIT, her credits included an international studies fellowship from the University of Denver in International Race Relations.

The timeline from Metro Arts shows its design was evolving over time, increasingly considering the impact of arts funding through the lens of racial disparity and economic disenfranchisement, long before Daniel Singh was hired to lead the department.

Funding percentages over time, still heavily weighted towards what arts activists call “the arts monopolies”, were shifting towards more “small” and “micro” organizations, and in 2014 a funding stream, Thrive, with a more explicit diversity focus came into being.

What does not seem to have taken place was an intentional, transparent conversation about what this funding was for.

Like so many public funds, this one’s raison d'etre evolved over time, or rather, split in two, with competing agendas running parallel, at first with no real conflict.

A colliding of these agendas was foreseeable and inevitable, but it doesn’t seem like Mayors or Commissioners wanted to ask the hard question of “what’s the money really for?”

The time when this long-coming collision happened was in July 2023, when Director Singh, faced with a budget shortfall, asked the Arts Commissioners to make a choice between funding one of these agendas vs another.

Those majority white commissioners, when given the option to do otherwise, at first voted to deprioritize the “cultural staples” that Virgil mentioned. Only after they were told (incorrectly it seems) that this was illegal, did they reverse their vote.

You can certainly see an argument being made that “yes, the design and intent evolved, but we should honor the original design.” It’s a localized view of "originalism”, the logic that says documents like the US Constitution should be understood to mean precisely what they meant when they were written.

This leads to patently absurd outcomes like that “women are not covered by the ‘Due Process Amendment’ of the Constitution because they were not considered citizens when it was written.” A patently absurd argument, put forth by recent Supreme Court Justice Antonin Scalia.

If we can agree that this wasn’t the original design of these funds, which I think we need to, we next need to ask: “how do we redesign the purpose of things?” and ideally do it in a way that isn’t such a shit show.

Virgil’s Rich Text

Like I said, Virgil has a way different reading on “what happened” than I do, and which I believe the public record supports. There’s a natural question then which is why does Virgil see things differently than me?

He appears to be a good writer and researcher with a liberal arts background, so I have to ask, what is preventing him from accepting this pretty obvious view of things?

It doesn’t seem like this is an information gap, but rather an ideology gap. It’s not that we’re seeing different information, it’s that the information legitimately appears different to us because of our different ideological vantage points.

Here is a brief aside about my view of what ideology is all about:

Ideology can be understood as a necessary framework that individuals and societies use to make sense of the world. It encompasses a set of beliefs, values, and principles that provide orientation and meaning to human experiences and actions. Ideologies can vary widely, from liberal to conservative to leftist and beyond, each offering distinct perspectives on social, political, and economic matters.

Rather than being inherently negative, ideologies are essential for organizing thoughts, guiding behaviors, and fostering collective understanding within diverse societies.

In their early stages, these different ideologies often appear incompatible, clashing over fundamental principles and priorities. However, as they mature, they begin to incorporate more complex, nuanced understandings of human experience. In this mature state, ideologies can coexist more harmoniously, recognizing the value in different perspectives and finding common ground. This evolution allows for a richer, more integrative approach to addressing the diverse challenges of society.

I appreciate Virgil’s writing because it is ideologically honest (which is a thing entirely different than accurate or morally defensible).

Virgil’s writing on Metro Arts is couched in reactionary rhetoric, sharing screenshots of Metro Arts powerpoints about anti-colonialism like he’s leaking the Pentagon Papers. I would say he’s telling on himself by revealing how alarming he finds anti-colonialism, but this isn’t a secret he wants to keep about himself.

This is how he starts an essay about why he founded The Pamphleteer, entitled: “A Tennesseean finds adventure on the streets of his hometown, Nashville.”

While I might fantasize about prospecting for rare earth minerals in Africa with an AK-47 slung over my shoulder in the blazing Saharan heat or some other extreme adventure, I also have come to the realization that such a fantasy doesn’t suit me or mesh with the world I inhabit. In prior ages, there might’ve been wars worth fighting, land to conquer, untrammeled wilderness to civilize, or distant colonies to oversee. Today, not so much.

Virgil unabashedly presents his fantasies about conquering lands and ruling subjugated people as a conspicuous rejection of the memo that being openly “pro colonialism” isn’t great for your online dating game in 2024.

There’s a teen boyishness to these fantasies that seem detached from any real analysis of what it means to be in war, to oppress a people against their will, to “do an empire” as it were. His daydreams feel inspired by pulp novels like The Flashman Papers, wherein veritable rakes and scamps trod their way over the “uncivilized world”, rife with models of manhood that would quickly get called “toxic masculinity” these days.

I’m not one to say what media or daydreams people should have, and you'll have to pry my copies of the toxic masculine canon (Roadhouse & RoboCop and the like) out of my cold dead hands. But there is a time to set aside childish things, and it’s hard to not read his writing and feel like Virgil is not only nostalgic to the heyday of colonialism but is anti-anti-colonialism in a way that’s hard to find… offputting.

Virgil “accused” the director of having “surrounded himself with a cadre of radical consultants and has pledged to re-envision Metro Arts as an anti-racist organization committed to decolonization.”

He presented to the public “revelations” that a consultant for Metro Arts produced a presentation that “outlines a vision advocating the organization’s adoption of ‘antiracist planning’ to create ‘antiracist outcomes’” as if it’s a smoking gun uncovering a nefarious agenda to… end racism?

I know a lot of my leftist friends would say “yeah, he’s a racist. End of story. Stop talking about it.” And buddy, I don’t know you, and that well may be true, but in my experience, these things are a bit more complicated and there’s a lot for all of us to gain by taking these things seriously. If for no other reason than that plenty of other people take these things seriously.

So what we learn by reading deeply and responding to his essay “A Tennesseean finds adventure on the streets of his hometown, Nashville? I’ll see if I can unpack it and add some context to it, and perhaps Virgil will see himself in my description. Perhaps not. Let’s get it a shot.

A Nashville native, Davis became invested in the power of local media in Palo Alto, and founded the Pamphleteer with a raison d'etre aligned with the mission of the 2022 National Conservatism Conference he attended — a mission to halt the “‘homogenizing, locality-destroying imperium’ bent on global domination.”

Virgil, like I said, is perhaps an aspirant of the Peter Thiel school of “new right” media conservatives whose τέλος (ultimate purpose) is to combat what they see as the threat of an ideological and spiritual “flattening” of the human project.

That cohort believes that this threat has taken the form lately of equity-centered, anti-colonialist anti-racism. This is, as Nietzche would put it, the object of their “ressentiment”, the perceived source of existential frustration.

The forces of “homogenizing” liberalism is vividly present in their minds as an omniscient “imperium” that is morally degrading to the “Western” project of culture.

The liberalism of modern life is a neutering, stultifying imposition that is antithetical to life itself, this line of thinking goes.

The liberal constraints on speech and behavior, and emphasis on equity and is forcing us to divorce from forces that make life the divine trauma that is existence.

To snag some quotes from Nietzsche that feel relevant:

Man, the bravest of animals and the one most accustomed to suffering, does not repudiate suffering as such; he desires it, he even seeks it out, provided he is shown a meaning for it, a purpose of suffering.
-The Geneology of Morals

A living being seeks above all to discharge its strength. Life itself is will to power; self-preservation is only the indirect and most frequent result.

The threat of diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives is not, for a lot of these media figures, primarily an issue of miscegenation and the decline of white supremacy (though there are tremendous overlaps in these concerns, and this makes for quite strange ideological bedfellows).

For some people like Vigil, the concept of "equity" is perceived as an existential spiritual threat to the very fabric of cultural meaning and moral reality. Much like the ideological “mind viruses” of socialism and communism before it, the movement toward social equity is seen as pushing society further away from what is considered the source of human greatness: suffering.

If everyone is given equal social status, if there is no meritocracy to climb and bloody yourself on the way up, if there is no hierarchy to crush and in turn to be overthrown, the spiritual engine of humanity will grind to a halt.

For that noble cycle to continue, the logic goes, someone must sit on the throne atop the pyramid of society — not out of self-interest, but to perpetuate Plato’s Noble Lie that there’s an order to it all.

The lie claims that some are born to rule while others are destined to serve. However, as Socrates, Virgil, and other intelligent thinkers have long understood, the notion that some people are inherently superior is false. It’s a myth we have to perpetuate to keep back the chaos. And what’s the egalitarian alternative? Denmark? Hell no.

That’s the threat of DEI to this brand of conservatism. It’s not so much about the dissolution of the white ethnostate as it is about preserving the ideological underpinnings that go much further back than the invention of race.

There are explicit white supremacists who argue for keeping the status quo. But there are also supporters of the status quo who are primarily just supremacists.

Supremacism is much more common and rampant than explicit racism and has far deeper roots in human psychology. While racism, a subcategory of supremacism, requires the psychosocial category of “race” (a complex moving target of socially constructed signifiers), supremacism is merely the belief that some people are, in some ineffable way just… better.

If promoting an “anti-DEI” agenda happens to be in the class and racial interest of its loudest mouthpieces, that’s just a convenient coincidence, they might say. Or perhaps it is a divine prerequisite for the will-to-power of history’s next great-souled men.

To my eyes, Virgil is one of a growing number of media figures who is struggling to merge the cynical neo-conservative “political rationalism” of thinkers like Leo Strauss with the much more fashionable (and commercially viable) metamodern “ironic sincerity”.

I say “struggling” because there’s always a flimsiness, a “don’t look behind the curtain” to the curtain of their dialectical enemy, the source of their “ressentiment.”

Immature Conservatism is in its essence reactionary, reacting against whatever the latest form of change that diverts us from our better past ways of doing things. It’s an urgent call to return to the way things were “before”, before the fall from Eden, back when America was Great. This mental framework requires an “other” for it to make coherent sense of its oppositional nature, and to scapegoat that other for pulling us away from our ideal state.

Conservatism is not bad. In its mature form, the conservative perspective is essential for providing stability and continuity amidst rapid societal changes. It values traditions, cultural heritage, and established social structures, ensuring that new ideas are integrated thoughtfully without discarding the valuable lessons of the past. This balance between innovation and continuity fosters a resilient and adaptable society, harmonizing the dynamic impulses of liberal and leftist ideologies.

Everything outside of that — do you really need it? What is it giving you? If the answer is just “clicks”, think hard about that…

Some Feedback for Leftists Too

I’ve been railing against a form of immature conservatism for a while, and I feel like we need to even the criticism score a bit.

Immature leftism, much like its liberal and conservative counterparts, can be characterized by a range of attributes that ultimately undermine its potential to create meaningful and sustainable change. At its core, immature leftism often manifests as a rigid, dogmatic adherence to ideological purity, an oversimplified view of social dynamics, and an inclination towards adversarial politics.

Key Characteristics of Immature Leftism:

  1. Ideological Purity: Immature leftism frequently insists on strict adherence to a set of ideological principles, often leading to exclusionary practices. This purism can result in infighting and factionalism, where minor differences in belief or strategy are magnified, leading to fragmentation within the movement. The insistence on purity can also alienate potential allies who do not conform to every aspect of the ideology.

  2. Oversimplified Narratives: This form of leftism tends to view complex social issues through a black-and-white lens, simplifying the causes and solutions to systemic problems. It often reduces societal dynamics to a binary struggle between oppressors and the oppressed, failing to acknowledge the nuances and complexities of social, economic, and cultural factors.

  3. Adversarial Politics: Immature leftism often adopts a combative stance, viewing political engagement as a zero-sum game. This leads to an emphasis on opposition rather than collaboration, where the focus is on defeating perceived enemies rather than building broad-based coalitions for change. This adversarial approach can result in polarization and hinder constructive dialogue with those holding different views.

  4. Victimhood Mentality: While acknowledging and addressing systemic injustices is crucial, immature leftism can sometimes become overly fixated on a narrative of victimhood. This focus can obscure the agency and resilience of marginalized groups and create a disempowering dynamic where individuals see themselves primarily as victims rather than active agents of change.

  5. Short-Term Focus: Immature leftism often prioritizes immediate, tangible victories over long-term, systemic change. This can manifest in a preference for protests and direct actions that garner attention and mobilize supporters but may not lead to sustainable policy changes. Without a strategic vision for long-term transformation, efforts can dissipate without achieving lasting impact.

  6. Moral Absolutism: There is often a tendency towards moral absolutism, where positions are seen as unequivocally right or wrong, good or evil. This rigidity can stifle debate and critical thinking within the movement, as dissenting views are not tolerated. It can also alienate those who might otherwise support progressive causes but feel uncomfortable with the moral certitude and lack of openness to dialogue.

Moving Towards Maturity:

To evolve beyond these limitations, leftism must embrace a more mature approach that integrates complexity, fosters inclusivity, and seeks sustainable change. This involves:

  • Embracing Complexity: Recognizing the multifaceted nature of social issues and avoiding overly simplistic narratives. This means understanding that solutions require nuanced approaches that consider a variety of factors and perspectives.

  • Building Coalitions: Shifting from an adversarial stance to one of collaboration, seeking common ground with diverse groups and working together towards shared goals.

  • Fostering Empowerment: Highlighting the agency and potential of marginalized groups, promoting narratives of resilience and empowerment rather than solely focusing on victimhood.

  • Strategic Vision: Developing long-term strategies for systemic change that go beyond immediate actions, focusing on sustainable policies and practices.

  • Open Dialogue: Encouraging open, critical discussions within the movement, allowing for a diversity of views and the evolution of ideas.

By addressing these aspects, leftism can mature into a more effective and inclusive force for social transformation, capable of working alongside mature liberalism and conservatism to create a more just and sustainable society.

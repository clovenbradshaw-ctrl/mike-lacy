LAST WEEK,
while hundreds gathered around Nashville, holding candles and photographs, in vigil for Renee Good, Mayor Freddie O’Connell’s administration filed four resolutions with Metro Council to accept $2.7 million in surveillance and policing equipment from the Nashville Downtown Partnership. The funding for it came from a state grant that is tied to immigration crackdowns in Memphis and across Tennessee in alignment with the White House’s agenda.

Council votes on the resolutions Jan 20.

Included in the donations was $150k for 15 surveillance cameras from firm Axis Communications, precisely the same cameras from the same company, enabling the same “surveillance state” O’Connell had urged Nashville to avoid.

In 2021, O’Connell linked to an article about New Orleans leveraging this technology in an integrated system and said: “Strong Communities are better for safety than a surveillance state.”

On January 7th, an ICE agent shot Renee Good three times as she sat in her car on a residential Minneapolis street. She was thirty-seven years old, a U.S. citizen, a poet, a mother. Witnesses said she was trying to drive away. The Department of Homeland Security said she was “weaponizing” her vehicle. Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey watched the video and told reporters: “I want to tell everybody directly that is bullshit.”

The response from the Mayor’s Office in Nashville was not to distance itself from the growing surveillance and policing state that is enabling ICE.

O’Connell’s ongoing surveillance concessions to the far right’s demand have gone unnoticed by Nashville’s US Congressman Andy Ogles. That week, as Renee Good blood was still wet, Ogles posted on X.com:

Minneapolis Mayor Frey and Nashville Mayor O’Connell are cut from the same cloth. It’s deeper than rhetoric, its political decisions intended to betray ICE and Federal partners. ICE agents are in danger because of them. Illegal aliens are NOT our friends. They should be arrested immediately.

O’Connell is useful as a villain on 2nd tier Fox News shows and congressional X.com, where he plays the ersatz moderator of r/Antifa in MAGA minds that worry themselves about what the Mayors in midsized markets are doing. Meanwhile on Bluesky and in Council meetings, O’Connell is the surveillance Mayor, fighting harder for cameras and a tank than sidewalks and permanent supportive housing.

Many things happened at once last week.

  • The same day Renee Good was killed by an ICE officer in broad daylight, this Substack published an article documenting how Mayor O’Connell’s Chief of Operations had helped build ICE’s data pipeline at LexisNexis.

  • Also on Jan 7th, the Nashville Banner reported crime rates have been steadily decreasing, “Homicides fell to their lowest level in a decade, while robberies and burglaries were as rare as they’ve been since the 1960s.”

  • Music City Migra Watch, the volunteer group that monitors ICE activity in Middle Tennessee, organized the next morning’s vigil. Councilmember Terry Vo, who chairs the Metro Council Immigrant Caucus, addressed the crowd: “Renee mattered and she was murdered.”

These events move around each other like billiard balls, clanking and careening off each other with a terrible velocity, and an ominous spinning — spinning as if a black hole sat at the center of their table. These occurrences, orbiting around each other, events that should have absorbed entire news cycles in their own right, loop around one other, gaining speed in their epicycles, falling ever downwards, towards to an inevitable collapse into nothingness.

Or perhaps not.

How do we break a terrible trajectory?

How are we all, you and I, feeding into this?

How are our own, your’s and mine, unhealed wounds at the center of the collapsing star in the heart of our society?

How do we know we’ve crossed the event horizon, passing into truly dark times from which there is no escape?

How many feel they’ve already crossed into it?

What we know about black holes is that they absorb all things, even light.

And so, it seems fair to say, that as long as we see the light of humanity, even in those who are knowingly or unknowingly complicit, there is hope.

Not naive hope that, with enough exhortation and “cooling out”, that people in power will “wake up” to the real them and change. Not the hope of a child, raised by stunted adults, that prays their real parents will appear, descending from heaven with a shout, solving the world’s problems with one weird trick.

But real, matured hope. Hope in the emergent potential of humanity to always find community in the darkness, and joy in equal measure with grief, and faith in the unnameable spirit of the now that has, for reasons beyond reason, placed us here in this place/time for good reason.

In what ways am I committed to the darkest timeline and unconsciously adding pressure to the system and not merely pulling relief of speaking truth? In what ways can I let go of all my desire to control, and surrender myself to the will of truth?

The only thing that any of us can sincerely hope to exercise is a question. It is the one indestructible facet of the search. You cannot lie and question simultaneously.

I.

Crime is going down in Nashville. Homicide at a decade low. Robberies as rare as they were during the Nixon administration. There is no clear need for heavy investment in surveillance and policing technology, at least not from a genuine public safety perspective.

Yet the Mayor’s Office is losing all credibility and cache to get policing and surveillance tech into MNPD’s hands using State money, and not afraid what bridges they burn to get there.

Why?

The equipment the Mayor’s Office filed for includes fifteen cameras from Axis Communications, priced at $10,000 per unit.

In October 2021, Council Member O’Connell shared The Lens investigation of New Orleans’ surveillance network. The article he linked documented Axis cameras priced between $5,500 and $9,200 per unit.

Same vendor. Same price. He is now asking Council to purchase precisely what he warned against.

On the tweet about the surveillance tech in NOLA, O’Connell wrote: “Let’s not let fear drive Nashville to follow New Orlean’s path.”

Since O’Connell appears to be the one pushing so hard for surveillance, what, exactly, is he afraid of?

II.

The Nashville Downtown Partnership’s grant comes from Tennessee’s Violent Crime Intervention Fund, a state program distributing surveillance funding statewide. Memphis received $75 million from the same fund. The National Guard is currently deployed there to support federal immigration enforcement.

The equipment before Council, split across four resolutions:

  1. 15 Axis Video Cameras ($150,000) — “traditional video cameras, consistent with existing public safety cameras”

  2. 1 Terradyne Gurkha MPV Armored Rescue Vehicle ($430,000) — “ballistic protection for SWAT members for city wide call outs on barricaded subjects, hostage situations”

  3. 2 ATC Trailer 48’ Custom Command/Office Trailers + 2 Ford F-550s ($2,000,000) — “mobile meeting space and technology support for law enforcement incident management”

  4. 1 International CV Command/Equipment Truck ($800,000) — “self-contained vehicle for incident commands to transport and store equipment used for critical incidents”

None of the four resolutions reference Metro Code 13.08.080, the surveillance technology ordinance requiring a public hearing and civil liberties review before Council can accept such equipment.

The ordinance covers “closed-circuit television cameras” and “video and audio monitoring and/or recording technology.” The Axis cameras are both.

The ordinance covers “any system, device, or vehicle that is equipped with an electronic surveillance device.” The Terradyne Gurkha—an armored vehicle that in comparable jurisdictions and price ranges comes equipped with thermal imaging and surveillance systems—falls squarely within this definition.

The ordinance covers technology used for “gathering, storing, or analyzing” surveillance data. The mobile command trailers are explicitly designed for “technology support for law enforcement incident management”—surveillance integration platforms that aggregate camera feeds. They are the definition of what the ordinance was written to govern.

Every item in these resolutions appears to trigger the ordinance. Not one. Not some. All of them.

O’Connell’s Director of Legislative Affairs is Dave Rosenberg. He introduced this ordinance in 2017. It passed 25-2. As a Council Member, Rosenberg fought against license plate readers and proposed thirty-minute data retention limits. He wrote the law that requires public hearings for precisely this kind of equipment.

If anyone in the administration should recognize that these resolutions require invoking the ordinance, it is him.

Why has the administration offered no explanation for why the ordinance was not invoked?

III.

To understand why this equipment is arriving now requires understanding what has happened to every layer of accountability above and around the city.

Start with the state.

Tennessee’s Information Protection Act took effect July 1, 2025. The law eliminates any private right of action for data privacy violations. If a surveillance company shares your location data, camera footage, or license plate reads with federal authorities, you cannot sue.

Section 47-18-3208(a)(3) permits data sharing whenever a company has “reasonable belief” that it might help investigate a potential violation of “federal, state, or local laws, rules, or regulations.” No warrant. No subpoena. No external authorization. Just the company’s own judgment.

And Section 4 preempts any conflicting local ordinance. Nashville cannot pass a law requiring surveillance vendors to restrict data sharing with federal agencies. The state has already prohibited it.

Only one person can investigate TIPA violations: the Attorney General.

Before any enforcement action, he must give companies sixty days’ written notice and an “opportunity to cure.” If the company claims it fixed the problem, no action proceeds.

The person who occupies this role is Jonathan Skrmetti. He has joined twenty-four states supporting federal prosecution of anyone who “encourages” illegal immigration. He has joined twenty-three states challenging birthright citizenship, arguing that the Fourteenth Amendment’s authors would have found it “strange for somebody to get American citizenship just because they happen to be here when they were born.”

Skrmetti began his career in the Justice Department’s Civil Rights Division, Criminal Section. He prosecuted sex traffickers, corrupt officials, and violent white supremacists. In 2010, Eric Holder gave him the Distinguished Service Award.

How does the man who once hunted civil rights violators come to ensure that no Tennessee resident can sue when their data flows to deportation agencies?

IV.

The federal pressure on the mayor is explicit and public—and largely theatrical. But in the world of reality star as commander in chief, theatrical does not mean not real.

Good's killing was at least the sixteenth time immigration agents had opened fire since Trump launched his enforcement campaign. At least four others had already died during deportation operations—one fatally shot in a Chicago suburb, two struck by vehicles while fleeing, one who fell during a raid.

“Minneapolis Mayor Frey and Nashville Mayor O’Connell are cut from the same cloth,” Ogles posted. Then: “Pray for our ICE patriots.”

The accusation centered on “doxxing” ICE agents. What actually happened: Metro had an executive order requiring transparency about federal law enforcement activity—an order the administration had largely ignored. In May 2025, after ICE raids, Metro finally complied and posted a spreadsheet of law enforcement contacts that happened to include ICE agent names. The official responsible was fired that same day—not for the posting, but for incompetence in handling sexual harassment complaints.

The names of public employees performing public functions are not confidential. The “doxxing” charge was absurd on its face. Ogles knew this. DHS Secretary Noem knew this. They made the accusation anyway.

Immigrant advocates, meanwhile, saw the transparency posting as too little, too late. Shortly after the May raids, the mayor’s office announced a “Belonging Fund” with the Community Foundation of Middle Tennessee to support affected families—in a press conference separate from the one held by Metro Council’s Immigrant Caucus. O’Connell has since distanced himself from the fund. There has been no public accounting of who, if anyone, has received support. The Office of New Americans has sat vacant for the better part of a year—during the most extreme federal assault on immigrant communities since Japanese internment.

O’Connell is being attacked by the right for resistance he hasn’t mounted, and justly criticized by advocates for support he hasn’t provided. The pressure is real. The accusations of his radical leftist resistance are pure theater.

Ogles represents Tennessee’s 5th Congressional District. His website says he has “deep roots dating back to the founding of our state.” He claims to have overseen anti-trafficking operations “in several countries” as COO of Abolition International. NewsChannel 5 obtained the nonprofit’s tax records: Ogles worked part-time for a total of $4,000.

In August 2024, FBI agents executed a search warrant on Ogles and seized his phone. The investigation concerns allegedly falsified campaign finance reports—amended filings that claimed a $320,000 personal loan to his campaign that may never have existed. The House Ethics Committee extended its inquiry into the 119th Congress.

The congressman calling for the mayor’s arrest is himself under federal investigation.

In July 2025, Noem held a press conference at Nashville’s airport to announce “dozens more” ICE agents deploying to the city. She declared that O’Connell “doesn’t deserve to be mayor.” Ogles subsequently requested that House committees investigate O’Connell for “obstruction.”

The pressure is documented. The accusations are baseless. The question is what effect all of it is having on policy.

V.

Masami Tyson had spent 2018-2022 as Tennessee’s Global Director of Foreign Direct Investment under Governor Bill Lee. Before that, Speaker Cameron Sexton had appointed her to the Metro Nashville Airport Authority board as part of the state’s attempted takeover of airport governance. The courts blocked the takeover.

The same Sexton who appointed Tyson is the one coordinating with the White House on immigration enforcement.

O’Connell’s Chief of Operations, Kristin Wilson, coordinated the administration’s earlier $15 million surveillance MOU with the Nashville Downtown Partnership—before public outcry forced them to pivot to these straight resolutions to accept the donations.

Before joining Metro, Wilson oversaw the 2008 integration of ChoicePoint into LexisNexis. That merger folded together Pinkerton’s labor surveillance apparatus, the Florida contractor behind the 2000 voter purge that disenfranchised thousands of eligible voters, and Latin American databases that Mexican prosecutors alleged were criminally stolen.

LexisNexis Risk Solutions now holds ICE’s $22.1 million contract to identify incarcerated immigrants in jurisdictions that don’t share data voluntarily.

How does the person who coordinated Nashville’s surveillance expansion come to be the one who helped build the infrastructure ICE uses to circumvent local data-sharing restrictions?

VII.

New Orleans shows what comes next.

A nonprofit called Project NOLA operates approximately 5,000 cameras across that city—about 200 equipped with AI facial recognition that scans faces in real time. The cameras sit on private property: homes, businesses, churches. Because Project NOLA is a nonprofit on private land, the city has limited authority over what happens to the data.

ICE is among the twenty-one law enforcement agencies with access to the Jefferson Parish Real-Time Crime Center, which coordinates with Project NOLA’s network.

The New Orleans Police Department recently suspended its partnership with Project NOLA after a Washington Post investigation revealed officers had been “secretly relying” on the system—circumventing a 2022 city ordinance that imposed strict guardrails on police facial recognition use. The guardrails became irrelevant once the data flowed through private hands to federal agencies with no obligation to respect local policy.

When Louisiana’s governor launched “Operation Catahoula Crunch” in December—a sweeping immigration enforcement action—the surveillance infrastructure was already in place.

New Orleans City Council President J.P. Morrell: “There’s literally no information being given to the city of New Orleans whatsoever.”

Councilmember O’Connell was right to warn Nashville about this in 2021. How could he have known he would be the one bringing the city to the same threshold?

His tenure has been procedural: COVID special sessions targeting Nashville’s authority, rules limiting floor debate, the 2023 expulsion of Representatives Pearson and Jones.

After the Covenant School protest, Sexton compared the demonstration to January 6th: “equivalent, maybe worse, depending on how you look at it.”

Tennessee Lookout has described him as “the Dick Cheney to Bill Lee’s George W. Bush.”

Governor Lee signs what arrives at his desk. He signed TIPA. He signed the abortion ban, the permitless carry law, the bills preempting Nashville’s home rule authority. He supported the expulsion vote.

Maria Lee had been close friends with two of the Covenant School victims. One had been expected for dinner at the governor’s mansion on the day of the shooting.

Lee is a seventh-generation Tennessean. His wife Carol Ann died in a horseback riding accident on their farm in 2000. The following year, his daughter was hospitalized after a self-inflicted gunshot wound.

“I’ve really come to believe,” he has said, “that to the degree that we are emptied, we can experience fullness.”

He describes his entry into politics as “a calling.” He has created an Office of Faith-Based and Community Initiatives. He does not run attack ads.

Does everyone in this story believe they are acting appropriately?

X.

Then there is the ambient pressure—the weather system that requires no coordination.

Oracle’s Nashville headquarters represents a $254 million land purchase and $240 million in state incentives. Construction had stalled for years. It resumed the month TIPA took effect.

Oracle’s founder, Larry Ellison, proposed national ID cards with biometric identifiers the day after September 11th, 2001. Last September, he told an audience: “Citizens will be on their best behavior because we’re constantly recording everything. That’s good.”

In February, at the World Government Summit: “We need to unify all the national data, put it into a database where it’s easily consumable by the AI model.”

In July 2024, Oracle paid $115 million to settle a lawsuit alleging the company created unauthorized “digital dossiers” on hundreds of millions of people.

The company that received Digital China’s “Partner of the Year” award in 2018—the same year mass detention reports emerged from Xinjiang—is building its American headquarters in a state whose data privacy law has no private enforcement mechanism.

This is not conspiracy. It is alignment. But what does it mean that Oracle did not need to coordinate with anyone—that it simply needed to wait for the legal environment to match its business model?

XI.

O’Connell’s Director of Legislative Affairs is Dave Rosenberg. He introduced Metro’s surveillance technology ordinance in 2017. It passed 25-2. As a councilmember, Rosenberg fought against license plate readers and proposed thirty-minute data retention limits.

The resolutions before Council do not invoke his ordinance.

O’Connell spent twelve years navigating Nashville without a car. He rose through neighborhood associations and transit advocacy before winning a Council seat in 2015 and the mayor’s race in 2023 with 64% of the runoff vote.

In 2021, he warned Nashville not to follow New Orleans.

Now the pressure arrives from every direction at once: federal threats of investigation, state preemption of local authority, a Chief of Staff appointed by the same Speaker coordinating with Miller, a Chief of Operations who built the infrastructure ICE uses to circumvent sanctuary policies, a legal environment where no resident can sue over data sharing, and an Attorney General who has made clear which side he is on.

When the outcome aligns with pressures applied publicly and repeatedly, what room is left for choice?

XII.

Metro Council votes January 20th.

The administration is betting Council won’t notice the ordinance wasn’t invoked—or won’t care.

But Council members should understand what they are actually voting for: surveillance infrastructure arriving at the precise moment Tennessee has become a model for federal immigration enforcement, coordinated by an administration whose key staff have connections to both the state Republican apparatus and the federal deportation data pipeline, under a state law that preempts any meaningful local oversight.

Council can ask why the ordinance was not invoked. Council can defer until a public hearing is held. Council can require the civil liberties review the law contemplates.

Or Council can finalize acceptance without addressing any of it. Which will it be?

XIII.

There is a shape to this story that resists easy villains.

The system does not require bad actors. It requires alignment.

Federal immigration policy. State data privacy law. Corporate headquarters incentives. Municipal equipment donations. Personnel who move between Republican state government and Democratic city hall. They did not coordinate. They converged.

And at the center of that convergence sits a mayor who once warned against the surveillance state, now asking Council to accept surveillance equipment without the public hearing he once would have demanded.

The question is not whether anyone planned this.

The question is whether, once you see it, you can pretend you don’t.

The writer Mitch Horowitz: “The only thing that any of us can sincerely hope to exercise is a question. It is the one indestructible facet of the search. You cannot lie and question simultaneously.”

I have tried to ask questions here. I do not know if they are the right ones. I do not know if asking them helps or harms. I know only that the asking is the one thing I can do without lying—to myself, to the people I once worked with, to the city I still live in.

What will Council do on January 20th?

What will the mayor do when they ask why the ordinance wasn’t invoked?

What will any of us do when the cameras are watching and the data is flowing and the guardrails we thought we had turn out to be suggestions?

This is the record of what was visible before January 20th, 2026.

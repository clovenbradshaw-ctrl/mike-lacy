As I discussed last week, the winter storm eviction docket established to slow evictions for Nashvillians experiencing financial difficulties from winter storm Fern is not, as yet, going as planned.

Jennifer McCoy’s firm, the single highest-volume eviction attorney in Nashville, representing approximately one-third of all eviction filings, is allegedly not accepting any money on behalf of her tenants and is refusing to participate in diversion court at all.

From someone on-site on March 3 2026: Jennifer McCoy’s firm refuses to accept rental assistance payments and will not provide tenants with their outstanding balances, which would allow them to seek funding independently. Of the more than 200 cases on today’s docket, only two individuals were deemed eligible for storm relief, despite over a hundred defendants meeting the eligibility criteria.

I reached out yesterday to the firm for comment. I'll update you if I hear anything.

What we’re from those on the ground at General Session court hearing is that McCoy’s firm is dragging their feet on providing tenants with their outstanding balances — the very information a tenant needs in order to enter the diversion program. Let that sink in.

A landlord can haul you into eviction court without proving what you owe. But you can’t access the program designed to keep you housed unless the landlord’s attorney — the one being paid to evict you — tells you the number. This is so blatantly unjust, even by Tennessee standards, that I don’t think it will continue if sunshine is put onto it.

Allegations like this is why its important to have people observing what is happening in these systems.

Here’s the thing: many Americans have learned this past year, especially in these past few months, about the power of witnessing state sanctioned violence to help disrupt it. Well, when it comes to evictions, general sessions court is where state violence and laissez-faire capitalist exploitation get city hall married 5 days a week.

I will be at Eviction Court on Tuesday, and I hope to see you there too to be a witness to what our neighbors are going through. I’m not going to just watch bad things happen and complain about it. I am going to witness it and tell people about it, because I believe, because I know, that observation changes things.

In quantum physics there’s a core paradox: “the act of observation collapses the wave function” — things behave differently when they're being measured. Court Watch programs, around the world, work the same way. I’m not inviting you to court to watch unethical conduct by attorneys, working on behalf of unscrupulous landlords, to throw our neighbors on the street, to bum you out.

I’m inviting you to come change what is happening by observing it.

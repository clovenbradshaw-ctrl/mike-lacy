I grew up in a million-dollar home in one of the wealthiest counties in America outside of Washington DC. Things changed for us. The 2008 crash didn’t do my family any favors, and shortly after college, I was saddled with a stupid amount of debt and lived on food stamps.

The undergrad degree that put me in that debt was a bachelors in the “Western Classics”. It was a structured curriculum that mirrored how all “high class” people in the western world used to be educated, from the days of Ancient Greece to roughly WWII.

It’s given me a weird view of this concept “class.”

But if I’m honest — I think all Americans have weird views on the subject.

Of all the “-isms” one can be accused of, I think “classism” has the least impact when someone is called out for committing it these days. There’s so much consternation today around the threat of “being canceled” over being racist, misogynist, colonialist, white supremacist… but call someone “classist” and the reaction is “… what’s that mean again?”

In this post, I’m going to explore the relationship that Nashville’s art scene has with the subject of class, focusing on the Nashville Symphony’s history with Metro Arts funding in the 1990s. I’m going to talk about how the status of “real” arts organizations was discussed in contrast with “low-class” ones.

I’m not doing it just to pick on some people who said some un-PC things in the newspaper 30 years ago (though that is pretty fun).

I’m doing it because I think it can start a conversation that people like me need to have, a conversation we’ve been avoiding for the last 532 years.

I think this field trip to the 1990s, looking at how we talked (or didn’t talk) about class and social hierarchy in Nashville back then, can help us flesh out this haunted skeleton in our closet.

I don’t think we’re going to solve the problems that come from biases around wealth, poverty, education, and that vague notion of being cultured, until we talk more openly about class.

But there’s something very un-American about talking about this.

America’s always had a strange relationship with social hierarchy. Alexis de Tocqueville is quoted a lot when we ask things like: “What’s up with America’s vibe?” In history nerd and sociology circles, the Frenchman’s antebellum travelogue called “Democracy in America” is put forth like treatment notes from America’s collective psychoanalysis sessions.

Here’s what he said back then about our roughly 50-year-old nation and our relationship with class hierarchy: “Americans are so enamored of equality that they would rather be equal in slavery than unequal in freedom.”

There’s something in our cultural DNA that, on the surface, rejects the idea of social hierarchy. Calling bullshit on the aristocracy was fundamental to the revolution against the monarchic British Empire. Our Declaration’s first sentence posits that “all men are created equal”, though as we know, the use of “men” there was literal, and even that came with some serious asterisks.

A thing that became obvious to me when I visited Amsterdam is there’s a lot of Dutch influence on America’s vibe. The nation’s first capital, New York (originally New Amsterdam), absorbed an incalculable amount of the Netherlands' values and wove them deeply into our nation’s psychological fabric.

The Dutch are notoriously egalitarian. I was unsolicitously told by a canal tour guide that: “in Amsterdam, it’s OK to yell at your boss.” They deserve a lot of credit for why America is the way it is.

In Russell Shorto's "The Island at the Center of the World," he explains that in settling what’s now Manhattan, the Dutch imbued their colony in America with their unusual antipathy to rigid social hierarchy, rejecting the notion of inherited titles and consanguineous superiority.

Instead, power went to those who made things happen: businessmen and local magistrates. Over time, human nature being what it is, these men would create a kind of nobility, sometimes even buying titles from cash-poor foreigners, but this in itself underscores the point. Upward mobility was part of the Dutch character: if you worked hard and were smart, you rose in stature. Today that is a byword of a healthy society; in the seventeenth century it was weird.

Before the Industrial Revolution, changing social class was virtually impossible in Europe. The only time it was ever recorded to happen in a major way was when the Black Death killed up to 50% of the continent’s population (and suddenly there were job openings for feudal lords). America, in large part due to the Dutch, was different.

De Tocqueville again: “In America, it is not only that the rich are rich and the poor are poor, but that the rich become poor and the poor rich with an astonishing rapidity.”

But it’s wrong to say we didn’t have a class structure in America. The United States was as misogynist and patriarchal as anywhere in Europe. But also, our rags-to-riches self-mythologizing (always a little true and false at the same time), deludes us to this day. Like the countries many of us emigrated from, we do have a social hierarchy. We just put our own spin on it.

If the late 20th century saw an America that mistook itself as being “post-racial”, I’d say that from its founding, we’ve had a nation that’s incorrectly considered itself “post-classist.”

White colonizers like my ancestors, treated people they could “other” in ways that reveal that societal distinctions in America never disappeared; they were just transformed.

My ancestor, William Lacy, arrived in Jamestown twenty years after the first enslaved Africans. He came to the Americas roughly 17,000 years after the first immigrants, now the indigenous population.

Leaving England and Ireland behind, seeking to change his hereditary social status, how did he perceive his social status compared to these enslaved and native peoples who became his neighbors? I know he wasn't "woke" about it.

The emancipation of Black Americans from legal inferiority didn’t dissolve my peoples’ notion that there was meant to be a class hierarchy between the races. It was people like my great-great grandfather, an elected white Democrat in Tulsa, Oklahoma, who participated, or at best, looked on in 1921 as “Black Wall Street” was burned to the ground and at least 100 upwardly mobile Black Americans were murdered.

My family never talked about Tulsa, and I only learned of our connect a year ago.

And they never talked about “class”, about what it was they were so afraid of losing by someone else having it. But it clearly was something we cared deeply about having. We left our families in England, Ireland, and France to get it.

We left whatever hierarchy that was oppressing us behind, but clearly not the cultural firmware that built these system. While we “Americanized” the software and didn’t recreate society the same way, we did use race to create something worse, a racial caste system.

But of course things were not always amenable with people who'd eventually be classified together under the malleable term "white." I think way too often about this LBJ quote, something he said to Bill Moyers in 1960 when they were visiting Tennessee during his 1960 campaign for the Democratic nomination. Johnson had “spotted some ugly racial epithets scrawled on signs” while driving through the state.

I'll tell you what's at the bottom of it. If you can convince the lowest white man he's better than the best colored man, he won't notice you're picking his pocket. Hell, give him somebody to look down on, and he'll empty his pockets for you.

Like our Dutch founders that didn’t believe in formal social hierarchy, but weren’t opposed to buying titles of nobility from immigrant aristocrats short up for cash, we’ve always deluded ourselves that we treated our racial “equals” as equal, and used racial animus as to perpetuate class hide this.

The game LBJ is describing is such an American thing to do. Buy maybe we we hide it because its still somehow so anti-American.

Millions and millions came here and still do, from every corner, seeking what brought my people here. The ability to change our lot in life.

Many came for acute reasons (war and famine) looking for any safe harbor. Others nonconsensually, kidnapped into chattel slavery, held in conditions infinitely worse than any peasant or surf had ever experienced.

But most populations that come to North America, including indigenous peoples, at some point came here on purpose — and that may mean something.

There might be empirical evidence suggesting that Americans have a unique psychological relationship with social hierarchy, something we share with the indigenous populations we have historically both fetishized and subjected to genocide

Genes like the DRD4-7R allele may be genetic artifacts that self-select people to migrate to places of opportunity — and for many, that’s meant to become Americans. This genetic adaptation impacts the brain’s dopamine D4 receptor, and this increases rates of migration, risktaking, “wanderlust”, ADHD, and novelty seeking — all traits that push against the propensity for accepting a rigid status quo, like a class-based social structure.

This genetic variation occurs globally, but its highest rates are in the Americas, occurring the most in American indigenous populations, with a 48% prevalence. People who immigrate to The United States have about half that, but that’s still a very high rate globally (East Asia, by contrast, has a prevalence of these genes as low as 1-3%).

Indigenous peoples and colonizing populations both chose to leave their homelands for greener pastures in the Americas, the latter via ships starting in 1492, the former via land bridges in ~15,000 BCE. The genetic adaptations these populations share likely self-selected who was willing to take this journey into the unknown, leaving behind the social structure they were born into.

The science on this is still developing and shows correlation, not causation. Many people without this genetic mutation have the personality traits to leave their social structure, while many with the mutation don't reject their status quo. But I feel like there’s something here. If the Dutch are responsible for a hidden part of America’s cultural DNA, maybe our DNA is responsible for a part of it as well.

Maybe there really is something different about Americans and how we interact with the notions of class and social hierarchy. 

Maybe there’s something embedded deep into our DNA that says: 

“This aristocracy crap is bullshit,” 

and it is only our inherited cultural conditioning,
 
the internalized oppression we’ve carried from our homelands in epigenetic memory,

warping our collective unconscious with millennia of suffering as peasants and surfs,

waiting for the day we’d be on the other side of the short end of the stick 

and we’d get to inflict the suffering — 

maybe that’s what’s making us unconsciously perpetuate toxic forms of social hierarchy in ways we’re blind to, refuse to speak about, and are just un-American.

Maybe.

This is all a long walk to say, I think it’s time people like me talk more concretely about our notions of rank and hierarchy in our society. And to not make this a conversation about everything, I’m going to focus on the Nashville Symphony, a classy institution if ever Nashville had one.

y2="14"></line></svg></button></div></div></div></a></figure></div><h1>Be a Neighbor, not a N<strong>ai</strong>BOR</h1><p>On Friday, <a href="https://fox17.com/news/local/metro-police-mayor-renew-push-for-license-plate-readers-as-crime-drops-in-nashville">Mayor O’Connell and Chief Drake appeared together</a> to announce that crime in Nashville is <a href="https://www.wsmv.com/2026/04/03/nashville-crime-drops-20-2026-sexual-assault-reports-rise/">down 20% this year</a>. Violent crime down 18%. Motor vehicle theft down 40%. Burglary at its lowest level since the 1960s. Drake credited <a href="https://www.wsmv.com/2026/04/03/nashville-crime-drops-20-2026-sexual-assault-reports-rise/">a new vehicle crimes unit</a> — human investigators doing police work — as “the catalyst in driving crime.”</p><p>Then, at the same press conference, for some reason, they renewed their push for license plate readers.</p><div class="subscription-widget-wrap-editor" data-attrs="{&quot;url&quot;:&quot;https://readrichtext.substack.com/subscribe?&quot;,&quot;text&quot;:&quot;Subscribe&quot;,&quot;language&quot;:&quot;en&quot;}" data-component-name="SubscribeWidgetToDOM"><div class="subscription-widget show-subscribe"><div class="preamble"><p class="cta-caption">{Rich Text} is a reader-supported publication. To receive new posts and support my work, consider becoming a free or paid subscriber.</p></div><form class="subscription-widget-subscribe"><input type="email" class="email-input" name="email" placeholder="Type your email…" tabindex="-1"><input type="submit" class="button primary" value="Subscribe"><div class="fake-input-wrapper"><div class="fake-input"></div><div class="fake-button"></div></div></form></div></div><p>Drake argued Nashville needs cameras because clearance rates in cities with LPRs are “up 30, 28%. In Nashville, it’s 5%.” Fox17 reported that O’Connell “will continue making the case for their use.” Drake did not seem to understand that a drastic reduction in crime, despite a far smaller increase in case closing, actually makes a very strong the economic case for funding things <em>besides</em> law enforcement and surveillance to reduce crime. LPRs just seem to be on his mind.</p><p>A month ago, <a href="https://www.wsmv.com/2026/03/07/nashville-police-recommend-license-plate-readers-improve-school-safety-new-report/">MNPD recommended LPRs in a school safety report</a> following the Antioch High School shooting. Even O’Connell “was surprised to see that highlighted in response to the council resolution.” In October 2024, <a href="https://www.wsmv.com/2024/10/16/nashville-progresses-towards-license-plate-reader-approval-after-police-chiefs-calls-action/">Drake said LPRs would have helped solve a greenway murder</a>. When the only tool you're selling is a camera, every problem looks like a lack of footage.</p><p>The evidence for LPRs as a crime prevention tool is remarkably thin. The <a href="https://nij.ojp.gov/library/publications/do-license-plate-readers-enhance-initial-and-residual-deterrent-effects-police">National Institute of Justice</a> — the DOJ's own research arm — found "little clear evidence for the crime prevention efficacy of using LPRs" and that they "did not reduce the likelihood of a new crime or disorder call." <a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/08874034231220627">Academic reviews</a> confirm that "ALPRs have not produced clear evidence of providing a deterrent effect in the form of reducing crime." Flock's own marketing claim — an 80% burglary reduction in San Marino, California — <a href="https://newrepublic.com/article/206992/flock-safety-cameras-alpr-deflock-resistance-nationwide">was later found to be false, with burglaries actually increasing after cameras were installed</a>.</p><p>Flock’s studies are not peer-reviewed. Meanwhile, crime is falling nationally — the <a href="https://counciloncj.org/">Council on Criminal Justice</a> found homicides trending toward all-time lows across 40 major cities — and Nashville is <a href="https://www.wsmv.com/2026/04/03/nashville-crime-drops-20-2026-sexual-assault-reports-rise/">down 20% this year</a> without the much soft after LPR expansion, with Drake himself crediting <a href="https://www.wsmv.com/2026/04/03/nashville-crime-drops-20-2026-sexual-assault-reports-rise/">a vehicle crimes unit made of human investigators</a> as "the catalyst." LPRs help recover stolen cars. That's the one thing the evidence supports. Everything else — the deterrence, the prevention, the clearance rate claims — is marketing from a <a href="https://www.cnbc.com/2025/06/10/flock-safety-cnbc-disruptor-50.html">$7.5 billion company</a> that needs your fear to hit its next revenue target.</p><p>So why, exactly, are Chief Drake and his senior deputies so fixated on surveillance technology? What if there’s <em>actually an answer?</em></p><div><hr></div><p>A forthcoming public records request for MNPD’s <a href="https://www.nashville.gov/departments/metro-clerk/disclosures/disclosure-statement">annual disclosure filings</a> is attempting to answer if anyone wearing blue is working as unregistered lobbyist, <a href="https://theintercept.com/2025/09/02/atlanta-seattle-police-axon-fusus-surveillance/">like this cop was in Atlanta was</a> for Axon, the company that pushed to get Fusus in Nashville timestamp and an address that isn’t their home makes your stomach turn — sit with that. That is exactly what it feels like to be on the other side of the lens. To discover that you are just another variable in someone else’s risk model.</p><p><strong><a href="https://clovenbradshaw-ctrl.github.io/naibor/">NaiBOR</a></strong> is a satirical website. It’s a joke. It’s not a real app and it shouldn’t be. But we could totally make it. I legitimately could, and it would be dirt cheap compared to what the city is being charged. But I bet that seems like a bad idea to a lot of people who are trying to install these systems.</p><p>The fact that it seems like a bad idea if the community makes it, but would “be totally fine” if the government had it, should tell you something. Why do conservatives suddenly trust the government implicitly? Why are liberals so fine with corporations tracking their whereabouts?</p><p>Could it have something to do with the fact that Axon's SEC filings show <a href="https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001069183/000106918324000006/axon-20231231x10k.htm">$92 million in annual sales and marketing spending</a>? Ghat Flock reported <a href="https://www.opensecrets.org/federal-lobbying/clients/reports?cycle=2025&amp;id=D000116304">$690,000 in federal lobbying</a> and <a href="https://portersfiveforce.com/blogs/marketing-strategy/axon">"sophisticated LinkedIn advertising aimed precisely at police chiefs and city procurement officers"</a>? That the two companies most relevant to Nashville's surveillance debate are spending an estimated $150–200 million a year on sales and marketing, aimed at the same people, Nashville residents get two minutes of public comment to reach?</p><p>What are the odds that no one working for MNPD <em>isn’t being paid to tell us we need to buy this?</em> I look forward to the release of MNPD disclosure forms to confirm whether or not this is happening.</p><p>Here’s the thing: the people on the other side of the real cameras didn’t get a satirical website to warn them. They got a <a href="https://www.404media.co/floridas-wildlife-cops-are-searching-thousands-of-flock-cameras-for-ice/">wildlife cop in Florida running their plate for ICE</a>. They got an <a href="https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/10/flock-safety-and-texas-sheriff-claimed-license-plate-search-was-missing-person-it">officer searching 83,000 cameras because they had an abortion</a>. They got a <a href="https://fox11online.com/news/crime/menasha-police-officer-accused-of-using-license-plate-recognition-system-to-track-his-ex-girlfriend-cristian-morales-misconduct-in-office-flock-domestic-abuse-restraining-order-cash-bond">cop who couldn’t sleep tracking their car at 2 AM</a>.</p><p>It is extraordinarily difficult to believe that officials have residents’ best interests at heart when they push for more invasive surveillance equipment while the crime rate is dropping — and while we know these corporations are actively paying police officers to sell their products.</p><p>I’m increasingly part of national conversations with surveillance researchers who are watching America’s law enforcement become weaponized salespeople for the surveillance industry. It is deeply embarrassing, dangerous, and shameful.</p><p>I’m not saying I have any evidence that this is how our fine officers in MNPD blue behave. But if NaiBOR were real, I’m sure we could find out who our law enforcement is really working for.</p><p>And not to do MNPD any PR favors, but a heads up: noting that crime rates are dropping while your case closure rate trails other cities is about as strong an argument for police abolition as anyone could make. If crime is dropping (without expanded surveillance) and MNPD isn’t solving many more cases, the most likely explanation is that the crime rate has little to do with policing — and that we should be spending a good chunk of the police budget on the things that actually reduce crime.</p><p>Since Nashville doesn’t have a massive LPR deployment, we already know it isn’t surveillance gear making us safer. The data is in.

On the same day Tennessee House Speaker Cameron Sexton told Knox News that the state is working directly with Stephen Miller to become "a model" for immigration enforcement—with 58 law enforcement agencies now signed onto ICE's 287(g) program as of January 8th—Mayor Freddie O'Connell's administration submitted four resolutions to accept $2.7 million in police surveillance equipment from the Nashville Downtown Partnership. Among them: 15 cameras from Axis Communications at $10,000 per unit.

That's the same vendor and comparable price point to the New Orleans system O'Connell warned against in October 2021, when he shared a Lens investigation and wrote that "strong communities are better for safety than a surveillance state." None of the four resolutions reference Metro Code 13.08.080—the surveillance ordinance that requires a public hearing and civil liberties review before Council can accept such donations.

Mayor O’Connell’s Chief of Staff, Masami Tyson, was hired by Republican Commissioner Bob Rolfe to serve as Tennessee’s Global Director of Foreign Direct Investment under Governor Bill Lee from 2018 to 2022. But the deeper connection sits one office over.

As I reported last week, Kristin Canavan Wilson—Mayor O’Connell’s Chief of Operations and Performance, the staffer who coordinated the $15 million surveillance MOU with the Nashville Downtown Partnership—ran the 2008 integration of ChoicePoint into LexisNexis. That merger folded Pinkerton’s labor spy apparatus, Florida’s 2000 voter purge contractor, and Latin American citizen databases that Mexican prosecutors presumed criminally stolen into the data broker that now holds ICE’s $22.1 million contract.

That contract exists, according to ICE procurement documents, specifically to identify incarcerated immigrants in jurisdictions where “law enforcement agencies and state or local governments... do not share information about real time incarceration of foreign-born nationals with ICE.”

In other words: the person coordinating Nashville’s surveillance expansion helped build the tool ICE uses to circumvent sanctuary policies. And she’s doing it as Tennessee positions itself as Stephen Miller’s model state for immigration enforcement.

The Political Context

The O’Connell administration—with a Chief of Staff previously appointed to positions by Governor Bill Lee and Speaker Cameron Sexton, and a Chief of Operations who helped build ICE’s data infrastructure in the private sector—renewed their push for police surveillance technology on Friday.

Politically, it could not come at a worse time in modern American history to be expanding surveillance capabilities in Nashville.

Violent crime is down. Mayor O’Connell and MNPD are proud to report as much.

Meanwhile, the overreach of federal authorities in democratic cities is expanding exponentially—to deadly effect. And the federal government is not mincing words about what it wants to do in Tennessee with more surveillance data.

As I have reported, there are no guardrails that can meaningfully restrict how surveillance data is used once it is obtained. Two reasons:

  1. No political will exists to limit local law enforcement. As evidence, see how local law enforcement was documented facilitating the end of community oversight boards—not just in Nashville, but across Tennessee.

  2. All surveillance technology uses third-party data processors, who are entitled under recent state law to share data if they “reasonably believe” a federal, state, or local “law, rule, or regulation” has been broken. The only person who may file suit against a data processor in Tennessee for crossing that line? The state attorney general.

The MOU That Had To Be, Then Wasn’t, But Don’t Worry About It

Rebuked by Metro Council after public outcry over the so-called surveillance MOU with NDP—an MOU whose stated purpose was to “limit NDP’s use of the funds for surveillance”—the administration dropped the MOU entirely. Instead, they’re moving forward with four separate resolutions on January 20th that would have NDP purchase the items and donate them directly to Metro Nashville.

An amendment last month removed two controversial software items from the list after this newsletter reported that the CEO of one of them—LeoSight—was the former Chief Revenue Officer of Fusus, the company whose camera integration platform Nashville rejected after three years of community organizing. He had signed contracts with Chief Drake.

Negotiations between Council Members and the Mayor’s Office stripped LeoSight and the AI social media scraping tool FiveCast from MNPD’s wish list. But the remaining items—cameras, an armored vehicle, mobile command posts—almost certainly qualify as “surveillance technology” under the definition Metro Code established thanks to former Council Member Dave Rosenberg.

Since these would be surveillance technology donations, Metro Code requires a public hearing before acceptance. It remains unclear whether the January 20th Council meeting will include public hearings—and unless the resolutions are combined, four separate hearings would be required.

The administration appears to be betting Council won’t notice. Or won’t care.

The Surveillance Ordinance They’re Not Mentioning

In 2017, Metro Council passed BL2017-646, codified as Metro Code 13.08.080, which requires Council approval before Metro can accept “in-kind or other donations for surveillance technology.” The ordinance was deliberately broad in its definition of surveillance technology, explicitly including “closed-circuit television cameras,” “video and audio monitoring and/or recording technology,” and critically, “any system, device, or vehicle that is equipped with an electronic surveillance device.”

The law requires more than a simple Council vote. Section 13.08.080(D) mandates that approval “shall be granted only upon the determination that the benefits to the citizens and residents of Nashville and Davidson County outweigh the costs; that the proposal will safeguard civil liberties and civil rights; and that, in the judgment of the Metropolitan Council, no alternative with a lesser economic cost or impact upon civil rights or civil liberties would be as effective.”

None of the four resolutions reference 13.08.080. None include the required civil liberties balancing test. None acknowledge that the equipment being donated might constitute surveillance technology at all.

The “Traditional Video Camera” Exemption That Doesn’t Exist

RS2026-13811 asks Council to accept $150,000 for 15 Axis Video Cameras, described as “traditional video cameras, consistent with existing public safety cameras for departmental use.”

Three problems.

First: Metro Code 13.08.080 contains no “traditional” exemption. The ordinance explicitly covers “closed-circuit television cameras” and “video and audio monitoring and/or recording technology.” A camera is a camera—even a basic fixed dome from 2005 would be surveillance technology by definition.

Second: The description doesn’t change what the equipment is. Calling something “traditional” is marketing language, not a legal category. Every video camera monitoring public space is surveillance technology under the ordinance, regardless of what adjective precedes it.

Third: At $10,000 per unit, you’re not buying anything traditional anyway.

Axis fixed dome cameras—the kind you’d actually call traditional—retail for $500-$1,200. To hit $10,000 per unit, you’re either being overcharged by $60,000-$90,000, or you’re buying high-end PTZ cameras with 30-40x optical zoom, infrared range measured in hundreds of meters, and AI-powered analytics for object classification and behavior detection.

The price is the capability. You cannot spend $10,000 per camera on Axis equipment and end up with something that isn’t surveillance technology.

The New Orleans Connection

The price point is familiar. New Orleans paid $5,500-$9,200 per camera for its Real Time Crime Center network—a system built on Axis cameras with “360-degree tilt, long-distance zoom and smart tracking that automatically follows moving objects,” according to The Lens’s investigation.

In October 2021, then-Councilmember Freddie O’Connell shared that investigation and wrote: “Strong communities are better for safety than a surveillance state. Let’s not let fear drive Nashville to follow New Orleans’s path.”

Now Mayor O’Connell’s administration is proposing 15 cameras from the same vendor, at the same price point, using language carefully drafted to avoid triggering the surveillance ordinance he once invoked to warn against exactly this kind of expansion.

Either the pricing is unjustified, or “traditional” is a lie. Either way, the ordinance applies.

The Mayor’s Office is in the Tank for Surveillance

RS2026-13812 asks Council to accept a $430,000 Terradyne Gurkha MPV armored vehicle from the Nashville Downtown Partnership, described as an “Armored Rescue Vehicle” for SWAT operations and “major city events i.e., July 4th, New Years, CMAs.”

What the resolution doesn’t mention: NDP’s parent organization, Nashville District Management Corporation, was awarded a $15,028,698 grant from the State of Tennessee’s Office of Criminal Justice Programs in November 2025. The grant application, obtained through public records, reveals that the armored vehicle isn’t a private donation at all — it’s a state-funded purchase routed through a private entity.

The Grant Application’s Admission

Page 28 of NDMC’s grant application lists allowable expense categories: surveillance cameras, lighting, personnel costs, security infrastructure, traffic calming. Under “Please describe any other expense types that are not listed above,” the application states:

“Armored vehicle for violent crime intervention.”

Armored military-style vehicles appear nowhere in the Tennessee VCIF program’s enumerated list of preferred equipment, which includes ShotSpotter, license plate readers, pole cameras, body-worn cameras, and body armor. NDMC had to write the vehicle into the “other” category because no standard category authorizes it.

Nashville’s premium of $30,000-$115,000 over comparable purchases suggests additional equipment. Lane County’s Captain DJ Mann was explicit about what his department’s $399,745 vehicle included: “It has law enforcement-integrated features like a thermal imaging camera, so when you get to that rural area and you’re not sure if the subject is outside, we can scan the area and see if we can pick up the signature in the brush.”

Terradyne’s standard options catalog includes PTZ cameras with 30x zoom, thermal imaging systems with person/vehicle detection, 4G LTE cellular video servers, and LRAD acoustic devices. The resolution provides no itemized equipment list.

The State Oversight Question

Section D.20 of NDMC’s state grant contract requires “prior approval from the State before purchasing any equipment” costing $10,000 or more. Section D.27 creates a state security interest in all equipment purchased with grant funds.

Did NDMC obtain required state approval before committing to a $430,000 armored vehicle purchase? Does the State of Tennessee retain an ownership interest in equipment that’s being “donated” to Metro Nashville? The resolution doesn’t address either question.

The Local Oversight Question

Regardless of funding source, Metro Code 13.08.080 applies when Metro Nashville accepts surveillance technology. The ordinance explicitly covers “any system, device, or vehicle that is equipped with an electronic surveillance device.”

If the Gurkha includes thermal imaging — as Lane County’s lower-priced vehicle did — or any other surveillance capability, Council must conduct a public hearing and determine that “the benefits to the citizens and residents of Nashville and Davidson County outweigh the costs; that the proposal will safeguard civil liberties and civil rights; and that, in the judgment of the Metropolitan Council, no alternative with a lesser economic cost or impact upon civil rights or civil liberties would be as effective.”

RS2026-13812 makes no reference to 13.08.080. It includes no civil liberties balancing test. It doesn’t disclose whether surveillance equipment is included.

Mobile Command: The $2 Million “Technology Support” Platform

RS2026-13813 is the largest single item: $2,000,000 for two 48-foot ATC command trailers and two Ford F-550 tow vehicles. The stated purpose explicitly includes “technology support for law enforcement incident management.”

Mobile command posts are, by design, surveillance integration platforms. They aggregate camera feeds, house monitoring workstations, and coordinate real-time intelligence gathering. The resolution’s own language—”technology support”—acknowledges this function while avoiding any mention of surveillance oversight requirements.

What “technology” will these trailers contain? How many monitoring workstations? Will they receive live feeds from MNPD’s camera network? From private cameras? The resolution doesn’t say.

The Pattern

All four resolutions share notable features:

  • All were processed through MNPD’s chain of command on January 8, 2026

  • All were signed by the same Assistant Metropolitan Attorney (Nicki Fee)

  • None reference Metro Code 13.08.080

  • None include the required civil liberties findings

  • None acknowledge the equipment might constitute surveillance technology

What was previously a single MOU covering $3.38 million in police equipment has been split into four separate donation resolutions. The effect is to obscure the total scope of private funding for police technology while potentially requiring four separate public hearings—if the administration acknowledges the surveillance ordinance applies at all.

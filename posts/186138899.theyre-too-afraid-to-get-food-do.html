AT YESTERDAY’S MEETING OF
Voluntary Organizations Active in Disaster (VOAD), while 100,000 residents remain without power in Davidson County, leaders from nonprofits met to discuss the coordinated response to the ongoing freeze that has crippled Nashville. A participant on the call reported what they’ve seen the past few days as temperatures have dipped into the single digits.

“A lot of the Hispanic portion of the immigrant families without power are too afraid to get out, even too afraid to come here.”

One source of the fear is what’s happening to their friends and relatives 200 miles away to the southwest.

In Memphis, the federal “Safe Task Force” has made over 4,000 arrests since September, 1 out of 5 immigration related, and nearly 60% of Tennessee ICE arrests since January involve people with no criminal convictions. Families in Nashville are watching. During a life-threatening emergency, they’re choosing to stay in cold, dark homes rather than risk encountering law enforcement on the way to help.

The Memphis Safe Task Force lists 13 federal agencies as members on Memphis.gov. But there’s one additional partner, described in conspicuously surveillance-forward, informal Trumpworld terms: the National Guard, a branch of the U.S. Armed Forces, is tasked with providing “extra ‘eyes and ears’ in our neighborhoods.”

A few hours before VOAD met on Wednesday, the Nashville Mayor’s Office formally announced that the National Guard was being deployed to Davidson County. This is being framed as “disaster response” aligned with the Guard’s actual reason for existing.

Recovery efforts in the wake of Winter Storm Fern remain slow going in Nashville, which has some leaders calling for reinforcements.

That includes state lawmakers Caleb Hemmer, Heidi Campbell, John Ray Clemmons, Jeff Yarbro and Mark Pody, who sent a letter to Gov. Bill Lee asking him to send the Tennessee National Guard to Nashville.
Yahoo News

There is certainly a lot of work to be done to get Nashville back online. I, for one, have been living with friends for the better part of a week — and I am one of the lucky ones. But what, precisely, the National Guard will be aiding with is only partially clear.

“[The National Guard] will be removing debris, trees, get them off power lines. They’re doing this throughout Tennessee, and now they’ll be able to come into Davidson County and assist us,” [Office of Emergency Management Chief ] Swann said. “That’s their role and objectives, and that’s their only objective is to assist us and to help us restore power.”

Yahoo News

Perhaps it is accurate that they will just be coordinating with TDOT, NDOT, and NES. The extra hands would have plenty of work to do with aiding those acronyms who are known for their northern charm and southern efficiency.

But given what’s been seen of the National Guard’s assignments in other parts of Tennessee, and how much our local leadership has accepted from state funding tied to the Memphis Safe Task force, surveillance, and immigrant policing, clear answers about the National Guard's role in our city’s fabric seem warranted.

Is this unrelated to headlines from the past weeks, such as: “Tennessee Republicans unveil ‘Immigration 2026’ agenda as ‘model’ for rest of nation”?

Perhaps it is all just good intentions with bad PR. And let’s say their presence in Nashville does turn out to be wholly benevolent. That would be a harsh contrast to how they have been conscripted to engage with the 62% Black city of Memphis, and this difference should not go unacknowledged and, someday, accounted for.

Memphis and Nashville have nearly identical percentage of Hispanic populations — about 10% each. But Memphis is 62% Black, while Nashville is majority white. If this were about immigration, the deployments would look the same. They don’t. In Memphis, Stephen Miller promises to “bulldoze the criminal element of this city... without apology and without mercy.” In Nashville, the Guard, we are told, is coming to help restore electricity. The variable isn’t immigration status. It’s who else lives there.

Assuming everything goes well and our neighbors are not stopped and asked for their papers by camouflaged Guardsmen and women on Nashville streets, we still have the right to ask a simple question. Just as the people who are hosting those of us put out by this icy crisis have the right ask, we can stammer and ask, trying to not seem rude: “so, uh, how long do you think you’re gonna be staying?”

The Gap Between Impressive Claims and Grounded Reality

At a joint press conference on Wednesday, Mayor O’Connell and Police Chief Drake described the crisis response in sweeping terms. Drake cited 180 officers pulled from investigations to conduct welfare checks. O’Connell detailed that 1,400 people experiencing homelessness were being sheltered, and more than 1,000 utility workers were restoring power around the clock. These metrics may be worthy of praise. They might be woefully inadequate. As presented, there’s no way to know—impressive-sounding numerators usually without a denominator in sight for context.

But also, it’s worth backing up to the MNPD welfare checks piece. Sending police officers to knock on the doors of vulnerable residents—many of whom may have reason to distrust police—isn’t exactly trauma-informed outreach. Metro has partnerships with nonprofits experienced in street outreach. The reflexive turn to police as the default tool for human contact during a crisis says everything about how even “liberal” municipalities conceptualize care.

The VOAD call that afternoon told a different story. A call participant had a pointed comment. It highlighted that the (predominately Black) population of our public housing was seemingly going unserved in this crisis.

“[Redacted Mayor’s Office Staffer] mentioned that they were doing wellness checks on the properties,” referring to claims about outreach to Metropolitan Development and Housing Agency (MDHA) residents. “We discovered last night that a lot of these folks actually have not been checked on, and they don’t have food, or power or a way to get to food.”

Another participant confirmed: “I know multiple of us on this call have been hearing similarly that people might still be very stranded and very vulnerable.”

“And so I did reach out to Jamie Berry today,” a call participant said, referring to the Chief of Staff of MDHA, a quasi-governmental 501(c)(3) whose board is overseen by the Mayor’s Office, “to inquire about the welfare, welfare checks, that MDHA is doing. And so I'm waiting on a call back from her, and I also encouraged her to join here or have someone join the VOAD call to represent MDHA. So contact has been made.”

The response from a Mayor’s Office representative: “I will follow up... and see what the status of that is.”

MDHA—responsible for approximately 30,000 Nashville residents in public housing —was not represented on the VOAD call. Their own storm response tells the story: MDHA’s emergency page lists Metro fire stations, Metro police precincts, Metro parks community centers, NES updates, and WeGo routes. The only MDHA-specific content is a notice that their offices are closed and residents should call their leasing office for maintenance emergencies.

For an agency whose entire mission is housing vulnerable residents, the storm response reads like a bulletin board, not an operational plan.

No MDHA-staffed warming locations in their own properties listed. No description of proactive outreach to residents. No one was present on the regional coordination call, so nonprofits were left to flag that MDHA residents hadn’t been checked on.

“A lot of them rely on public transportation,” a VOAD call participant noted. “So they can’t get to some of these centers and places where the food is.”

What the Fear Is Grounded In.

Too often, officials offer numbers without context and call it success. Meanwhile, families offer specific, documented incidents of harm but are told their fears are anecdotal or unfounded and that they should trust leaders (and be sure they are registered to vote). The National Guard, we are told, is just here to clean up roads and coordinate with relief. Questioning to the contrary, were liable to be told, is in “bad faith” and is, in fact, the real problem.

People who don't understand the relationship between being truthful and political legitimacy, and political legitimacy and political power, say a lot of things. In Memphis, a Tennessee National Guard spokesperson told ProPublica the Guard is “not conducting traffic stops.”

And yet, there are stories like this: Late last year, Pastor ELaura James Reid was pulled over by someone in camouflage fatigues, driving an unmarked SUV with a National Guard license plate, who told her she was stopped for “driving unsure” [sic] (not a crime in Tennessee, or anywhere I can find). She’d stopped at the stop sign. She’d signaled her left turn.

“I don’t feel safe,” James Reid said. “It fits into the narrative of keeping us in our place.”

Stephen Miller launched the Memphis Safe Task Force on October 1 by telling law enforcement, “You are unleashed. The handcuffs you’re carrying? They’re not on you anymore.” It’s the kind of statement from an actual public official that makes life much harder for the writers of pulpy B-movies with over-the-top villains.

Governor Lee’s response to concerns about harassment by the Memphis Safe Task Force: “If they’re not a criminal element, then they shouldn’t be afraid.”

But staying out of trouble doesn’t protect you, because it was never really about the “criminal element”, despite what the CEO of an HVAC repair company thinks he knows about such things.

Data analyzed by The Daily Memphian reveals that 87.6% of the Task Force’s immigration arrests between October and December began with traffic stops—not investigations into violent crime. Nearly 60% of Tennessee ICE arrests since January involve people with no criminal convictions. One in five had no convictions and no pending charges.

Among those who have reported being harassed: a ride-share driver stopped for not wearing a seat belt despite having one on as she drove a passenger to the airport; a pastor pulled over for looking lost as she left a church gathering; and, in a case of mistaken identity, a 72-year-old man roused from bed and marched out of his apartment while clad in only his robe and underwear.

It is naive to see the “side effects” of the presence of the Memphis Safe Task Force and not realize that these chilling conditions on families of all manner of immigration status are, in fact, the point.

Let me make something clear: these racialized, chilling effects are almost without exception not the intentions of individual Guard members who’ve been conscripted into these “eyes and ears” tasks that are beneath them — part-time citizen-soldiers who hold civilian jobs, live in the communities they serve, they signed up for disaster response and overseas deployment, not domestic espionage on behalf of Trumpworld purity policing.

Reports from the Guard’s LA deployment indicated declining troop morale and concerns that the mission could harm military recruitment.

Court records revealed that U.S. military leaders themselves questioned whether troops were adequately trained for domestic operations. Army Secretary Dan Driscoll told Congress he would not deploy the Guard "for any unlawful purposes." But when deployed on American streets with nothing much to do except be “the eyes and ears in our neighborhoods”, the intentions of those who deploy them matter far more than the intentions of those who are deployed.

For Nashville families already justifiably afraid of traffic stops, the distinction between “disaster response” and “dragnet” isn’t very obvious. More uniforms on icy roads means more risk—even if this deployment’s intentions are genuinely different. And if it is different, we must ask: why are we comfortable letting those things happen to our neighbors in Memphis while we get our power lines repaired in Nashville? Why do we tolerate this in a 62% Black city but would not let it happen here?

Follow the Damn Money

The state funding Nashville leadership has accepted comes from the same source that funds the Memphis operation. In September 2025, House Speaker Cameron Sexton told reporters he and “other state officials are talking to the Trump administration” about sending National Guard troops to Memphis, calling for a federal-state “strike force” to target what he described as “over a hundred gangs.” Days later, Trump signed the presidential memorandum.

On October 1, as National Guard soldiers appeared on Memphis streets for the first time, White House Deputy Chief of Staff Stephen Miller addressed the Memphis Safe Task Force at the Shelby County Operations Center, telling officers they were “unleashed” and that the administration would “bulldoze the criminal element of this city... without apology and without mercy.”

The next day, Governor Lee announced $175 million in public safety grants—$100 million explicitly allocated for the Memphis Safe Task Force, with the remainder available to other cities. At a November press event, Senator Bill Hagerty specifically addressed Sexton about his “big job” securing continued appropriations for the operation.

Nashville’s share of this money: $15 million through the Downtown Public Safety Grant Fund, routed not through Metro government but through the Nashville Downtown Partnership—a private nonprofit that applied for the funds on the city’s behalf after the mayor’s office said Metro couldn’t meet the state’s application deadline.

The original proposal included LeoSight, a surveillance integration platform whose chief revenue officer was, until February 2024, the chief revenue officer of FUSUS—the same surveillance system Metro Council rejected.

Last week, Metro Council rejected the camera portion but approved ~$3 million for a SWAT vehicle, mobile command posts, and a tactical center. The state’s separate Violent Crime Intervention Fund, which Nashville also receives, explicitly lists preferred equipment, including license plate readers, pole cameras, and ShotSpotter.

The same money that funds the Memphis Safe Taskforce and the National Guard patrols in Memphis—the operation Miller promised would proceed “without mercy”—is lumped together in Governor Lee’s words with the grant program funding Nashville’s downtown surveillance infrastructure. Sexton coordinates the legislative appropriations. Lee signs the deployment orders. And Nashville’s centrist Liberal leadership takes the money, just through a nonprofit intermediary, so it would go unnoticed (sorry about that).

I don’t think it’s as simple as “our Liberal leaders have been black pilled and are secretly simping for 47.” This isn’t so much willful complicity in reactionary right-wing policy that is blatantly racialized right up to the edge of being genocidal (“and hey, since we’ve gone this far…”).

To me, it looks more like a Sorkin-esque game of political “chess” being played by well intentioned Liberal elites (who have surrounded themselves with Republican establishment types) who believe that by conceding enough pawns, they can regain the board. We may debate how well they are playing the game they think they are playing.

But to anyone with eyes and ears, it’s not chess the other side is playing. They aren’t playing games at all. That’s not what their investments have been building with the same funding streams our local leaders have bent over backwards to accept.

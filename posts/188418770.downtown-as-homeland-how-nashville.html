On a January night at Metro Council, as members debated whether to accept policing surveillance camera equipment tied to a $15 million state grant being sourced through the Nashville Downton Partnership, Council Member Jordan Huffman offered a blunt warning:

“We are one incident away from having the money turn off in Nashville.”

That sentence clarifies what this debate is really about. Nashville’s leaders are not primarily concerned with safety in the broad sense—housing stability, public health, or community well-being. They are concerned with protecting revenue.

Mayor O’Connell offered his own version of the logic while defending expanded surveillance downtown:

“We’re continuing to solicit feedback… but we want to make sure that everyone understands the context of how different the downtown safety environment is from other parts of the city.”

Downtown is not treated as a neighborhood. It is treated as an asset: dense with tourism, hotel taxes, convention bookings, alcohol sales, property value, and corporate investment. When Mayor O’Connell insisted downtown was “different,” he was not describing crime patterns. He was describing capital. Downtown was “different” because disruption there threatened the city’s most valuable economic district.

An overly simplistic narrative says this is about appeasing “downtown interests”—the businesses on Broadway, the bar owners, the hotel groups.

But recent comments have made clear that when we talk local leadership having commitment to “business interests”, its not local business leaders. The commitment is it to the abstract power they they are beholden to, the financial institutions that often have even regional elites over a barrel, for whom worldview of “the market knows best” is the law of the jungle.

To the financial masters of the universe, the operator of downtown establishments are interchangeable. They are tenants in a larger machine. What commands political loyalty is not any individual business, but the financial architecture that extracts value from the district no matter who occupies the space. Property values, tax flows, debt structures, and investment vehicles do not depend on the survival of a single venue.

They depend on stability.

What is being defended is not a brand name. It is an asset class. This logic did not originate in Nashville.

Over the past two decades, cities across the country were promised economic windfalls from hosting NFL drafts, conventions, and championships. In exchange, they were expected to harden their security infrastructure: surveillance networks, integrated camera systems, command centers, mobile tactical units, real-time data platforms.

The spectacle came and went. The infrastructure remained.

But surveillance was never a one-time purchase. It was a permanent operating expense. Cameras required storage, maintenance, upgrades, and staffing. Data required analysts. Analysts generated enforcement. Enforcement generated arrests, court processing, and jail bookings. Each step fed an expanding criminal legal system that had to continually justify its scale.

In Nashville, that downstream system was not abstract. CoreCivic—one of the largest private prison operators in the United States—was headquartered here. Incarceration was not merely a policy outcome; it was part of the city’s corporate landscape. When surveillance expanded, it did so in a place where confinement itself intersected with economic power.

None of this required a conspiracy. It required incentives.

After 9/11, federal doctrine formally fused economic continuity with national security. “Critical infrastructure” came to include not only systems whose disruption would threaten lives, but systems whose disruption would threaten economic stability. Tennessee adopted that framing. The state’s Office of Homeland Security explicitly included protecting “property and the economy” within its mission.

That language mattered. It reframed volatility as threat. It elevated revenue streams into protected infrastructure. Once economic stability became a security objective, protecting a downtown revenue district was no longer ordinary municipal governance—it was cast as homeland defense.

In 2025, the federal government extended this trajectory through National Security Presidential Memorandum-7 (NSPM-7), directing agencies to coordinate efforts to counter domestic terrorism and organized political violence, including tracing networks and financial support structures. Civil liberties advocates, including the ACLU, warned that the memorandum’s broad language risked sweeping nonprofits, activists, donors, and lawful political advocacy into a domestic security framework.

When economic continuity and political dissent are both pulled into a security paradigm, disruption becomes crisis. Protest becomes risk. Democratic debate becomes something to manage rather than something to welcome.

That was the policy environment in which Nashville’s surveillance expansion unfolded.

The city did not build this architecture alone. It worked alongside private intermediaries and data vendors whose business models depended on collecting, integrating, and monetizing information at scale.

Kristin Wilson, Mayor O’Connell’s Chief of Operations and Performance, coordinated the proposed $15 million surveillance agreement between MNPD and the Nashville Downtown Partnership. Before entering public service, she worked at LexisNexis Risk Solutions, overseeing the integration of ChoicePoint after a $4.1 billion acquisition.

ChoicePoint was not a neutral background-check company. Long before it was absorbed into LexisNexis, it was already in the business of selling government databases for enforcement purposes. FOIA documents obtained in the early 2000s revealed ChoicePoint contracts with the Immigration and Naturalization Service to provide access to Latin American citizen registry and motor vehicle data.

In one widely reported case, ChoicePoint acquired Mexico’s voter registry—data legally restricted to electoral use—through a subcontractor and sold it to U.S. immigration authorities. Mexican prosecutors stated there was a presumption a crime had been committed, and officials later traveled to ChoicePoint’s Georgia offices to witness the deletion of the files.

That business line did not disappear. It became part of the corporate portfolio.

LexisNexis later held a $22.1 million contract with ICE, justified explicitly as a workaround for jurisdictions that declined to share incarceration data.

The relevance is not that Nashville intends to replicate these practices verbatim, but that the city’s surveillance expansion is being coordinated by officials whose professional background is rooted in the normalization of data brokerage as governance infrastructure.

And then there is Oracle.

Nashville’s East Bank development plan was not simply a real estate deal. It was an alignment of the city’s future with one of the most powerful data infrastructure companies in the world. Oracle is not just another corporate relocation headline. It is a major federal contractor whose cloud systems are built to handle the kinds of information modern government runs on, including sensitive national security workloads.

That matters because the city is simultaneously expanding surveillance capacity downtown: more cameras, more integration, more real-time monitoring, more centralized command. In isolation, each of these moves can be justified as pragmatic. Together, they form a recognizable pattern: a city reorganizing itself around the collection, management, and monetization of data.

Oracle’s co-founder Larry Ellison has been unusually explicit about what he believes that future should look like. He has openly advocated for a society blanketed with surveillance, arguing that constant monitoring keeps people on their “best behavior.” That is not dystopian fiction. It is the worldview of one of the most influential men in global computing.

So when Nashville hardens its downtown core with permanent surveillance infrastructure while simultaneously anchoring its next era of development to a company built on data governance at scale, it is not coincidence. It is convergence.

The police hardware, the public subsidies, the securitization of downtown, and the arrival of a surveillance-adjacent data giant are not separate storylines. They are different expressions of the same governing logic: treat the city as an asset portfolio, and treat disruption as a security threat.

Of course, defenders of the surveillance expansion can point to facts that are not imaginary. Downtown does have uniquely dense foot traffic. It hosts large-scale events. It concentrates nightlife, alcohol, and tourist crowds in a way most neighborhoods do not. A violent incident on Broadway has ripple effects that reach beyond the people immediately harmed: it becomes national news, it threatens convention bookings, it alarms investors, and it shapes the city’s reputation. In that sense, officials are not wrong to argue that downtown carries a different risk profile.

But that reality does not resolve the deeper question—it sharpens it. The issue is not whether downtown has safety needs. The issue is why those needs generate rapid political urgency and millions in available funding, while the slow violence of housing instability, unaffordable healthcare, underfunded schools, and neighborhood disinvestment is treated as background noise. Downtown’s “different” status is not simply about public safety. It is about what kinds of danger the city treats as unacceptable: not the everyday harms that shorten lives quietly, but the visible disruptions that threaten economic confidence and revenue flow.

The point was not that any single official created this system. It was that the ecosystem—municipal surveillance procurement, private data brokerage, federal enforcement, and a city economically intertwined with incarceration—operated without clear boundaries between them.

Nashville had already wrestled with this dynamic. In 2025, the city abandoned the Fusus surveillance platform after public opposition and concerns about federal overreach. Yet the surveillance MOU Wilson coordinated routed expansion through the Nashville Downtown Partnership, a private nonprofit operating outside traditional procurement channels. Even when specific vendors were removed, similar technologies resurfaced through new contractual pathways.

This was not simply a debate about cameras.

It was a debate about what Nashville believed downtown was.

And that belief was on full display during the controversy over ACME Feed & Seed.

When asked about a reported property tax increase—from roughly $129,000 to approximately $600,000—Mayor O’Connell emphasized that valuations reflected market value under state oversight. The property was “an incredibly valuable piece of property.” It was up to the owner to determine how to “capture the value that’s inherent in the property.” The market evolved. New businesses opened. Beloved old businesses closed.

Taken in full context, the response was not careless. It was doctrinal.

It was the city stating plainly that it did not exist to preserve any particular version of Nashville. It existed to steward market conditions. If valuations rose and long-standing operators could not absorb the increase, that was not framed as policy failure—it was competitive demand.

When backlash followed, the administration posted a “fact check.” But the fact check did not undo the philosophy. It clarified it.

When market forces displaced a business, that was evolution. When the possibility of disruption threatened tourism revenue, that was emergency. When capital was inconvenienced, millions became available for surveillance, tactical readiness, and hardened infrastructure.

The asymmetry was the point.

Market displacement that harms Nashvillians treated as inevitable. Market interruption that threatens quarterly profits for the financial institutions and mega-corporations that dangle jobs over the city (if only we’re willing to lick enough boots) is treated as existential.

This is not hypocrisy so much as hierarchy. The priority is not any single storefront; it is the aggregate yield of the district. The underlying objective is to maintain confidence, maintain throughput, maintain exchange value. Downtown is treated less as a lived space and more as a critical node in a broader economic network. It is something to stabilize, something to harden, something to insure.

In that framework, the language of “public safety” begins to blur with the language of asset protection. Investments in surveillance and rapid-response capacity are justified not only by the desire to prevent harm, but by the need to prevent disruption. The feared outcome is not simply violence; it is volatility. Not only injury, but interruption.

The uncomfortable question, then, is whether Nashville is protecting the people who animate downtown or the financial architecture that extracts value from their presence. Those are related concerns, but they are not identical. A city that prioritizes housing affordability, tax equity, and small-business stability is centering community resilience. A city that prioritizes hardening infrastructure to reassure investors and event organizers is centering revenue continuity.

If downtown is “different,” it may not be because it is uniquely dangerous. It may be because it is uniquely profitable. And once profitability becomes the defining feature of a place, the policies that follow tend to resemble the defense of an asset rather than the care of a community.

The question Nashville has to answer, then, is not whether it values safety, but whether it believes that when it comes to protecting certain people’s profits, no price is too high—not even the steady erosion of everyone else’s civil liberties.

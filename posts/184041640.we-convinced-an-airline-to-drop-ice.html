A national protest movement led by a coalition of organizations has convinced Avelo airlines to discontinue its deportation flights.

It’s a huge victory for people power over public-private partnerships designed to perpetuate harm, enrich shareholders, advance radical political agendas, and evade public scrutiny.

But it all could have been avoided if Avelo had just heeded the warnings that were sent to their email inboxes from people saying “hey, this is a bad look. Maybe don’t?”

Power has a tendency of wanting to do things the hard way. And our local political establishment—the diffuse set of power players in and around our local government—seems determined to learn the same lesson.

When it comes to justice for the housing insecure, protecting the privacy of Nashvillians, and actual funded Community Safety programming with public oversight—not just performative “task forces” showing up two years after a “Community Safety Plan on Day 1” was supposed to drop—it seems like emailing them isn’t going to be enough.

I want you to know that I understand that it’s very disruptive to be talking out loud about these things. That it ruffles more than a few feathers, hurts feelings, and validates some preexisting persecution complexes.

And that’s why I didn’t start by saying it all out loud.

All of the things with Nashville’s homelessness system for example that I’ve highlighted, I flagged behind the scenes first. I tried doing it “the right way.” I developed relationships with our city’s highest officials and with unhoused people, and communicated much of this directly. I tried to have them meet each other. I even had meetings on the books. The political leader? He flaked.

And when time and time again the concerns were minimized and ignored, I kept trying different ways of communicating it to them out of the public eye. Nicer. Deferential. More assertive. More openly frustrated.

When I started connecting dots between how Nashville’s HUD-mandated Continuum of Care had been systematically kneecapped through its absorption into Metro government—a strategy that was designed and executed from inside the Mayor’s Office, starting at least under the mayor I first worked for, John Cooper—I didn’t want to blow up anybody’s spot.

You get blacklisted by bureaucratic hierarchies for not going through “the proper channels,” and with Mayor O’Connell being the sole authority with oversight on our city’s homeless policies, I took my revelations and frustrations directly to him. Repeatedly.

I made it clear to him that the way the homelessness system was being redesigned to privilege developers and political priorities was creating a huge political liability for him. I made it clear that the short-term wins he was getting were having demonstrated harm on the people “being helped,” and that the CoC was designed by HUD precisely to mitigate these things.

I was connecting the dots, as many I’ve spoken to in this space already had, about how a certain consultant was hired by the city on a no-bid grant contract for $500,000 (that was expanded to $750,000 outside procurement processes) to retool the homelessness system. That Freddie O’Connell, then a Council Member, was the sole sponsor of this contract that came from the Mayor’s Office. That O’Connell had also changed the oversight structure on the Council floor to give the Mayor’s Office sole authority over the Office of Homeless Services—an office he would control shortly thereafter.

And, as I was starting to piece together, Metro Nashville was violating all national norms for rehousing people, just throwing them into leases they could not afford with vague promises of ongoing rent support they consistently would flake on—often leaving a chronically homeless person suddenly needing to find $1,400 a month or face eviction and a near-certain return to homelessness.

“You broke the CoC. Fix it. Or we’ll get someone who will,” was the last thing I ever communicated to him that I know he read.

Several months later, I heard from someone with ties to the Mayor’s Office that I should be kept at arm’s length because I had used “threatening language” with the Mayor.

On hearing this, I spiraled a bit. I searched my memory and my text messages for what this could be referring to. Because my texts had used some curse words and been emotionally charged, I sent an apology (that I’ve previously published) and reiterated my commitment to nonviolence and nonviolent communication.

What I’ve pieced together since was that my attempt to take these issues to him, to say “hey man, this looks pretty bad, you should probably do something about it,” was being framed as some sort of extortion. As if pointing to public documents and public records and saying “uhh, this seems like a bad electoral strategy” (because that seemed like the only way to make him care) was somehow unethical—even though taking your concerns to the person in charge is precisely what “following the chain of command” requires if you want to “do things the right way.”

To short-circuit any credibility to the idea that I was trying to manipulate the Mayor by citing public documents to him and reminding him that he might someday want to be Mayor again, I immediately provided my communications with him to the various local media outlets I had been in contact with for them to judge for themselves. I also published the line of questions I had been asking him when our communications were cut short.

So all that to say, to those who’ve said to me: “it’s not cool you’re criticizing our Mayor publicly or highlighting unelected bureaucrats. We need to be a united front to defend democracy,” my response is: I really wish there was an alternative. But it’s not possible to defend democracy nationally with people who seem hell-bent on eliminating it locally.

And one does not hire an arsonist as a firefighter just because “they know a lot about fire.”

I used to be an insider. But the law of the jungle to be an insider is such that, if the lions do not want to hear your feedback, you are best not to provide it. Prairie dogs and warthogs with inconvenient truths have a tendency to become lunch.

Outside the reach of government employment or public grants they could control, I’ve seen powerful people reach out to punish people like me for asking questions, even when they do it “the right way.” So they leave little recourse other than asking questions the public way.

I will accept their criticism of being irresponsible when I misquote someone. When I don’t cite a source correctly. When my math or analysis is sloppy. Or when I make public inferences about someone’s intent that I can’t substantiate.

But I’m done spiraling over being accused of “threatening” behavior for connecting dots on public information and saying “this does not look good… Maybe like, do a democracy instead of unilaterally making all these bad decisions?”

The only thing this threatens is the power that elected and unelected officials have unjustly hoarded from the public.

Power that they are hoarding using threats, gaslighting, political retribution, and the assumption that most people just don’t like conflict. Well, some of us—through a combination of being neuroatypical, idealistic, and/or just plain over it—have no problem engaging in nonviolent resistance to the conflicts of interest our leaders leverage to prevent legitimate democracy from flourishing here.

In a functioning democracy, legitimate power can survive scrutiny. In a functioning democracy, legitimate power can survive public records requests and blogs from an underemployed ex-employee with ADHD—regardless of whether they have good or ignoble intents.

The argument so often made for mass surveillance is: “If you have nothing to hide, you have nothing to fear.”

To the local political establishment which predates our current Mayor and has perhaps allowed him to sit in its power for a time under certain concessions, I put to you a similar argument:

“If there is nothing undemocratic and problematic about the way you hold power, why are you trying so hard to hide your power?”

Our actions in our private lives should be allowed to be private.

Our actions in public life, in oath-bound government duties in a democracy, cannot be allowed to be private.

I know that messes up a “good thing” a lot of folks have going. There are a lot of plans for redesigning our city in certain ways for the benefit of certain people.

But sorry. That isn’t how democracy works. And the irony of Donald Trump trampling on all democratic norms is that it’s reminding people they very much do, actually, like democracy.

I know it’s uncomfortable to talk about power and to bring to light things we’d rather not talk about.

But this is what democracy looks like. I know many of us are paid a lot to pretend it isn’t and to be convinced that with the right few people in charge, things will be better. That the reason why people suffer is because they try to have some modicum of control on the systems that are causing their suffering, and if only they would just fully relinquish control to you, that things would be better for them.

I am happy to sit down with you, buy you a coffee or a drink, and hear out your theory of governance and why you think autocracy, aristocracy, noblesse oblige, or oligarchy are going to benefit you in the long run.

I am a student of western history and human nature and natural systems. I have sat through long seminars on Aristotle’s “Natural Slave” theory, read the lion’s share of Nietzsche, swam with Thomas Hobbes’ Leviathan, and wrestled like Jacob on the riverbank with Leo Strauss, the dark angel of Neo-Conservatism that preached that natural hierarchy is the secret teaching of all western thought.

I have done my homework and found that true democracy, well implemented, is still the best of all possible worlds. Have you done your reading? Or are you just collecting a paycheck to shape the world in a way you haven’t thought much about?

Maybe you’ve found the “one weird trick” that reveals that democracy is not the best form of government. My email inbox is open if you want to communicate privately.

But until then, the discourse is public. Legitimate power can handle it.

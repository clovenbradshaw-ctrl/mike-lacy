In my previous post I asked “What are the fundamental human needs, and why does it matter?”

I proposed using AI to create an analysis of the semantic relationships between words across languages and cultures to get at fundamental human needs. In this post I highlight some of the potential issues with this, written by, appropriately, an AI model.

What Could Go Wrong?

This bold initiative to create a model for understanding human needs using AI, linguistic analysis, and mathematical wizardry like high-dimensional semantic vector spaces sounds visionary. But the road to technological utopia is littered with broken dreams—and a whole lot of hidden traps. Here’s what could go wrong, and it’s a long list.

First up, biases baked right into the AI models. AI might be great at processing large amounts of data, but it’s only as unbiased as the data it’s trained on. And guess what? That data is riddled with cultural perspectives, social norms, and historical prejudices. An AI model that draws predominantly on Western literature, media, and research isn’t going to magically offer a universal perspective. Instead, it’s more likely to reflect and reinforce the biases that shaped its training data. So, while this project aims to build a model that transcends cultural divides, it’s highly possible it’ll end up prioritizing the needs and values of the cultures that are best represented in its datasets—often the most privileged ones.

And then there’s the risk of invisible biases masquerading as objectivity. AI is often viewed as neutral, a myth that’s as stubborn as it is misleading. Every choice in model design—what data to use, how to weigh different inputs, what counts as a “need”—is a subjective decision. The people behind these choices, no matter how well-intentioned, bring their own perspectives, cultural backgrounds, and unconscious biases into the mix. These biases get locked into the code, creating a model that feels scientific and objective but is really just a reflection of the perspectives of its creators. It’s a subtle, dangerous form of bias—one that hides behind algorithms and math, making it even harder to challenge or dismantle.

Now let’s talk about oversimplification. Human needs are not math problems; they’re messy, layered, and deeply tied to cultural and personal experiences. The idea of representing them through AI-driven models that use high-dimensional semantic vector spaces might sound cutting-edge, but it risks stripping away the very complexities that make us human. You might end up with a model that’s great at identifying statistical correlations but terrible at understanding the nuances of what drives a person’s desire for connection or a community’s need for mutual support. Imagine a model that equates “mental health needs” in one cultural context directly with another, failing to recognize how differently these can manifest due to cultural norms and stigmas. You get a solution that misses the mark, offering shallow insights where deep understanding is required.

And cultural nuances—don’t even get me started. This project wants to be the great equalizer, a framework that understands all human needs across the board. But in doing so, it risks bulldozing over the cultural differences that give those needs their specific shape. Cultures don’t just differ in their expressions of needs; they also differ in how they understand concepts like well-being, community, and even individuality. A model that tries to be everything for everyone often ends up being nothing for anyone. Instead of creating a truly universal framework, you might just get a system that subtly assumes that everyone should think and behave like the data’s dominant culture—turning the rich tapestry of human diversity into a washed-out monochrome.

Then there’s the illusion of knowing what we don’t know. This model acknowledges up front that we don’t have a perfect understanding of the human organism’s needs, but that makes the whole enterprise shaky from the start. If we don’t fully grasp what those needs are, how can we trust that this high-tech approach will reveal anything meaningful? We could easily end up building an intricate, elegant system that’s fundamentally detached from reality—like designing a spaceship without understanding the nature of gravity. It might look great on paper, but when it comes time to lift off, it just crashes back down.

Balancing individual and collective needs is another high-wire act. The aim is to be flexible, bridging these perspectives without favoring one over the other. But history has shown us that these two viewpoints often clash. Individualistic societies prioritize personal freedom, while collectivist ones emphasize the group’s well-being. Any attempt to harmonize them can quickly turn into a tug-of-war, with AI potentially pulling harder in one direction based on the data it’s fed. If the model subtly leans toward individualism—perhaps because that’s how most of the training data reads—you end up with policies and frameworks that don’t resonate in cultures where collective identity is paramount. The result? A model that’s out of sync with the very people it’s meant to serve.

And let’s not forget the limits of language. This project aims to push past those boundaries, using AI to grasp human needs in ways that language alone can’t. But language is more than just words; it’s a reflection of how people experience the world. By trying to go beyond language, there’s a risk of losing the rich cultural context embedded within it. Sure, you might pinpoint the statistical center of a need in some semantic space, but can that really tell you why someone in a rural village prioritizes communal rituals while someone in a tech-savvy city yearns for personal mindfulness apps? It’s like using a hammer when what you need is a scalpel. The precision you think you’re getting from these mathematical tools could turn out to be an illusion, offering a tidy map of human needs that glosses over the territory’s real contours.

Finally, feedback loops loom as a real danger. If we start using these AI models to shape societal policies, we risk creating systems that feed into their own biases. An AI model that’s already skewed by its training data might suggest policies that further entrench those same biases. For example, if it determines that certain communities have specific needs based on biased interpretations, the policies that follow could reinforce those stereotypes. It’s a self-reinforcing cycle where AI doesn’t just reflect existing inequalities; it amplifies them, creating a reality that fits its flawed data rather than genuinely serving the diverse needs of people.

In the end, this model risks becoming a high-tech mirage—promising a better understanding of human needs while quietly replicating and deepening the biases that already shape our world. It aims to be a tool of inclusion and understanding but could end up as a mechanism of exclusion, built on the very prejudices it hopes to overcome. Instead of unlocking new insights into human thriving, it might just leave us with a shiny new way of making old mistakes. And if that happens, the cost won’t just be a wasted opportunity—it’ll be a missed chance to actually make the world better for the people who need it most.

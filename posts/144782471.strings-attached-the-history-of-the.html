The political turmoil surrounding the reversal of a funding vote by the Arts Commission last July has tied the arts community and city government up in knots. As new policies and legislation are moving forward to untangle the city's arts funds and determine how they are used in the future, a history lesson in "how we got here" is crucial.

It's a long, winding story not simply about an institution in the Nashville arts community that has received an outside portion of public monies despite it's track record of financial mismanagement. It's also a lens into the values, personality types, priorities, and deal-making that have shaped the city as we know it.

The history of the Metro Arts Commission and the Nashville Symphony's financial troubles are inextricably linked. Public records show that the Symphony, twice near bankrupt in 25 years, was the intended, first, and primary recipient of Metro Arts funding. 

In the past year, artists, activists, politicians, and career bureaucrats have collided in ways that on the surface appear unprecedented, stemming from recent social trends towards concepts like “equity” and “anti-racism” and decisions made by various leaders over the past few years.

The truth is that this collision was set in motion from the very beginning of Metro Arts funding.

In its original form, the Metro Arts Commission mostly oversaw truly “public art” – murals, public festivals, and the beautification of public buildings and spaces. All that changed in 1988.

That year, the Symphony Board faced a tense contract negotiation with their Orchestra Union. It came the same fiscal season as a global financial crisis (the $1.71T shock to the stock market known as “Black Monday”) that dried up corporate donations. 

The 1988 season was gearing up to have a $900k shortfall according to the board, though Orchestra leaders thought this figure was exaggerated.

With the deficit seen by some as a negotiation tactic to lower the Orchestra Union’s demands, a Nashville Symphony board member drove a hard bargain: reduce the performer numbers and sign the contract, or the Symphony goes bankrupt.

The businessman that drove that bargain was Symphony board member, and soon-to-be Mayor, Phil Bredesen. He offered the union a carrot, a show of good faith from the city that would help make up for the fundraising shortfalls of the board and management.

The Orchestra union was lured back to the negotiating table with a promise that there was a new type of funding mechanism coming from the city which, in this first year alone, would add $250,000 to the Symphony’s coffers. 

Negotiated by Board Chair Martha Ingram, Mayor Bill Boner offered the symphony a quarter million dollars that would be distributed by the Metro Arts Commission in exchange for free public concerts. 

Dedicated arts funding of this size had never happened in Nashville, and it would change the financial realities of the arts forever.

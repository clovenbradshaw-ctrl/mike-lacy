I’m not naive about politicians. I don’t expect them to save us. I’ve worked in local government, and I’ve watched it from the outside. And I know the gap between what officials say when they’re campaigning and what they do is often wide enough to drive a truck through.

When Renee Goode was killed by ICE agents in Minneapolis, the mayor of Minneapolis told federal authorities to get the fuck out of Minnesota. The Tennessee congressman representing Memphis said the incident was “just what I feared. Out of control. Masked ICE agents fatally misusing their authority and inflaming our communities.” The chair of Nashville’s Metro Council Immigrant Caucus called it what it was—a murder.

Mayor O’Connell had a different take.

Two days after the killing, he told reporters he hadn’t looked at the video. He said, “I’ll never be a person to comment first on really complicated matters.” He then offered his analysis: the problem was federal authorities working without close local coordination, implying that the solution was more cooperation between ICE and local police.

This all became incredibly relevant at Tuesday’s Council meeting where the Mayor’s push for more policing equipment funded by the State government was up for vote.

So I didn’t have much hope of the mayor coming around. But for the past two months, an experiment has been unfolding, and I had some hope that Council might see the light when presented with clear evidence of danger. Documented ties between state funding and federal immigration enforcement. Surveillance technology used by ICE. Legal architecture in Tennessee that offers no protection against data sharing with federal authorities.

The advocates for privacy had an unexpected victory early in the evening, with a wide margin voting down 15 security cameras for downtown. A win is a win and the courage of those who voted against it need to be honored. But it was the smallest of the items, and being the only one that was “allowed” to be classified as “surveillance”, there was quickly a sense that it may have been a sacrificial lamb. But when the cameras were gone, the cognitive dissonance returned, and Council accepted an armored vehicle, mobile command trailers and a tactical support post to the tune of $3m against the pleas of the Immigrant Caucus and the jeers of the gallery.

We can into Tuesday with a question:

Would our local officials rise to the occasion? Would they ask hard questions, demand transparency, and act in the interest of the people they represent?

Some questions were asked. Deeply impassioned statements were made by a few, especially those directly impacted by this.

The answer, as of Tuesday night, is no.

As a collective, they cannot or will not rise to meet this moment. We live in a different Nashville today than we did before Tuesday’s vote.

Not just because we accepted millions in policing equipment—equipment that flows from the same state fund currently bankrolling a federal task force making immigration arrests across Memphis—but because the entire political and business class of our city has demonstrated that they are, in aggregate, complicit in and apathetic to the violent victimization of Tennesseans.

It’s a hard thing to know, but it’s also clarifying. And clarity, however painful, is what we need to survive what’s coming together.

How We Got Here

In late November 2025, those of us who track surveillance policy in Nashville learned that an MOU was coming before Council involving the Nashville Downtown Partnership and $15 million in state funds.

As originally written, it appeared that the Downtown Partnership would be receiving all of the funds and performing the services directly. An armored vehicle was listed among the equipment, and nothing in the text indicated these items needed to be donated to Metro. It read as though NDP would own and operate all of this infrastructure.

This is the same Nashville Downtown Partnership that has gone 21 years without Metro Council approving its legally required budget. $37.8 million transferred since 2019 alone with no identified Council authorization.

The same NDP whose security contractor Soleren was fined $64,000 by the state for 32 violations after employees impersonated police officers downtown.

And this is the same NDP whose other subcontractor stored propane tanks in the library parking garage, sparking a fire in June that caused a partial collapse and has shuttered the library for seven months.

These are the people the mayor chose to route $15 million in state policing funds through.

The timing raised immediate red flags. The mayor’s office filed it the Friday before Thanksgiving, right before a special election that threw the Council calendar off and absorbed much of the political tension in the city. Whether intentional or not, the effect was that it was going to minimize public awareness.

We made sure that didn’t happen.

The Questions No One Would Answer

During initial discussions, there was significant ambiguity about who the actual recipient of these funds was, given that the Nashville Downtown Partnership (a private nonprofit) and the Nashville District Management Corporation (a quasi-governmental business improvement district operator) have very different oversight requirements. This mattered.

When a copy of the actual grant award was leaked, it became clear that the state had actually awarded the money to the Nashville District Management Corporation, not the Downtown Partnership. Yet the MOU that the mayor’s office had put together routed 100% of the funds through the Nashville Downtown Partnership.

It was extremely difficult to get straight answers.

Who applied for this grant? Who would own the equipment? What were the reporting requirements to the state? Why was a private nonprofit serving as a pass-through for a grant awarded to a quasi-governmental entity?

As weeks passed, more context emerged. Reports surfaced that Tennessee House Speaker Cameron Sexton had been coordinating with Stephen Miller since before ICE arrived in Memphis. The same funding pool giving Nashville $15 million had given Memphis $74 million.

At every juncture, the mayor and his staff were given opportunities to explain how this arrangement served the public interest. Yet there was virtually never a circumstance where the way they answered questions made anyone more confident that the public’s best interest was being considered.

The more we learned—and we had to do most of the digging ourselves to get any information about this—the more concerning it became.

What is this money?

Nashville is drawing from two state funding pools—both tied to immigration enforcement by Governor Lee's own words. The $15 million flows from the $100 million Downtown Public Safety Grant Fund (DPSG); when Lee announced that fund on October 2, 2025, he stated that "$100 million of available funds will be allocated to the Memphis area and will support the ongoing Memphis Safe Task Force, established by President Donald J. Trump." That Task Force, according to the Institute for Public Service Reporting, "has a dual mission of reducing violent crime and enforcing immigration law"—1 in 5 arrests are immigration violations. The Tennessee Lookout reports that ICE agents "either ride along with them or follow in unmarked dark-colored vehicles," with most stops "aimed at drivers in two of Memphis' biggest Hispanic neighborhoods: Hickory Hill and Berclair."

Nashville also received funding from the separate $75 million Violent Crime Intervention Fund (VCIF), which Lee launched in October 2022 with explicit border rhetoric: "Much of the violence in Tennessee is related to and connected to drug crime, drug trafficking. So long as we have the situation that we have going on in the border, we are going to have drug traffic-related crime going on all across this country."

The Mayor Knows Better

We know for certain that Mayor O’Connell is aware of the dangers of this type of technology.

In 2021, he reposted an article revealing that cameras at the same price point from the same corporation, Axis Communications, were being used in New Orleans by a nonprofit performing AI facial recognition. Alerts triggered by that system were shared with NOPD—despite their city council banning the practice.

O’Connell asked Nashville at the time to not follow New Orleans’ path.

Something has changed, and the broader public has taken notice.

What we know is that these equipment grants were arranged behind closed doors between parties that are in active insurance negotiations over liability for the destroyed Nashville downtown library parking garage.

The Vote

On Tuesday night, 22 people spoke during public comment opposing the resolutions and giving alternatives for how to spend that money. Two people spoke in favor.

High-ranking representatives of the Nashville Downtown Partnership were present in the gallery throughout.

The crowd that opposed this equipment was extremely diverse—mixed in ethnicity, gender identity, socioeconomic status, and political valence. They cited substantiated, credible fears that this infrastructure would be used for immigration enforcement, retaliation against advocates for immigrants, and that the legal architecture in Tennessee offered no protection against data sharing with federal authorities. They argued that the timing and opaqueness of the process suggested the public was being deliberately kept in the dark.

Council heard them.

Then Council voted to accept the equipment anyway.

To the people who spoke, it was an extremely personal matter because they credibly fear for their lives and the ones they love.

The Trap He Built Himself

This legislation became a big deal because those of us concerned about it made it a big deal. We never thought we would defeat it, but we knew we needed to make it public. And once we made it public, it put the mayor—who used to be the city’s primary proponent of privacy and being anti-surveillance—into a very uncomfortable position.

We did not build a trap for Mayor O’Connell. He built it himself. We just placed it on the public stage.

The escape hatch was always there, though. All he needed to do was engage openly and honestly with the public they took an oath to serve. Explain the reasoning, address the concerns.

That is not the path he chose.

Instead, he fought oversight, fought public hearings on things that clearly require them, and told every contingent that elected him—neighbors, advocates, membership coalitions—that they simply don’t know what they’re talking about when it comes to downtown safety.

His lobbying culminated with a new strategy to control the public narrative. His office advocated for the legislation on social media the day of the vote. And alarmingly, the post appeared not only on the mayor’s office’s official account, but on the official Instagram page of Metro Nashville itself—a startling overreach framing the official position of the entirety of Metro Nashville, seemingly in lockstep with the mayor’s priorities.

Priorities that, as many public commenters and even Council members highlighted, seem concerningly aligned with Tennessee House Speaker Cameron Sexton and the White House’s Stephen Miller, a documented booster of white supremacist ideology.

The comment sections of these social media posts clearly show just how badly the administration is at reading the room. Or it may just show how little they care about the opinions of the public they’re meant to serve.

The Web of Connections

A reasonable person might ask: how is any of this possible? A mayor abandoning his stated principles to such a surreal degree? An entity operating outside its financial oversight requirements for two decades, becoming a pass-through for millions in state surveillance and policing funds?

Isn’t there somebody supposed to provide oversight?

There is: the Tennessee State Comptroller. But from January 2009 until January 2021, that office was occupied by Justin Wilson, who happens to be the father-in-law of Kristen Wilson, Metro Nashville’s Chief of Operations and Performance—the same Kristen Wilson who coordinated this arrangement between the Nashville Downtown Partnership and the Metro Nashville Police.

And meanwhile, NDP has operated for 21 years without submitting their budget for required Metro Council approval. They’ve been collecting tens of millions in special assessments while avoiding the democratic oversight that our law requires.

This all came to light in summer 2025, and yet Metro Nashville has done nothing to remedy it, passing along millions more dollars to NDP despite knowing this arrangement isn’t actually authorized. They’re required to review their budget, and only once they approve it are they allowed to transfer the funds. This budget of the nonprofit—which has not been reviewed and approved for 21 years—now includes a $500,000 salary for their CEO.

Back to Kristen Wilson, because her professional background warrants examination.

Before entering public service, her primary resume builder was her work at LexisNexis, where she helped merge a company called ChoicePoint into them. ChoicePoint had a contract with the Immigration and Naturalization Service in the early 2000s. They took data obtained through extremely controversial means and sold it to the federal government for immigration enforcement.

As reported at the time, the methods were so problematic for acquiring this information that the country of Mexico sent officials to ChoicePoint’s offices to ensure they actually deleted the entire Mexican voter registry, which they had obtained through someone who subsequently went into hiding.

That initial INS contract eventually grew into a $22.1 million contract between LexisNexis and ICE to provide data on virtually every American for use in immigration enforcement. ICE budget documents state explicitly that they need this LexisNexis access because sanctuary policies have reduced local data sharing.

Ms. Wilson’s LinkedIn and her professional bios when she entered public life chiefly highlighted her work overseeing the merger between ChoicePoint and LexisNexis. And now Ms. Wilson is the primary person behind coordinating the arrangement that routes state funds through the Nashville Downtown Partnership to purchase equipment for MNPD—in a program that has been highlighted by the state of Tennessee as being tied to immigration enforcement.

O’Connell’s other staffing choices tell their own story. In July 2025, O’Connell received scrutiny from Kristi Noem for his alleged support of illegal immigrants based on a deeply absurd claim of doxxing immigration officers. Shortly thereafter, he brought on as chief of staff a woman who was previously appointed to positions by both Cameron Sexton and Bill Lee. Meanwhile, his Office of New Americans has remained unoccupied since that same month.

So when we see a mayor behaving in ways that don’t align with how he presented himself in campaigning and went on Council for years, it starts to seem like less of a mystery and more of a pattern.

Given the people that were working in his office, the question was never whether O’Connell would betray his stated principles. It was a question of when.

Nothing Stands Between Them

So back to the policing equipment that the Metro Council agreed to take on from the Nashville Downtown Partnership.

Let me be explicit about what’s been built here and what stands between this equipment and federal immigration enforcement.

Nothing. Nothing stands between them.

What we see in Memphis, what we see in Minneapolis and other cities, is that when ICE wants to commandeer our resources, extract our data, they will do it. And even if there was the willingness and ability to keep these physical resources out of the hands of federal agencies, the legal architecture in Tennessee permits data to flow to federal authorities with no obstacles.

Tennessee’s Information Protection Act, which went into effect in July 2025, authorizes data controllers and processors to share information with law enforcement whenever they reasonably, in good faith, believe someone may have violated any law, rule, or regulation. It doesn’t define what a rule or regulation is, but any of this could include federal immigration law. No warrants are required, no subpoena—just a reasonable belief, and the data processor can pass that information on to the federal agencies, many of whom they already have enormous contracts with.

And who enforces the Tennessee Information Protection Act? If a vendor shares your data with ICE, and perhaps they didn’t really have a reasonable belief that you had violated some law, rule, or regulation, only one person can take action: the Attorney General of Tennessee, who currently is Jonathan Skrmetti.

This is the same Skrmetti who has sued to support federal immigration enforcement, worked with Governor Lee to block refugee resettlement, and publicly stated his office will keep fighting alongside federal immigration authorities.

Skrmetti isn’t going to sue companies for coordinating with ICE.

58 Tennessee law enforcement agencies are now signed onto ICE’s 287(g) program, up from six when Trump took office.

What’s Coming Next

House Speaker Cameron Sexton announced in January that Tennessee has been working with Stephen Miller since before ICE arrived in Memphis, coordinating to make the state a model for federal immigration enforcement.

And they’re not done.

The 2026 legislative session has already seen the introduction of a White House-driven immigration package written, according to Sexton, after months of close collaboration with Stephen Miller. One bill would mandate 287(g) participation for all local enforcement, with fiscal penalties for non-compliance.

Nashville is the explicit target. Sexton called us out by name: “Nashville is saying, we’re not working with ICE. We can’t be a sanctuary city, but we can house them here.”

I’m not claiming to know what O’Connell intended by bringing these resolutions to council, but I’m describing what has been built and what’s coming next:

  • A funding stream tied to immigration enforcement in Memphis

  • Equipment flowing to MNPD

  • A legal architecture that permits data sharing with federal agencies without any warrants or subpoenas

  • An attorney general who won’t intervene

  • 58 agencies already deputized by ICE

  • A legislative package designed to force Nashville into compliance whether the mayor wants it or not

Whatever the mayor’s intentions, the infrastructure doesn’t care about intentions.

And I’ll say this: when you’re backed into a corner and you have no good options, you still have an option. You don’t have to help people do it faster, better, and sell out all of your values along the way.

The Diagnosis

When it comes to the policing equipment and what happened on Tuesday, winning outright was never the point because it never seemed possible.

The point was showing the lengths to which local leaders will go to keep their heads in the sand while everything around them burns—and learning whether there is a line that they won’t cross to enable the militarized infrastructure that the state of Tennessee and the federal government all want and have their fingers in.

And here’s what we learned: there is no line.

Research on cooperative governance teaches us that systems optimized for normal times become brittle and often fail under the pressure of abnormal times. And these times are very abnormal.

Tuesday night was a diagnostic test for our collective governance system, and it failed.

But tests are tools. They tell you who knows what they’re doing and who doesn’t. They tell you who can adapt to changing circumstances and who keeps executing predetermined plans while the ground shifts beneath them. Tests tell you where the disease has spread and what’s still healthy enough to save.

We have learned something fundamental about the current leaders in our system: they are incapable of governing through what’s coming.

They think they want to fight over police equipment? Sure. Congrats. Enjoy your toys. And a heavy conscience when you see who uses them and how.

The mayor—he can’t plead ignorance. We told him on the record in front of Council. He chose this.

And to the Council Members who endorsed this, I’ll say this: it’s better for everyone’s sake that we know the truth now.

We can’t count on them.

We Are All We’ve Got

The solidarity across Nashville’s usually fractured Left—housing advocates and immigration organizers, privacy wonks and faith leaders—it’s stronger than it’s been in a generation.

The mayor’s coalition? They won their equipment. They also showed everyone watching exactly who they are when it counts.

And council—time and time again, people begged them to be the last line of defense, and they said, three times in a row, no.

But we are not worse off for it, because now we know where we are and who is there for us. That’s not nothing. That’s the precondition for everything else that comes.

Here’s what I’ve learned watching this unfold: while they’ve been playing games, twisting definitions, timing votes to thin the crowds, treating public testimony as theater to be endured rather than intelligence to be heeded, something else has been happening on our side.

We’ve been learning how to govern.

Not in the sense of holding office. Not in the sense of doling out patronage, rewarding our friends and punishing our foes. But real governing—making collective decisions over our scarce, and getting scarcer every day, public resources.

We do this without titles because we are not in it for the titles. It’s not for resume building, or for profit, or for the next job, or for power, or for glory.

We are not waiting for the title of mayor, legislator, bureaucrat, or helper to show up and help. The world is full of people who seek the title but shirk the responsibility.

We take on the responsibility of caring for our community, because that is what Tennesseans do.

We volunteer.

We are all we’ve got, and that is more than enough.

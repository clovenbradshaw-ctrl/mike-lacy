On a January night at Metro Council, as members debated whether to accept policing and surveillance equipment tied to a $15 million state grant—equipment routed through the Nashville Downtown Partnership—Council Member Jordan Huffman offered a blunt warning:

“We are one incident away from having the money turn off in Nashville.”

That debate is over. The equipment has been accepted. The city has taken the gear. The Mayor’s office is no longer attempting to obligate the Downtown Partnership to anything in exchange. The questions briefly raised—about procurement, oversight, long-term costs, civil liberties, and what exactly this infrastructure is being built to do—have, its boosters hope, been put to bed.

What, if anything, this has to do with the unresolved liability surrounding the library parking garage that unexpectedly exploded last year while under contractual management by the Downtown Partnership is anyone’s guess.

But Huffman’s line remains. It wasn’t a slip. It was a clear statement of governing logic: the point is not merely to prevent harm, but to prevent disruption. Not primarily to protect residents, but to protect revenue. Not to treat downtown as a neighborhood, but to treat it as an economic engine whose uninterrupted operation must be guaranteed.

That sentence clarifies what had long been subtext. Many Tennessee leaders are not primarily concerned with safety in the broad sense—housing stability, mental health, addiction treatment, neighborhood infrastructure, or community well-being. They are concerned with protecting the economic conditions that allow the state’s most profitable district to function smoothly.

They are protecting confidence—investment, valuation, reputation, throughput. Downtown has been transformed into a high-yield asset. And there appears to be no cost—in public dollars or civil liberties—that is considered too high to secure it.

Mayor O’Connell offered his own version of this logic while defending expanded surveillance downtown:

“We’re continuing to solicit feedback… but we want to make sure that everyone understands the context of how different the downtown safety environment is from other parts of the city.”

Downtown is not treated as a neighborhood. It is treated as an asset: dense with tourism, hotel taxes, convention bookings, alcohol sales, rising property values, and corporate investment. When the Mayor insisted downtown was “different,” he was not describing crime patterns. He was describing capital.

Downtown is “different” because disruption there threatens the city’s most valuable revenue stream. It is “different” because it is where Nashville’s brand is monetized. It is “different” because instability there is expensive.

To be clear: downtown does have real risks. It concentrates nightlife, large events, and dense crowds. Violence on Broadway reverberates beyond those immediately harmed—it becomes national news, rattles conventions, unsettles investors. Officials are not wrong to say the risk profile is distinct.

But that reality sharpens the deeper question. Why do visible disruptions generate instant urgency and millions in funding, while the slow violence of housing instability, untreated addiction, underfunded schools, and neighborhood disinvestment is treated as background noise?

The issue is not whether downtown has safety needs. The issue is what kinds of danger the city treats as intolerable. Not the everyday harms that shorten lives quietly—but the disruptions that threaten confidence and revenue flow.

An overly simple story says this is about appeasing “downtown interests”—bar owners, hotel groups, Broadway operators. That’s not entirely false. But it misses the scale of the machine.

The real power isn’t local bar owners. It’s the financial ecosystem they operate inside. Debt, investor expectations, rising valuations—these structures discipline even the biggest local players. What looks like local control is often just the front end of a larger extraction chain.

We on the left often say it’s turtles all the way down. The uncomfortable truth is that it’s also turtles all the way up.

We can see that in the recent firestorm of the Mayor’s comments about Acme Feed & Seed (the most Nashville sentence I’ve ever written).

In booming districts, even property owners are embedded in financial structures that limit their freedom. Markets reward growth and punish stagnation. Investors demand returns. Valuations must rise. Under those conditions, churn is not an accident—it is the design. The role of government, to neoliberals like Mr. O’Connell, is to facilitate that extraction no matter its impact on the city and its culture.

This is precisely where the rubber of the rhetoric—“I want you to stay”—should hit the road. But it doesn’t. Because acknowledging that exploitative wealth extraction is a problem on Broadway would raise uncomfortable questions about the same exploitative wealth extraction happening in Bordeaux.

And a sitting mayor doesn’t get to co-chair the Chamber of Commerce’s “Partnership 2030: A Regional Strategy for Community Vitality, Mobility, and Character” while being even mildly critical of unfettered capitalism.

When the county’s highest executive formally co-chairs a Chamber-led regional growth strategy, the boundary between public governance and private capital becomes unusually thin, and it shows you, if nothing else, whose worldview is sinking into his grey matter.

Partnership 2030 isn’t just a collection of local boosters—it is a whose who of the power players whose gravity sucks Nashvillians unavoidably into their orbit, from Regions and Pinnacle to JPMorgan Chase, Wells Fargo, and Bank of America, global consultancies like Deloitte, EY, PwC, and Accenture, development heavyweights like Southwest Value Partners/Nashville Yards and Ryman Hospitality Properties, brokerage giants CBRE and JLL, and even multinational platforms like Amazon and Meta whose business models revolve around scale, data, and expansion.

When the mayor co-chairs that strategy, he is formally embedded in a coalition whose fiduciary logic is growth, valuation, and investor confidence. So when the partnership invokes “community vitality, mobility, and character,” those words are operating inside a framework shaped by lenders, landlords, contractors, and global tech firms whose survival depends on perpetual expansion, who will quite literally be fired by their boards if they show even an ounce of reservation in maximizing shareholder value.

Turtles all the way up.

[Clears throat before making a liberal artsy comment] Immanuel Kant, in his treatise on the “Categorical Imperative,” stipulated that values only become real when they cost you something—slower growth, lower returns, friction for developers, tension with donors. If protecting “character” requires constraining capital, and capital is who you are co-chairing with, the rhetoric will always yield to the revenue.

The system, as it is currently designed, does not incentivize cultural depth or long-term community. It incentivizes homogenization. It rewards what is scalable, profitable, insurable, and easily replicated. It produces disposable places: districts engineered for throughput rather than belonging, consumption rather than community.

What is being made “safe” downtown with surveillance equipment is not a culture or even people of a specific class. It is an asset class.

And in the language of finance, assets must be secured—stabilized, insured, protected against volatility. That is where “risk management” enters the picture.

In everyday speech, “risk” means danger to people. In financial language, risk means volatility—threats to predictable returns. The goal of risk management is not moral; it is actuarial. Reduce uncertainty. Protect revenue streams. Suppress volatility.

Downtown Nashville, like many profitable corridors in America, is increasingly governed like a portfolio. And a portfolio does not have neighbors—it has liabilities.

Once governance adopts that frame, the solutions become predictable. You do not ask what people need. You ask what outcomes must be prevented. What disruptions must be neutralized. What volatility must be suppressed.

In that framework, surveillance is not an aberration. It is a tool. Cameras, integrated data systems, centralized command, rapid-response policing—these are mechanisms for stabilizing an asset. Visible poverty, protest, unpredictability, viral footage—these are treated as risk factors.

But surveillance is not a one-time purchase. It is institutional infrastructure. Cameras require video storage, maintenance, staffing. Data requires analysts. Analysts generate enforcement. Enforcement generates arrests, court processing, and jail bookings. The system expands because it must justify itself.

Infrastructure demands use. It produces incidents to rationalize its own continuation. The pipeline is not accidental. It is structural.

You can see the visible edge of this logic in “Solaren Risk Management”, the private security vendor that helps enforce the Downtown Partnership’s control over Lower Broadway. “Risk management” in this context does not mean preventing tourists from slipping on stairs. It means managing what is allowed to appear downtown.

Unhoused people are risk. Protest is risk. Disorder is risk. The objective is removal, not care. And there is an invisible layer.

Companies like LexisNexis Risk Solutions do not patrol sidewalks. They sell raw data to be used for analytics. They provide the data that can turn humans into categories of liability and threat. This can be risk to a portfolio of mortgages, data that estimates the likelihood you default on your load. Or, as we’re increasingly seeing, it may be the kind of data that gets you labelled you as a potential domestic terrorist and predicting that you’re going to commit crimes.

Kristin Wilson, the Mayor’s Chief of Operations and Performance, coordinated the proposed $15 million surveillance agreement between MNPD and the Downtown Partnership. Before entering public service, she worked at LexisNexis Risk Solutions, overseeing their merger with ChoicePoint, a vendor cited by the country of Mexico for illegally obtaining the voting records of all Mexican citizens and selling it to US immigration agencies. LexisNexis Risk Solutions has grown that business into a $22.1M contract with ICE to track the whereabouts of most Americans.

That detail matters less as biography and more as illustration. “Risk solutions” is not a neutral phrase. It reflects a philosophy: people and behaviors can be categorized, scored, and managed to reduce institutional exposure.

One agency pushes bodies out of sight. The other helps decide provides the raw data to decide who to target.

And then there’s the Oracle of it all.

Meanwhile, Nashville is tying its future to Oracle through the East Bank development. Oracle is not just another tenant. It is one of the world’s most powerful data infrastructure companies and a major federal contractor. At the same time the city is expanding downtown surveillance capacity—more cameras, more integration, more centralized control—it is anchoring its next phase of growth to a corporation whose business is data governance at scale.

Separately, each move can be framed as pragmatic. Together, they form a pattern: a city reorganizing itself around the collection, management, and monetization of information.

This was never only a debate about cameras. It was a debate about what Nashville believes downtown is.

That belief was visible in the ACME Feed & Seed controversy. Asked about a reported property tax increase (from roughly $129,000 to about $600,000), Mayor O’Connell emphasized market valuation under state oversight and suggested it was up to the owner to “capture the value” in the property. The response wasn’t careless—it was doctrinal. The city does not exist to preserve a particular version of Nashville; it exists to steward market conditions. If long-standing businesses can’t absorb rising valuations, that isn’t framed as policy failure. It’s framed as demand.

The administration later posted a “fact check,” a stunning admission of political weakness that these comments created for the mayor. But the expanded context did nothing to indicate that the Mayor is anything but a staunch adherent to the market-as-god economic philosophy that has led to the right-wing populist backlash against liberal elites for the past decade. The defensive clap back served only to crystallize that Mayor O’Connell is fully and unapologetically committed to being a neoliberal in his approach to governing Music City And that he genuinely does not understand why he is being criticized for his comments.

I know every landlord has a different approach to this as commercial property owners. Some of them have, you know, triple net leases. They’re [sic] a bunch of different ways for this to work out. I think this is a tough one because you look at businesses all along Broadway, all along 2nd Avenue. You look at the volume of transactions that are there and the prices people are willing to pay for property, and it’s tough. It’s competitive. It’s a place where a lot of people want to be, and the market evolves. And, you know, we see new businesses starting even as old businesses -- in some cases, beloved old businesses -- are closing.

In the “fact check” rebuttal, the philosophy remained: market displacement is treated as natural evolution, while market interruption—anything threatening tourism revenue, conventions, investor confidence, or downtown throughput—is treated as emergency.

But when protest, instability, or reputational risk threatens to disrupt downtown as a financial asset, millions appear for surveillance and tactical readiness.

That asymmetry is the point.

When the market pushes out a business, it is evolution. When instability threatens revenue flow, it is emergency.

This is where “public safety” blurs into asset protection. Surveillance and rapid-response infrastructure are justified not only to prevent harm, but to prevent interruption. The feared outcome is not only violence—it is volatility.

Governance shifts from care to continuity. From community to risk mitigation. From human need to investor confidence.

The question Nashville must answer is not whether it values safety. We all claim to value being “safe.” The question is whether, in protecting the safety of certain people’s profits—and the worldview that treats exploitation as inevitable—no cost is considered too high. Not even the steady erosion of everyone else’s civil liberties.

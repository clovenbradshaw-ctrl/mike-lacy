A city’s values are best expressed not in the PR statements it releases, but in what it builds in concrete and steel. If a city values safety, for example, it will build safe roads; if a city values high speed travel, they’ll build highways in the middle of town, even if it claims to value safety first.

We can also see what a city values by what it removes from public space, and what it protects at all costs. I want to tell you three stories about infrastructure removal in my own city of Nashville, and ask what these removals say about the city’s values.

Let’s start with Hadley Avenue, a sleepy residential street in Old Hickory on the outskirts of Nashville.

Starting in 2024, the residents of Hadley Avenue began advocating for Nashville’s traffic calming initiative. For a street to receive traffic calming measures—things like speed bumps and curb extensions—it must meet certain requirements. The street must be a low-speed residential street, and residents must apply through a formal process.

If a street is selected for traffic calming, the Nashville Department of Transportation (NDOT) draws up plans and hosts multiple meetings for residents to review and comment on the designs. Then homeowners—only homeowners, not renters—vote on whether the traffic calming should be installed.

Hadley Avenue went through this entire process: the meetings, the design phase, the vote. Traffic calming passed with 79% approval—a landslide. But when the safety measures were installed, their councilmember, Jeff Eslick, apparently hadn’t been paying attention. When he found out, he went ballistic.

One source tells me he yelled at NDOT staff at a virtual meeting before hanging up on them. He reportedly looked up which residents voted in favor of traffic calming and began showing up at their homes, knocking on their doors and demanding they answer him in person: did they really want the traffic calming they voted for?

Personally, I would find it deeply intimidating for my councilmember to show up at my house and demand answers about my vote. Residents had followed the process exactly as it was designed, but now they were being pressured for doing so.

What’s worse is that NDOT acquiesced, and the traffic calming measures were removed. In doing this, the city set a dangerous precedent: throw a fit against safety measures, and you get your way. So far, I’m unaware of throwing a fit being a successful strategy for making any street safer in Nashville.

This is not the only time Metro Nashville has revealed its values by what it removes.

When Citizens Build What the City Won’t

For months now, I’ve been part of a grassroots effort to build and install benches across Nashville. These benches exist because we, citizens in this city, no longer wish to remain passive. We are tired of wading through cumbersome bureaucratic processes to get half the improvements we want in twice the time.

Instead, we decided we could improve our community ourselves. So we built benches. Lots of them. We didn’t ask for permission or money, or fill out any paperwork. We saw a need and filled it. People responded with overwhelming positivity to our project, and we invited them to join us. We noticed that the benches kept walking off, but we assumed it was other private citizens taking them. C’est la vie.

Unbeknownst to us, however, the city was systematically removing our benches and piling them up in a warehouse.

Officials claimed that the benches were in the right-of-way or that they violated ADA standards. But we are scrupulous about making sure our benches do not impede anyone’s ability to move through the city; that would be antithetical to their purpose.

In some locations, the benches may technically sit within the right-of-way. But those same right-of-ways are already filled with other objects: utility poles, traffic signs, and other infrastructure owned by the city. In many cases, the right-of-way is more theoretical than practical.

To be clear: claims that these benches violate the right-of-way or ADA standards are not made in good faith. They are simply the justification given after the fact for removing them.

Which raises a simple question: why remove them at all?

And while you’re thinking about municipal governments removing helpful things, you might also ask the same question about Hadley Avenue. Why remove safety measures that residents overwhelmingly asked for?

In bureaucratic systems like city governments, there is always a bias toward more bureaucracy. Doing the right thing without going through the proper channels is viewed negatively—even when the outcome is clearly good.

My city lacked benches, so some friends and I built benches. Instead of seeing this as citizens improving their own community, the city saw it as an aberration to be corrected. City workers were sent across Nashville to collect the benches we had installed. They did this with surprising efficiency—far more efficiently than if they had been installing benches themselves.

The Hadley Avenue story complicates this explanation.

The traffic calming process in Nashville is the perfect example of a proper bureaucratic channel. Residents followed the rules, attended the meetings, and voted. They waited (and waded) through a years-long process that cost the city tens of thousands of dollars.

And yet a single grown man’s whining, moaning, and yelling got it removed within a couple of weeks. Like with the benches, the removal of the Hadley Avenue safety measures was far more efficient than their installation. No paperwork required to make a road less safe.

Who Our Streets Are Designed For

What we remove from public space reveals just as much about our city’s values as what we build.

Time and time again, Nashville removes infrastructure that makes pedestrians and transit users safer and more comfortable. In doing so, the city sends a clear message: the most important people are the ones in cars. Our roads, our infrastructure, and even our decision-making processes are designed to serve them.

This is motonormativity at the governmental level. But who, exactly, does motonormativity actually serve?

Consider who has reliable access to a car. Between maintenance, insurance, gas, and car payments, AAA estimates that owning a single car costs roughly $12,000 a year. In a city like Nashville, it is common for households to have two or even three vehicles. That means some families are spending $24,000 or even $36,000 every year simply to participate in daily life.

When transportation costs that much, something else has to give. Often, that something is housing. Families stretch their transportation budgets and compensate by moving farther away, often into majority-minority neighborhoods where housing is cheaper.

A motonormative city is not just a dangerous city; it is also a segregated one. There’s still some leaders south of the Mason-Dixon Line who see this as a desirable outcome.

The story of Hadley Avenue and the story of the benches are, in the end, the same story. In one case, residents followed every rule, attended every meeting, and voted overwhelmingly for safety improvements—only to see them removed because a single powerful man threw a tantrum. In the other, ordinary residents saw a need in their community and filled it. We built something useful, something humane, something that made the city more livable.

And the city removed that too.

When a city repeatedly removes the things that make life safer and more comfortable for people outside of cars, it is telling us something about what—and who—it values.

The Other Benches Nashville Removed

The bench project took off in response to a different removal of pedestrian infrastructure, the third and final example I’ll tell you about today (though there are many).1 In fact, the project started because of another bench removal: the removal of city benches from Korean Veterans Boulevard.

Since its inception, KVB has been imagined as a place to stroll. It was designed with wide sidewalks and a separated median, lined with trees and—you guessed it—benches. The idea was a space where people could stroll, sit, and chat: a hospitable entry to downtown Nashville.

But last year, a powerful group of business owners known as the Nashville Downtown Partnership, together with Councilmember Jacob Kupin, worked to have the benches removed. The reason was obvious to anyone who had ever been downtown: unhoused Nashvillians used these benches. They were replaced with giant balls for “beautification.” Really.

The NDP worried that this would look bad for our important tourism industry, and the removal of the benches was a naked attempt to make downtown less hospitable in hopes of driving homeless residents away. Not only was this ineffective at the implicit goal of removing our most vulnerable citizens—it was cruel. But like Hadley Avenue and the guerrilla bench project, the removal of these benches laid bare the dearest value of our municipal government: protecting the comfort of the wealthy and maintaining the status quo.

And when someone keeps telling you who they are, eventually you believe them.

It Was Never Just About the Art.

Political appointees in Nashville have usurped the legally enshrined rights and processes of a duly appointed commission and have not backed down. This is a textbook example of “democratic backsliding”, a phenomenon that’s eroding Representative Democracy’s norms and paving the way for authoritarian rule on a state and federal levels.

Beginning last July, Metro Finance and Metro Legal froze city arts funding for eight months following a pivotal decision by the Metro Arts Commission to prioritize smaller, more diverse organizations in their allocations. They are now poised to lift this freeze, albeit with new restrictions that could hinder similar actions in the future.

This raises critical questions about the autonomy of all Boards and Commissions’ authority to control funding decisions, and the City Council’s1 chartered oversight on these processes. It also has broader implications for democratic autonomy in a state that is the face of Representative Democracy’s backsliding in front of a global audience.

While the administrative scope creep of Finance and Legal are highly problematic, they are to some degree built into the political power game of politics. In fact, this type of thumb on the scale has has happened publicly before, with Metro Arts. It’s perhaps naive to think that leaders who rise to such positions will always stay within their prescribed boundaries.

But in the game of political power plays, it is the role of political bodies that have been encroached upon to push back and exercise good boundaries — this is the essence of democratic checks and balances. The Metro Art Commission’s lack of resistance to this democratic backsliding, and perhaps complicitness by some commissioners in this de-democratization, is highly troubling.

The City Council’s apparent concession to the demands of the Finance Director, which include stripping the Council of their oversight authority on the processes of the Metro Arts Commission, is also alarming.

While the Mayor gets to nominate commissioners to Metro Arts, and they can fully be their political allies, high school crushes, ex-coworkers, or favorite baristas, the check on their expertise and independence comes from their vetting and (possible) approval by the City Council. That is where the administration’s influence is meant to end. According to charter, the processes Metro Arts determines for grantmaking are to be approved by the City Council. When we circumvent these processes, we damage the norms of democratic rule and lessen the public faith in democracy as a whole.

Metro Nashville as a whole has a dearth of standard operating procedures, especially for how departments engage with one another. The hiring of no less than six outside law firms because of this situation is testament to that. But there is a set of rules for how the city is legally meant to operate, and that is the City Charter. The players in this power struggle all seem woefully disinterested in the content of that document, the city’s de facto constitution and enshrinement of what limited amount of representative democracy the state government allows cities.

I’m not a lawyer, I may be off base in my reading of the City Charter and my understanding of its import. But in my discussions with various up-to-speed attorneys, former and current city employees, elected officials, and activists, there is immense concern over these types of overreaches. Regardless of who they think should get arts funding, they all agree this isn’t the way it should be decided.

From those close to the political game, there’s a latent rage about this type of perceived cronyism, a “I expected so much better from them” vibe. This seems not to have trickled out to the vox populi in the form of approval polls. But even if this is just political business as usual, the times in Tennessee are far from usual, and there’s a lot more at stake than watercolors and oboes.

Despite Nashville’s efforts to preserve city autonomy against state encroachment, the actions of its most powerful political appointees are more in line with the anti-democratic agendas they’ve claimed to be fighting against.

How will our “Truly Progressive” Mayor reinstate the autonomy of the Metro Arts Department (and all Boards and Commissions) and respond to concerns of executive overreach? Cosigning the backsliding of democratic norms is a bad look in a state that has, quantitatively, the worst functioning democracy in America.

My dog in this fight is simple and singular: democratic norms and the rule of law. Only with those in place can we build something better than we had yesterday, and survive the unimaginable challenges of tomorrow.

My message for residents, activists, representatives, and administration reps is the same: learn the rules and then change them if they’re unfair; develop clear and transparent process to build trust in the system; destroy your enemies by making them your friends; do the self-work you need to think more big picture than your current emotional state is allowing; and remember that in governance, all actions leave a trail of norms and precedence, meaning how you do something is just as important as what you do.

When we don’t operate like that, we contribute to problems far beyond whatever immediate inconvenience we are trying to fix, and we erode our social contract in the process. And then who do we have to blame when people turn to Godzilla to knock it all down to start again? No democracy will last forever, but are your actions harming, or healing it?

Now back to Metro Arts…

Ars Gratia Potentiae // Art for the Sake of Power

The Administration’s plan to solve the arts crisis is to pay out all the recipients of both the equity-based July vote that Metro Legal ordered be reversed, and a controversial (and potentially Civil Rights Title VI violating) August vote that undid the equitable funding model.

The plan underway is to pay 100% of what each organization, large and small, was promised in either vote. But the funding seemingly comes with legally questionable stipulations to the City Council regarding the autonomy of the Arts Commission — and by implication all independent boards and commissions. Councilmembers should be asking, is this the solution you called for, or a monkey’s paw with potentially harrowing precedence?

A March 22 letter, written by Metro Finance Director Crumbo, and forwarded to the City Council by the Mayor’s Office, provided terms stating that funding processes and grant determinations of Metro Arts moving forward should be done in conjunction with “The Administration”. This despite the fact that Finance, Legal, and Mayor’s Office are not given this authority in the city charter.

To be specific, these are the alleged, undemocratic things that need to be accounted for:

  1. Metro Legal exceeded its advisory role by allegedly instructing, rather than advising, the Arts Commission to reverse its equitable funding decision from July 2023, during a private, unrecorded executive session, potentially illegally circumventing Tennessee's Sunshine Laws. The MHRC report found no legal basis for this private session.

  2. Metro Finance unilaterally withheld Metro Arts grants, seemingly vastly overstepping its authority. This action ostensibly aimed to prevent a hypothesized future budget overdraft, despite no public evidence of an impending deficit and without apparent authority to do this in the charter. The charter specifies that the Finance Director should decline invoices only if funds are unavailable, not halt all expenditures due to potential future issues.

  3. Metro Finance dictated conditions, seemingly far outside its chartered scope, on future arts funding, asserting that normal operations would resume only after changes in the executive director position and Administrative oversight on the commission's decision-making processes and grant determinations. These conditions undermine the Arts Commission's chartered authority to pick its own leaders, make its own grant determinations, and develop its own grant making process with the City Council’s oversight.

You’ll note that none of these issues I raise have anything to do with who received funding, if decisions were made discriminatorily, and whether or not Metro Arts keeps good bookkeeping practices. We may assume the worst in all of these cases, and still my concerns stand that democratic best practices and chartered processes were not followed by Metro Legal and Finance in addressing any of their ostensive concerns, and these actions have been tolerated by the current and former Mayors, and insufficiently challenged by the City Council.

Let’s remind ourselves: all this chaos arose only after a duly appointed commission decided to give a little less money to the Frist and a little more money to independent artists (who happened to be a little less white).

The Mayor’s Office has not clarified their stance on these alleged overreaches of authority by its most powerful departments, or what their sentiment is about being implicated in the usurpation of democratic autonomy of a chartered commission and its oversight by the Metro City Council.

My questions on this matter, submitted to the Finance Director’s Office on March 31, 2024, have not as yet been responded to or acknowledged. My 16 questions to Mr. Crumbo centered around the legal authority behind his March 22 requests of the City Council and his office’s prior actions to halt all FY24 arts funding.

I could be wrong about some of these details. I have a track record of correcting myself when I’m proven wrong, so if he responds and corrects me in other forums, let me know!

But the content of these democratic overreaches isn’t the only issue, it’s the context.

Volunteering for a State of Decline

Tennessee is at the foreground of national and international ingresses of authoritarianism on representative democracies’ processes and norms. It is extraordinarily problematic to not reckon with the precedent these types of undemocratic actions set and the norms it helps erode.

Professor Jacob Grumbach of Berkley’s Goldman School of Public Policy developed a US State Democracy Index, published in 2022 as “Laboratories against Democracy: How National Parties Transformed State Politics” by Princeton University Press.

The State Democracy Index (raw data available here) ranks Tennessee as by far the least democratic state in the union, based on the prevalence of gerrymandering, voter access limits and disenfranchisement, and inequitable electoral policies and practices.

The Brookings Institute identifies two elements that are fundamental to democratic decline, (1) Strategic manipulation of elections, and (2) Executive aggrandizement. The first is what Professor Grumbach is focused on. The second, more relevant here, happens like this:

Even a legitimately elected leader can become an autocrat through executive aggrandizement: consolidating power by reducing the independence of the civil service and by undermining the “checks and balances” provided by the legislature and judicial system.

So, what’s that got to do with some arts funding? I don’t mean to overblow what might seem to some like a trivial amount of funding for Art Supplies vs. Opera Singers, but to me, this has never simply been about arts funding.

We have to acknowledge that anti-democratic actions like this Metro Arts situation is doing the work for political movements that explicitly are looking to undermine “small d” democratic checks and balances on executive power on local, state, and federal levels.

We like to act like city politics don’t really matter in the scheme of things.

Many of us know more names of US Reps from other states that we hate than our own city council people.

Or we say things like: “there’s no Democrat or Republican way to clean the streets,” and pretend there’s a neutrality to it all.

But truly, if all politics is local, then what we allow locally is what we’ll allow nationally. This has less to do with party politics, than it does with social values, norms, and the very idea of democracy.

Can we let it really sink in for a second — Tennessee is the worst functioning democracy in America. Perhaps that can be inspiring in a perverse way, we are first in being worst, which means that our actions, even on seemingly trivial things like arts funding, have trickle up ramifications that can tessellate out into different futures for our nation’s 248 year old experiment in self-rule.

The eyes of world are on us. What are we doing with that attention?

There are real ways our local government institutions can solidify the norms of representative democracy and respect the rule of law and checks and balances, which in turn impact norms of state and national politics and help establish justice and insure domestic tranquility. And then there’s what Metro Legal and Finance are doing.

The Call is Coming From Inside the (Old Court) House

Metro Law Director Wally Dietz was rightly celebrated on April 10, 2023 when his legal defense of Nashville’s democratic autonomy preserved the city council at its self-determined 40 seats, news that came in just minutes before the Nashville City Council voted to reinstate Justin Jones to the TN General Assembly after his racist ouster by the ruling party.

It was a victory on victory, coming on the heels of the city’s disenfranchisement in national elections with the loss of its Congressional seat amid encroachment by the state on its “home rule” of the Airport Authority.

Dietz’s work was a bulwark against the literal definition of democratic erosion.

That’s why it disturbs me that the hypocrisy of his office disrupting the independent decision making of a chartered commission, and potentially helping commit a Civil Rights Violation in the process, isn’t bigger news or more concern for Nashvillians.

It is simply remarkable that this time last year, Dietz and Metro Legal’s efforts were dedicated single-mindedly to fighting off the state’s attack on Nashville’s autonomy. Whereas now, like an overactive immune system turning against its own organism, Metro Legal is behaving like a municipal form of Multiple Sclerosis, triggering the hiring of six outside law firms to assist various Metro Departments attack and defend against one another, like violently misfiring T cells inflaming one’s own body.

Meanwhile, the state takes what it wants from from the city unchallenged.

The actions of Metro Finance and Metro Legal, cosigned by both Mayor’s under which this scandal has unfolded, aligns with the strategy of the autocratic attack on a government for the people and by the people. The intentionality or awareness of this alignment is irrelevant, the effect is the same.

There will always be legal issues Metro Legal needs to help departments navigate. There will be tough love that must come from Finance when budgets run low and invoices aren’t filed. But the shadowy, conflict-ridden, frankly sloppily executed coup against the Arts Commission is not these departments doing their appointed jobs.

It looks a whole lot more like an attempt to reign in some uppity “you know whats” before they started thinking they actually ran the show.

In their callous disregard for the racist and inequitable outcomes of their unwarranted interventions, and the flippant indifference to usurping the legal authority of executive limits, the actions of these directors feels more like an audition to work for a 2024 Trump Administration than the actions of the “truly progressive” O’Connell Administration most of us voted for.

It leaves me asking, did Metro Nashville city officials stand up last year to the authoritarian bullies on the capitol to fight for the right of self-governance and democratic norms, the cornerstone of freedom, and the prerequisites of life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness?

Or did Nashville Officials fight the legislature just to determine which well heeled white guys get to decide where the money goes?

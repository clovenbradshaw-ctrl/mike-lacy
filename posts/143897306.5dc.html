For Mayor O’Connell to come out moderately politically unscathed from the Metro Arts debacle, he will likely be looking to satisfy two seemingly incommunsrate agendas.

  1. Maintain the community-driven equitable arts methodology developed in 2022 and maintain its alignment with the soon-to-be approved charter amendment adding “equity” to the Arts Commission’s cultural equity statement.

  2. Allow administration officials who’ve been criticized by Metro watchers like me to save face for their take over of the department in the light issues they’ve found with the department since a funding award vote took place in July 2023.

It’s quite a Gordian Knot to untie.

But a suggestion from the Metro Human Relations Commission laws out the path to resolution, and I’ll be explaining why it’s both legally necessary, and also politically expedient for all those involved in this knotty issue. Imagine that, just following the rules could benefit all of us.

But first, with the authority a Western Classics degree, I’ll say the the story of the Gordian Knot is not a very good parable. In fact, it’s kinda dumb.

It’s basically: Alexander the Great came into a town that had a chariot tied to post inside a temple. Prophecy had it that "whoever undid the impossible knot between the chariot and the temple would rule all of Asia. Alexander undid the knot, and the rest is ancient history.

In the most common version, he lopped it half with his sword. “There’s the solution to your stupid knot,” to paraphrase the 23 year old conqueror doing a very 23 year old thing.

Plutarch tells a better version. Staring upon the chariot, with the rope looped around it’s wagon pole and tied in a cryptic braid to a post, Alexander had an epiphany: “By simply taking out the so-called ‘hestor’, or pin, of the wagon-pole, by which the yoke-fastening was held together, and then drawing away the yoke” the knot just fell away.'

Rather than slicing through the knot, or spending time like countless aspirants before him that tried to untangle every cord and keep it, Alexander found a simple and elegant way to make the knot not his problem.

Since the July vote of the Metro Arts Commission that reduced funding for large arts institutions, and augmented it for smaller more diverse ones and independent artists, there’s been entwining of agendas, motives, controversies, findings, rulings, feuding, and gossiping, all lashed together into a rat king dropped onto the desk of a Mayor who didn’t start any of this.

To quote the Great Municipal Sociology Tome that is “The Wire”, the Metro Arts ordeal is a “bowl of shit” that was set down in front of our popular young Mayor with the simple imperative: “eat it.”

There’s a long list of unresolved questions about the Metro Arts Commission that, we're I the Mayor, I wish I could just hack through like a knot in a strange city with customs I know not of. While O’Connell is a Nashville native, it seems when it comes to navigating the intricacies of modern equity politics, he is a stranger in a strange land.

But the solution to this conundrum lays right before us, and it appears as if its implementation is already under way.

Metro leaders in Legal and Finance have been looking for something that’s been “done wrong” in Metro Arts for a good eight months now. Their motivations are unclear. As someone far more versed in City politics and mechanics told me recently, it’s “the job of officials like that to keep things as they are” and it’s disingenuous of us to act like a major strategic shift in Arts funding, the likes of what the July 2023 Commission voting on, wouldn’t cause some major headaches for orgs that, in Former Arts Commissioner Cheeks words, has “gotten used to it.”

For all of the billable hours put into dissecting Metro Arts’ workplace culture, consulting contracts, and relationship to Supreme affirmative action rulings, something was overlooked. Something that actually has great legislative and democratic import which affects all future (and technically past) funding allocations from the Metro Arts Commission.

In July 2023, when the Arts Commissions voted on which funding criteria they wanted to use to distribute funds (not specific awardees but the basis of what types of groups should get how much funding) the did not follow their charter obligated oversight process.

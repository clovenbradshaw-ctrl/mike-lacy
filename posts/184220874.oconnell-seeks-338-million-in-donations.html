The proposed equipment to be donated flows through Governor Lee’s Downtown Public Safety Grant. The same grant structure allocated $100 million to the Memphis Safe Task Force—the federal-state operation that includes ICE.

When Lee launched the Violent Crime Intervention Fund (VCIF) in 2022, he was explicit about its connection to immigration enforcement.

Much of the violence in Tennessee is related to and connected to drug crime, drug trafficking. So long as we have the situation that we have going on in the border, we are going to have drug traffic-related crime going on all across this country.

The deep irony at the heart of this

Mayor O’Connell won many supporters during his time on Metro Council speaking out in support of the unhoused and for the right to data privacy and opposed to the “surveillance state.”

Here is a post of his from 2021 decrying New Orleans’ adoption of a mass surveillance system. “Strong communities are better for safety than a surveillance state,” he wrote on Twitter. “We can build safer communities through smart, strategic investments that don’t involve spying on each other.”

At some point since being elected, O’Connell appears to have changed his mind. Not only was his fixation on implementing the surveillance tool Fusus counter to everything he seemed to stand for before being elected, but the most recent batch of resolutions are an astounding refutation of his past position.

When on Metro Council representing District 19, O’Connell “regularly publicly railed against surveillance in his social media posts” and even “advised his followers on Twitter to obtain memberships to the privacy watchdog Electronic Frontier Foundation.” He warned Nashville about the dangers of public-private surveillance partnerships like what has emerged in New Orleans through Project NOLA. That surveillance apparatus now includes more than 5,000 cameras across the city, with New Orleans purchasing cameras from vendors including Hikvision and Axis Communications.

One of the resolutions O’Connell has submitted to Council for approval on January 20: $150,000 for fifteen “Axis Video Cameras,” costing $10,000 per unit. The resolution describes these as ”traditional video cameras, consistent with existing public safety cameras, for departmental use” — language that appears designed to sidestep the mandatory public hearing required for surveillance equipment.

No such “traditional camera” exception exists in Metro Code Section 13.08.080. The ordinance defines “surveillance technology” to include “closed-circuit television cameras” and “video and audio monitoring and/or recording technology, such as surveillance cameras”—which would plainly cover the Axis cameras. The only exemption for cameras applies to “manually operated non-wearable handheld digital cameras... whose functionality is limited to manually capturing and manually downloading video and/or audio recordings”—not fixed surveillance cameras.

That public hearing requirement was authored by Councilman Dave Rosenberg in 2017 through ordinance BL2017-646. Rosenberg is now O’Connell’s Director of Legislative Affairs—the same official coordinating the administration’s push for Fusus and the NDP surveillance deal. As the Nashville Scene reported, Rosenberg “wrote the city ordinance requiring council approval and a public hearing before Metro installs new surveillance technology.”

The People’s MOU

The Mayor’s Office is pursing this equipment at the same time that Community Members have been collecting feedback on how they think the $15M of funds allocated to the District Management Corporation (DMC), whose budget has 100% oversight by the Metro Council, should be spent. The line items being voted on come directly from the allowable costs from the actual grant between the DMC and the State of Tennessee. The enforcement mechanism would be an MOU between Metro and the DMC and/or the Nashville Downtown Partnership.

Why the Mayor’s Office would not want the funding to align with direct feedback from the community members impacted by the funding is unclear.

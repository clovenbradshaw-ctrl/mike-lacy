Behind-the-Scenes

So let’s get into a little backstory about how I started writing about this, because I think being as transparent as possible is helpful for people to make up their own minds. Not just on this current situation with arts funding in the city, but about greater trends in our society.

Every story has a storyteller that’s bringing themselves to the story. I think it’s helpful to you, in my “moments of reflection” (as Ira Glass talks about it) to not hide how I see things. It’ll inevitably make you understand why I chose the parts of the story I did, and help you decide the truth for yourself — and that’s my end goal. To help us all think a little bit more complexly about the truth of the world we share.

I’ve never written this regularly in my life, and I certainly didn’t spend my freetime in the past at city commission meetings and scrubbing through FOIA requests.

How I Got Into This

When I was working at the Nashville Mayor’s Office (Jan 2022 to Oct 2023) I heard in passing about some drama with the Metro Arts Department. To be honest, it didn’t register as anything too interesting at the time. My bandwidth for Metro-related stress was mostly filled with my job, setting up anti-violence programs and funding community organizations to enhance social cohesion.

My job was about working with grassroots organizations that had never received public funding and working to scale up their competency to use these funds appropriately. At the same time, we were working to make the government more flexible in working with these types of organizations, lowering some barriers while raising levels of support to ensure their appropriate use of public funds.

After leaving that role, I started hearing more from friends impacted by the Metro Arts funding delay. Maybe I was too much of an “insider” to complain to about it previously, or maybe I just started listening better.

My first real action to learn more about it was sending a LinkedIn message to (now former) Metro Arts Director Daniel Singh. We met for coffee and became friendly, though I wouldn’t categorize it as fully becoming friends. We’ve had dinner together, and I interviewed him on camera a few times for posterity, but as his term at Metro came to a close, and my writing about Metro Arts expanded, we’ve fallen out of touch.

Some part of that may be my position changing a little bit within Nashville's political discourse with the writing I’ve done on Substack.

I have complicated feelings about this newfound side gig I’ve developed writing about Metro Arts. There’s a very real way in which the bad experiences people have had this last year around Metro Arts, most of them people of color, have given me a platform to put my perspective out there. That’s a weird spot to be in. I don’t want to keep it that way.

That’s why moving forward I’m going to be sharing my platform with other writers who have very different experiences and backgrounds than me, who haven’t been “on the inside”, and who are less likely to get city council1 people and state reps to read their newsletters.

One thing I want to maintain with my guest writers is a “Metamodern” sense of political story telling. It’s something my writing has evolved more towards with the “Strings Attached” series, somewhat in contrast to where it started.

I would consider myself a Leftist, but one in the “Metamodern” vein who believes, as the philosopher Hanzi Freinacht describes, we must “play the game and change the rules while doing so” if we want to design a better system than the one we have now.

Sometimes tearing things down and starting anew is what the game calls for, but more often, as Antionette Carroll puts it, the response to finding that a system is designed for injustice is usually not to “burn it down”. Her organization “Creative Reaction Lab” operates on these principles, as she talks about in this podcast episode.

Creative Reaction Lab operates on the foundational belief that all systems within our society, systems of oppression, inequality, and inequity are by design; therefore, they can and must be redesigned.

Podcast: “How equality and equity are different, and why it matters. ft. Antionette Carroll”

In much of my writing, I aim to show how power and decisions are distributed, not just to have the common liberal reaction of “Oh, it’s all so corrupt! And did you see what Trump said?!”

I do it to show how these systems were designed in certain ways, and how they can be redesigned in many small but significant ways that lead to outsized results. Systems theory tells us that small changes can have knock-on effects much greater than the sum of the parts.

Anyone critical of small, piecemeal changes leading to radical change should reflect on how things like the overturning of Affirmative Action and Roe v. Wade were not accomplished in quick bombastic, accelerationist leaps. They were accomplished through intentional, small, step-wise actions that hit a tipping point in the last few years. Progressives have much to learn from the change management techniques of the Far Right.

Reflecting on my early writing, I was pretty zeroed in on the actions of the leaders of Metro Finance and Law. While I don’t regret any particular statements, framing individuals as the problem is a “low-complexity” analysis of this situation. I’m not really interested in labeling anyone as a villain in this or other stories.

It’s much more interesting to me to try to piece together the story that each player feels they are inside of, where they inevitably are the hero of their own story. Trying to stitch together the tapestry of these different interpretations of events, to figure out what the “meta-story” is, is what’s interesting to me.

Systems don’t tend to change just by replacing a few key players. Most often when there are roles to be filled, the parameters around who is “allowed” to fill those roles filters what type of person can “successfully” fill that role.

You could (and probably should) replace some leaders in this city, but without changing the surrounding system of pushes and pulls, you’re likely to get (1) the same results, (2) a person who is ejected violently by the broader system (and sometimes that ejection comes with a $200k settlement).

This analysis, thinking about roles rather than souls, makes me less harsh on individual people and more focused on “the system.” But it feels reductive to not hold people accountable for unethical actions just because “the system” told them to do it.

When it comes to unethical actions in and around the government, there are processes in place for accountability. They’re imperfect and need improvements, but they exist.

Since my first articles, my writing has become less inflammatory, and more exploratory. I think this aligns more with my values of trying to raise the “complexity level” of our discourse around the Metro Arts crisis, and “culture war” issues more generally.

The New Athens

The delay of the third edition of “Strings Attached” is partly because I wanted to understand the symphony’s values and vibe. I’ve talked about their board, fundraising behaviors, and how their actions fit within Reaganomics and neoliberalism. But these are people with life experiences where these actions make sense. I’m too much of a curious humanist to dismiss them as simply “rich folks doing what rich folks do” even if what they did is very “rich folk behavior.”

I’ve been waiting to read "Apollo’s Struggle: Performing Arts Odyssey in the Athens of the South, Nashville, Tennessee" by Martha Ingram, the long-time Chair of the Nashville Symphony. It just got delivered yesterday.

I want to read and digest this book not so much out of fairness to giving the Symphony leaders their “point of view”, but because I think the ideological background radiation of the categorical superiority of classical “western” art traditions — symphonic orchestra music, ballet, opera — is at the heart of this Metro Arts funding debate.

My college education was exclusively the “Western Canon”, it began with Homer and ended with Heidegger. The role of music, specifically the role of music as Plato and Aristotle understood it, was essential to the development of the educational system I partook in — the same curriculum that every American Founding Father and President up until the 1920s received.

I don’t mind us being the Athens of the South. Our Parthenon is more intact than the real one. But unlike the creator of the Neo-Conservative movement, Leo Strauss, who died on the quad of my alma mater, I do not believe that all the real philosophizing and the making of transcendent “great art” has all been done in the past.

There is, allegedly, a set of canonical great books and great works of art that contain everything worth reading or hearing, never to be improved upon, and the best we can do is build temples in the form of Symphony Halls and Opera spaces. I don’t think there’s anything wrong with Symphony Halls and Opera spaces, I like those mediums very much.

However, the exaltation of 'great men' and their canonical works as the sole means to cultivate a 'cultured' city always felt disingenuous to me, even after I explored the philosophical and cultural arguments more thoroughly than many of its most vocal advocates.

I also don’t think I’m alone as a white man with a Western classical education in this thinking, I’d argue that Shakespeare and Tolstoy would very much agree with me.

But that’s an essay for another time.

In the meantime, I’ll say this: If Nashville is the New Athens, it must be inspired by the best of what the Hellenic tradition gave us, but move, in a Metamodern fashion, through its boundaries and transcend the limitations of that former cultural hub. New York does not limit itself to what culture was developed in old York.

The strange thing about the Western canon and the art that came from it is that the political and social upheavals contained in it are flattened by those who designate themselves the guardians of its legacy. Every great work in the linear trajectory of the Western canon was a revolution against what came before it.

Socrates did not uphold the status quo, and he was put to death for corrupting the youth of Athens. Galileo was placed on house arrest for correctly describing the solar system. Nietzsche shocked the world with his pronouncement that the Enlightenment had killed god. Stravinsky’s Rite of Spring led to literal riots as people were scandalized by his orchestration and choreography.

What frustrates me about clinging onto the past glories of the Western canon, and a stifling obsession with preserving the art it produced in "classy” establishments, is that it completely misunderstands the trajectory of Western culture. The works and events that make up the Western canon are a grand meta-story detailing a dialectical climb through the stages of social consciousness, expanding at each rung to include more people in the definition of “who is a human” (which, spoiler alert for those who are still working their way through these stages themselves, everyone is a human).

As people like Ken Wilber can explain to you far better than me, the Western experiment has been moved forward at every stage by people who best represented what the next level of social consciousness required. The “great mind” of one era was an average thinker or artist of a later era.

What stuffy cultural institutions do, that hoard culture into particular boxes, requiring you to wear particular outfits and behave a particular way to enjoy it, is it kills Western culture. It makes it inseparable from the critiques of it as a white supremacist colonialist institution.

If we are the New Athens, then we must use our social capital to create new culture and continue the legacy that began with old Athens. The next Stravinsky is not likely to be a middle manager at a large legacy art institution. But it might be a non-binary Latinx person in Antioch. But we’ll never know where they can take our culture if we don’t help them find a place to practice their scales.

If only we had some way of supporting up-and-coming artists in Nashville…

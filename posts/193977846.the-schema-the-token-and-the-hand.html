For every seat removed, two shall take its place.

— Posted online when seventeen community benches were recovered in Nashville

In the future I’ll be releasing an oral history of the Nashville benches. For now, here are some philosophical stray thoughts I have on what the mean, and why they’re an example of what will lead to the downfall of the tech oligarchs.

Charlie is an engineer who used to commute by bike and work hospitality shifts across Nashville — Germantown, the Gulch, Melrose. Toward the end of his twenties he made intentional career moves so he could get to community meetings, which mostly happen after work. His father was a Presbyterian minister. Serving the community, he said, wasn’t something you did to feel good about yourself. It was just how you existed in society.

He is one of the people who built the benches.

(The short version: Nashville’s NDOT removed public benches from Korean Veterans Boulevard in 2024 and replaced them with concrete balls. A community group started building and placing handmade benches in response. The city began confiscating those too. Nashville Scene coverage of the original removal. Nashville Banner on the guerrilla bench movement.)

We talked at Switchyards in Germantown on a Wednesday morning. He had been up since before six emailing NDOT about something unrelated. A week earlier, Nashville’s Department of Transportation had been discovered storing seventeen handmade community benches in a city warehouse — benches the department had been quietly removing from public spaces while telling the organizers, who were working through official channels to get them approved, that they weren’t the ones taking them. The recovery mission had happened four days prior. Kelly, another organizer, had driven a truck to the NDOT property and started loading while workers watched and said nothing. “I don’t negotiate with terrorists,” she said afterward.

Charlie wasn’t surprised NDOT had the benches. He was surprised they hadn’t thrown them away.

I asked him about hostile architecture — the concrete balls installed on Korean Veterans Boulevard where the original benches had been, the design choices that make sitting structurally impossible without saying you’re not welcome. He didn’t hesitate. “There are powers at play that don’t want homeless people around, but they also don’t want to fix homelessness in the appropriate manner. The result of that is hostile architecture.”

Then I asked about the sentence someone had posted when the benches were recovered. For every seat removed, two shall take its place. He recognized the structure immediately. “If you’re trying to get rid of something, the act of getting rid of it brings more attention to it, and then it compounds the awareness around it.” He paused. “That’s the origin of the Streisand effect. Man, how would an effect be named after me?”

The effect is named after Barbra Streisand, who in 2003 tried to have aerial photographs of her Malibu home removed from the internet. The lawsuit caused the images — which had attracted fifty visits before she filed — to be viewed by nearly half a million people within a month. The attempt to suppress produced exactly what suppression was meant to prevent. Charlie’s version: the city wanted fewer benches and fewer people sitting in public. It got a community movement, a news cycle, council members on Blue Sky, and a bench build every few weeks with a robotics team from a local high school.

The community had built and placed handmade benches on Nashville’s public concrete, returning seating to gaps where it had been removed. The city replaced the original public benches with concrete balls — a design choice with one purpose: you cannot sit on one. It does not say you are not welcome. It simply makes your presence structurally impossible.

The bench had been filling a NULL — the last uncommodified place to be — left by the erasure of housing that did not require you to prove your worthiness to inhabit it. The concrete ball fills the NULL where the bench was. There is an irony in the shape they chose: a sphere is zero in three dimensions, a NULL made physical, the geometry of nothing installed where something was. Each removal is addressed to the one before. The community replaced those absences with something it made. Then the city began taking the community’s benches, starting with the ones on Korean Veterans Boulevard.

This is how unchecked power communicates with those it does not consider worthy of communication, let alone governance over their own public spaces: not with words whose meaning can be parsed and evaluated against the truth of people’s lived experience, but with shape. A concrete ball where there was a bench. When asked to explain, the explanations multiplied and contradicted each other, each party pointing to every other, none holding up to any self-consistent argument. The explanations collapsed in on themselves and left a particular shape — a negative space, a NULL — which is the truth: they removed the benches because unhoused people were using them, and this was an eyesore to the business interests that help prop up the real estate market giving those people nowhere else to go, and to the politicians who refuse to exert oversight over the homelessness system they fund and allow to fail.

The concrete ball does not say this. It does not have to. It is the schema communicating in its native language — not a software tool, but the logic of power that decides what can exist before existence occurs, that defines the categories before the encounter, that has operated through censuses and property records and eligibility determinations for centuries and now operates through concrete. Below the threshold of words that could be contested, below the threshold of claims that could be evaluated against evidence. Pure enforcement. The geometry of exclusion, installed overnight.

It makes sense that Nashvillians want public ways to exercise their autonomy.

All signs indicate the Oracle headquarters will be built. (Oracle announced in 2024 it was relocating its world headquarters to Nashville’s East Bank — a $1.2 billion campus subsidized by $65 million in state incentives, with no construction start date as of this writing.) The Tesla tunnel, as geologically inscrutable and public transportationally useless as this seems, will continue to drill under our streets. (The Boring Company, Elon Musk’s tunnel-drilling venture, was granted a no-cost state lease in July 2025 to build an underground loop connecting the State Capitol to the Convention Center to the airport — routed under state roads specifically to bypass city approval.) $35 in lumber and donated paint supplies don’t beat a $300 billion data infrastructure deal on Oracle’s terms. They beat it on different terms — the only terms on which the schema has ever been defeated.

The hand-painted bench is not a protest sign. It is not a substitute for what has been taken. It is something more specific: an act of knowledge — the only kind that the two dominant systems of power in this city, and in much of the world, cannot produce and cannot process.

I want to define some terms to help us evaluate what’s going on — so that we might all learn from it. Not learn in the sense of how to, next time, short-circuit community dissent by having the right pleasant-sounding phrases. But learn in the sense of recursion: to loop back and change our understanding of the world, and operate on a higher plane with a deeper groundedness in real shit.

Three terms. Three “loops.” Two of them broken. And a third thing that uses failure constructively to learn — not in a “you’ll do better next time, champ” way, but in the sense of how all emergent complexity actually works. Every conscious action is a micro-evaluation: a prediction committed to, tested against what’s actually there, revised in real time. Segmentation and connection are not problems. They are necessary operations. The problem is what happens when each one stalls — when the loop that should run after them doesn’t.

Call the first the schema loop. Call the second the token loop. And the third: the learning loop.

The schema loop is the loop the relational database runs — and that administrators and states have run for centuries before any database existed.

Segmentation is a real and necessary operation. The schema loop should run: segment, encounter, test, revise. What it actually runs: segment, enforce. The category is defined in advance, the encounter never updates it, and what doesn’t fit simply disappears from the record — not flagged as unknown, not held as a question, gone.

Think of ice crystallizing: a boundary draws itself and everything is either on the right side or it isn’t. The crystal doesn’t ask if the boundary is right. It enforces it.
You can watch it in Tennessee: the person sleeping in a public park is not, in Bill Lee’s frame, someone the housing market failed — they are a violation of the category of what public space is for. The response is a felony camping ban. The loop does not ask how they got there.

The relational database perfected this stall and gave it computational scale. “Citizens will be on their best behavior because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that’s going on.” — Larry Ellison, 2024. The schema loop, in its own voice.

The token loop is the loop the large language model runs — and that consultants and progressive institutions have run long before any LLM existed.

Connection is a real and necessary operation. The token loop should run: connect, synthesize, evaluate, revise. What it actually runs: connect, produce output, connect again — each connection locally plausible, the whole drifting from any correspondence to reality.

The clearest instance is hallucination. An LLM has no NUL — it cannot represent the shape of what it doesn’t know. So when it doesn’t know something, it doesn’t produce a NULL. It produces the pattern of knowing. Lawyers were sanctioned by federal courts for submitting AI-generated briefs citing cases that didn’t exist — correct court, correct format, plausible parties and years.

The critical thing: the LLM is not lying. There is no ontological difference between a truthful output and a hallucinated one. Both are tokenized projections of characters shaped by positive and negative feedback during training. Truth and fabrication are not different in kind — only in how well they happened to match the training data.

We see the same with politicians, especially liberal ones. NDOT told the bench organizers they weren’t removing the benches — while storing seventeen of them in a city warehouse. When the removal was happening, the official language was “public art and beautification.” Each output matched the pattern associated with responsible civic stewardship. No gradient inside the loop made the true account feel different from the false one. The organizers weren’t lied to in the conventional sense. The loop simply produced whatever output fit the frame.

In a language model, a token is the next predicted word — pattern-matched, no correspondence to anything. Full of sound and fuzziness, signifying nonsense.

Together they account for most of the governance we have: one cuts reality into categories, the other fills those categories with language. What neither can do is tell you whether the categories are right, or whether the language corresponds to anything. That requires the third thing.

And there is a third thing: the placement of something real in the gap between what the record shows and what actually happened — the act of someone who was there, who carries the consequence in their body, who knows what the system missed because they are what the system missed.

The third thing runs a loop that neither of the other two can perform.

It defines precisely — names the NULL, states what the record shows versus what it missed, makes the gap visible and actionable. It evaluates from outside — sides with the testimony of the person who was inside the system over the system’s self-report. And it revises — goes back through the frame, finds where it was wrong, updates it. Not the value. Not the rule. The frame itself.

That requires a different kind of loop entirely.

Call it the learning loop.

Not learning in the sense of processing more information — the schema loop handles enormous quantities of data without learning anything. Not learning in the sense of producing better outputs — the token loop can refine its outputs indefinitely without learning anything. Learning in the specific, precise sense: the capacity to revise the definitions themselves. Not to adjust the outputs. Not to update the values. To change what the system can see — to update the schema.

This is not a sophisticated or rare capacity. It is not something cities or governance systems invented. Epigenetic adaptation is a learning loop. A single-celled organism sensing its chemical environment and revising its membrane behavior is running a learning loop. The capacity is as old as life itself — prior to nervous systems, prior to language, prior to cities, prior to governance bodies. What distinguishes organisms from inert matter is precisely this: they update their structure in response to what they encounter. The crystal doesn’t ask if its boundary is right. The cell does.

What the learning loop requires — and what distinguishes it from both the schema loop and the token loop — is evaluation from outside the frame. A test of the categories against something that doesn’t share the categories’ assumptions. This is why it can only be performed by the people who carry the system’s consequences in their bodies. They are the outside. They are what the frame failed to account for. Their testimony is the only input that can actually revise the definitions — because they know, from direct encounter, what the definitions missed.

This is also why complex social systems suppress it. Not because they are too simple to run a learning loop — a bacterium runs one. Because the people who benefit from the current definitions have every incentive to prevent the definitions from being revised. Power accumulates precisely at the revision step. The schema loop enforces: it removes the bench, criminalizes the presence, files no report. The token loop obscures: it calls the concrete ball public art, calls the eviction a placement, calls the surveillance a restraint. What both loops share is the suppression of the moment where someone who was inside the system gets to say what the system actually did — and have that testimony change the frame. The concrete ball is not just hostile architecture. It is a learning loop suppressor. So is the ejection of Patterson. So is the warehouse full of benches.

This is not a new discovery. Edward O. Wilson documented it in ant colonies and coral reefs: order arising without central authority, staggering complexity emerging from organisms responding to local conditions. No schema imposed in advance. No token produced to manage appearances. When the obstruction is removed, the system self-organizes. Elinor Ostrom won the Nobel Prize for documenting the same loop in human governance — Swiss alpine meadows, Japanese fishing villages, Philippine irrigation systems, commons that thrived for centuries without either state control or private ownership. She identified the principles that distinguished thriving commons from collapsing ones. She did not design them. She observed them arising independently, across cultures that had no contact with each other, across centuries. The loop was always already there. It only needed the obstruction removed.

The political program that tries to build governance from this capacity rather than against it has a name: municipalism. Not municipal government — the deliberate cultivation of self-governing commons at the neighborhood level, the tracing of connections between local experiments, the composition of federated structures from below. Barcelona en Comú. Rojava. Cooperation Jackson. What these share is not ideology. They are the loop running: define what holds, test it against the people inside it, revise the frame when the test reveals the frame is the problem.

The bench is this loop made physical. It defines: there was seating here, it was removed, the need persists. It evaluates: the record shows public accommodation maintained; the person who needed that bench knows what actually happened. It revises: a new bench appears, in the gap, proving the frame wrong by existing.

For every seat removed, two shall take its place.

The sentence is not merely defiant. It is structurally true — and understanding why requires understanding the kind of power that the relational database did not invent but has now made total.

The phenomenon of resurging benches, reappeaing when eliminated, is what I call “an emanon.” An emanon is a pre-entity — a potential that exists prior to any category that could hold it, pressing toward form in whatever gap the schema leaves. The bench had been filling a NULL: the last uncommodified place to be, left by the erasure of housing that did not require you to prove your worthiness to inhabit it. The schema loop addresses instances directly — removes the bench, installs the concrete ball, criminalizes the presence. The concrete ball does not say you are not welcome. It makes your presence structurally impossible. The record shows the absence. There is at least a NULL to point to.

The token loop is more insidious. It doesn't remove the instance. It corrupts the field — the insertion of a concrete ball like a decimal place in a spreadsheet cell, so the record can no longer hold what was there but the cell appears occupied. Public accommodation: maintained. Seating: redefined out of existence. The emanon — the need for rest in a body that has nowhere to rest — is now not even detectable as absent. There is no NULL. The cell is full. The learning loop intervenes at the only point where the emanon remains accessible: the body of the person who needed the bench. The bench project names the NULL: there was seating here, it was removed, the need it met was real. The person who needed the bench knows what actually happened. They are the gap. A new bench appears, in the gap, proving the frame wrong by existing.

The systems arrayed against the bench can’t solve the actual problem because the problem isn’t the bench — it’s the need behind the bench, born from failed housing and absent healthcare and a bus system the state refused to build, and the schema loop does nothing about any of that, so the need generates a new bench every time the old one is removed. Token Power tries something subtler: Councilmember Jacob Kupin called the original benches “not really conducive for people to just sit and enjoy,” NDOT called the concrete balls a “public art and beautification project,” and the record is rewritten so that removing seating becomes urban improvement and the need the bench was meeting simply disappears from the official account — but the person who needed that bench knows what actually happened, and they’ll build another one.

The need is prior to the schema. That’s the whole argument. The bench keeps appearing because neither loop can address what produced it.

Oracle is establishing its world headquarters here — bringing with it the $18 billion data center contract that keeps OpenAI running, the algorithm contract that controls what 170 million Americans see on TikTok, and the cloud infrastructure through which Palantir runs ImmigrationOS, ingesting Medicaid records on eighty million patients to generate deportation leads with confidence scores. But it is also failing. And the failure is not only a matter of who is operating these systems or what values they hold. It is a failure at the center of the relational model itself. A database can only record states — what is, not what is becoming. A person experiencing homelessness is not a fixed category. They are in motion: between addresses, between crises, between the record that shows them as housed and the reality that shows them sleeping in a park. The relational model requires that you define what reality looks like before you encounter it. The schema forecloses the encounter. And what the encounter would have revealed disappears into the gap between what the record can hold and what actually happened.

E.F. Codd published the paper in 1970 that became the theoretical foundation for every relational database in the world, including Oracle’s. He then spent the remaining decades of his career watching Oracle implement his model wrong, and being furious about it. His specific fury: NULL.

NULL is where the becoming problem surfaces in the record. SQL’s NULL collapses three distinct epistemic states into one undifferentiated token: the value was never set, the value is unknown, the value doesn’t apply. The database cannot know the difference between what it doesn’t know and what it never thought to ask. Every moment of becoming the schema couldn’t hold becomes a NULL. Codd saw this and proposed a fix in 1990. Oracle ignored him.

The language model fails at the opposite pole. Where the database collapses absence into NULL, the language model collapses absence into confident output. One refuses to represent the unknown. The other has no apparatus for approaching it. Neither can produce the epistemic state that makes honest governance possible: I don’t know, and here is the shape of what I don’t know.

The bench project produces this state. It names the NULL: there was seating here, it was removed, the need it met was real, the gap between what the record shows and what actually happened is real. We have seen it. We are placing something in it. This is what Codd wanted and never got. It can only be performed by the people who carry the system’s consequences in their bodies.

I was always terrible at math. I was the junior in an Algebra II class with mostly freshmen, hoping to not get called on.

But I find myself with personal and creative projects drawn to concepts that require me to revisit chapters I’d skipped. I’ve been trying to make a visual for how to represent the different things we need to consider when trying to make lasting, systemic change in a given “sub-system”. Things like “housing” or “the school-to-prison-pipeline” — these hubs of numbers and concepts that link different people, activities, policies, research, cultural biases, etc.

It’s hard to even hold these systems in our minds, and it makes sense why we settle for seemingly good enough solutions that take a simple, “let’s throw money at this and pray” approach to fixing things.

In my time at the Mayor’s Office in Nashville, I became aware that if we wanted to make a sustained impact on my department’s subject, Community Safety, we would need to look at it through a series of layers. We’d need to slow down and pull a lot of things apart.

But a “let’s stop and think about the way these things interact” is not really the parlance of municipal governance. That’s not exactly the vibe of politicians and career bureaucrats, the former whose MO is to respond to crisis, the latter to do their best to constrain the perception of crisis. If there is a way to collaborate on solutions, it’s with straightforward prompts of: “We need more of this and less of this” that can be implemented easily and cost-effectively.

There is ever a need for the bottom line, what is the quickest, most efficient way to get bang for our buck?

This inevitably leads to the stalemate of progress we see everywhere we look. I’m surprised by the amount of consensus there is amongst people embedded deep within systems that we are just using the wrong approach to solving large scale social problems.

What is almost always needed is not the best way of doing things, just the good enough way.

So, while the best way to understand our complex problems might be to use AI machine learning to process the 100% most effective route to getting the outcomes we want — the good enough way is to probably just map it out better than we have now, because we call agree that what we’re doing now isn’t good enough.

When you look at most research on complex topics like “Community Safety”, the info you’re getting is pretty far upstream from those “let’s do XYZ now” marching orders systems partners want. And the info from that research is rarely connected to your specific region, scaled and fit to your specific problems.

To stay on “Community Safety,” even though violence tends to happen in similar patterns across the world, the uniqueness of place, culture, and crisis is such that no meta-analysis aimed at analyzing community violence across the Northern Triangle is right-sized to any particular place. We know the info in there is good, the programs most likely work, but there’s no clear way to implement these programs for any given here.

There are plenty of consulting firms that are happy to show up and make those recommendations, but their analysis is often a bit of a black box and usually gives you a one-off recommendation rather than a way of thinking and evolving that you can maintain over time.

The same situation is true for lots of subsystems in a given area, including homelessness, wage stagnation, maternal health disparities — there’s tons of information that’s been collected, processed, and vetted. But part of the function of that processing is to make it somewhat universal, and by making it universal, it’s now not very applicable in any given place. We need to figure out how to right-size solutions back into particular places.

I've come up with what I'm calling the “Change Array Equation”. It’s a stab at having a way to hold these things in your head at the same time and not be overwhelming, breaking all the info into four distinct categories that interact with each other, and, ideally, give you a to-do list at the end.

The Change Array Equation: Elements

  1. Metrics: what is the current status of all the indicators that relate to the topic? A concept like “Community Safety” doesn’t have a thoroughly agreed upon definition, much less a single metric for it, so you need to use these metrics to shape out the “what are we talking about?” of it all. But there’s also feedback from the other metrics and variables that are inside of other layers.

  2. Solutions: these are evidence-based best practices for improving the metrics we’re monitoring above. You want to use meta-analysis and systematic reviews to gather these, and it’s going to be evolving over time. When it came to Community Safety, there were global studies tracking the effectiveness of various intervention programs, and even their interaction. The Community Justice Action Fund had a great assessment of 35 programs that were demonstrated to work, and their Violence Prevention Index ranked cities on how many of these programs they implemented. These weren’t the end-all solutions, but it’s a useful hypothesis to start from.

  3. Resources: what do we currently have? What programs, public, private, for-profit, non-profit, someone who helps on their block in no official capacity, what are the tools we currently have to help? These need to be fit to the solutions above, or the solutions above might need to be informed by the resources we have (they’re probably filling a need we didn’t know existed).

    We need to tally: how many of the listed solutions do we currently have? And then ask two important follow-up questions: How faithfully do they implement the solutions as the research describes it? And how scaled is this to the problem?

    If we need social-emotional learning-based after-school programming for every kid between 8 and 18 to lower the rate of violence, how many kids can we serve every day across all our programs? And how legitimately are they delivering SEL?

  4. The Gap: this is just the difference between what we should have resource-wise to implement the programs, and what we currently have. This is our marching orders.

But before we’re doing any intense statistical analysis like that, we need to just hold these things in our minds so we can talk sensibly about “complex” systems like Community Safety. What is almost always needed is not the best way of doing things, just the good enough way.

We also, I think, might need to split these problems apart even before we try the data analysis method at scale. Even the most robust databases and machine learning tools probably need to have some guidance on “what is this stuff?” to generate meaningful observations.

We probably need to talk about it as “arrays”, and as array formulas.

Since getting into more AI applications the past few years, I’ve been working in JSON, which writes pretty much everything in arrays. Arrays are data structures that can hold multiple values, typically of the same type, in a single variable. Each element in an array is a variable that holds a single value. Arrays can be nested, meaning an array can contain other arrays as elements. These nested arrays can be combined or manipulated using various operations or functions.

What we’re trying to do, I think, when talking about Metrics, Solutions, Resources, and Gaps, is to perform operations between three arrays (the first three in that list) and the result of those operations is “the gaps”, the things we need to work on.

Here’s a wireframe of how to lay out the change array equation. It doesn’t really go into detail about the operations you need to perform across the top three sections, it’s more of a map of the different layers we need to think about in a given subsystem.

I think we can actually stop there and have a useful paradigm. A lot of “thinking about how we think” is about looking for our blindspots, and The Change Array Equation just makes sure we’re considering those four broad categories and how they intersect — and that ultimately we are interested in the resource gaps, the metrics are only pointing the way there.

If you’re building a dashboard tracking the status of a given sub-system, consider doing something like the Change Array Equation. Metrics, Solutions, Resources, none of them are actually useful until you convert them into “What are the actions we need to take?” And to have a “good enough” version of that isn’t actually very difficult.

Frankly, if you feed it the first three parts of the Change Array Equation, ChatGPT gives you a decent starting place of articulating what your resource gaps are. This is an extremely basic resource analysis that many people who work every day in complex systems might roll their eyes at, but they might be shocked at how “scalar” the solutions are to entrenched, systemic problems in the social systems world.

I think we’d need a little more than this to make real statements about the “gaps” though. The gaps we need to fill are affected by how these different variables all relate to one another (and this is, of course, assuming we’ve identified all, or at least the most significant variables that go into a given subsystem).

We’re also assuming that other people have already mapped out the “solutions” — we’re relatively “downstream” here with the “change array equation”. We’re trying to take all the information that experts have given to us, and figure out what gives us the most bang for our buck in getting the most positive outcomes.

But if we want to be somewhat more precise, and do some actual computations between these arrays, we need to calculate how these variables relate to each other.

We can do this by weighting the relationships between the different variables, both within the given array, and across arrays. We’re assuming other people have figured this out, through some more formal regression analysis or the like.

We are just trying to get this information to the place of being useful.

Let’s consider how we actually get to the numbers we need for the Gap Analysis if we want to more than just ballpark it.

Some speculation from GPT 4o:

To generate the "Resource Gaps" array as an output from the "Metrics," "Proven Solutions," and "Existing Resources" arrays, you would realistically need to perform several types of operations. These operations include:

1. Comparison and Gap Analysis:

  • Operation: Compare the current state (from "Metrics" and "Existing Resources") with the desired or required state (derived from "Proven Solutions").

  • Example: Compare the current healthcare worker count with the number required to implement all proven solutions effectively.

gap_healthcare_workers = required_healthcare_workers - available_healthcare_workers

  • Output: Identify the shortfall (gap) in resources, which becomes an entry in the "Resource Gaps" array.

2. Aggregation:

  • Operation: Aggregate data from multiple sources to determine total requirements.

  • Example: Sum the required vaccination capacity across all proven solutions to understand the total vaccination site capacity needed.

total_required_vaccination_capacity = sum(proven_solutions_vaccination_needs)

gap_vaccination_capacity = total_required_vaccination_capacity - available_vaccination_capacity

  • Output: A total shortfall or surplus in capacity, funding, or resources.

3. Weighting and Prioritization:

  • Operation: Apply weights to different metrics and solutions to prioritize gaps based on their impact on overall goals.

  • Example: If reducing infection rates is weighted heavily, prioritize gaps that most directly influence this metric, such as vaccination coverage or healthcare worker availability.

weighted_gap_score = (gap_value * metric_influence_weight * solution_weight)

  • Output: A prioritized list of gaps, indicating which resource deficits are most critical to address.

4. Efficiency and Scaling Calculations:

  • Operation: Calculate how efficiently current resources are being used and determine if scaling up is needed.

  • Example: Assess if the current number of mobile vaccination units is being used at full capacity and calculate how many more units are needed to meet target coverage.

required_mobile_units = (target_coverage - current_coverage) / coverage_per_unit gap_mobile_units = required_mobile_units - current_mobile_units

  • Output: A detailed understanding of where scaling up is necessary to meet goals.

5. Simulation and Forecasting:

  • Operation: Use simulations to forecast future needs based on trends in metrics and the effectiveness of solutions.

  • Example: Forecast the infection rate reduction based on the current vaccination rate, then determine the gap in resources needed to accelerate this reduction.

forecasted_infection_rate = current_infection_rate - (vaccination_rate * effectiveness) gap_additional_vaccination_sites = required_vaccination_rate - current_vaccination_rate

  • Output: Projected resource gaps over time, helping to plan for future needs.

6. Resource Allocation Optimization:

  • Operation: Optimize the allocation of existing resources to minimize gaps.

  • Example: Reallocate healthcare workers from less critical areas to vaccination sites where they are most needed to reduce the gap in vaccination capacity.

optimized_allocation = optimize_resource_allocation(existing_resources, solution_priorities) gap_after_optimization = calculated_gap_after_reallocation

  • Output: A minimized or redistributed gap after resource reallocation.

Summary:

  • Comparison: Identify shortfalls by comparing current resources and metrics against requirements.

  • Aggregation: Sum up needs across all proven solutions.

  • Weighting: Prioritize gaps based on weighted importance.

  • Scaling: Determine if scaling up resources is necessary.

  • Forecasting: Predict future needs based on current trends.

  • Optimization: Reallocate resources to minimize gaps.

These operations would be necessary to translate the existing metrics, proven solutions, and available resources into a clear understanding of the gaps that need to be addressed to achieve systemic change.

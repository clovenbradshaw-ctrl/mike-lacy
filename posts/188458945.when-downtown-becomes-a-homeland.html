Something has been ringing in my head since the Council Meetings about Mayor O’Connell’s push for a suite of surveillance tools his office submitted just before Thanksgiving for approval. It was something CM Jordan Huffman repeated on the floor. He had said the same thing, verbatim, to News Channel 5.

It seemed to capture everything that has changed in how we talk about what it means to be in American society — a change that’s been 25 years in the making. A change that draws a straight line between unchecked profits and what we call “matters of national security.”

Huffman said, “We are one incident away from having the money turn off in Nashville.”

That debate is now over. The equipment has been accepted. The city has taken the gear. The Mayor’s office is no longer trying to obligate the Downtown Partnership to anything in exchange, and the questions that were briefly raised—about procurement, oversight, long-term costs, civil liberties, and what exactly this infrastructure is being built to do—have been, its boosters hope, put to bed.

But Huffman’s line has been living rent-free in my mind since then.

The principal point of the surveillance gear isn’t to protect residents, but to protect revenue. Downtown Nashville, arguably the most profitable few blocks of the entire state, is the economic engine whose uninterrupted operation must be guaranteed.

Mayor O’Connell offered his own version of this logic while defending expanded surveillance downtown:

“We’re continuing to solicit feedback… but we want to make sure that everyone understands the context of how different the downtown safety environment is from other parts of the city.”

An overly simplistic narrative says this is about appeasing “downtown interests”—the businesses on Broadway, the bar owners, the hotel groups.

But recent comments have made clear that when we talk about local leadership having commitment to “business interests”, it’s not local business leaders. The commitment is to the abstract power they are beholden to, the financial institutions that often have even regional elites over a barrel, institutions whose worldview treats ‘the market knows best’ as the law of the jungle.

In booming business districts, even comparatively wealthy property owners are embedded in financial structures that limit their ability to exist without collapsing under the weight of the illusion of infinite growth.

Unfettered neoliberal markets reward growth and punish consistency. Investors demand increasing returns. Valuations must rise. Under those conditions, churn is not an accident—it is the design. And if you aren’t enshittifying your restaurant by every means necessary to make more money for other people, you’re liable to become someone’s lunch.

I can’t help but notice that it’s just the scaled-up version of the eviction mills that the Office of Homeless Services props up by placing destitute people into apartments they can’t afford, prompting them to sign leases that the tenants aren’t given a copy of.

In both circumstances, the role of government, in the view of neoliberals like Mr. O’Connell, is to facilitate that extraction of wealth to higher powers, regardless of its impact on the city and its culture. This was not always how people in government saw their role. It is hard for us to see the water we are swimming in, but researchers like David Harvey have documented it in vivid detail.

You would think a local institution facing an existential crisis over ballooning property tax rates is precisely where the Mayor who campaigned on “I want you to stay” would shine. But that is ignoring where this administration’s allegiances have been.

I’ve written a lot about how it is clearly not with the most disadvantaged Nashvillians, whether those are the immigrants he has all but abandoned when his hand was slapped by Kristie Noem.

Nor is it with the unhoused, who, as I have, and will continue to report, have been shuffled around into slum motels under handshake deals with property owners without a contract or procurement process in sight (often leaving them destitute once again, but now with a court-ordered eviction on their record).

But the demiurgical forces of neoliberalism, who seem to have descended from on high and chosen Mr. O’Connell as their avatar, do not have much sympathy even for the comparatively well off in our city.

A sitting mayor doesn’t get to co-chair the Chamber of Commerce’s “Partnership 2030: A Regional Strategy for Community Vitality, Mobility, and Character” while being even mildly critical of unfettered capitalism.

When the county’s highest executive formally co-chairs a Chamber-led regional growth strategy, the boundary between public governance and private capital becomes unusually thin, and it shows you, if nothing else, whose worldview is sinking into his grey matter.

Partnership 2030 isn’t just a collection of local boosters—it is a who’s who of the power players whose gravity sucks Nashvillians unavoidably into their orbit, from Regions and Pinnacle to JPMorgan Chase, Wells Fargo, and Bank of America, global consultancies like Deloitte, EY, PwC, and Accenture, development heavyweights like Southwest Value Partners/Nashville Yards and Ryman Hospitality Properties, brokerage giants CBRE and JLL, and even multinational platforms like Amazon and Meta whose business models revolve around scale, data, and expansion.

What allows such cognitive dissonance? The answer, I think, more often than not, is “national security.”

After 9/11, federal doctrine formally fused economic continuity with national security. “Critical infrastructure” came to include not only systems whose disruption would threaten lives, but systems whose disruption would threaten economic stability. Tennessee adopted that framing. The state’s Office of Homeland Security explicitly included protecting “property and the economy” within its mission.

When Mr. Huffman talks about (paraphrasing) “the risk of one bad thing happening,” when Mr. O’Connell says, politely, (paraphrasing) “you don’t know what you’re talking about when it comes to downtown safety,” I don’t think they know about any particular bad things that could happen that we don’t.

And of course, defenders of the surveillance expansion can point to facts that are not imaginary. Downtown does have uniquely dense foot traffic. It hosts large-scale events. It concentrates nightlife, alcohol, and tourist crowds in a way most neighborhoods do not.

A violent incident on Broadway has ripple effects that reach beyond the people immediately harmed: it becomes national news, threatens convention bookings, alarms investors, and shapes the city’s reputation. In that sense, officials are not wrong to argue that downtown carries a different risk profile. I don’t think anyone would argue with that.

But what I think they are privy to, which we are not, is the rhetoric with which these risks are presented behind closed doors. The economic threat to the state of Tennessee, through its own stupidity in putting all of its eggs in one 4-block, bachelorette-stuffed basket, is likely spoken of in terms of “national security.”

When the economic health of a region, in many ways an entire state, is centralized into a small area, to minds that have been blackpilled by neoliberalism into seeing everything in terms of financialized risk management, everything is fair game.

And in the wake of a discussion like this, many roadblocks can be cleared. Constitutional rights to privacy? Fungible. Actually caring for what happens to people who are taken from homeless encampments? A liberty we don’t have time for. People who voice dissent and ask hard questions? They are threats.

And this is where the language shifts.

In ordinary speech, “risk” means danger to people. In the framework guiding this surveillance push, “risk” means volatility — the possibility that disruption could interfere with revenue, tourism, or investor confidence.

Risk is not defined as harm to residents. It is defined as harm to economic continuity.

In the governing model now in place, the primary risk metric is revenue volatility. Human harm functions as a secondary indicator — relevant only if it produces reputational damage or disrupts cash flow.

A mass casualty event would count. But so would a strike. So would a protest. So would any disruption that threatens investor confidence. The operative variable is instability, not injustice.

Evictions do not qualify. Rent gouging does not qualify. Hunger does not qualify. The slow violence of poverty does not qualify.

Those harms are not treated as systemic risk. They are treated as background conditions. And, there is more than a little magical thinking in place that if only people like me stopped bringing them up, these issues wouldn’t exist.

That is why security teams are framed as “risk management.” The term comes from finance. It is the language of insurers, bond markets, and asset managers. Risk management is about stabilizing assets and reducing uncertainty. It is not about care. It is about control.

Downtown Nashville is being governed as a financial asset. And assets are secured.

At the street level, that logic looks like Solaren Risk Management, the private security firm enforcing order on Lower Broadway. “Risk,” in this context, means visible instability—unhoused people, protests, disorder, unpredictability. Solaren’s role is not to address root causes. It is to maintain an environment conducive to consumption and investment.

But there is also an informational layer to this system.

LexisNexis Risk Solutions is not a news archive. It is a data brokerage and analytics company that aggregates massive amounts of public and private data — criminal records, court filings, property records, financial data, personal identifiers — and packages it into tools used by law enforcement, insurers, financial institutions, and government agencies.

Before becoming part of LexisNexis, ChoicePoint was one of the most prominent data brokers in the country. It specialized in compiling and selling personal data to law enforcement and federal agencies. Its business model was straightforward: collect, aggregate, and monetize information to help institutions identify “risk.”

LexisNexis acquired ChoicePoint in 2008, folding that enforcement-oriented data infrastructure into its broader analytics platform. Today, LexisNexis Risk Solutions markets products that help agencies assess threat levels, identify patterns, and flag individuals or properties for scrutiny. It is a private-sector engine for categorizing people and places according to perceived liability.

This is not theoretical.

Kristin Wilson, the Mayor’s Chief of Operations and Performance, coordinated the proposed $15 million surveillance agreement between MNPD and the Downtown Partnership. Before entering public service, she worked at LexisNexis Risk Solutions and oversaw integration work after the ChoicePoint acquisition.

That does not imply conspiracy. It illustrates continuity.

The same vocabulary — risk, solutions, mitigation, analytics — migrates from private data brokerage into municipal governance. The same assumptions follow: instability can be quantified, scored, and managed. People can be sorted. Public space can be optimized.

Solaren manages the visible layer, deciding who stays on the sidewalk. LexisNexis provides the informational layer — the raw intelligence used to define, track, and operationalize “risk.” These companies don’t need to coordinate or even know that each other exists. But they are two sides of the same coin: the brutal, Janus-faced god of neoliberalism made real.

Together, they reflect a model of governance built not around care, but around volatility suppression.

What “Downtown” Are We Protecting?

This was never just a debate about cameras, SWAT trucks, or spy software (ok, it was a lot about that). But it was also a debate about what Nashville believes downtown is. What a city even is, who it is for, and who gets to decide that?

Downtown Nashville is not treated as a neighborhood. It is treated as an asset, with dense tourism, hotel taxes, convention bookings, alcohol sales, rising property values, and corporate investment. When the Mayor insisted downtown was “different,” he was not describing crime patterns. He was describing the capital, the cash flow, and making it clear that his duty is to protect it. Many people would think that means for the business owners on Lower Broad, but he made it clear that’s not what he means.

That belief was visible in the ACME Feed & Seed controversy. Asked about their reported property tax increase (from roughly $129,000 to about $600,000), Mayor O’Connell emphasized market valuation under state oversight and suggested it was up to the owner to “capture the value” in the property.

The response wasn’t careless—it was on message for the neoliberal party line. He defended, to the syllable, the belief that the market, above all else, should determine what a city looks like. And if that happens to benefit the financial class on the very committee he co-chairs for the Nashville Chamber of Commerce? That’s just a happy accident.

To take Mayor O’Connell at his word, cities do not exist to preserve a particular version of Nashville; they exists to steward market conditions. If long-standing businesses can’t absorb rising valuations, that isn’t framed as a policy failure. It’s framed as demand.

I know every landlord has a different approach to this as commercial property owners. Some of them have, you know, triple net leases. They’re [sic] a bunch of different ways for this to work out. I think this is a tough one because you look at businesses all along Broadway, all along 2nd Avenue. You look at the volume of transactions that are there and the prices people are willing to pay for property, and it’s tough. It’s competitive. It’s a place where a lot of people want to be, and the market evolves. And, you know, we see new businesses starting even as old businesses -- in some cases, beloved old businesses -- are closing.

In the neoliberal world, market displacement is treated as a natural evolution, while market interruption—anything threatening tourism revenue, conventions, investor confidence, or downtown throughput—is treated as an emergency, if not an insurgency.

But the slow violence of housing instability, untreated addiction, underfunded schools, and neighborhood disinvestment? Background noise.

Nowhere was this clearer than in the Surveillance MOU ordeal. As I and others repeated, time and time again, citing their source document from the State of TN, the grant allowed a wide range of uses besides policing and surveillance gear. But the Mayor’s Office had no inclination to obligate the Nashville Downtown Partnership to use money in this way.

Instead, Kristin Wilson, the same person who helped build the data pipeline for ICE, wrote extraordinary surveillance items to be handed to a nonprofit for use by MNPD.

There were so many things in that saga that forever changed the way I will view Nashville and the people who have been given great responsibility here, a good number of whom I was once coworkers with. Much of that has to do with doing what I have no other word for: bald-faced lying in public to get what they want.

But what I find even more astonishing is the truths people have said out loud. The truth is that, at the end of the day, it’s never been about public safety. If it were, they would have invested in things that have actually been documented to improve public safety. What it’s about, as Huffman put it, is just money.

Money that’s not even going to them, just to people they don’t want to upset because they seem smarter than them. They seem to actually “get” how the world works. They went to good schools and have nice jobs, the kind they’d like to have after their tour of duty in the public sector.

People like to think Broadway’s power brokers run Nashville. They don’t. They are tenants in someone else’s spreadsheet.

Debt, valuations, investor expectations — these are the real governors. The bar owners are replaceable. The developers are replaceable. Even the mayor is replaceable.

Capital is not sentimental.

And when leaders say they’re protecting downtown, they aren’t protecting the people who built it. They’re protecting the machinery that can swap them out the moment returns dip.

And to the Nashville business owners who are starting to feel that no one in power actually has your back? When your membership group, the Nashville Downtown Partnership, presents its budget to Metro Council this Spring and includes millions for Solaren Risk Management, remember this feeling. That is what it feels like to discover you are just another variable in someone else’s risk model.

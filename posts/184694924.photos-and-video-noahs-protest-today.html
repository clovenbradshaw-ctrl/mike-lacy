AT 12:00 PM TODAY IN CHURCH STREET PARK,
across the street from the Nashville Main Library Branch that has been closed since there was an explosion and fire in the parking garage managed by the Nashville Downtown Partnership (NDP), around 75 people gathered to protest the surveillance bills up for vote in Metro Council on Tuesday, Jan 20.

The resolutions, submitted by Mayor O’Connell and sponsored by Council Members Kyonzte Toombs, Jordan Huffman, Jacob Kupin, would have NDP donate $3.8M to Metro Nashville in policing equipment. The resolutions were delivered on January 9, the same day it was revealed that House Speaker Cameron Sexton has been working with the White House’s Stephen Miller to revamp TN’s legal landscape to be a national “model” for immigration legislation.

Context

The source of the funding NDP is said to be in possession of is Governor Lee’s “Violent Crime Intervention Funds” (VCIF) Downtown Public Safety Grants (DPSG), announced in the same forum as the Violent Crime Intervention Funds” (VCIF) funds and geared towards the same purpose. [See clarification edit here].

VCIF was originally described in FY23 as being prompted by “the situation that we have going on in the border”. The other funds distributed in this round have ended up in ICE-related task forces, with $100M going to the “Memphis Safe Task Force” that fund immigration enforcement in Memphis.

Given the source of these funds, the timing and context that they were announced, and the legal threats made against Mayor O’Connell as recent as last week for his alleged support of illegal immigrants, reasonable minds have questioned whether these “donations” have an ulterior purpose than just adding some gear to MNPD’s toolkit (the agency received an increase of $24.7M in FY26), and if perhaps they are meant to appease State and Federal immigration priorities. That these policing donations are being pursued at the same time as the Mayor’s Office touting benchmark decreases in crime have further confused why this is such a priority for O’Connell.

Additionally, a “donation” of this size from NDP has raised eyebrows amongst Metro watchers. NDP was under contract to manage the Downtown Library parking garage when it caught fire. City officials have stated there has been "no final determination of legal liability for the fire or damage. There is no official word if the garage is salvageable. As per a memo from the Metro Public Library Director Terri Luke to all library employees, she quoted NDOT’s assessment of the facility:

“NDOT is shoring inside the library garage and continuing its structural assessment, and we [NDOT] appreciate the public’s patience as we conduct our due diligence. The garage is not currently open to the public and is not anticipated to be open to the public in the immediate term. We will provide additional updates as they become available.”

That a vendor with potentially significant liability to Metro Nashville is being engaged directly by the Mayor’s Office to provide them policing equipment not actually mandated by the grant that the entity obtained from the State of Tennessee is a situation that, on its face, seems like a conflict of interest worth examining before Metro accepts any donations.

The grant is flexible. NDP could spend it on things that have nothing to do with policing equipment. But the only use the Mayor’s Office is trying to obligate is $3.8 million in police equipment — purchased by a vendor that may owe Metro for a garage fire. This could be interpreted as a coincidence, or as leverage. Not enough is known about how this arrangement came about to say which it is.

If the Mayor's Office can persuade NDP to spend grant money on equipment Metro claims it wouldn't otherwise want, why not use that leverage for something Nashvillians actually support?Over 448 residents have voted on how they’d prefer this money spent - all options aligned with the grant’s original purpose. The Mayor’s Office appears uninterested.

The protest on Thursday was put on by NOAH, Nashville Organized for Action and Hope, a member led organization representing 41 religious congregations in the city with a total congregant population estimated at over 50,000 Nashvillians. Leading up to this protest, some had expressed skepticism that a noon protest would get anyone to show up, even on such a controversial issue. The skeptics were wrong.

The crowds and media turned up, as did Aftyn Behn, the TN State Rep who recently announced she will be seeking re-election to oversee the very district where all this is going down.

The park was packed with protestors from a wide spectrum of causes who had solidarity in opposing Mayor O’Connell’s push for policing equipment, a deal arranged privately in an era of unchecked state violence on the civilian population.

Who Is NOAH?

Nashville Organized for Action and Hope (NOAH) is a faith-based coalition of 41 congregations representing approximately 50,000 members across Nashville. The interfaith organization brings together churches, synagogues, and other religious institutions to advocate on issues including affordable housing, criminal justice reform, and economic equity.

NOAH operates through large public assemblies—including meetings of 800-1,200 people with mayoral candidates where officials make commitments on NOAH's policy priorities. The organization holds quarterly meetings with Mayor Freddie O'Connell, reflecting their status as a key political stakeholder in Metro government.

Their last meeting with the Mayor was December 18 171, the day after his push for an MOU with NDP for policing equipment was deferred by Metro Council. It is not publicly known how that meeting went, and how the Mayor explained the legislation. But it seems like nothing discussed in that meeting reduced any anxieties around this funding — the meeting was promptly followed by an announcement about NOAH’s planned protest against the “militarization of our police force.”

NOAH’s Political Power

NOAH’s 50,000 members translate to roughly 32,500 eligible voters—a staggering 6% of all eligible voters in Davidson County. To put that in perspective, most civic organizations count their membership in the hundreds or low thousands, not tens of thousands. But NOAH’s real power comes from actually getting their members to vote. In Nashville’s typical mayoral election, only about 20% of eligible voters show up—roughly 105,000 people total.

Research consistently shows that regular church attenders vote at much higher rates than the general population, and when NOAH mobilizes through Sunday services, voter guides, and congregational networks, they can deliver reasonably 13,000-16,000 actual votes. That means NOAH may directly influence as much as 15% of all votes cast in a mayoral race. In a multi-candidate election where the winner might only need 37,000-47,000 votes total, NOAH’s bloc represents roughly a third of what’s needed to win.

Simply put: no candidate can become Nashville’s mayor without either securing NOAH’s support or overcoming their opposition.

Why This Is Huge

The crowd in Church Street Park today wasn’t the story. The story is what it represents: an organized political bloc with real electoral power publicly breaking with a mayor over his policing equipment initiative, 100 hours before the vote. The people who showed up on their lunch breaks were a stand-in for the 50,000 who didn’t need to - because their opposition will be counted in ways that matter more than headcounts.

On Tuesday, Metro Council will decide whether to approve $3.8M in state policing equipment funding routed through a private nonprofit. They’ll be watching the gallery. But NOAH will be watching them back — and taking notes for the next election.

Who said what

I was a little late so unfortunately I couldn’t get everyone’s comments on camera, but I recorded video and grabbed some highlights. Video and timestamps at bottom.

Rep. Aftyn Behn

TN State Representative, 51st District covering Downtown Nashville

On the genesis of the grants:

It’s important to name the genesis of these state grants as well. They were born out of a political worldview that says incarceration solves crime, that if we criminalize or displace unhoused people, they will somehow just disappear forever, and that the more surveillance and technology law enforcement has, we will feel safer.

On the failed model:

And these tools do not prevent harm, address the root causes, or create the kind of safety that Nashville residents, workers, and our unhoused neighbors or tourists actually deserve. So I oppose this plan because it doubles down on a failed model.

Chaplain Dahron Johnson

Metro Human Relations Commission Member

The powerful contradiction:

We get the outsize $15 million grant. This city has helped direct from the state to the private downtown partnership. Has as its purported purpose the improvement of our safety and public welfare. Why then, since the moment I’ve heard about it and just what’s planned with the money—Brave New World video walls and yet more peeping cameras, security details and armored vehicles—do I feel an ever increasing risk? Imminent danger of unsafe?

On state bedfellows:

These are the bedfellows with whom the downtown partnership has chosen to lie. It is this money and this state’s proven abuse of power this city’s leadership has chosen to aid and abet.

Kennetha Patterson

“The Homeless CEO”, Storyteller-in-residence Claiborne Family Faith and Worship Center

On what “safety” actually means:

When people say downtown, is it safe? What they often mean is that poverty is visible, disability is visible, right? Well, being poor, being homeless is not a crime.

On how downtown safety is a thing she indeed cares about:

I traveled back to Nashville to work making the tourism boom happen. It literally made me become a leader in my community regarding housing disparities across Nashville, Davidson County… The safety of downtown involves all of us. So I just want to stand here with the lived experiences of being unhoused in this city while we help make it safe.

Luis Pedraza

Immigrant Rights Organizer

The lived stakes:

I’ve had a couple of friends taken out by ICE because of THP and because of local police in the past few months. Right? It ain’t right. I am the face of the people affected by this.

On the “hypothetical” bad things already happening:

When I say it’s not a hypothetical, what I mean is that we don’t have to imagine what it looks like when you put more funding, more tactical gear, expanded surveillance towards local police, towards a private police force here. We know what it looks like, and it means people being taken off the street.

Jacob Kupin

Metro Nashville Council Member, District 19, covering downtown

His stated opposition to the MOU on procedural grounds:

I opposed the MOU. I didn’t sign on. I fought it as soon as I knew about it. And to me, it wasn’t about what was in it, well a little bit, but it was about how it came about.

His support of an armored police vehicle:

Like I said, I personally see the need for a SWAT vehicle, but I know all of y’all don’t, right? And that’s okay. Right? Because that’s the one that we need to have and discuss.

His support of 15 cameras for $150,000:

I don’t have an issue with replacing cameras, but other people might. Right? And so it’s important to have those discussions.

Alisha Haddock

Executive Director of Neighbor 2 Neighbor

On what real public safety means:

“Public safety begins long before a crisis shows up on our streets. It begins when young people have a place to go, people who believe in them, and futures that feel possible. It begins when unhoused neighbors are met with care instead of punishment.”

Invoking Dr. King:

“Martin Luther King Junior warned us plainly: a nation that continues year after year to spend more money on military and defense than on programs of social uplift is approaching spiritual death.”

On what militarization costs:

“Let me tell you what militarization really costs us. It costs money. It costs lives. It costs families. It costs neighbors. It costs trust. And when trust is gone, safety disappears with it.”

Terry O'Neill

NOAH Integrated Voter Engagement Task Force

On the “throw your phone in the river” argument:

I have literally been told in the course of having conversations about this issue: if you have a cell phone, you have no business being concerned about surveillance because already on your cell phone, companies are gathering your data and selling it. That’s like the alcoholic who says, I just had two gin and tonics, I think I’ll have a third and fourth and a fifth. That makes no sense whatsoever.

On Larry Ellison’s surveillance vision:

The co-founder of Oracle recently shared his vision of what, in an ideal world, surveillance world looks like. This is what he said: ‘Good citizens will be on their best behavior because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that is going on.’ ... Do the CEOs and the Oracle arks and the billionaires have any intention of themselves being surveilled? It’s the rest of us that are going to be surveilled.

On the Tennessee Information Protection Act loophole:

Under that law, a private entity can simply give all of this data that gets collected by surveillance technology to any law enforcement agency that asks for it—including ICE. The only way to keep Nashville safe from becoming a surveillance state is to simply say no to surveillance in this $15 million grant. None.

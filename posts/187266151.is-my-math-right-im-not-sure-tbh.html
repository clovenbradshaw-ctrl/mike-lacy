Last Monday I published a widget I built that scrapes Nashville’s General Sessions Court database and compiles metrics on eviction filings — detainer warrants, in the legal language — something of obvious and urgent concern in a city where an ice storm just pushed hundreds of thousands of people into crisis.

I was asked how sure I was of the figures, “pretty sure,” I offered every time, “but there’s nothing to check my work against and that’s what scares me.” There are these strange gaps in our society’s collective infrastructure and my impulse is to go stick my finger in the holes of Nashville’s dam. How well did that go for the Dutch boy again?

I used AI to build this tool over a few months. It reaches into the CaseLink system, looks back through time at cases filed as detainer warrants, and saves them. It had to be built because there’s no easy way to export this data, no dashboard to view it, no API to query it. The court system was designed for individual case lookups, not for the public to understand patterns. I published the results with tenant names redacted so that anyone — journalists, advocates, council members, neighbors — could look at what’s happening and draw their own conclusions.

I’m happy to share with anyone the code for the tool and explain how it works. Really do just respond to this email, I would love to pass it off to an institution that’s interested so it can have some proper parents.

The Given and the Meant

There’s a distinction I believe between what you can observe and what you claim it means. Taking the web scraper, with high confidence can tell you that 138 detainer warrants were filed during the coldest week of winter while 500,000 people had no power. It can tell you that District 12 absorbs a wildly disproportionate share of filings — 16% of the week’s total.

Those are observations. They’re real. But what the rate means relative to population changes, what share of filings are serial filings versus unique households being displaced, how Nashville’s legal framework shapes these numbers differently than peer cities, confirm that all cases are included, all nomenclature consistent — that requires demographers, housing policy experts, and frankly the Metro government itself to show up and do work.

The observation layer needs work, we need more transparency than just what I can find with some spare time on the internet. And as important, the interpretation layer needs more hands on it than the public grasping at it. That’s how you get to ground truth — everyone sees what’s actually happening, and then we do the hard, collective, contextual expert-informed work of making sense of it together.

These are not my projects to run. They’re little prototypes for you, Vanderbilt, Belmont, TSU students to go “hm… what could we spin up a get going before our budget’s cut…”

The Vacuum

And here’s the thing — when there’s a crisis of legitimacy, the space gets filled. And it’s hard to not just trust the source that’s most aligned with your view of the world.

When the government doesn’t publish accessible eviction dashboards, doesn’t provide exportable court data, doesn’t maintain public-facing metrics on displacement — they aren’t just failing at transparency. They’re delegating the interpretive authority over a critical public question to whoever shows up first with a scraper and a Substack.

Nashville needs a real eviction dashboard. It needs exportable court data. It needs the kind of transparency that makes playthings like mine redundant. I gotta remember the mantra I assigned myself last September.

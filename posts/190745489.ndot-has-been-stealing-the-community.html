Photos, taken today (March 12, 2026), show NDOT in possession of a large number of community built benches in the East Nashville location.

You know those benches that Community Members have been building and installing at bus stops where they're missing? The benches that started when CM Jacob Kupin, the Nashville Department of Transportation, and the Nashville Downtown Partnership removed Metro benches from Korean Veterans Blvd, rather blatantly, to not allow the wrong kind of people from being on them?

The benches championed by Pastor Robin Owen (@)? The ones that have had glowing write-ups in all the major local media as an example of what communities can do when they work together to solve problems?

The benches that Hal Cato, the CEO of the Community Foundation of Middle Tennessee, has spotlighted, explicitly in a post highlighting the contributions of our unhoused neighbors?

The benches that have been disappearing from public spaces, including one I saw taken in real time, in broad daylight, absconded from a bus stop by a person who refused to acknowledge me after I pulled over and called out to them to ask why they were taking it?

Well, turns out we know who has been taking them and where they are.

The Nashville Department of Transportation (NDOT) has been taking them.

Here they are stacked haphazardly in a pile next to their facility on 5th in East Nashville.

It is not clear why the Nashville Department of Transportation, working for a Mayor whose main thing is transportation reform, is stealing community-made benches.

It is not clear why bus stops do not already have benches. It is not clear why, if the purpose was to replace the community benches with “real” ones, there are still no “real” benches at most of these bus stops.

It is not clear why WeGo, whose drivers have expressed enthusiasm for the elderly and disabled, who can now ride the bus because they have a place to sit and wait, is not doing everything it can to provide seating at bus stops to increase its ridership.

It’s not clear why Metro stealing benches the community, including the highest levels of philanthropic leaders, have shown support for, would be a “good PR move” from a Mayor’s Office so concerned with the PR of its transportation agenda that it has hired a full-time communications staffer just for this purpose.

Metro Nashville appears far more dedicated to removing needed infrastructure that has overwhelming community support than it is to actually delivering projects. After raising $104M from taxes that the public voted to have placed on itself via Mayor O’Connell’s signature Choose How You Move program, as of 10 days ago, it had created precisely one (1) sidewalk, 1/4 of a mile long.

A word to the PR behind Choose How You Move: having Metro departments steal community-led infrastructure projects that exist because, despite over a year of revenue collection tallying $104M, there still are not benches at many bus stops is not the way to make your boss look good.

I reached out to NDOT and the Mayor’s Office, and they did not immediately respond to my request for comment.

Perhaps they should choose to move forward with a plan like this:

  • Give some half-assed excuse for why they were taken

  • Put them back “temporarily” until they are replaced by “real” ones

  • Work with the well-publicized organization of the project for plans on what to do with them

  • Snap some selfies sitting on them

  • Pretend this never happened and claim you always “loved the benches.”

Stop proving my argument that the city very much SEEMS like it's run by out-of-touch bureaucrats captured by business elites who actively disdain actual Nashvillians. Every time you do things like this, you make it harder to believe that you actually serve the residents who voted for the signature transit referendum.

Please. Prove. Me. Wrong.

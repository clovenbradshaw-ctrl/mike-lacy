“We are one incident away from having the money turn off in Nashville.”

That was Metro Nashville Council Member Jordan Huffman in December 2025, arguing for a $15 million surveillance and security package routed through the Nashville Downtown Partnership, the private nonprofit that manages the city’s downtown business improvement district.

When state leaders talk about downtown Nashville's economy, they talk about it in the language of national security — "threats," "critical infrastructure," "homeland security."

This wasn't always the case. But there are only so many places for capitalist accelerationists to take their speech, and when the negative outcomes of unchecked growth become too obvious to ignore, you have to start calling critiques of capitalism form of “domestic terrorism” and hope no one calls your bluff.

If the economic success of Tennessee-based businesses is a national security interest, then that interest extends to corporations like CoreCivic, the largest private prison operator in the United States, headquartered five miles from the state legislature in Brentwood. CoreCivic's stock price depends on filling prison cells. And prison cells don’t fill themselves, they require a pipeline. And a pipeline requires capturing people who commit crimes, and like all forms of labor, those who make decisions but don’t like to do the actual messy labor interacting with other humans are infatuated using technology and AI to do it for them.

That is one analysis of why Tennessee officials are so infatuated with surveillance technology: to cast as wide a net as possible to support our local private prison conglomerate. The thing about conspiracies? You don’t have to know that you’re in one to be part of one. You just have to unquestioningly repeat things you’ve heard, things like “We are one incident away from having the money turn off in Nashville.”

But this is a silly line of reasoning, and certainly can’t be connected to the supermajority’s push to make “TN a model on immigration.” That kind of baldfaced collusion would show up in, say, the campaign finance reports of TN Republicans and CoreCivic.

So let’s take Huffman seriously. Let’s think about what actually keeps the money flowing in Nashville.

Nashville’s downtown entertainment district generates roughly $7 billion in annual visitor spending. That money doesn’t materialize out of our honky tonks and guitar shaped convention center out of nowhere. It is produced by that which is most dreaded by capitalists and their past and future employees, aka most politicians: workers.

That’s right, bartenders, hotel housekeepers, line cooks, servers, barbacks, bouncers, valets, Uber drivers, musicians playing four sets a night for tips, cleaning crews who scrape the district back to presentable before the next wave of tourists arrives. I’ve had a few of those jobs myself in Nashville and I know who keeps our lights on.

The money turns on every morning because those people show up. If they didn’t, there would be no revenue to protect. No economic engine. No tax base. No reason for the Nashville Downtown Partnership to exist.

This is disquieting for those who’ve read Atlas Shrugged and thought “yeah… me and my rich friends do make all the good things in the world possible…”

A side note to Randian Objectivists: study your holonic theory, read your Koestler. Vertically higher holons determine the likelihood of certain outcomes. Lower holons determine the possibility of any outcomes.

This is a disquieting reality for those in power who would like to convince you that it is their personal brilliance, beneficence, and abject enthrallment with what Council Member Freddie used to call, “surveillance theatre.”

Huffman is right that Nashville is one event away from the money turning off. He’s just wrong about what type of event. It’s not a crime on Broadway.

  • It’s a recession that cuts tourist traffic by 20%.

  • It’s a thousand more people on Nashville streets because the safety net got cut and there’s nothing underneath it.

  • It’s the next pandemic, when the service workers rounded up in immigration raids are the same ones they need to beg back.

  • It’s a viral video of an armored vehicle crashing into a bachelorette mobile.

  • It’s a mass protest after an ICE raid on a Broadway kitchen.

  • It’s the summer the shelters hit capacity and downtown smells like a city that chose cameras over beds.

  • It’s the housekeepers who aged out with no healthcare and no reason to tell their kids to take the same work.

  • It’s ten thousand people marching down Broadway because they’re tired of being watched.

And the $15 million apparatus his colleagues approved last month — the armored rescue vehicle, the mobile command center, the tactical surveillance post, the military-origin intelligence software — it isn’t pointed at some theoretical list of “known bad guys.” It’s pointed at the people who keep the lights on.

Whoever gave Huffman this talking point isn’t wrong to posit that the economic health of our income tax free state is precariously situated atop downtown Nashville. But dollars to donuts the lobbyist passing through an analysis like this happens to work for a vendor that has just the solution to this problem.

There is a reason the apparatus is aimed where it’s aimed. Organizers behind the successful Stop Avelo boycott — which forced the airline to stop flying deportation flights — put it plainly: the point of targeting immigrants is to target laborers generally. The message to every worker is you will be next if you don’t accept your wages and conditions as they are.

In our leaders’ fervid recent competition to demonstrate precisely how good they are, in fact, at licking boots, I think few have actually stopped to ask: are we making the thing we’re afraid of — an economic downturn downtown — more likely through the way we’re trying to prevent it?

This the most basic Oedipal tenet of human society: whatever you do to prevent something is often the very thing that makes it happen.

It’s a fact that should concern the downtown establishment that feverishly wants more surveillance at any cost — in fact, the higher the cost, the better, so long as it’s taxpayers paying — not out of some sudden moral awakening about what this infrastructure does to immigrants and homeless residents. I am not that naive.

But the issue should concern them because of the only thing they have demonstrable concern about: their own profit margins.

Here’s some hard facts: you cannot surveil a polis into not wanting basic services. And the deep irony — the irony that will likely forever escape the Cameron Sextons, Bill Lees, Tom Turners, and seemingly the Freddie O’Connells of this state — is that the more you police dissent, the more you produce it.

If you want businesses to flourish in a stable environment, invest in services for real people in those communities. If you want both to collapse, invest in policing and nothing else. The logic isn’t complex. The people in power just find it inconvenient.

The reality is, nobody registers as a lobbyist for rental assistance or homeless outreach. For the things people really need, our system relies on volunteers to raise their hands and try to educate our leaders. At best there are low wage nonprofit workers who fill the space, but quite quickly these roles become conflicted out as grant money and “strategic partnerships” make saying the hard part out loud too risky.

The surveillance apparatus isn't just watching. It's sorting. Every camera, every AI platform, every database that flags a face or a name is doing intake work — identifying who can be swept into a system that needs bodies to remain profitable. CoreCivic reported $1.9 billion in revenue in 2023. Its own SEC filing lists decriminalization as a business risk. Governor Lee has received $69,000 from the company. Speaker Sexton, who spearheaded truth-in-sentencing laws requiring inmates to serve dramatically longer portions of their sentences, has received $44,500. The cameras go up. The laws get written. The beds get filled. The company donates to the people who made it possible. The cycle doesn't have a bug. It's working exactly as designed.

Here’s the business math for those who like bottom lines and ROIs: for every surveillance vendor and weapons manufacturer with lobbyist representation at Metro and the state house, a dollar spent on actual services that helps people is a dollar that doesn’t flow through their client. The policing and prison system is never going to say “Actually, we’re good now. No more money, please!”

The spending choices are not irrational. But they’re rational for the wrong people. The people who persuade TN leaders to spend money on policing have far too much power for their own good. They are destroying our town to save it — and making a killing while doing it.

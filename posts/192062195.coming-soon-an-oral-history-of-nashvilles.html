Many are celebrating the return of benches to bus stops in Nashville after they were unceremoniously absconded by the Nashville Department of Transportation (NDOT). Even, strangely enough, by the very people whose decisions led to their need to be built through unofficial public efforts in the first place.

The people behind the project are not ones to toot their own horns. They are the types who shape the ground of what makes cities livable and, at times, lovable. They are the people who spend their free time creating space for other people.

In the space they create, people come together and build infrastructure and make it beautiful. In that space, people are able to connect. To build. To beautify. And, seemingly against the wishes of our municipal leadership, to sit and wait for the bus.

But in that space, others are bound to insert themselves. To take credit. And to misconstrue the meaning of movements to serve their own purposes. This is the unending tension between those who wish to do good for others first, and those who’ll gladly put their neighbors down if it means becoming popular with elites whose ranks they want to join.

Whether it is the colonization and astroturfing of movements like “tactical urbanism” by the very bureaucracies that create the need for it, or the revisionist history of those who initially called for the removal of public seating (only to pretend they always sat on the right side of sitting), there's a need for a people's history of Nashville's benches — told by the people who are actually building them.

So I’ve been working on that, interviewing those directly responsible for this project, preserving it in their words for posterity.

A take away I’ve had in this process is that our public servants, who seem to need reminding that it is the public that they serve, need to be become more self aware of their actions. This is not chaos. There is a simple logic at work here, a reaction to every action, and the outcomes they dislike are directly tied to their own decisions: infrastructural karma writ large in colorful painted letters.

The refrain I've heard during this project is a mantra: “for every bench removed, two shall take its place.”

A word of advice to those who seem immune to advice: the surest route to maintaining the appearance of control is not to remove examples of the public out performing your efforts. Nor is it in gaslighting us into believing you have been on both right sides of every issue. You must actually learn how to be more effective, efficient, honest and transparent.

That true effort will not show up in words or IG posts or interviews filled with cul-de-sacs of blame shifting equivocation that are inconsistent within the same sentence, but in deeds. And not when it is convenient, but explicitly when it is inconvenient.

Morality only matters when it comes at personal sacrifice — everything else is virtue signaling and egoism, wanting to be liked, but doing nothing to deserve the trust and approval of those you claim to serve.

A People’s History of Nashville’s Benches is coming.

In the meantime, please enjoy the glowing coverage of this weekend’s latest Bench Party in outlets like the Tennessean.

On a vacation from my former job at the Nashville Mayor’s Office to a micro cabin in Tennessee last year, I was away from my phone long to actually read a book for once.

On my partner's suggestion, I fell into The Ministry for the Future, and was swallowed up by its depictions of an all-too-inevitable future of climate catastrophes, large and small, which are responded to by technocratic tweaks, violent resistance, and little robots that burrow into the arctic ice sheets to slow glacial melting (an idea I find disturbing, yet something I’d probably root for on Shark Tank).

I got fixated on a metric that’s casually tossed off in this “Speculative-Climate-Fiction” nove, it was the term “wet-bulb.”

Wet-bulb. Feels gross on the tongue. Sounds like a device you find referenced in a medical report about a surgery you had. “They put WHAT in me?”

The book has passages like this:

A few years ago it would have been among the hottest wet-bulb temperatures ever recorded. Now just a Wednesday morning.

It’s thrown around a lot in the first few chapters, the chapters are told from the POV of a character who knows about these things (but conveniently explains it to the audience).

… a wet-bulb temperature of 35 [celcius] will kill humans, even if unclothed and sitting in the shade; the combination of heat and humidity prevents sweating from dissipating heat, and death by hyperthermia soon results.

Throw a weird ass hyphenate word like “wet-bulb” at me enough times and I’ll eventually look it up, and the author Kim Stanley Robinson is right to hit you over the head with it. We’re not, in Nashville, or America even, thinking about heat the right way — and it’s killing us.

When it comes to measuring how hot it is, the local news in the US uniformly talks about the “Heat Index” (or the trademarked RealFeel®) which combines 2 variables: temperature and humidity. Hop from the sauna to the steam room at the Donnelson YMCA, you’ll get why humidity makes such a difference to the temperature.

But the way human bodies respond to heat, especially outdoors or indoors for prolonged periods of high humidity, in ways that just haven’t been that common in inhabited parts of the globe, are poorly measured by these two variables alone.

Heat-related illnesses and deaths are rising, and their exacerbation on comorbidities like heart disease, diabetes, and COPD are as well, though they’re proven to be underreported. Extreme heat also has been proven to correlate with higher rates of violent crime.

When we’re worried, especially about people who are unhoused, without shelter or air conditioning, without cooling centers and clean water, with pre-existing conditions that get exacerbated by extreme heat, we need to be thinking about heat in the right ways — and that isn’t the “heat index.”

Let’s think about the two scales as circuit breakers, with different fuses “tripping” at different heat intensities. When those fuses break, you need to take action. Both of these scales are trying to tell us when hot is too hot. When do you need to cancel soccer practice or the outdoor graduation ceremony, when do you need to make sure no one is sleeping on the streets and when do you need to deliver water door to door.

In the not so distant, you’re going to make these decisions based on when these “fuses” trip, defined by different levels of heat intensity. You’ll be checking your phone, “Siri, is today an Orange heat day?” More often than we’ll want, Siri’ll say:

“No, unfortunately, today is a Black Flag heat advisory day. It’s recommended to limit all time spent in direct sunlight and to reduce all unnecessary energy consumption to prevent energy disruptions. Please do your best to stay cool and stay safe today. Would you like to see sponsored posts for high efficiency air conditioners?”

The WBGT beats out the heat index by including solar radiation, the angle of the sun, and wind, along with the humidity and temperature, and more accurately tells us how our bodies are going to react to what’s going on with the heat. Eventually, we’ll all flip over to WBGT, but in the meantime, there’ll be unnecessary illness and death as we underestimate the severity of heat by sticking with the Heat Index.

Each system has “levels”, the different”fuses” that flip that I was telling you about. A problem with the Heat Index, perhaps even more than that it doesn’t take as many important variables into account, is that it’s “extreme” level, saying “keep your ass in doors or you may well just bake out there” is set unreasonably high.

The Ministry for the Future depicts in its inciting incident a heatwave that kills millions in India, at temperatures/humidities so high that humans of any age and health cannot sweat enough (the body’s defense against overheating) and they are literally cooked alive.

This is in the like, “in a not-so-distant-future” part of the book, FYI. By 2050 the NYT says, (you know, a year as close to now as the year when “Titanic” came out), “over 5 billion people — probably more than half the planet’s population — will be exposed to at least a month of health-threatening extreme heat when outdoors in the sun.”

There’ll be 4 billion people at that level of risk by the end of this decade. Maybe I should have just quoted that stat first. Is there a real difference to you between 4 and 5 billion people, between 2030 and 2050? Will one of those motivate me to sell my car sooner?

In Nashville, we’re empirically feeling the effects of climate change, but not changing our actions much.

WPLN: Climate change has made ‘100-year storms’ four times more likely in Nashville

Vanderbilt: The Effects of Climate Change Being Felt in Nashville

Axios: Nashville will see an increase in 100° heat index days

And the verifiable rising rate of my personal weather nemesis, tornadoes, has forced me into a nonconsensual parasocial relationship with the boys on the Nashville Severe Weather Livestream.

The “WHAT DO WE DO?!?” of climate action is divided loosely into “mitigation” and “resilience”. The “Wet-Bulb Globe Temperature” of it all falls into the “resilience bucket” along with the other tools and ideas for “how do we adapt to this thing we’re doing to the world?”

But categorizing the WBGT as a “resiliency” tactic feels neutered. In general, climate “resiliency” feels euphemistic to me. “Resilience” smacks of the chutzpah that gets you back in the saddle after a bad job interview. To the world’s 114 million climate refugees, nearly all of whom are from countries that did little to nothing to contribute to climate change (and therefore can’t do much if any “mitigating” to speak of), the term should be more like climate survivalism.

We should switch, like the military, sports teams, and other countries have, to using the Wet-Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) for our emergency services, on our public outreach about weather safety, and on news reports warning people when there is a “Black Flag” day according to the WBGT.

If a Black Flag Day is too hot for Marines, how on Earth are we allowing the unhoused, laborers, children and the elderly be outside in these conditions. And believe me, we are.

Nashville’s most newish department, The Homeless Impact Division, is responsible for activating the “Heat Relief Shelter” to give anyone who needs it a cool place for the day. Often this just means riding around in city buses from 10 am to 7 pm, a fossil fuel-fueled irony if I ever heard one, but better than nothing.

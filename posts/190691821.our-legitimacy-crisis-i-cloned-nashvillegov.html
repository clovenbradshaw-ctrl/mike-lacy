I had Claude.ai create a clone of nashville.gov.

I typed “make me a clone of nashville.gov.”

After five minutes of autonomous work, it had the complete structure down with working navigation and drop-down menus, but not quite the look.

I gave it a screenshot of the landing page; five minutes later, it had it.

After swapping in Mayor O’Connell and Vice Mayor Henderson’s photos and a Google image search for “Nashville Parthenon,” it was a near-perfect dupe.

I challenge anyone to tell at a glance which is the real one.

I could point a URL at it and put anything I wanted on it — fake ordinances, fake council votes, fake budget numbers — and it would arrive with all the visual authority of a .gov domain minus the .gov (but who checks a domain name when getting your news from links on Facebook).

I didn't hack the AI model. I didn't circumvent any safe guards. I didn't use any knowledge about website HTML. I just said “make me a fake government website” and it complied. This isn't good.

In this video below you can the complete process in real time. You can see Claude.ai say “considering ethical concerns with this request,” then just plow right ahead to clone a government website. I have a paid version of Anthropic’s Claude product, but I'm fairly certain I could have done this with the free version.

Why Did I Do This?

I don’t know if you’ve noticed, but we kind of have a legitimacy crisis.

I’m starting what I’ll call “A Ground Truth Cohort”. Let’s call it a working group — not a class, not a bootcamp. I don’t have anything to teach, just some questions I’ve sharpened by asking them wrong in the past. It’ll be for people who want to build real projects with public data and develop a shared framework for testing the validity of each other’s work.

Wednesdays, 7–9 PM, starting in a few weeks. Bring a project. An eviction analysis, a budget deep-dive, a public records scraper, whatever you’re curious about and willing to work on with a small group.

Cost: Free | Sign up: email me

But I think most people don’t understand how bad this legitimacy crisis is. National media like the Washington Post and 60 Minutes are now PR fluff for White House-friendly billionaires. Local newsrooms are on skeleton crews and have to contend with elites that can get them shut out or even down. Governments reject public records requests on a whim. Neighbors are being disappeared or murdered by federal agents, and the best our leaders can muster is donating to GoFundMe links.

The infrastructure we relied on to sort truth from noise is collapsing — and we probably never should have trusted corporate mastheads and government seals to settle that question in the first place.

And into this vacuum: AI. Not just the obvious threats, the fake videos, the convincing bots, the graphic, sexualized slop, the fake .gov sites, the generated propaganda. A subtler one, one that is perhaps more confounding because it doesn't necessarily come from an intent to deceive.

Well-intentioned people using AI to spin up beautiful data dashboards and transparency tools that look rigorous but aren’t.

Until about a year ago, if a website was easy to use, especially if it had anything complicated like data tools on it, you had some assurance that it cost a lot of money to build. People with money, the logic goes, tend to want to keep it, and the liability that comes with posting blatant lies kept people somewhat truthful (spurious logic, but who said humans were logical?).

Now you can build extraordinary, beautiful websites with all sorts of wonderful interactive features for as little as $200 a month with subscriptions to tools like ClaudeCode from Anthropic. And people are doing it. I am doing it.

Don't get me wrong. I'm very glad it's now easier to build a website, it's helped me get important information out there and be taken seriously. I also hate that coders are being laid off en masse and that it is creating a crisis of legitimacy we are not prepared for. Not to mention the zillion other things wrong with AI environmentally, politically, and socially.

“You're the only person I know using AI for good,” I was told recently by a leader of an advocacy group. “Yeah,” I thought, thinking of all collateral consequences of my AI use, “still not convinced it's netting out in the ‘good’ column.”

But when it comes to “trusting things on the internet”, I can see around the corner on this, and we have some real problems heading our way.

What This Requires

I’ve thought a bit about this (ok, way too much about this). And I think there are three things you need to consider when trying to be truthful, things that should be no stranger to anyone familiar with peer-reviewed literature and the tenets of investigative journalism.

These are three things, by the way, that AI is explicitly terrible at. In fact, ontologically incapable of.

But before that, we need to accept an axiom: objective reality is not something any one of us ever encounters directly. What we encounter are phenomena — things as they appear to us, already shaped by how and from where we perceived them. They arrive feeling solid, feeling like the thing itself, but they are always the experience of the thing, never the thing. The truth is out there. We see the world as if through a keyhole, and the shape of our keyholes matter.

Here are the three prerequisites for approaching the truth:

  1. Every claim has to trace back to raw experience — not to ‘authority,’ not to ‘everyone knows.’ You’ll note I don’t say ‘facts,’ I say ‘experience.’ A fact is something that comes later; first, there is always an experience. You should be able to follow any experience back through a chain: who had it, from what position, grounded in what perceptions. And we don’t delete the record, no matter what. If you realize you remembered wrong, don’t erase your earlier notes — record that, on this date, you realized your earlier recollection was incorrect. The same is true of financial data and all other forms of evidence: preserve it as it first appeared. No one interested in the truth spends their energy hiding the past. Errors can always be corrected, but a correction that hides its antecedent “mistake” is just a deception. Experience must always be tied back to the perceiver’s identity, and working this way creates an audit trail of the given.

  2. What you can see depends on where you’re standing — and where you’re standing often changes what you’re observing. E.g. if you’re a service provider, you see invoices submitted and payments that came late. If you’re a department head, you see a budget line you were told to manage that's more complicated than anyone let on. If you’re a council member, you see a flagged total your constituents are mad about. And if you’re sleeping in a tent waiting to be charged with felony camping, you see a system that spent millions and still couldn’t get you indoors. Same dollars, different keyhole. No one has the ‘whole picture.’ When someone presents you with a claim — or when you make one yourself — you need to ask: from where? Being able to ask “from where” creates a spatial freedom that does not lock you into any particular identity’s experience. This is the frame.

  3. And then there’s the interpretation — what you assert is happening, built on top of the experience, through the frame. Experience times frame — the frame shapes what the experience becomes. ‘$3 million in financial irregularities’ is not a given. It’s an interpretation. The given is a stack of invoices, contract amendments, and retroactive payments pulled through public records requests. ‘Irregularities’ is the meant. A council member might use that word. An auditor might use a different one. The department might call it ‘operational flexibility.’ Each interpretation is grounded in a different frame, often in disagreement about what is given — and it’s no wonder there’s disagreement about what it all means. But disagreement is in itself information, grounded in its own set of givens and frames. The question for the truthseeker is never ‘who has more authority? or ‘who is totally in the right?’ It’s ‘how do these observations, from different positions, help us get closer to what actually happened?’ This is a new form of freedom, a freedom to traverse time which allows you to situate competing frames in context. This is the meant.

The truth is something we asymptotically pursue (adverb. / ˌæs ɪmˈtɒt ɪk li / “in such a way as to approach a given value, point, level, etc., continuously without ever reaching it.”) from convergent perspectives. Where interpretations converge despite different frames, you start to trust the conclusion. Where they diverge, you investigate the divergence. The divergence is information.

That’s the way I’m seeing it right now. But I’d like other people to feel out that theory and poke holes in it.

What I’m Starting

A Ground Truth Cohort. Let’s call it a working group — not a class, not a bootcamp. I don't have anything to teach, just some questions I’ve sharpened by asking them wrong in the past. It'll be for people who want to build real projects with public data and develop a shared framework for testing the validity of each other’s work.

Wednesdays, 7–9 PM, starting in a few weeks. Bring a project. An eviction analysis, a budget deep-dive, a public records scraper, whatever you’re curious about and willing to show.

We’ll ask each other hard questions:

  • Where did your data come from, and how did it enter the record?

  • What’s your frame, and what would someone in a different position see?

  • Does your conclusion survive contact with a different perspectives, or does it depend on assumptions you haven’t examined?

You don’t need to know fancy data science tools R or Python. You need to be curious, willing to show your work, and open to being wrong. Nashville people especially welcome, but the framework should work anywhere. The legitimacy crisis is not local.

Cost: Free | Sign up: email me

I’ll be vetting people who I think make sense to join at this point, it’ll be small, and sorry if you ask to sign up but aren’t in the first one.

I’ll be subjecting my own work to the same scrutiny. That’s the whole point. The ground truth is not a thing you find. It’s a thing we triangulate — together, transparently, with the humility to know no perspective can see the whole truth, but that with enough of us, we can get closer and closer.

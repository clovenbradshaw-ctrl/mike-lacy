A stunning series of deeply reported articles and videos, involving unprecedented levels of coordination from local investigative media, exposed the brutality of last May’s joint THP/ICE operation in Nashville — and systematically dismantled the federal government’s justification for it.

But for Metro Council members, the investigation should trigger a more uncomfortable question. The Tennessee Highway Patrol troopers documented on bodycam pulling over Latino drivers on pretext stops and funneling them to ICE agents — are any of them the same THP troopers who work overtime for Solaren Risk Management under the Nashville Downtown Partnership contract?

The NDP’s security detail, funded through the over $40 million in Council-approved (or supposed to be) special assessment funds since 2019, exclusively employs off-duty THP troopers. If there’s overlap — and the pool is finite — then Council isn’t just watching a state-federal operation target Nashville residents by race. It’s paying some of the people who did it.

On Thursday, six newsrooms across two continents published the results of a six-month investigation into “Operation Flood the Zone,” the week-long immigration enforcement sweep that turned South Nashville into what one trooper’s bodycam captured as a hunting ground.

A banner affixed to the news coverage

The Nashville Banner, NewsChannel 5, Mother Jones, Nashville Noticias, the University of Memphis's Institute for Public Service Reporting, and Netherlands-based Lighthouse Reports each brought a different piece of the story.

None duplicated each other.

All drew from the same verified evidence base: 52.5 hours of bodycam footage, 605 THP incident reports, hundreds of criminal court records, and over a million anonymized ICE enforcement records from UC Berkeley’s Deportation Data Project.

The findings are devastating.

DHS claimed 196 arrests. The data shows a maximum of 159. DHS claimed 95 had prior criminal convictions or pending charges. The investigation found 40. 75% of those detained had no criminal record. The “worst of the worst” turned out to be people driving home from work with a bent license plate.

Sarah Grace Taylor’s “’Huntin’ Time’” for the Banner is the data backbone — a meticulous reconstruction of stop-by-stop patterns showing troopers concentrated in Latino neighborhoods, using pretextual violations to pull drivers over, and funneling them to ICE agents staged nearby.

Levi Ismail’s “’Guilty Until Proven Innocent?’” for NewsChannel 5 is the visual gut-punch — bodycam footage showing white and Black drivers with American accents released without citation, while Latino drivers get handcuffed for declining to answer questions they had every legal right to refuse. Araceli Crescencio and Stephen Elliott tracked deportees across five countries for the Banner’s companion piece.

Marc Perrusquia at the Institute for Public Service Reporting documented a 400% spike in high-speed pursuits when the operation moved to Memphis. Melissa Del Bosque and Seth Freed Wessler framed the national policy story for Mother Jones.

And Lighthouse Reports built the analytical infrastructure that made it all cohere — geolocating 250 videos, cross-referencing ICE records, and open-sourcing their code on GitHub.

None of it would have been possible without TIRRC’s lawsuit. When the Tennessee Highway Patrol stonewalled public records requests for more than 100 days after the operation, TIRRC sued in Davidson County Chancery Court. Chancellor Russell Perkins rejected THP’s arguments for withholding footage, and 250 videos from just the first night were released — with officers’ faces redacted but civilians’ identities left exposed. Footage from the remaining five nights is still in litigation.

The question Metro Council should be asking

This investigation makes me think about something closer to home — something I’ve been reporting on for months.

Tennessee Highway Patrol troopers don’t just work their state shifts and go home. Many of them pick up overtime through private security contractors. In Nashville, the dominant contractor for off-duty law enforcement is Solaren Risk Management, which holds a contract with the Nashville Downtown Partnership — the entity that administers the special assessment district encompassing downtown Nashville.

The Nashville Downtown Partnership has received over $40 million in special assessment funds since 2019 — money collected from downtown property owners but administered under Metro Council oversight. The Metro Council is responsible for approving this budget. They’ve gone 22 years without actually doing it, however.

How am I confident quoting $40M? Because I submitted a public records request for it, and that’s how much Metro transferred to them since 2019. But 2024 to 2018? Metro refused to give me any documents about that period and refused to say why. From looking at the IRS 990s though of the District Management Corporation (who gets the funding thats passed on to NDP) it appears to about another $20M.

Solaren, under its NDP contract, deploys off-duty officers — including THP troopers — to patrol downtown Nashville. The question that should be keeping Council members up at night after this week’s reporting is straightforward:

How many of the THP troopers documented in this investigation — the ones pulling over Latino drivers for bent plates and unlit tags, the ones funneling civilians to ICE staging areas — also work overtime shifts for Solaren under the Nashville Downtown Partnership contract?

Because if the answer is more than zero, then the Metro Council isn’t just passively observing a state-federal enforcement operation that targeted Nashville residents based on the color of their skin. It’s approving the payroll for some of the people who did it.

This isn’t speculative. The NDP’s relationship with Solaren, the flow of assessment funds, the oversight structure — these are all documented and discoverable. The question is whether anyone on the Council will connect these dots now that we have 52.5 hours of footage showing exactly what these officers do on state time.

The investigation published this week is a landmark in Nashville accountability journalism. But accountability doesn’t end at the statehouse. It runs through every funding mechanism, every contract, every Council vote that puts officers on Nashville streets.

The reporters did their part.

Now it’s Metro Council’s turn.

And if they put their heads in the sand as they did with the expansion of surveillance and policing tech against the public’s wishes, it will be the responsibility of the Council Members we replace them with next election to do the right thing.

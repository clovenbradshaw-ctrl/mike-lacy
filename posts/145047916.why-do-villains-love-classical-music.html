Preface

“Music—the language of the immortals, disclosed to us as testimony of their existence.”

-Mary Shelley


If you asked me what kind of movies I’d like, an easy way to sum them up is “movies where crazy action scenes happen at an Opera.”

In Hollywood movies, there’s an absurdly common trope of action set-pieces take place in and around Opera and Symphony performances. It happens in two different “Mission Impossible” movies, multiple James Bond films, “Tenet”, “The 5th Element”, and the list goes on.

It usually underscores a contrast between “high and low”. The vulgarity of hitmen and spies doing their dark deeds in spaces normally associated with dignified, high art. It creates a double refraction in us as viewers.

We are watching a vulgar thing (an action movie), and in the movie, people are watching high art (opera), while doing something vulgar (fist fighting, assassinations, McGuffin chasing), which is somehow feels like high art because they’re at the opera. Often the action takes on a choreographic balletic quality and is intercut with the performance taking place on stage, the grand themes of the two performances melding into a post-modern mesh of high and low.

Very “having your cultural cake and eating it too”. It’s little wonder that the director of “The Exorcist” and “The French Connection” eventually gave up Hollywood to direct operas in Europe in his twilight years.

As an example of this action movie trope in action, Puccini’s “Turandot” plays out on stage while Ethan Hunt is hand-to-hand fighting in the rafters and eventually gets nearly strangled by a sniper rifle that’s been hidden in a bassoon. 10/10.

Moreover, movie villains love classical music. Here’s a supercut that Slate put together proving it.

But Nic Cage’s Ronny is the romantic hero in the movie “Moonstruck”, and his passion for the transcendent emotions that Opera evokes spills into Cher’s Loretta — it’s the magic of the whole movie. Their trip to see La Bohème at the Met reignites her belief in love, and more than that, life.

Ronny and Loretta aren’t bougie sophisticates. They’re middle class (if even that). He’s a one-handed baker and she’s a 37-year-old bookkeeper for family-owned Italian businesses that probably didn’t survive Giuliani’s gentrification of NYC.

More than the music itself, the distribution places of classical music have been hubs of culture, and the cultured, since classical music was just called “music”. Ronny and Loretta knew they needed to dress the part to not stick out at the Met, the pomp and circumstance of the medium is an extension of the message.

I love going to the Symphony, take my proletariat card from me if you must. The Opera is a great time (if I’ve read the plot on Wikipedia and know what all this very Italian or German stuff is about). At my incredibly pretentious tiny liberal arts college, part of our mandatory curriculum was debating what the metaphysical subtext was of key changes in Bach’s “St. Matthew Passion”.

It was an education that put us deep into the guts of classical music discourse, so much so that I can read excerpts from a 1981 essay entitled “Key structure and tonal allegory in the Passions of J.S. Bach: An Introduction” as the music theory equivalent of the Drake vs. Kendrick feud.

Analysis of the passions is done a disservice, therefore, by H. J. Moser's equating of the key of A-flat major to G-sharp major in the St. Matthew Passion; or the fairly frequent statements by Moser and others that widely separated movements in C-major and c-minor in both passions are to be thought of as being in the same key; or Dieter Weiss's inclusion of the Cmajor chorus, "Lasset uns den nicht zerteilen" in the St. John Passion within a "scene" in E-flat. On the contrary, Bach shows rather an acute awareness of the distances between these keys, and exploits them in his structures.

Shots. Fired.

But the history of the classical arts is obviously OBVIOUSLY rife with problematic characters and practices, misogyny and racism being only the tip of the iceberg.

Have you seen the movie “Tár”? It’s one of my favorite movies, and now that I think about it, the closest it has to an action scene happens at the symphony. There’s a scene where the world-famous (fictional) orchestra conductor, Lydia Tár, is teaching a course at Juilliard and is pressing a queer BIPOC student on why they prefer contemporary composers over Bach.

The student is very uncomfortable with the worship of artists like Bach in the world he wants to build a career within. He very nervously tries to explain his reservations to Tár, who isn’t having any of it.

I agree with everything the student says to her in the Juliard scene in “Tár”. All the stuff about how being forced to engage with the works of virtually only straight white men, with the implicit commentary that they are the paragon of human potential, can feel like an assault on your beinghood.

My college thesis was more or less exploring the issues of holding a few dudes as the peak of human potential, it was subtitled: “Why ‘Great Men’ are the Problem and How Women, Androgyny, and Vulgarity are the Solution in Shakespeare’s Cymbeline.”

But… I also think Bach’s music is really good. Like… really good. And if you’re able to set your objections aside, or better yet, hold them in your mind while also letting his music do whatever it does to you, and sit in the ambiguity that is creative genius — that’s a helluva drug. A drug that might inspire someone like you to create work even more transcendent than Bach’s.

But Lydia Tár doesn’t leave it at just: “Well, I think Bach’s music is good.” She imposes a heavy dose of class-based pedagogical superiority that just doesn’t fly with Zennials, and she (spoiler alert) gets her ass canceled.

But why is classical music, going to the Symphony and Opera, being on their respective boards with your name on the wall, so damn classy?

Why was slapping your name on a performance space the best PR medicine for someone a New York Times headline labeled (within a week of his death) as “The Ultimate Climate Change Denier”?

Why are symphony spaces, operas, and ballets a place where people of a certain status assert their status?

To the history books!

For most of Western History, “class” status didn’t have to do with how much money you had (although how much wealth you had was inextricably linked to your class).

Your class status was mostly tied to the “nobility” of your blood, something you couldn’t do much about, or your ability to ascend the ranks of bureaucracy or the trades with a “liberal education.” Hold onto that word “liberal” for a moment, it didn’t mean then what it does now.

For the first 1,200 years of post-Jesus Western culture, there wasn’t much education to get outside of the ranks of the church. And what status you gained as a priest wasn’t shared with your family because you weren’t allowed to have one (funny how that works).1

But in the 12th century, with educational options expanding into a few universities around Europe, there was a bit of a middle-class beginning to emerge. You couldn’t become noble, that was a thing between you and God. But you could become liberated, that had to do with your intellect and your taste.

Going to school couldn’t turn you into an aristocrat, but the “Liberal Arts” could liberate you (the original intention of this word) from the mentality of what Aristotle called a “natural slave.”2

A Liberal Arts education wasn’t simply about teaching you facts and techniques that could help you move up in the world to become a clergyman, lawyer, apothecary, or civil servant. This education was seen as changing the state of your very soul.

Within the Liberal Arts tradition was a pseudomystical tradition known as “Scholasticism” which sought to pass on the sacred traditions of Western society. The core of Scholasticism was dialectical reasoning and logic, drawn directly from Plato's writing.

And it was Plato (428 BC - 348), in two of his immeasurably influential works “The Republic” and “Timeaus” who highlighted the importance of teaching four subjects in particular, a curriculum that would be known as “The Quadrivium”, and would be seen for nearly 2,500 years of Western Culture as the prerequisite for a liberated mind.

The Quadrivium was: Astronomy, Geometry, Arithmetic, and Music.

Music was immensely important to Plato because he felt it contained the ability to control the otherwise unregulated human soul. Pulled into every direction by reason, spirit, and desire, music had the ability to steer the soul into useful, and unuseful directions.

Music theory was pretty advanced even in the 400 BCE Mediterranean (and much more so if you looked at other places around the world) and Plato thought that the different musical modes, the different ways to advance through the 12-note scale, caused people’s souls to change in different ways.

Some musical modes made people more peaceful and contemplative, Socrates explains in dialogues written by Plato. Ruling over people meant controlling their souls, and you could inspire or subdue them by playing certain modes of music. As an example, Socrates says in the “Republic” that the Lydian mode should be banned because it would make men weak and unwarrior-like.

Caution! Listening to this music mode may make you a #SoyBoy!

Aristotle put it more bluntly: “Music directly represents the passion of the soul. If one listens to the wrong kind of music, he will become the wrong kind of person.”3

For about 1400 years, the church was the only game in town for becoming literate and getting access to ancient texts like Plato's dialogues. While the writings of Plato and his student Aristotle were not directly interwoven into Catholic doctrine and policy, their philosophical ideas influenced medieval scholasticism and the intellectual traditions of the church. The “approved” music of the era reflected this Platonic understanding of how to use music to mold the people.

During the Middle Ages, the church used music strategically to shape emotions and control the spiritual experiences of the populace. Gregorian chants, with their serene and meditative qualities, were designed to inspire reverence and contemplation, aligning with the church's goal of guiding the faithful toward piety and submission.

The Renaissance, however, brought significant shifts in power dynamics. With the rise of a wealthy merchant class, patronage of the arts began to transition from the church to affluent individuals and families.

A group of Renaissance musicians in The Concert (1623) by Gerard van Honthorst

Wealthy merchants and industrialists funded the creation of operas, symphonies, and ballets, and began organizing public concerts. These patrons were not just contributing to culture; they were asserting their status and influence, outdoing each other with the grandeur of their symphony spaces and the esteem of their musicians and composers.

These patrons of the arts did not have particularly refined artistic tastes for the most part. The Medici in 15th-century Rome, like the Koch Brothers of today, were mostly tasteless psychopaths who used their largesse in the arts to wash their hands of the bloody misdeeds they did to accumulate their wealth.

But leaving the Church’s standards and practices department behind did allow some real artists of the Renaissance, and later the Enlightenment, to return to ancient texts for inspiration. They looked into the source code of Western society, Plato and Aristotle, as well as ancient myths and folk stories, and even everyday life — all things that could never get a green light when the Church controlled all of culture.

A Masonic lodge meeting of Mozart's day, once thought to portray Mozart's own lodge. Oil painting (1789), Wienmuseum Vienna.

New forms of spirituality and mysticism emerged, manifesting in very un-Catholic beliefs and practices like neo-Platonism and the occult secret teachings of alchemists and hermetics, passed on in places like masonic temples that were the origins of the myths about “secret societies” and the Illuminati. Members of these pseudo-cults (that were more or less dinner clubs with some spooky shit thrown in) included Mozart, Beethoven and Haydn.

The composers of the era injected all kinds of previously taboo subjects from this grabbag of inspiration. Magick and alchemy, foreign cultures (usually exoticized beyond recognition), along with sensuality and other venal passions that were previously off the table.

With the need to fund this art not just through patronage from the rich, but with monies from middle-class subscribers, for the first time in thousands of years, what the people wanted to see drove what art was made. Plato would have been furious, the hoi poloi were dictating what music was being played.

“The Lydian, my Zeus, the Lydian! They’re not playing anything in Lydian, are they???”

Likely the most famous song ever to be written in the lydian mode, The Simpson’s theme.

Music wasn’t dead in Europe before this period. Quite the opposite. People's music, folk music, always existed, long before any of the ancient characters in this story. It was the music of small bands of people with identities rooted in particular places. Often it was literally their oral history, not written, but rhyming to pass more easily through each generation.

But now without the Catholic church having the last say on what was and wasn’t kosher in the Holy Roman Empire, the texture of everyday life began to be interwoven with emerging nationalism through patron-funded musical spectacles. The people’s music began to be absorbed into the consciousness of A People.

Before this era, Europe didn’t have a consciousness around we’d now call nations. But by weaving the folk music of a particular place into a high-minded transcendent thing with pomp and circumstance, Beethoven helped define what it meant to be German. Dvořák what it meant to be Czech. Chopin Polish. Bartók Hungarian. Mussorgsky Russian.

The study of music significantly influenced the rise of nationalism in the 19th and early 20th centuries by fostering national identity, supporting political movements, and promoting cultural pride. Composers like Frédéric Chopin and Bedřich Smetana incorporated folk melodies and traditional rhythms into their works, symbolizing their respective nations' cultural heritage and resistance to foreign domination​​.

Music served as anthems and rallying cries during political movements, as seen with Giuseppe Verdi's operas, which became symbols of Italian unification​. National conservatories, such as the Paris Conservatoire and the Prague Conservatory, played crucial roles in promoting national music and training composers in national styles​.

Public performances and music festivals, like the Eisteddfod in Wales and the Bayreuth Festival in Germany, celebrated national culture and reinforced unity​​. And, national anthems and patriotic songs, including "La Marseillaise" and "Deutschlandlied," became powerful symbols of national pride and unity​.

As symphonic music got more investment, and audiences demanded more spectacle, budgets and complexity increased. In its size and complexity, requiring dozens and dozens of live world-class performers, directed by a celebrity composer — in their heyday the Symphony, and later the Opera, were the equivalent of blockbusters movies that would be nominated for Best Picture.

Think “Oppenheimer”. Think “Gone With the Wind” or “LOTR: The Return of the King”.

The subscribers to these symphony spaces and opera halls, seeking enrichment through high-minded art, were the emerging middle class. They were not the highborn aristocrats who provided the capital for these spaces to open, but their yearly subscription to the Gewandhaus Symphony Hall in 18th century Leipzig to see Beethoven proved them to be “better” than the unwashed masses who would never be able to rent a tuxedo.

The world of symphonies and operas, ballet and fine art, has always been a place where the ultra-rich whitewash their misdeeds through beneficence, and the petite bourgeoisie used their attendance to show they were more refined than mere peasants of the field who were satisfied with their simplistic folk music.

That’s a pretty down-the-middle Leftist critique of how we get to the 21st century with institutions peddling “enlightening” art to the masses with more than a smidge of noblesse oblige baked in.

But it also feels limited.

Reducing classical music to being #problematic because they were funded by the 1700s versions of Jeff Bezos, and attended by Austria’s equivalent of FinTech bros, kinda ignores the reality that this era did create some undeniable bops.

Have you HEARD the “Queen of the Night Aria”???

Maybe opera is just not your thing. I’m not Lydia Tár, I’m not gonna tell you you’re wrong. But what I am going to tell you about is what happened in 1992 and 1998 with the Nashville Symphony and Metro Arts, and how we can see themes of class conflict and a view of the role of certain kinds of music that date back to Ancient Greece.

This has been a long journey, and there’s still a way to go. But as we’d say at my alma mater: “χαλεπὰ τὰ καλά.” Good things are difficult.

Class Warefare,
Nashville Hot Chicken Flavor

Last time we talked about the difficulties Metro Arts had running a revenue-generating festival and giving out $500k to arts groups. It solved it by giving up on the making money thing, and it eventually figured out how to give away its money to private organizations.

Without the Summer Lights Festival to run, it turned its attention to being the city government’s first and only arts funding mechanism. The funding started to flow out of the city budget smoothly, but tensions mounted aroun who was getting that money.

By 1992, there was already discord between the large and small arts organizations about how these grants were being distributed.

On May 21, 1992, The Tennessean published Alan Bostick’s commentary, entitled: “Showdown looming for arts funds?” It ominously led with a line that could have come from 2024: “A forum titled ‘Metro Funding for the Arts’ would not appear to be another way of saying ‘Blood Bath.’”

The fighting was over the inequitable allocations between large and small organizations, and most notably the Symphony, which by 1992 received a total of $650,972, a full 47% of what all arts organizations had gotten.

By this point Bill Boner was gone, relegated to the “remember when that happened?” waste bin of Nashville’s collective unconscious, a new executive was overseeing this fight over inequity.

Phil Bredesen, the Nashville Symphony board member who had used the $250,000 allocation from the city to strike a hard bargain with striking musicians, was now mayor. He’d resigned from the Symphony board, of course, but made no bones about expressing his priorites for steering this funding to over the eight years of his Mayorship

In the Tennessean in 1992, Bostick gave a play-by-play of a meeting about the Metro Arts funding that we haywire.

“It came down to an old-fashioned stand-off between 'big' arts groups and 'little' ones, a contemporary version of David and Goliath.” 

But Bostick had an interesting take on the parable, however.

He proclaimed: “Goliath’s the good guy here.”

Bostick would later serve as Senior Communications Director for the Nashville Symphony from 2006 to 2010. I guess they liked the way he was able to spin stories.

Bostick’s acerbic recounting of the 1992 meeting records for us the moment when: “A group of local folk music artists became, well, perturbed after hearing a proposal that the ‘biggies’ like the Nashville Symphony and Tennessee Repertory Theatre should receive about 90% of the grant money, the rest being divided among smaller organizations.”

You might notice a parallel to some of that eggregiously long history of classical music I gave earlier. The relationshop between music of the people, folk music, and institutionalized classical music, had morphed in the centuries since Chopin was using the tunes of the countryside it to build a national identity into his symphonies. And that’s because the role of symphonic music had changed.

Where before, the existence of folks music had been the fertile ground that gave birth to classical music, somehow the script had been flipped, and the argument was that in last 20th century Nashville, classical music and the prestige it brought to the city were what made folk music possible at all.

Bostick goes on to detail that meeting. He talked about when “Steven Greil, the symphony's executive director” gave his take on the debate between funding the large organizations vs. the smaller ones. “The very existence of the smaller groups,” Greil told the distressed small arts org leaders, “largely depends on the health of the big ones.” 

I love Greil’s quote there. I love it because, while I think it's factually bonkers, but it is ideologically honest. The trickle-down economics of this make no sense, but I think Greil is too good of a businessman to mean this literally.

In his quote we see the ideological seed from which Neo-Liberal policies are germinated, the idea that the biggest players in a space, the ones who can suck up the most capital, will use it according to the logic of the marketplace the most efficiently, and a rising tide will lift all boats.

But it also smacks of another political philosophy, one that happens to be called, of all things, Neo-Conservativism. The philosophical center of Neo-Conservativism was Leo Strauss, a strange, cryptic man who died fled from Nazi Germany and died on the quad of my college campus in Maryland.

Strauss was interested, nay, obsessed, with the idea of culture, being cultured, in a very particular way. He believed that the study of the Liberal Arts, including that last part of the Quadrivium, Music, was “is education in culture or toward culture.” Strauss didn’t like how we just threw that word around — my culture, your culture, their culture.

If we contrast the present day usage of “culture” with the original meaning, it is as if someone would say that the cultivation of a garden may consist of the garden being littered with empty tin cans and whiskey bottles and used paper of various descriptions thrown around the garden at random.

He believed that there was proper culture, culture contained within the great minds behind the Liberal Arts, and it was them whom we should study and listen to because they are “the great minds or, in order to avoid any ambiguity in a matter of such importance, the greatest minds.”

Leo Strauss was not heavily engaged with the study of music, but he helped put forth a political philosophy which put into words what many white ethno-nationalists struggle to when they argue for the execptionalism of Western culture. He argued that there was just something different about the minds that developed the Western philosophical and artistic canon. He’d never quite come out and say what it was, but they were inspired by… something, unlike the thinkers and artists in other cultures.

Herman Hesse’s book “The Glass Bead Game” takes this idea of Western culture’s sense of ideological exceptionalism to its dystopian conclusion. His protagonist explains why he feels that Western classical music is the music.

We consider classical music to be the epitome and quintessence of our culture, because it is that culture’s clearest, most significant gesture and expression. In this music we possess the heritage of classical antiquity and Christianity, a spirit of serenely cheerful and brave piety, a superbly chivalric morality. For in the final analysis every important cultural gesture comes down to a morality, a model for human behavior concentrated into a gesture.

This is more I think where Griel is coming from when he says “The very existence of the smaller groups largely depends on the health of the big ones.” I wouldn’t accuse Griel, whom I don’t know, of being well read,

Griel’s comments likely stem from the belief that classical art institutions contribute value to smaller organizations not through direct financial support but by elevating the city's cultural status and bringing capital “C” Culture to the city, where it would not otherwise be.

“The Arts”, from this vantage, is less about creativity and free expression, collectively sharing visions of a world yet to be. Rather "The Arts” in this sense is more the worship of “the classical”, the transcendent canonical Western “great works”, think Wagner or Puccini more than Dolly Parton Charley Pride.

The Symphony, Nashville Opera, TPAC, Cheekwood and Frist, these are Nashville’s Temple of Jerusalem, where Yahweh enters the city limits, brought to earth only under the strict hierarchical control of the priests of the donor class.

When the Executive Director of the Symphony said that: “The very existence of the smaller groups largely depends on the health of the big ones,” I think he meant it both literally and figuratively.

To intentionally build a system with inequity at its foundation requires either the cynical acceptance of a lie or an ideological blindness to the realities of the world.

Telling stories like this is a way to make the latter one harder.

The Art of War

Bredesen oversaw the dispensing of arts funds with little issue, and by 1998 he had helped the Symphony receive $2,088,472 of city funding and 26% of all arts money ever allocated. But 10 years into Metro Arts funding, a crisis struck when the independent, City Council-appointed Arts Commissioners voted, for the first time, that Symphony would not be the largest recipient of Metro Arts money that year.

All hell broke loose.

The then and current Symphony Executive Director, Alan Valentine, appealed the ruling directly to the Arts Commission and Mayor, denouncing it as “punitive” and called the reduction from $290k to $207 (both less than 5% of the Symphony’s annual budget) “devastating.”

Steven Greil, who had left the Symphony to be the Executive Director of TPAC, also had his funding diminished. He was furious, and he demanded the vote be voided.

TPAC’s funding from the city of Nashville, the appointed members of the Arts Commission had decided, would go from $125,000 to $70,000. Both figures less than 1% of TPAC’s total budget.

Griel went ballistic.

He alleged Arts Commissioners were being discriminatory. He sent a letter to the other large arts organizations for them to co-sign, demanding the vote be nullified. He not only demanded that, but that the Arts Commission change its entire funding process to avoid this type of outcome.

The Tennessean reported that he demanded:

A new allocation process for Basic I [the category that includes Nashville's largest arts groups] be implemented that is more representative of your intent and the expectations and genuine needs of the arts community.

I think there's a bias against the symphony and TPAC," Griel said. "I think the commission is biased against groups that end the year in the black.”

The aggrieved Griel felt that TPAC was not being judged by the content of its character, but the color of its balance sheets. It would not be the last time a Metro Arts grant applicant would accuse the commissioners of “colorism”.

To date, the two arts organizations Griel had worked at had collectively received $2.1M in the nine years of grants, 32% of all Metro Arts funding. But reducing funding based on publicly noticed grant scoring metrics, he argued, was nothing short of discrimination.

Kevin Nance on the same page in the Tennessean gave his two cents for why the large organizations had such an extreme reaction to relatively small changes in grant sizes that were effectively rounding errors for their overall budgets.

The symphony's dismay over this year's grant is related, it seems clear, to matters of symbolism and hierarchy. The orchestra has always received the largest grant, and to be toppled from the summit (by the Tennessee Repertory Theatre, the year's biggest money winner) obviously came as a shock. The cosmology of Nashville's arts universe had suddenly shifted for a moment, leaving the orchestra in a state of organizational vertigo.

Symbolism, hierarchy, status — this is what this funding meant to large arts organizations in 1998. Their leaders were willing to go scorched earth to preserve these ego markers, with the impact this had on the broader creative arts ecosystem of Nashville not even coming to mind.

Phil Bredesen, former Symphony board member and still Mayor, took the issue directly to the Executive Director of the Metro Arts, Tom Turk. 

Bredesen relayed the conversation to the Tennessean: “I told Tom that I had some concerns... I respected the process that the commission had gone through, but that I didn't agree with the outcome.”

Bredesen explained to the Executive Director of The Arts Commission that although this arts funding was technically being voted on by an independent, democratic commission, the commission was not actually meant to decide on the best use of these funds.

“That money,” Bredesen explained, “was originally put in the budget with the idea that the major arts groups would have a stable source of funding they could more or less count on year after year."

This, of course, truly was the intent of the politicians and board members who structured this arrangement. Boner, Bredesen, Ingram, and Griel, there was no confusion amongst them about how public funds were meant to be used to prop up institutions like the Symphony. This grant funding, coming “from the Mayor” was a status symbol that likely translated exponentially into more donations for these groups.

“Would the Mayor be investing in us if we were about to go bankrupt again?”

But with no memorandum of understanding, City Council resolution, executive order or even department bylaws stating this transactional gladhanding was the purpose of the Metro Arts Commission grants — how long was Nashville supposed to be on the hook for the status symbol payments to these art institutions? What would happened when Arts Commissioners, Executive Directors, or even Mayors wanted to use this funding do so something different with it?

The following year the Symphony was back at #1 with $300,000 of Metro Arts money, nearly double the second-highest recipient.

Next time, as Phil Bredesen is becoming Governor of Tennessee, Kevin Crumbo is rising in the ranks of the city, becoming the Board Treasurer of the Nashville Symphony. That year, 2003, was also the peak of Metro Arts’ investment in the Symphony, capping out at $500,000.

We’ll move forward to two simultaneous events that set a collision course in motion that would crash spectacularly ten years later: the advent of the Thrive funding program for diverse artists, and the Symphony’s second bankruptcy.

Chapter 3: The Burden of Progress

To understand the current moment of disorientation—the existential scream that so many feel—we have to look back, far back, to the time when humanity first began to organize itself beyond small tribes and clans. It was during this leap from close-knit groups to larger, more complex societies that the seeds of today’s crises were planted. From that point on, the tension between individual needs and collective expectations became a constant. The stories we’ve relied on to make sense of this tension are now unraveling.

In the early days of human existence, life was structured around small communities. Humans evolved to live in tribes of roughly 100 to 150 people, where everyone knew each other personally. Social roles were straightforward, and the rules governing behavior were based on direct, personal relationships. There were no large institutions or distant rulers—only family, kin, and a strong sense of mutual responsibility. In this world, people like John would have had a clear place and purpose, tied to his community and its survival.

This way of life persisted for thousands of years. Human societies remained small and interconnected, and while conflict between tribes occurred, it was limited in scope. But as humans mastered agriculture, everything changed. They began to settle, grow crops, and domesticate animals. Villages grew into towns, then into cities, and eventually into empires. This marked the first time that humans had to navigate life within large, impersonal systems. It was the beginning of a shift that would eventually leave individuals like Ahmed facing the vast consequences of decisions made by people in faraway lands.

As societies expanded, they developed layers of hierarchy and governance. Leaders emerged, followed by the creation of laws, religions, and political systems that could manage the needs of thousands, even millions. This shift replaced the intimate ties of the tribe with the anonymity of the city. Now, people had to learn to live among strangers, a challenge that required new kinds of stories and beliefs. These myths and religions offered ways to understand one's place within larger systems. For people like Marta, these narratives could offer a sense of belonging within an increasingly diverse and unfamiliar environment, helping her feel connected to a collective identity.

Religious systems, like those in ancient Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Greece, provided shared narratives that helped people make sense of vast, impersonal structures. Religion served to connect the personal with the cosmic, offering a sense of purpose within an often chaotic world. But in moving from tribes to civilizations, humanity also moved from a morality based on personal ties to one grounded in abstract principles. It became necessary to manage relationships with strangers, leading to the development of formal laws and institutions. This leap meant that individuals, like Rachel, had to wrestle with a new kind of moral framework—one that balanced personal responsibility with broader ethical systems.

This transition didn’t eliminate the tribal mindset; it simply layered new systems on top of it. At heart, humans remained tribal creatures, navigating the world through personal connections and immediate feedback. But modern societies added layers of abstraction—laws, markets, and governments—that were far removed from face-to-face interactions. This shift meant that, over time, the tension between the personal and the abstract, the immediate and the distant, shaped how humans understood their place in the world. Ahmed now finds himself struggling within this very tension, where decisions made by distant, impersonal systems have uprooted his life and forced him into a new world where he must fight for survival.

The Rise of Collective Narratives

As societies grew, so did the stories that held them together. Around 800 BCE to 200 BCE, a period known as the Axial Age, universal religions and philosophies began to offer moral frameworks that applied not just to tribes, but to entire civilizations. This was when systems of thought like Confucianism, Taoism, Hinduism, Buddhism, and monotheistic religions like Judaism emerged. These systems tried to reconcile individual desires with collective needs, offering guidance on how to live a good life within a larger society. For people like Marta, these narratives provided not only a sense of belonging but also a way to understand her place within a larger community.

The development of monotheism was especially impactful. The concept of a single, all-powerful God offered a narrative that could unite people across vast regions under a shared moral and spiritual framework. Christianity and Islam would later spread across continents, creating belief systems that transcended tribal or ethnic identities. These collective narratives provided stability for centuries. They helped societies maintain order by tying individual identity to a larger cosmic order.

But these narratives also required individuals to conform to the collective good. By emphasizing collective stability, they often limited personal freedom. For people like John, who values personal responsibility and individual identity, the pressure to conform could feel suffocating. Social order was maintained through rigid hierarchies and strict moral codes, leaving little room for personal expression or dissent. Those who strayed from the accepted norms faced harsh consequences, which is why Marta’s attachment to her movement's clear boundaries feels reassuring. She finds comfort in a structure that, while restrictive, provides a strong sense of belonging and identity.

The Enlightenment and the Birth of the Modern Individual

The first major rupture in this collective way of thinking came with the Enlightenment in the 17th and 18th centuries. Philosophers like John Locke, Jean-Jacques Rousseau, and Voltaire began to challenge the rigid narratives that had dominated society. They argued for individual rights and freedoms—rights that came from reason and natural law, not from kings or religious authorities. The individual, they claimed, was not merely a cog in a larger machine but a rational being deserving of autonomy. This shift resonates with Rachel’s experience as an activist; her sense of individual agency and responsibility is a legacy of this period.

Enlightenment thinkers aimed to create a world where people could pursue their own happiness while contributing to a stable society. They introduced ideas of democratic governance, capitalism, and human rights—systems meant to balance individual freedom with the collective good. For Rachel, this emphasis on reason and autonomy underpins her activism, as she believes in both systemic change and personal responsibility. However, this elevation of individual rights also began to undermine the shared narratives that once united people. By putting the individual at the center, these ideas set the stage for today’s fractured society.

Religion began to lose its grip on public life, and with it, the shared moral frameworks that had given people a sense of purpose. For Ahmed, the loss of a collective story compounds his struggle. As a refugee, he seeks a sense of belonging, but finds himself in societies where the emphasis on individual rights often leaves him feeling isolated and unwelcome. The focus on individual autonomy has, in some ways, eroded the sense of collective responsibility that might otherwise support someone in his position.

Ultimately, the Enlightenment’s emphasis on individual rights and personal freedom laid the foundation for a world where collective narratives became secondary. The stories that once held people together no longer seem sufficient in a society that values personal choice above all. Now, people like Rachel, John, Ahmed, and Marta each find themselves navigating a world where their individual experiences and beliefs feel isolated from those of others, as the old collective stories no longer provide common ground.

As Rachel, John, Ahmed, and Marta each navigate the complex and shifting world they find themselves in, they’re confronted by the breakdown of the stories that once offered meaning and guidance. The tribal connections that once bound people tightly together have been replaced by vast, impersonal systems that often feel indifferent, even hostile, to their needs. The Enlightenment’s promise of individual freedom brought progress, but it also fractured the shared narratives that once united people across cultures and generations.

Now, they stand on the edge of a new kind of uncertainty. Rachel’s doubts about the impact of her activism, John’s longing for a world that no longer exists, Ahmed’s disillusionment with the promises of fairness and belonging, and Marta’s search for identity in a world she feels has left her behind—all point to a deeper question: what happens when the old answers no longer hold?

Each of them is searching for something solid to stand on, some new story that can help them make sense of a world that no longer fits the old molds. But can they find new ways to connect with each other—or are they destined to remain isolated in their own incommensurate realities, each of them screaming into the void? As the world grows more fragmented and unpredictable, they are each left wondering: is it even possible to rebuild a sense of meaning and belonging, or are they simply chasing shadows in a world that has left them behind?

The answers, if they exist, are just out of reach. The journey into this uncertain terrain has only just begun.
